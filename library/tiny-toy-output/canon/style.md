# Style Guide

## Point of View
Third-person limited, bound tightly to Maren Voss. The narrator has access to what Maren perceives, notices, and does — not to what she feels in any abstract sense, and not to any other character's interior. When the narrative records interiority, it records it as perception or as action, not as sentiment.

## Tense
Simple past throughout.

## Narrative Distance
Close but not confessional. The register is that of a careful naturalist's field note: precise, external, without editorial comment. The narrator does not explain Maren's emotional state; it records what she does with her hands, what she looks at, how long she looks. The reader infers. The narrator does not reach in to help.

> **Correct register:**
> *The lamp clicked over to its new bearing. She noted the time: 03:14. Outside, the fog was thickening, though she could not have said when she had started knowing this.*

> **Wrong register:**
> *She felt a deep, unresolvable unease about the future and what it meant for her sense of herself.*

## Sentence Rhythm
Short to medium declaratives are the default. No sentence should strain for its meaning. Longer sentences, when they appear, are lists of concrete observations — not subordinate-clause analysis. One long sentence earns its keep by accumulating specific details, not by carrying an argument. Variation in length is used for pace: a sequence of short sentences after a longer one reads as stillness after motion.

## Vocabulary
Maritime and mechanical. Weather has exact names: overcast, close, backing southwest, running swell, short steep lop, clear with haze on the horizon. The lamp has parts: Fresnel lens, mercury float, clockwork drum, mantle, chimney glass, paraffin pressure line. Maren does not reach for metaphor the way she would not reach for a tool she doesn't own. She would not think "the sea was silk." She might think "the sea was flat enough to reflect the cloud-line twice — once above, once below — and both were the same gray."

## Dialogue
Sparse. Cal Dresh's visit in Chapter 2 is the only substantial dialogue in the story. It is functional and flat. Its subtext is entirely what is not said. Maren gives less information than she receives. No speech marks should feel loaded — the weight is in the pauses and the subject changes.

## Interior Life
Rendered obliquely through action and attention:
- She winds the clockwork drum a full turn past what it needs.
- She re-copies a logbook entry that has no error in it.
- She does not open the bottle for three days.
- She looks at the barometer twice in an hour, though the reading has not changed.

These are the interiority. The reader is trusted to translate.

## Treatment of the Bottle and the Message
The bottle is described physically and precisely — size, color, wax seal, the folded paper inside — in the same neutral language as any other material object Maren handles. The message's specific content is parceled out carefully: the reader learns what it says only as events make each line relevant, and the full text is not assembled until Maren writes it at the story's close. Do not editorialize about the bottle's impossibility. Maren does not. The narrative does not.

## Chapter Shape
Three chapters of approximately 1,000 words each. Each chapter is bounded or anchored by a lamp-winding event (every four hours), which grounds the story's time. The chapters are:
- **Chapter 1:** The finding. The bottle is discovered; the message is read; Maren says nothing to the radio, nothing to the log.
- **Chapter 2:** The interval. Cal's supply visit. Events the message described begin to arrive. Maren watches.
- **Chapter 3:** The closing. The final predicted event occurs. Maren takes out paper and pen.

## What to Avoid
- The word *felt* used for emotion. (*Noticed*, *recognized*, *understood* are acceptable in their precise senses; "felt a wave of" is never acceptable.)
- Any romantic or elegiac language about the sea.
- Explanation of the bottle's mechanism — causal, supernatural, or speculative. Not even a gesture toward it.
- Exclamation marks.
- Rhetorical questions in the narrative voice.
- Any simile that Maren would not herself form — i.e., any simile that reaches outside her material world.
