# World

## The Island: Harrow Point
A small, treeless island of black basalt and dead gorse, roughly two miles long and a half-mile wide, situated in the North Atlantic approximately 14 nautical miles off the coast of a sparsely populated mainland county. No permanent inhabitants except the lighthouse keeper. In November the days run to eight hours; the storms come in from the southwest, backing to west overnight. The island is known locally for its eastern cliff face, which rises sheer from the sea and catches swells from three directions at once.

## The Lighthouse
The Harrow Point Lighthouse is a white-painted stone tower, 28 meters tall, built in 1891. The lamp room houses a third-order Fresnel lens seated on a mercury float, rotated by a clockwork drum that Maren winds by hand every four hours. The fuel is pressurized paraffin oil; the mantle burns white-hot in a glass chimney behind the lens. A two-room keeper's cottage is joined to the tower base by a covered stone passage: one room for sleeping and eating, one for the logbooks, the barometer, and the radio set. A coal-burning range. An oil lamp for the cottage (the lighthouse mechanism draws no current that could power domestic lights). Single-sideband radio for weather bulletins and emergencies; Maren uses it to receive, almost never to transmit.

## The Keeper's Log
A bound ledger, black ink, every entry dated and timed to the nearest five minutes. Maren records lamp-windings, weather observations, vessels sighted, and any material event. The log is collected by the supply tender and forwarded to the Board of Lights. She writes in it the way other people talk: steadily, without flourish.

## Supply and Contact
The supply tender *Morven* calls every two weeks, weather permitting, landing coal, lamp oil, tinned goods, fresh water in demijohns, mail, and occasionally a Board of Lights notice. The crossing takes three hours in good weather. If storms close the channel, the *Morven* does not come. Maren keeps stores for six weeks. She has a battery-powered torch, a hand-cranked fog signal, and a Very pistol. She has no telephone.

## Geography of the Finding
The eastern shore is the cliff face: a 40-meter drop to a broken boulder shelf that disappears at high tide. A narrow iron stair bolted to the cliff face descends to the boulder shelf — Maren uses it to check the cliff drain and the mooring ring after storms. The bottle will be found wedged in a crack in the basalt at the foot of this stair, above the high-tide line, in the first gray light of a November morning after a southwest gale.

## Atmosphere and Sensorium
The island smells of brine, coal smoke, wet gorse, and lamp oil. Sound: the clockwork clicking in the lamp room overhead, wind pressing under the cottage door, the low grinding of swell on the boulder shelf below. Maren knows the island the way you know a house you have lived in alone for years: by the sounds it makes at 3 a.m., the cold patches on the stone floor, which window rattles in a southwest wind. The color palette of November is gray-white-black: cloud, foam, basalt, lamplight. Warmth is a coal fire and a tin mug. No softness enters uninvited.
