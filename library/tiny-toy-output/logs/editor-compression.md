### Meta-commentary on cognition

**Cut:** `because correcting it would require noticing it`

Full original: *"a habit she had never corrected because correcting it would require noticing it"*
Revised to: *"a habit she had never corrected"*

The concrete detail (the r is a two-stroke gesture she has never fixed) already identifies the handwriting as her own. The explanatory clause reaches in to explain the psychology of self-blind habit — the narrator glossing what the reader can infer.

### Redundant transition / re-summary

**Cut sentence:** *"She knew the *Morven* was due Wednesday."*

Original context: *"The first line said the* Morven *would come on Tuesday, not Wednesday, and that the forward crate would have a cracked demijohn. She knew the* Morven *was due Wednesday."*

"Tuesday, not Wednesday" already encodes her expectation. The following sentence restates that implication explicitly, flattening what "not Wednesday" does on its own.

### Sentence-after-the-sentence

**Cut sentence:** *"She could not see what was or was not in it."*

Original context: *"From this height she could not see the crack. She could not see what was or was not in it."*

The first sentence (physical fact: the crack is invisible from the lamp room) does all the work. The second unpacks what not-seeing-the-crack means — spelling out the choice still pending. The reader already knows what the crack contains. The chapter ends cleanly on the physical observation.

## 02-the-interval

### Meta-commentary on cognition

**Cut 1 — "without deciding to"**
> *Before:* "the low and slightly uneven rhythm she had catalogued without deciding to, over nine years of fortnightly calls"
> *After:* "the low and slightly uneven rhythm she had catalogued, over nine years of fortnightly calls"
>
> Reason: "without deciding to" annotates the involuntary quality of her awareness. The nine-year count of fortnightly calls already does that work; the clause names the mode of a cognition the reader can infer.

**Cut 2 — "before she had fully resolved what she had heard"**
> *Before:* "She was dressed and on the stairs before she had fully resolved what she had heard."
> *After:* "She was dressed and on the stairs."
>
> Reason: The image (dressed, on the stairs) already implies she moved before full waking-consciousness. The subordinate clause labels a cognitive state the action makes self-evident.

## 03-the-writing

### THE SENTENCE-AFTER-THE-SENTENCE (gloss on simile)

**Cut:** `: confirm it, set it down`
**Original:** `She read each entry the way she would check a lamp part: confirm it, set it down.`
**Revised:** `She read each entry the way she would check a lamp part.`
**Reason:** The colon clause explains the simile ("check a lamp part") to the reader. The simile is complete and self-evident in this story's world; the expansion is a gloss.

---

### REDUNDANT TRANSITION / RE-SUMMARY

**Cut:** `and were gone`
**Original:** `watched until the edges caught and the lines darkened and curled and were gone, and then not even the shape of them remained.`
**Revised:** `watched until the edges caught and the lines darkened and curled, and then not even the shape of them remained.`
**Reason:** "were gone" is rendered redundant by the stronger and more complete "not even the shape of them remained," which follows immediately. Leave-one: kept the better formulation.

---

### THESIS RESTATEMENT

**Cut:** `, exactly as it always had`
**Original:** `The lamp turned in the tower, exactly as it always had.`
**Revised:** `The lamp turned in the tower.`
**Reason:** "Exactly as it always had" is a thematic caption on the closing image, directing the reader toward the story's central concern (circularity, continuity). The image is already load-bearing; the clause announces what it is meant to convey. Per the style guide: theme should be load-bearing, never announced.
