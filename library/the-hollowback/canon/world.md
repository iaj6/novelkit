# World

## The Kingdom of Aldenmere

Aldenmere is a functioning human-dominant kingdom somewhere in the middle of its own history — not collapsing, not expanding, just running. It has a professional army, a state Church that issues licenses for things the state Church thinks it should control, a royal charter system that assigns territorial rights to noble houses and the occasional ambitious commoner, and a trade district in every city worth naming that does not much care about any of the above as long as the coin is good.

The kingdom is not the point of the book. It is the frame. The reader learns it by texture: the army's insignia on Corvin's old coat, the Church seal conspicuously absent from Agda's kit, the merchant guild paperwork the party never fills out correctly.

## Thorngate

A mid-sized trading hub, prosperous enough to have two competing cartographers and a street that sells nothing but rope. The trade district is the book's ground floor — the alleys where jobs get posted, the taverns where parties form, the offices of minor merchants who need things done and cannot quite afford to hire real professionals.

The job board where the Ashford contract sat untouched for six days is here. The party assembles here. Maret Ashford's warehouse-front office smells like turpentine and old paper. The city itself is not the setting — it is the departure point — but it has texture: a city that is accustomed to things leaving and not always coming back.

## The Hollowback

The forest east of Aldenmere's settled border. Technically within the kingdom's claimed territory. Practically: not. The trees are old-growth and close. The road the Vethic Empire built through it has been swallowed down to a track that a loaded wagon cannot navigate and a party on foot should navigate only with attention. Charter lines are real but disputed and sometimes enforced by the charter holders themselves rather than any crown official.

The Hollowback has the texture of a place that was once important and is now mostly ignored. Travelers who know what they're doing take the northern route. The ones who take the east road through the Hollowback do it for speed, poverty, or stubbornness. Local people in the nearest border villages know the forest and leave the old station alone.

**Sensory baseline:** Wet bark. The sound of water that cannot quite be located. A specific quality of late-afternoon light through old canopy that makes distances hard to judge and makes travelers feel slightly watched. The ground is soft and holds impressions — it is the kind of forest that remembers it was once a road.

**Fauna:** The normal dangerous kind — boar, bear, opportunistic wolves — and a few things that aren't. The further in you go, the older the strangeness.

## The Eastmark Waystation

A ruin. Built by the Vethic Empire as a garrison and resupply station at what was once a junction of two imperial roads — roads that are now mostly forest floor and conjecture. Three centuries of rain and root-work have dismantled the outer walls and most of the upper structure, but the lower level is intact: engineered Imperial stonework using a setting compound harder than local limestone, assembled with a precision that current masons can observe and cannot replicate.

**Interior layout (rough):** A puzzle-trapped antechamber — the Empire didn't leave its stations undefended even in ruin. Beyond it: a series of vaulted storage and bunk rooms, some collapsed, some not. Deeper: something the Empire was keeping, that the Empire apparently never dealt with before the Empire stopped being a thing.

The waystation appears on some maps as an annotation and on others not at all. It has not been formally excavated by any scholarly body. The locals' collective stance is: it is there; leave it there.

## The Vethic Empire

The civilization that preceded everything currently flying a flag. Three centuries gone, collapsed under pressures that scholars argue about and the Church prefers not to discuss in detail, because the discussion tends to involve what religious practices the Empire maintained and those practices do not fit tidily into current theology.

What it left behind: roads (mostly swallowed), engineered stonework (stubbornly present), a few artifacts that still function and shouldn't, survey notations in a shorthand that most cartographers treat as decorative, and unfinished business.

## Magic

Magic exists, is real, is learned, and costs. The cost is physical: casting draws on the caster's own vital energy. A small spell leaves a wizard light-headed. A large one leaves them incapacitated. A wizard who pushes past threshold risks real harm — nosebleeds first, then worse. The academies that formally train wizards teach threshold management before they teach anything else, because the first-year fatality rate used to be significant.

Sela is an apprentice, which means she has formal knowledge, partial skill, and a threshold she has not fully mapped yet. Her four reliable spells: a light-generation cantrip (low cost, she can hold it for an hour), a minor force-push (medium cost, she has used it wrong twice), a sleep induction that only works on single unaware targets (high cost, unreliable under stress), and something she calls the unraveling — a mechanical-lock dissolution that succeeds about two-thirds of the time and leaves her with a headache either way. Anything beyond these four is improvisation at a cost she cannot predict in advance.

The Church licenses magic use. Unlicensed practitioners — hedge-healers, village cunning-folk, self-taught hedge-wizards — operate in a legal gray zone that varies in enforcement by proximity to the nearest Church official.

## Technology Level

Swords, bows, horses, sailing ships. Inns with fireplaces and bad wine. Paper books that cost money. No gunpowder, no mechanical printing, no clockwork. The Vethic Empire had engineering that looks almost impossible from the current era — load-bearing arches, sewers, the hardened setting compound — and whatever they knew about magic has not been recovered, only approximated.

## Tone of the World

Not grim. Not cynical. The world contains real danger, real injustice, real poverty, real bureaucratic stupidity. It also contains people who do their jobs well, inns that actually have hot food, and forest paths that are beautiful before they become dangerous. Competence matters. Luck is not guaranteed. Bad decisions produce real consequences, not comic consequences. The default register is: the world is difficult and also interesting and these are not in opposition.
