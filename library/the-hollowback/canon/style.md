# Style

## POV and Tense

Third-person limited, past tense. POV rotates across all four party members. Rotation is motivated, not mechanical — each chapter belongs to the character whose emotional or practical situation is most under pressure in that chapter. Sela carries the majority (~6–7 chapters); Corvin, Evren, and Agda each carry two to three.

Do not head-hop within a chapter. Each chapter is one character's interiority from first scene to last. Chapter transitions may shift POV; scene breaks within a chapter do not.

## Narrative Distance

Close. The narration thinks in the rhythms and vocabulary of the POV character. The prose voice is not uniform chapter to chapter — it bends toward whoever is in the seat:

- **Sela's chapters:** Organized, slightly formal, precise. She catalogs. She notices the angle of light and what it means. Her chapters feel like excellent notes taken under pressure.
- **Corvin's chapters:** Spare, tactical, declarative. Short sentences doing physical work. He locates things in space. Combat chapters are his register at its purest.
- **Evren's chapters:** Fast and associative with jokes that arrive mid-observation. Four things happen in quick succession; the fifth is the one that matters. Serious moments are surrounded by the other thing and are therefore starker.
- **Agda's chapters:** Concrete and gruff, with warmth that surfaces in what she does rather than in how she narrates what she does. Dense. Her chapters are the shortest and the most emotionally loaded.

Narrative distance stays close even in action: what the POV character perceives, in the order they perceive it, filtered through their cognitive style. A fight scene in Corvin's chapter reads differently from the same fight in Sela's.

## Sentence Rhythm

Sharp. Sentences move. The default sentence is medium-length, specific, and arrives at something — an image, a fact, a shift. Long sentences earn their length with escalating detail or mounting rhythm. Short sentences and fragments are permitted, especially in action, where they can be the whole unit: *She grabbed the doorframe. Too slow. The thing was already inside.*

Avoid sentences that circle back to where they started. Avoid doubled synonyms ("tired and exhausted," "fast and quick"). Avoid adverbs doing work that verb choice should do. Avoid "-ly" attributions when a specific action can carry the line.

Dialogue attribution defaults to **said** or the verb of whatever the character is physically doing. Clever dialogue tags ("she conceded," "he ventured," "she admitted reluctantly") editorialize and break rhythm.

## Dialogue

Constant and load-bearing. Banter is structural, not decoration — it carries character information, sometimes plot, and always the current state of the relationships. When the party argues, they argue the way people who are learning to trust each other argue, which is distinct from strangers fighting and distinct from close friends bantering.

Each character has a distinct spoken register:

- **Sela:** Precise, slightly careful, often one beat behind the emotional content of what she's actually saying. She is articulate in interiority and slightly more restrained out loud.
- **Corvin:** Dry. Short. Will not say the revealing thing unless there is no other option.
- **Evren:** Fastest speaker at the table. Joke lands first; the real thing comes after, if it comes at all. Has never met a silence he didn't try to fill.
- **Agda:** Direct. Delivers uncomfortable truths with the same flatness she delivers comfortable ones. Her jokes exist and land deadpan. She will use two words where Evren would use twenty.

No character speaks in the other's register. If a line of dialogue could be said by any of the four, it belongs to none of them.

## The Comedy-Stakes Relationship

This book is funny and the danger is real. These are not in opposition and must never be resolved into opposition.

When characters joke through danger: the danger remains present, physical, and specific. The joke is the character's response to that danger — it reveals something about the character. The danger does not become backdrop.

When the stakes land: do not deflate them with a punchline to release the tension. Let the tension land. The next chapter can be funny.

The comedy is not centralized in Evren. Agda's deadpan is its own vehicle. Sela's dry internal observations are funnier than her spoken dialogue in ways she doesn't notice. Corvin is occasionally and unintentionally funny in exactly the way a very competent person who does not think about social performance can be — by being extremely literal at the wrong moment.

## Action Sequences

Physical. Specific. Located in space. The reader should know at all times where everyone is, what they're holding, and what it feels like when it goes wrong. Fight choreography moves by character decision — what did this person choose to do and what happened as a result — not by blow-by-blow catalog. Stakes are present from the first exchange. Something is always at risk beyond the fight itself: information, trust, the party's ability to do something they will need to do later.

## Romance

Subplot. Present throughout, resolved nowhere. Write the tension, not the resolution. A character can notice, want, choose not to act — and that is a complete scene with a real payoff. The near-miss is the mode.

Three pairings, none settled by the final chapter: Sela/Corvin (inter-party slow burn), Evren/Agda (inter-party comedic-becomes-real), Sela/Ren (external, complicated by the mission). Each pairing gets at least three notable scenes. The reader knows, by the end, where everyone stands. The characters mostly don't.

Steamy ceiling: genuine tension, longing, one kiss that costs something. No graphic content.

## Worldbuilding

Emerges through use, never through expository monologue. Terms are explained by what they do, what they look like, or what happens when they're invoked. If a character needs to explain something to another character, they explain only what that character would actually ask about, and they do it the way one person explains to another — not the way a text box explains to a player.

The reader learns what magic costs by watching Sela pay it. The reader learns what Duhr is by watching Agda's rituals. The reader learns what a charter holder does by what this charter holder does to the party.

## Failure Modes

The AI-author patterns most tempting in this story's register. The drafter should actively avoid these; the compression pass should cut them.

- **Meta-commentary on cognition** — Sela's close interiority makes "she noted," "she registered," "she observed," "she realized" deeply tempting. Trust the observation to carry itself. Delete the label. If she saw it, she saw it; the narration doesn't need to announce the seeing.
- **Post-scene subtext naming** — after a romance beat or a character moment, the prose will want to explain what just happened emotionally. Don't. The scene means what it means. The next paragraph is plot. Let the reader carry the weight.
- **Dialogue glosses** — "as if to say," "which was as good as admitting," "in a way that meant." This register wants to honor sharp banter by interpreting it. Resist. The dialogue already said what it said. Gloss is the narrator not trusting the scene.
- **Emotional structural analysis** — especially tempting in Agda's and Corvin's chapters: dramatize a feeling, then explain its component parts ("it was the kind of fear that comes from..."). Name the feeling once or don't name it at all. Do not diagram it.
- **Thesis restatement** — a paragraph near a chapter's end that steps back and re-states the theme. This book's themes are carried by action and dialogue. The narrator does not step forward to label them.

## Lean-Into Patterns

Patterns the drafter and editors should NOT treat as failures, because this register depends on them.

- **Sentence-after-the-sentence as comic engine** — in Evren's POV chapters especially, the setup sentence followed by the sentence that completes or undercuts the joke is structural, not decorative. Do not cut the beat line as over-articulation. It is working. Example: *The room smelled like history. Specifically, the parts of history no one had cleaned.*
- **Corvin's physical inventory** — short declarative sentences locating objects, angles, distances, weapons. This is his voice doing its job, not clipped prose failing to be literary.
- **Agda's flat delivery of warm content** — she will say tender things in a matter-of-fact way. Do not smooth it into performed warmth. The flatness is the point.
- **Sela's cataloging interiority** — her organized observations are not over-articulation; they are character. Her POV chapters are allowed to feel like excellent notes taken by someone paying close attention. This is a feature.
