# Brief

## Premise

A motley party of four young adventurers — a junior wizard's apprentice still learning what magic costs, a former kingdom soldier with a discharge nobody talks about, a half-elf rogue who treats everything as a joke and lies in his sleep about why, and a dwarven hedge-healer whose god the Church doesn't officially recognize — accept the cheapest job nobody else would take: find the missing nineteen-year-old brother of a minor merchant and bring him home.

The job pays badly because everyone in the trade district already knows the brother walked east three weeks ago into the **Hollowback** — a stretch of half-civilized forest on the kingdom's border that contains the ruin of an Imperial waystation older than any kingdom currently flying a flag. The waystation is supposedly empty. The *supposedly* is doing a lot of work in that sentence.

What follows is an episode-by-episode descent through encounters the party did not choose and did not anticipate. A road ambush by deserters. A debate-turned-fight with a hill-dwarf charter holder over the border line. A puzzle-trapped antechamber in the waystation itself. A boss creature that should not exist. And — somewhere in the middle of the ruin — the missing brother, who is alive, who is not particularly interested in being rescued, and whose reasons for being there are going to make the party's contract very complicated.

By the time they leave the Hollowback, they have answered the merchant's question and three other questions they would have preferred not to ask. The party is, against all reasonable expectations, fond of each other. None of the romance subplots that started brewing on the road have resolved. The kingdom does not know yet what is sitting in the Imperial ruin. The party knows.

## Length and shape

- Target length: ~56,000 words
- Number of chapters: 16

Structure is loosely **episodic**. Most chapters center on a single encounter (an ambush, a tavern confrontation, a dungeon room, a chase, a fight). Two or three chapters are travel / character / banter beats between encounters. The final three or four chapters tighten toward the climax inside the ruin.

## Voice

Third-person limited past, **rotating POV across the party**. The wizard's apprentice carries the slight majority of POV chapters (~6–7) — she's the newest party member and the reader's anchor into how the world works. Each of the other three gets two or three POV chapters. POV rotation should be motivated (whose chapter is this *about*?), not mechanical.

The prose register is **sharp, contemporary-coded, comic-aware**. Banter is a constant. Sentences move. Action sequences are physical and specific. Each POV character has a distinct internal voice:

- The **apprentice** thinks in careful, structured observations she rarely speaks out loud. Her interior is more articulate than her dialogue.
- The **soldier** is dry and declarative, sees combat with professional clarity, and hates being asked about his past.
- The **rogue** is the comic engine of the book — fast, deflective, lies for sport, sometimes serious only when no one is looking.
- The **healer** is gruff out loud and warm in private. She has strong opinions about her god and about the Church's opinions of her god.

Comedy and stakes are not at odds. The book should be funny **and** the danger should matter. When characters joke through real danger, the joke does not deflate the danger and the danger does not erase the joke.

Romance is **subplot, not main plot**. Two or three pairings brew across the road; none resolve fully by book's end. Steamy is okay — tension, longing, one kiss landing at a real cost. Graphic is not okay — no on-page sex.

## Constraints

- **YA-readable.** No graphic sex. No torture-pornography violence. Combat is concrete and bloody enough to have stakes, not gratuitous.
- **No chosen-one framing.** The party is competent because they have skills and luck, not because of prophecy. No "you are the only one who can" plot device.
- **No grimdark register.** Things can be serious — the body count is real — but the prose stays warm, witty, and propulsive. Doom-and-gloom interiority is not the mode.
- **Worldbuilding emerges through use, never through expository monologue.** The reader learns what an Imperial waystation is by what's inside it, what a charter holder is by what one does, what kind of god the dwarven healer follows by the rituals she performs.
- **At least four named action set pieces** across the 16 chapters: one road ambush, one dungeon-puzzle room, one combat against something the party shouldn't beat, one confrontation that resolves through character rather than combat.
- **Every action chapter must have real stakes.** No filler combat. If a fight is in the chapter, something is at risk besides hit points.
- **No DM-coded prose.** The narrative does not address the reader as a "player," does not break the fourth wall about D&D mechanics, does not have characters reference rolls or dice. This is a novel set in a fantasy world; it is not a campaign log.
- **Three named romance pairings, none resolved.** Two should be inter-party (within the four) and one should reach outside the party (an NPC met during the journey). Each pairing gets at least three notable scenes across the book. By the final chapter, none are settled — the reader knows where everyone stands; the characters mostly don't.
- **The party is fond of each other by the end.** Whatever else happens, the relationship arc across the book is "strangers → reluctant colleagues → people who would actually die for each other." Earned, not declared.
- **At least one moment of real awe.** Somewhere in the Hollowback, the party stops and the prose breathes — they see something the world hasn't shown them before. Wonder is permitted; it must be earned by what surrounds it.
- **The brother is not a damsel and not a villain.** He is a nineteen-year-old who made a complicated choice and is living with it. His reasons are real.

## Genre touchpoints (for register, not plagiarism)

The register the book wants to live in: the middle hours of a good Critical Role session translated to YA — banter, real stakes, low-key brewing romance, a GM who actually built the dungeon, action that lands. Closer comps: Leigh Bardugo's *Six of Crows* for ensemble voice and structure; Naomi Novik's Scholomance for the fun-and-dark-at-the-same-time tonal balance; early *Mistborn* for the heist-crew-as-protagonist energy. Not Dragonlance's solemnity; not adult grimdark; not romance-forward YA fantasy.
