# Chapter 10 — Found

**One-line summary:** They find Ren. He argues in complete sentences. He shows them why he stayed.

**POV character:** Sela Vane
**Tense:** Past
**Setting / time:** The Eastmark Waystation, lower level — a passage deeper than Ren's workspace, and then the level below. Day 5, dusk. They can no longer see the entry.
**Target words:** ~4,000

---

## Chapter Shape
**Single scene.** Near real-time. The party follows the sound and Ren's maps into the sub-level and finds Ren. The entire chapter is this encounter: finding him, his response to being found, the argument that follows, and the thing he shows them at the end. Sela's POV is correct for this chapter — she is the one who will have the most complex response to Ren, and she is the reader's anchor into how the world works; she should be the one through whom Ren is first fully rendered.

## Ending Mode
**Turn.** Ren shows them what's in the containment chamber. They now know what he knows. The contract — find the brother and bring him home — has become considerably more complicated.

---

## Goal
Find Ren. Convince him to come home. Complete the contract.

## Obstacle
Ren doesn't want to be convinced. He has reasons. The reasons are real. And once he shows them what he found, the contract's simplicity is no longer available to anyone.

## Action Beats

1. **Finding him.** The sound leads them down a secondary stair, rougher than the main entry, clearly a service passage rather than a designed approach. Two levels below Ren's workspace: a room that was a Vethic archive, mostly collapsed except for one corner where a lantern is burning. Ren Ashford is at a writing desk made from salvaged rubble and board. He hears them coming and does not look panicked — he looks up, registers that there are four of them, and says: *You're from my sister.* Statement, not question. He does not stand up.

2. **First impressions.** He is nineteen, which means he is younger than he thinks he is, but he carries himself like someone who has been alone in a serious place for three weeks and has made peace with it. He is dressed practically: camp clothes, good boots, a cartographer's case on the table beside him. He is a genuinely bad swordsman — Sela can see a short sword propped against the desk, its angle indicating someone who doesn't reach for it often and hasn't cleaned it recently. He is not afraid of them. He is, she observes with some precision, slightly irritated to see them.

3. **The contract argument.** Corvin delivers the contract's terms in clear declarative language: they were hired to find Ren and bring him home to Maret. The job is to come home with them. Ren says he left a note saying he'd be back. Corvin says three weeks is beyond what the note promised. Ren says: *I found something I cannot leave unfinished.* Corvin says: *That's not a counter-argument.* Ren says it actually is and explains his position in complete sentences. He does not ask for help. He does not claim he cannot manage alone. He says: there is something in this waystation that requires documentation before any decision can be made about it, and he is nearly done, and he will come home when the documentation is complete.

4. **Evren's interruption.** Evren asks: *What something?* This cuts through the contract argument in a way that Corvin's practical pressure couldn't, because Evren is not asking bureaucratically — he is asking with the specific quality of someone who has a theory and wants to know if he's right. Ren looks at Evren. Something shifts in Ren's posture — he was not expecting someone in this rescue party to ask the substantive question. He asks Evren where he's from. Evren says: *Thorngate.* A beat. They both know that's not an answer to the question Ren was actually asking.

5. **Sela asks the real question.** She says: *Your maps show a containment chamber three levels down. The notation next to it says "it is still here." What is it?* Ren looks at her. This is the first time he's looked at her the way he's been looking at Corvin and Evren — as a specific person and not as a rescue-party member. He says: *How did you read those maps?* She says: *Evren read them. I translated from his read.* Ren says: *Evren can read Vethic notation?* He looks at Evren with considerably more interest than before. Evren says nothing. Sela watches this exchange and notes: Ren is not alarmed by the notation fluency. He is interested. He files people by what they know.

6. **Ren decides.** Ren looks at his maps for a long moment. Then he says he'll show them, because they've come this far and because a decision this large should not be made by one person in a basement, and he has been thinking for a week that he needs someone else to look at it with him. He picks up the lantern. *Three levels down. Stay close. Don't touch the walls past the second landing.*

7. **The approach to the containment chamber.** Narrow stairs, hand-cut, older than the garrison rooms — Vethic Imperial construction from the station's founding, not modified. The walls here have a different quality: the pale stone is not just dry and cold, it has something in it, a faint grain or structure that Sela's light cantrip catches wrong. She keeps the cantrip on and feels it pull slightly more than it should. She does not comment. Her headache from the antechamber has been fading but does not fully fade here. The gradient of the staircase is descending below the water table, she estimates, which the stonework is dealing with without visible joints or seepage. The Empire built this to last.

8. **The containment chamber door.** Iron, massive, significantly heavier than the antechamber door — multiple locking mechanisms, all open, because Ren has been through here dozens of times. Beyond it: a vaulted room, larger than anything above, with something in the center. Sela holds up her light. The something is visible but not immediately legible — a structure of Vethic metal and stone that is clearly a purpose-built housing, like a cage but more precise. Geometric. Inside it: something that does not move but that is not still. There is a quality to the air inside the cage structure that is the ozone-before-a-storm smell from the upper level, concentrated. And inside the cage, at its center — something human-shaped. Mostly. *There,* Ren says. *That's what I found.*

---

## Outcome / Turn
Ren has been found. He does not want to come home yet. He has shown them what he found: the containment chamber, the cage structure, the thing inside it. The party now knows what Ren knows — or the beginning of it. The contract's simplicity is over. What happens next requires a different kind of conversation.

## New Question / Pressure
What is the thing in the cage? How long has it been there? Is the containment still holding? (The answer to the third question is: not for much longer. But they don't know this yet.)

---

## Continuity Notes
- Ren is introduced exactly as the brief requires: not a damsel, not a villain. He is nineteen, earnest, slightly younger than he thinks he is, argumentative in complete sentences. He has been alone for three weeks by choice. He is not afraid of these people, and he is not wrong about the substantive issue.
- Sela's magic: the light cantrip is pulling slightly more than it should near the containment chamber. This is the Hollowed One affecting the local magical gradient — it wants energy and the cantrip's output is a signal. Sela notes this but cannot explain it yet.
- Ren's interest in who can read Vethic notation: he is a cartographer's apprentice who reads it himself. He immediately reframes Evren from "rescue-party member" to "possible collaborator" when he learns about the notation.
- The Hollowed One is introduced here visually but without a name or a full explanation — human-shaped, not still, inside a precision cage structure. The explanation comes in Ch 11.

## Romance Threads in This Chapter
- **Sela/Ren:** First major beat. She asks the real question; he looks at her as a specific person. She observes him filing people by what they know — and notes that this is a thing she does too. She finds him interesting in a way that alarms her. The alarm is informative. Neither of them does anything about this.
