## 03-the-question-nobody-asked.md

**Borderline pathetic fallacy — needs writer's judgment**

The two sentences closing the post-school outdoor section read:

> The wind had come around to the northwest. The harbor smell was gone.

The harbor smell was established in the chapter's opening paragraph as a specific morning datum Marcus incorporates and does not stop noticing. Its absence here is placed directly after the passage in which Marcus's thinking approaches "a door" concerning the dead man's probable provenance — i.e., immediately after the chapter's most charged interior moment. The symmetry (harbor smell present at opening → harbor smell gone at emotional turning point) risks reading as pathetic fallacy, which `style.md` prohibits outright: "the weather does not mirror the emotion."

A meteorological argument can be made in defense: the wind shifting from east to northwest is accurate cause for the smell to disperse, and Marcus is the kind of observer who would note it. But the placement — following the Sherbrooke-adjacent interior and preceding the section break — weights the two sentences with emotional resonance the prose should not be carrying.

This cannot be fixed at the word or clause level. The sentences themselves are fine in isolation; the problem is structural (position relative to the charged interior passage). Options for the writer: move the weather observation earlier in the scene (before Marcus's grading interior), or add one neutral functional sentence between the interior and the outdoor observation to break the implied connection.

**Fixes applied in this pass (both line-level):**

1. `"Professionally speaking," he added.` → `"Professionally speaking," he said.` — `added` is a non-permitted dialogue tag per style guide.
2. Two instances of `Marcus said, not looking up from the board:` — stage directions embedded in dialogue tags, prohibited by style guide. Both separated into prior action + plain tag:

**Present-tense intrusions inside free indirect discourse.** `style.md` prohibits all present-tense intrusions except period documents. Two clauses in this chapter are in present tense:

1. *"the same way he had taken in the split post: here is the situation; what does it require."*
2. *"the assessment moving from the post to her and then settling somewhere practical, like: this is the situation now; this is what it is."*

The effect is strong; the rule violation is real.

## 06-english-only.md

**Chapter POV:** Marcus Klein.

---

### Issue 1 — Logically contradictory sentence, unfixable locally (flags Marcus's precision)

> *"But a fifth of what it might otherwise have been was still the remaining four-fifths, and the position was still a position worth occupying."*

As written this is self-contradictory: a class reduced to "a fifth of what it might otherwise have been" has lost *four*-fifths of its value, not one-fifth. Saying it "was still the remaining four-fifths" inverts the arithmetic. For Marcus — whose defining register is exactness and whose interior monologue here is explicitly a precise accounting of what can be preserved — an internally confused fraction is a voice failure. The intended meaning is probably one of two things:

- The class loses roughly a fifth of its value; the remaining four-fifths is worth having. In that case: *"But the class would retain four-fifths of what it might otherwise have been, and the position was still a position worth occupying."*
- The class is reduced to a fifth of its potential, and even that fifth is worth occupying. In that case: *"But even a fifth of what it might otherwise have been was still a position worth occupying."*

Cannot fix without knowing which proportion the writer intends. Needs writer's judgment.

---

### Two small voice fixes applied (see write_file)

1. **"What he felt was not that he lacked an opinion. What he felt was that the opinion that had formed was not a teacher's assessment."** — The doubled *What he felt was* foregrounds emotion in a way that sits off-register for Marcus, who categorizes rather than confesses. Fixed to: *"It was not that he lacked an opinion. The opinion that had formed was not a teacher's assessment."*

2. **"She was writing about a poem in a textbook, and what she had seen was the thing he had not found adequate words for."** — "had not found adequate words for" is slightly soft and confessional for Marcus, who has just established (in the same paragraph) that he *has* the vocabulary — the lack is something prior. Fixed to: *"She was writing about a poem in a textbook, and she had the language for it."* This also creates a clean implicit contrast with the earlier self-observation ("he had the vocabulary") and echoes Hannah's own figure of the home/housing of meaning.

## 18-the-columbine.md

**Unfixable at line level — writer judgment needed.**

The anger paragraph (beginning "The anger came up specific and without surprise") contains a metaphor that draws attention to itself and has Hannah performing analytical self-articulation that sits outside her established register:

> "…the columbine on the post had walked through her carefulness without breaking it, just changed its grammar, the way adding one word to a sentence changes what the sentence is saying."

Two problems that cannot be fixed by changing a word or clause:

1. **The metaphor is literary in a way Hannah's voice is not.** The sentence-grammar conceit is clever and writerly; it reads as the author stepping in to gloss Hannah's feeling rather than Hannah experiencing it. Per `style.md`, the sea (and by extension all concrete detail) "is not a metaphor" — and here a fence flower is being converted into a grammar lesson. Hannah's register digresses from things she is *touching*; this sentence floats into abstraction and then names the abstraction.

2. **The surrounding sentences compound the problem.** "She had been careful about it, more careful than she had let herself notice" (fixed from "acknowledged to herself until now") is improved but still describes Hannah catching herself in an act of self-knowledge — which is close to a character explaining herself to herself. And "she was angry about what that meant and angrier about the fact that she had no intention of throwing it away" closes the paragraph by narrating her own future intentions, which the reader can observe directly from her action.

The *action* in this scene is beautiful and exactly on register: picking up the flower, carrying it to the house, pressing it in the Duden. The problem is the paragraph that explains what the action means. Suggestion: trim the anger paragraph to the anger itself ("The anger came up specific and without surprise") and trust the Duden scene to do the rest. But this is a structural cut, not a line fix.
