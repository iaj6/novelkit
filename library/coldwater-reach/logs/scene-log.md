
## 01-the-night-watch
**Summary:** Eira performs her post-blow 2 a.m. rounds on the night of October 11, 1943. On the north end of the wharf she sees an unidentified man in a continental-cut wool coat standing near the far mooring bollard. She decides not to hail him — the pause has already happened before she has a name for what she's looking at. When she looks back from the landward corner, he is gone. She writes the routine log entry and nothing else. Over the following two days the blow continues and the harbor resumes its rhythm; she tells herself she isn't thinking about the man. On the morning of October 14, Dakin the lobsterman knocks on the office door: he has found a body on the north breakwater. Eira goes out with him, observes the body before the Coast Guard arrives, and notes: European-cut wool coat, smooth-knuckled hands with ink under two fingernails, age approximately 40–55, no papers in outer pockets. She does not touch the body. Two Coast Guard men from Rockland — petty officer Farris and an unnamed seaman — arrive, take her notebook, ask four questions. She answers each accurately. She does not mention October 11. The body is removed. She stands on the empty breakwater, then returns to the office and writes the closing log entry. Chapter ends at the log entry and the shortwave turned down.
**New facts:**
- Eira's 2 a.m. post-blow rounds are a routine she kept from Owen Bowman, who instituted them after a mooring line failure in their first winter.
- October 11, 1943 log entry (exact text): 'Mooring lines north wharf — all secure. Wind NW 12 knots.' Nothing about the man.
- October 14, 1943 log entry (exact text): 'Body discovered on north breakwater, 0612. USCG notified and present 0740. Body removed 0830. Breakwater clear.'
- The body was on the north side of the breakwater, approximately ninety feet from the landward end, resting at the high-tide mark.
- Body details as Eira observed: continental-cut dark wool coat (charcoal or dark brown), green-tarnished buttons, smooth-knuckled hands, no rope callus, dark substance (ink or ink-like) under first two fingernails of the right hand, age estimated 40–55, face water-damaged, no papers in outer pockets.
- Coast Guard response: petty officer Farris and one unnamed seaman from the Rockland station. Farris took Eira's notebook and asked four questions. He said they would be in touch.
- Dakin (lobsterman, age 61) found the body at first light on October 14 and came directly to the harbormaster's office. He left the body undisturbed.
- The Lavoie dragger (captain Rémi Lavoie) was in the north slip during the blow. He went out on the afternoon of October 12 against Eira's advice and returned with nothing.
**Loose threads:**
- Who was the man on the north wharf at 2 a.m. on October 11? Is he the same person as the body?
- Why didn't Eira hail him — and is the pause-before-hesitation a pattern she will recognize in herself later?
- Eira has now withheld October 11 from the Coast Guard. The omission presented itself as answering within the perimeter of what was asked. She has not named it as a decision — but it has become one.
- Farris said he would be in touch from Rockland. What does their investigation turn up, and when does he return?
- The body's identity: European coat, desk-worker's hands, ink under the fingernails. No papers in outer pockets. Inside pockets unchecked. Thread 1 (Eira's Witness) enters here and will run through all ten of her chapters.

## 02-arrival

## 04-what-the-ledger-holds
**Summary:** Early November 1943. Lt. Farris arrives at the harbormaster's office with the Rockland analyst's report: the dead man's coat and jacket were Continental European manufacture, probably French or German, prewar (made before 1940), with a replaced lining; no papers; one distinguishing mark, a scar on the palm of the left hand, old and well-healed. Farris asks his questions about the days before the blow — unusual vessel activity, unregistered traffic, individuals on the dock not belonging to a registered vessel, the north wharf specifically. Eira answers each question accurately and within the perimeter of what was asked. She does not mention October 11. Farris delivers the revision to the time-of-death estimate: the man was in the water closer to one day than three; he died at the tail end of the blow or after it, not during the blow's peak; he did not come off a vessel in the storm. The Coast Guard is treating it as accidental death, probable drowning, pending further inquiry. After Farris leaves, Eira takes the harbor log from the shelf and reads back through the first two weeks of October, searching for anything she missed — unusual mooring requests, fuel purchases, unregistered transient traffic. There is nothing she cannot account for. She reaches the October 11 entry and reads it three times: 'Mooring lines north wharf — all secure. Wind NW 12 knots.' Afternoon: physical work on the dock — a dragging mooring float, the fender pilings, the breakwater's two displaced facing stones. She encounters Dakin briefly (bearing problem on his pot hauler; she notes he will need the part before month's end). She walks to the north bollard on the north wharf and finds nothing — the blow has scoured the granite clean. In the evening, she assembles in her mind — not in the log — what she now has: European coat, prewar, scar on left palm, no papers, desk-worker's hands, dark matter under the first two right-hand nails. Combined with October 11: a man matching that description, alive, on the north wharf. Combined with Farris's revision: the man was alive through the entire blow, sheltering somewhere, and died at its tail. The man was alive when she saw him. This arithmetic is new. The log entry is unchanged. She turns up the shortwave for the evening weather report, then turns it back down, and goes out to check the evening tide.
**New facts:**

## 05-the-bavarian-accent

## 06-english-only
**Summary:** Marcus's chapter. Mid-to-late November 1943. Wakefield stops him in the corridor before first period and delivers the school board's instruction: modern language classes are to be conducted in English — a wartime measure, in practice applied only to German. Marcus asks how he shall teach pronunciation without speaking German; Wakefield offers sympathy and walks away. Marcus sits at his desk between Latin periods and thinks through the instruction with characteristic precision: it is legal, it is defensible, it is also designed to make German instruction nearly impossible. He decides — without performing the decision — that he can preserve four-fifths of the class's function by writing German on the board, calling on students to read aloud, and responding in English. He teaches German that afternoon in English for fifty straight minutes. He calls on Hannah Pfeiffer to read a textbook passage; she reads accurately and with native-register pronunciation because her father speaks German at home. Marcus says "Correct." No one in the class notices he has not spoken a word of German. After class, Hannah lingers a moment; he tells her her accent is very good; she says her father speaks German at home; a beat; she leaves. Evening: Marcus returns to the Crow house and finds Henry at the kitchen table mending a coat. Henry tells him, without preamble, that his name came up at the board meeting. Alderman Marsh asked whether the school had done a thorough review of all personnel with European backgrounds; Wakefield said Marcus had been vetted and had his papers; Marsh said he wasn't talking about papers and repeated the phrase "thorough review" in the manner of a man waiting for someone else to name his specific concern; Wakefield defended Marcus on grounds of professional utility — "the only German instructor within thirty miles" — and Marsh let it go. She did not answer the assigned prompt. She wrote instead about what happens to meaning when a poem is translated: the approximate thing survives; the poem is made of the difference between close and exactly; what is lost is not the meaning but "the home of the meaning." Marcus reads it three times. He recognizes in her perception something he has been carrying without adequate language. He picks up his pen and writes in the margin: *See me.* He crosses it out. He writes it again.

## 07-december-protocol
**Summary:** Early December 1943. Eira and her two dockhands — Ames (64) and Shep (15) — work through the harbor winterization from six in the morning: pulling the seventeen summer mooring field cans, setting the nine winter-field markers, inspecting and painting the six vulnerable south dock pilings with orange bands following Owen's December protocol. At noon a government sedan with a Portland plate pulls up. The man who enters introduces himself as Hale, Naval Intelligence, Portland district; he shows his card but pockets it before she can read it fully. He is younger than Farris, smooth, trained in the widening-then-narrowing method of questioning. He tells her the Portland office's working theory: the dead man crossed from the Canadian Maritime Provinces — Nova Scotia or New Brunswick — by small unregistered craft. He reads the October harbor log entry by entry, notes the October 11 and October 14 entries, and then asks whether she saw anything on her rounds that didn't make it into the log. Her answer: "I record what I find. If I'd found something significant, it would be in the log." This is true in the shape she has arranged. He closes his notebook and says he may be back. After he leaves, Eira sits at the desk with the BC-1206 on (northeast wind, clearing by evening) and holds Hale's information against what she already carried — the calculation was pointing this direction without reaching the number; Hale has given her the number. She does not revise the October 11 log entry. She returns to the harbor in the afternoon: corrects two of Ames's misplaced winter field floats (wading knee-deep into December water at position three), works through items four through twelve of Owen's four-page winter protocol document. Shep finds two flex points in the south dock fenders. At four o'clock the light is gone; she locks the office; she drives home.

He tells her: back around October 10–11, before the blow, he was hauling traps off the north beach at low tide and found a dory pulled above the tide line — small, foreign-built (wrong planking, thwarts in wrong place), no markings he knew. He hadn't touched it. A canvas bag under the bow thwart, unexamined. He thought someone was sheltering; he put it together after the body came in and Farris came around asking, but by then it seemed too long past. Eira thanks him and he leaves. She closes the office and walks to the north breakwater beach at low tide. The beach is clean — nothing from October remains. She stands where the dory would have been and does the geometry: north breakwater beach (landing), north wharf (where she saw him at 2 a.m.), breakwater (where the body came in) — all on the same side of the harbor, a northwest line of roughly three hundred yards. The man moved east to west along it, or didn't, or the blow caught him somewhere between. She thinks about the canvas bag. She does not know where the dory is now: the Coast Guard didn't mention it, it may have been taken by the weather, someone may have moved it. She returns to the office and makes a log entry: non-Maine dory, north beach, approx. Oct. 10–11, not registered, not reported at time, canvas bag unexamined, no remains present on inspection. The log entry is oblique but in the record — the first entry that touches October's perimeter beyond the body discovery itself. She sits back and thinks about the canvas bag; what was in it she will likely never know. She returns to the registry.
**New facts:**
- Dakin's dragger is named the Alice D. She has been in the covered slip since before Thanksgiving 1943, moored at Berth 9S.
- Dakin observed a non-Maine-built dory pulled above the tide line on the north breakwater beach approximately October 10–11, 1943 — before the three-day blow. He did not touch it and did not report it at the time.
- The dory was foreign-built: planking and thwart placement differed from Maine construction. Dakin had no context for its origin. He assumed a sheltering fisherman.
- A canvas bag was stowed under the bow thwart of the dory. Dakin did not examine it. Its contents are unknown and will remain so.
- The geometry of the crossing: north breakwater beach (accessible at low tide, out of sight of harbor proper) served as the landing site; the north wharf (where Eira saw the man at 2 a.m. on Oct. 11) is at the landward end of the same breakwater; the breakwater itself is where the body came in. All three points lie on the same northwest-running side of the harbor, within roughly 300 yards.
- Eira walked to the north breakwater beach at low tide (approximately 11:40 a.m., late March 1944) and confirmed no physical remains of the dory or any October material were present after six months of winter tides.
- Eira entered the following in the harbor log: 'Dakin, A. reports small craft (dory, non-Maine build) observed on north breakwater beach, above tide line, approx. Oct. 10–11, 1943. Not registered. Not reported at time of observation. Canvas bag under bow thwart, unexamined. No remains of craft present on inspection today, 11:40 AM, low tide. B.' This is the first log entry that refers, even obliquely, to the October circumstances surrounding the body.
- The Coast Guard, when they came for the body, made no mention of a dory to Eira. Either they did not find it (weather may have taken it), found it and did not tell her, or someone else had moved it in the interval.
- Eira notes she should ask Dakin whether he saw the dory again after October 11 — she does not act on this in the chapter.
- Ames is Eira's deputy/relief harbormaster who covers mid-day shifts; he is competent and his log entries are correct.
**Loose threads:**
- What was in the canvas bag under the bow thwart? Established as a deliberate permanent unknown — one of the novel's unresolved threads.
- What happened to the dory after October 11? Three possibilities: taken by the blow, taken by the Coast Guard without informing Eira, moved by a third party. None confirmed.
- Did the Coast Guard find the dory? Their silence on it to Eira is itself information, but the reason for that silence is not established.
- Eira intends to ask Dakin whether he saw the dory again after October 11 — this question is deferred, not pursued in this chapter.
- The log entry is now in the record, oblique but present. Does it create future exposure? A later reader with context (Farris, the December Naval Intelligence note) could assemble something from it.
- Thread 1 (Eira's Witness): advanced — the dory closes the geographic picture of the crossing; the canvas bag is the new loose thread; the log entry is the chapter's turn — she has written something connected to October for the first time. October 11 itself remains unlogged.

## 15-wills-letter
**Summary:** Hannah's chapter. April 1944. She walks the four miles home without opening it. At the kitchen table she reads it twice. She is holding it when Albrecht comes in from the field. She gives it to him. He reads it standing at the sink, coat still on. He puts the letter on the table, sits down, and cries — barely a sound, shoulders moving, one hand over his face. Hannah looks out the window at the dogwood. She does not go to him: this is knowledge of him, not coldness. After roughly two minutes he takes his hand from his face and says, in German, to himself as much as to her: Er lebt. Das ist genug. She picks up her hoe and walks toward the barn. Passing the fence at fifteen yards, without stopping or looking over, she says: Er lebt. She does not know if he heard. She goes into the barn. She stands in the barn aisle and thinks her father's full sentence — Er lebt. Das ist genug. — and notes that she said only the first half, and that she is not sure about the second. She goes to start the milking.

## 16-licensing

When Eira asks why he is telling her this, he says: because he thinks she may already know it, and because he doesn't want to be the only one carrying it. He thinks in German: Die Last ist real, aber sie ist nicht neu. He looks at the swollen bottom drawer when he returns to his classroom but does not open it. ## 17-the-log-entry
**Summary:** May 1944. The *Sainte-Marguerite*, a small lumber freighter out of Moncton, puts in for a half-day transit stop. A young French-Canadian crewman correctly identifies a misplaced bow fender and speaks to Eira about it — his English correct but formal, the English of a man thinking in French. This does not resolve anything in the moment; Eira processes the transit and completes a full afternoon of harbor work. But the quality of the sailor's English — the same careful construction she heard from Marcus Klein ten days ago — follows her. At four-thirty she puts the harbor log in her bag and takes it home, which she almost never does. At home she stalls: dinner, dishes, kettle, tea. Then she opens the log to October 11 and reads: *Mooring lines north wharf — all secure. Wind NW 12 knots.* She reads forward through October 12, 13, and 14 — the body entry. She sits with what she put in and what she left out: the man on the north wharf is not in the log; the visit at 2 a.m. is not in the log. Marcus Klein's parting line enters her again — *not anyone's fault, or not anyone here* — and she holds it without resolution. She closes the log at eight-thirty, washes the teacup, goes to bed. In the dark, turning over the north wharf scene one more time, the thing she has never processed arrives: he was silent because he was afraid of her. Not of her specifically — of what she represented: the lantern, the coat, the official markings, the apparatus of authority in a country he had no claim to. She thinks it once, in a single flat sentence, and it settles. She does not run the October sequence again. She sleeps.
**New facts:**
- The *Sainte-Marguerite* is a small coastal lumber freighter (approximately 110 feet) out of Moncton, New Brunswick, captained by a man named Aubert. She runs lumber south to Portland and makes regular transit stops at Coldwater Reach — this is at minimum her third season calling here.
- A young French-Canadian crewman aboard the *Sainte-Marguerite* speaks to Eira about a misplaced port bow fender (eye-bolt threaded too high on the cleat). He is correct. His English is Acadian-accented, formally learned, the grammar of a man translating as he speaks.
- Eira takes the harbor log home on a Tuesday evening in May 1944 — the first time in approximately twelve years as harbormaster that she has taken the log home.
- Eira reads October 11 (*Mooring lines north wharf — all secure. Wind NW 12 knots.*), then October 12, 13, and 14 (the body entry) in sequence, at her kitchen table. This is the second time the October 11 entry appears verbatim in the narrative.
- Eira consciously inventories what she put in the log and what she left out: the 2 a.m. north wharf check, the man she saw, her decision not to hail him — none of it is in the log.
- Eira's final interpretive piece: the man on the north wharf did not speak because he was afraid of what she represented — a figure with a lantern, an official coat, harbor authority — in a country he had arrived in without papers or claim. He went still and silent because that was the safer answer. She thinks: *He was afraid of me.* The thought settles without requiring action.
- The *Eleanor T.* out of Berth 6 is a converted hauler that has been carrying cod all winter; her captain is named Vose. She registered an equipment change in May 1944.
- Eira's house on Merchant Street: two blocks from the wharf, a cape. The front room has Owen's reading chair in the corner — brown leather, worn patch on the arm where his elbow rested. Eira does not look at it directly in her home scenes.
**Loose threads:**
- If the man was afraid of Eira as an authority figure, he may have been afraid of whoever came next — or afraid to approach whoever was supposed to meet him. Did someone fail to come?
- Was the man supposed to be picked up from the north wharf that night, or from a different point? The fear interpretation suggests he was waiting, not acting — which implies a contact who didn't arrive.
- Does Marcus know specific individuals in the sponsored-passage network — names who might have been the man's intended contact in Coldwater or the surrounding area?
- Eira now holds two things simultaneously: the settled fact (he was afraid of her) and Marcus's absolution (not anyone's fault, or not anyone here). These are not contradictory, but they are not the same. She has not decided what to do with either.

## 18-the-columbine

## 19-which-end
