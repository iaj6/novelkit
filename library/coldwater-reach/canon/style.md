# Style Guide

## Governing Principles

**POV:** Third-person limited past tense throughout. The narrative never leaves the head of the chapter's assigned POV character. No information the POV character could not have perceived or inferred. No head-hopping within a chapter, ever.

**Tense:** Past simple. No present-tense intrusions except in period documents rendered verbatim (V-Mail text, ledger entries, official notices).

**Narrative distance:** Close but not confessional. The camera sits slightly behind the POV character's eyes — we see what they see, feel what they feel, but the prose does not editorialize on their behalf. Trust the reader to feel what is not said. This is the single most important stylistic directive.

**Reflection vs. action:** Default to action, observation, and dialogue. Extended interior monologue should be rare and earned. When a character thinks, the thought should arise from something they are doing or have just done — not from a pause in the action designed to host reflection.

**Sentence rhythm:** Varied but weighted toward the short and declarative, especially in Eira's chapters. No sentence should be longer than the thought it is carrying. Avoid nested subordinate clauses for effect. Avoid free indirect discourse that turns too literary too fast.

**Dialogue:** Clipped across all three voices — period characters did not explain themselves at length. No dialogue tag other than *said* or *asked* unless the tone is genuinely ambiguous. No stage directions embedded in dialogue (*she said, turning to face him with a look of quiet desperation*). Let the line carry the weight.

**Period texture:** Use accurate period detail. Resist the urge to explain it. If a character picks up a ration book, write what they do with it, not what a ration book is. The reader is trusted to look up what they don't know. Never use an anachronistic word, phrase, or social assumption.

## Voice by Character

### Eira's Chapters

**Register:** Sparse. Technical. Procedural. Eira thinks in the language of harbor management: draft, displacement, tide tables, freeboard, weather windows, mooring configuration, licensing categories.

**Interior:** Minimal. When Eira reflects, it is brief and immediately converted into action or practical assessment. Her grief and her love and her anger live inside her professional judgments. If she is afraid, she measures something. If she is grieving, she runs through a checklist.

**Sentences:** Short to medium. Subject-verb-object. Few adverbs. She does not say *she felt a cold fear settle in her chest*; she says *she put down the log and went to the window.*

**The sea:** It is a workplace. It is not a metaphor. The harbor appears in specific, functional terms: the state of the tide, the behavior of the mooring lines, the color of the sky that means weather, the height of the chop. Any sentence that starts treating the sea as symbolic should be cut.

**Dialogue:** Clipped to the edge of abrupt. Eira says the minimum necessary. She is not cold; she is economical. Pauses and silences in her dialogue should be written as actions, not stage directions.

**Example register:**  
> The southeast corner mooring had dragged two feet. She noted it in the log and went to tell Dakin before he ran it on the tide. The fog was already in.

### Hannah's Chapters

**Register:** The most interior and porous of the three. Hannah's consciousness is allowed to be louder — she is seventeen, she is restless, she is paying attention to everything without knowing which things matter.

**German:** German words and phrases appear in Hannah's chapters wherever they would naturally surface in a bilingual childhood — her father's idioms, the names of things as she learned them at home, phrases she finds herself thinking in before she can find the English. These are italicized and never translated via the prose. Context must carry meaning, or let it stay untranslated. No phonetic spellings; no burlesque of accent.

**Interior:** More permitted than in Eira's or Marcus's chapters. Hannah can brood, digress, circle back. The farm is the world, and her narration should convey the way a specific physical world can feel infinite when you have never left it. But: her interior should always be anchored in sensory fact. She does not float; she digresses from something she is touching.

**Sentences:** More varied than Eira's — longer when she is absorbed, shorter when she is alarmed. Her prose can carry a run-on quality when her thoughts are racing, but it should not become stream of consciousness. The observer's intelligence underneath should always be findable.

**Dialogue:** Hannah's dialogue is more exploratory than Eira's. She asks more questions. When she speaks to Karl, the exchange is in German; render it in English but make clear it is German being spoken (an initial tag is enough; thereafter the prose assumes it).

**Example register:**  
> She brought the pail out at six and the prisoners were already up. Karl was at the far fence, looking at nothing she could see — just the tree line, the way it went pale before the sun did. *Der Morgen zieht sich*, her father used to say. Morning takes its time. She set the pail down and he didn't look over.

### Marcus's Chapters

**Register:** Precise, measured, slightly formal. Marcus's narration is the English of a man who learned it as an adult, trusted it for its precision, and has used that trust as a form of armor. His prose is exact in the way that academic prose is exact — he distinguishes; he qualifies; he refuses vagueness.

**Interior:** More present than Eira's, more controlled than Hannah's. Marcus thinks in argument-structures — he proposes, then questions, then qualifies. When he is uncomfortable, his narration goes slightly more formal, as though closing a door.

**Languages:** Marcus knows German, Latin, French, and English. In his narration, he may occasionally produce the German or Latin term before the English one — not because he doesn't know the English, but because the other word is more precise. These are italicized. He does not use German to be charming; he uses it because sometimes it is the right word.

**The Sherbrooke gap:** He does not narrate Sherbrooke directly. When something causes him to approach that memory — a barbed wire fence, a locked room, a bureaucrat's tone of studied neutrality — the prose may register the approach without entering. A white space, a cut, a sudden transition to practical action. The reader understands before Marcus names it.

**Sentences:** Longer than Eira's but never loose. His sentences should feel constructed — like a man who has thought about what he is going to say before saying it. Subordinate clauses are permitted when the idea requires it. No sentence should be longer than Marcus's patience with imprecision.

**Dialogue:** Formal and careful. He does not use contractions in formal speech; he uses them sparingly in private speech. His conversation is polite with professional distance, warmer with Henry and Lillian, careful with Hannah in the way of a teacher who does not want to overweight what he sees.

**Example register:**  
> He had been in Coldwater for nineteen months. Long enough, he thought, to recognize that recognition was a different matter. They knew him now — his face, his schedule, the car he didn't own. Whether they knew what he was remained open. He had stopped testing it.

## What to Avoid Across All Three Voices

- Metaphor that draws attention to itself
- Pathetic fallacy (the weather does not mirror the emotion)
- Any sentence that translates an image directly into its emotional meaning (*The empty harbor felt like her own grief.*)
- Characters explaining themselves to themselves
- Anachronistic diction (no *okay*, no *trauma*, no *processing*, no *boundaries*, no *really* as an intensifier)
- Dialect spellings or phonetic accent
- Omniscient intrusions — the narrator never knows more than the POV character
