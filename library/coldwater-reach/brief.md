# Brief

## Premise

A small Maine fishing town, autumn 1943 to spring 1945. Three lives intersect across the second half of America's war.

**Eira Bowman**, fifty-two, has been the harbormaster of Coldwater Reach for eight years — since her husband Owen, the previous harbormaster, drowned during the November storm of 1935. She is the only woman in her position on the Maine coast, holds the office because no man wanted it after Pearl Harbor took the able-bodied, and runs it with a competence that has stopped being remarked upon. In October 1943 a man's body washes up on the breakwater after a three-day blow. He is not from Coldwater. He carries no papers. The Coast Guard takes the body and most of the silence with it. Eira knows what she saw on the dock the night before he came in.

**Hannah Pfeiffer**, seventeen, lives on her family's dairy farm three miles inland with parents who left Bavaria for Maine in 1922 and have not been the same since the war started. Her father, Albrecht, has stopped speaking English at home. Her mother, Magda, has stopped speaking at all. Under the federal POW labor program, four German prisoners are housed in the renovated icehouse and work the Pfeiffer fields under armed escort by an aging Coastal Patrol private named Lonzo Reed. The youngest of the four, Karl Reinmar, nineteen, is from a town fifty kilometers from where Hannah's father was born.

**Dr. Marcus Klein**, forty-seven, arrived in Coldwater in March 1942 from Cambridge, where he had been on the Emergency Committee in Aid of Displaced Foreign Scholars rolls since the autumn of 1940. He teaches German and Latin at the town high school. The school board hired him because the previous instructor enlisted; they have not yet decided how they feel about hiring him. He is Viennese, by way of Paris, London, and an internment camp in Sherbrooke, Quebec, that he does not discuss. He boards with the school's chief custodian, **Henry Crow**, a Black man whose family came north from Norfolk in 1924, and Henry's wife **Lillian** — the only household in town where Marcus is treated as guest rather than specimen.

The story unfolds across roughly eighteen months. The body on the breakwater is the inciting object the three of them, separately, end up carrying. None of them planned to. None of them sets it down before the war ends.

## Length and shape

- Target length: ~80,000 words
- Number of chapters: 30

The chapters rotate POV roughly evenly across the three (about ten chapters each). Chapter order follows narrative time, not POV cycle — the rotation is asymmetric, with longer or shorter runs in a single POV when the story requires. There may also be two or three short, named chapters from a fourth perspective (Henry Crow, or Lonzo Reed, or Karl Reinmar) if the structure calls for it — but they should be exceptional, not regular.

## Voice

Third-person limited past, bound tightly to whichever of the three POVs the chapter belongs to. Each voice has its own register, set by `canon/style.md` and reinforced by per-character notes in `canon/characters.md`.

- **Eira's chapters**: spare, procedural, observant in the way a working harbormaster is observant. Few interior monologues. Dialogue clipped. The sea is a workplace, not a metaphor. She thinks about tide, weather, draft, and load before she thinks about anything else, and even her grief comes to her in the language of harbor management.
- **Hannah's chapters**: closer to the noise of interior consciousness; adolescence permitted. German phrases threaded in where her father's voice has reached her. The farm is a world unto itself. Her register is the most porous of the three — it lets things in.
- **Marcus's chapters**: precise, slightly formal, with the academic's habit of measuring his sentences. The displaced scholar's particular relation to language: every word weighed for what it betrays. His narration carries the texture of a man translating his own thinking as it occurs.

Across all three: restrained prose, period-accurate texture, action and observation rather than reflection. The story trusts the reader to feel what is not said.

## Constraints

- Real-world historical accuracy. The POW labor program in Maine was real (Camp Houlton and its satellite work camps); the harbormaster regulations of the period are real; the home-front rationing system, the U-boat scares of 1942 and their decline by 1943, the casualty telegrams, the V-Mail, the ration books, the BC-1206 wartime radio sets — render these correctly, but never as set decoration. They are the texture the characters live inside.
- No spy thriller plot. No Nazi agents in Maine. No coded messages, no submarine landings. The war is in the radio, the letters, the missing men, the prisoners on the farm — not in plot mechanics.
- The body on the breakwater is the spine question. Who he was, how he came to be there, what happened that night — these unfold across the three POVs. None of the three sees the whole. The reader assembles it across thirty chapters.
- Each POV must have its own internal arc that is not subordinate to the central mystery. Eira's arc is about authority and what it costs to hold a post built for someone else's body. Hannah's is about what she does with the slow accumulation of small choices that adolescence is. Marcus's is about whether displacement ever ends — and if it does, what the person displaced becomes when it stops.
- The three never meet as a group of three. They share the town. Their threads cross — sometimes deliberately, sometimes by accident — but the structural unit is the individual chapter and its POV.
- The ending lands in spring 1945, just before VE Day. The war ends in the background; the story's resolutions are smaller and older than the war's ending.
- At least one thread should be deliberately unresolved. The breakwater question itself may or may not be fully answered — but the three characters' relations to it should resolve, even if the underlying fact stays in shadow.
- Race is a fact of the town, not a subject lecture. Henry and Lillian Crow live a specific life in 1943 coastal Maine. The prose should render that specificity without monologuing about it.
- Dialect and second-language English should be handled with restraint. Hannah's father's English is not phonetic stage-German. Marcus's English is the careful, slightly overcorrect English of a Viennese intellectual who learned it on the page. Both can be felt without being burlesqued.
- The wartime period is real history; the town and characters are invented. Names of real places (Portland, Bangor, Houlton, Cambridge, Sherbrooke) may appear; names of real people should not.
