# Chapter 5 — The Bavarian Accent

**One-line summary:** Hannah speaks to Karl through the fence for the first time; her father, hearing the dialect from the kitchen window, comes to the doorway and then says nothing — but speaks German at dinner for the first time in months.

**POV:** Hannah Pfeiffer  
**Tense:** Third-person limited past  
**Arc phase:** Setup

---

## Setting / Time

Pfeiffer farm. Mid-November 1943. The field work is winding down; the prisoners' main tasks are shifting to barn repair and woodcutting. It is cold — frost on the ground at dawn. The days are short. Lonzo's truck comes at seven-thirty and leaves at four.

---

## Goal

Hannah's goal: get the morning chores done before school. She is not looking for a conversation. The goal of the chapter is to establish the terms on which Hannah and Karl can speak — which means the first exchange has to happen by accident, not by plan.

---

## Obstacle

Lonzo Reed watches the prisoners. He watches Hannah. He has no explicit order to prevent the farmer's family from speaking to the prisoners, but everyone understands this isn't the arrangement. The obstacle for Hannah is not fear of punishment but something more ordinary: the strangeness of speaking to a stranger, in German, when you have spent two years not being the German farmer's daughter if you could help it.

---

## Action Beats

**Morning — the fence:**
- Hannah carries two pails from the barn to the far trough along the east fence — the fence that runs beside the icehouse yard where the prisoners take their break.
- She is used to not looking at them. She has been not-looking for three weeks.
- This morning Karl is at the near corner of the yard, crouching, examining a fence post that has heaved in the frost. He is doing what looks like actual assessment — running his hand along the split in the wood, checking the wire.
- He says something in German — not to her, to the post, or to himself: *Das hält keinen Winter mehr.* (This won't last another winter.)
- Hannah stops. She says, in German, without deciding to: *Dann musst du es jetzt reparieren.* (Then you'll have to fix it now.)
- He looks up. A pause — he is recalibrating. He says: *Sprechen Sie Deutsch?* (Do you speak German?)
- She says: *Ich bin hier aufgewachsen.* (I grew up here.) — meaning: here on this farm, with German spoken in it. He understands.
- She picks up her pails and keeps walking.

**The accent — Albrecht:**
- The exchange takes maybe thirty seconds. But from the kitchen window, Albrecht has been watching her cross the yard.
- He has heard Karl's voice — not what was said, but the sound. The Bavarian inflection. He comes to the kitchen doorway and stands there. Hannah sees him from across the yard and keeps walking.
- She brings the pails to the far trough. When she comes back, Albrecht is no longer in the doorway. He has gone back inside.

**After school — the kitchen:**
- Hannah does her homework at the kitchen table. Magda is in the next room. Albrecht is outside.
- She is thinking about the exchange — not about Karl specifically, but about the word *aufgewachsen*. She grew up here. The German for "grew up" is *aufgewachsen* — literally "grown up," in the sense of rising. She thinks about what rises in a place and what doesn't.

**Dinner:**
- Albrecht says grace: *Komm, Herr Jesu.* Then, to Magda: *Das Essen riecht gut, Mama.* (The food smells good.) In German. Not a proclamation — just: a sentence.
- This is the first German he has spoken at the dinner table in over a year.
- Magda passes the bread. Hannah looks at her plate.
- Nobody names it. The sentence is there and they eat around it.

---

## Outcome / Turn

The chapter turns at dinner. Albrecht's German sentence is not a decision — it's a reflex triggered by hearing Karl's dialect. The sound of a particular regional German pulled something loose in him that had been locked for a year. Hannah knows this but cannot act on the knowledge; she can only hold it.

---

## New Question or Pressure Raised

- What will Karl and Hannah talk about next? Is this going to be a problem?
- What does Albrecht's reaction to Karl's dialect mean about his past — about what he left in Bavaria and why?
- Is Lonzo going to notice? (Ch 10 will answer this.)
- What is Magda doing while all of this is happening in the room?

---

## Continuity Notes

- Establishes: First dialogue between Hannah and Karl. It is in German; the prose renders it in English. The exchange is brief and practical.
- Establishes: Karl's dialect as Bavarian — specifically he uses the construction Albrecht would recognize.
- Establishes: Karl's character — observational, practical. He was examining a fence post. He is working, not performing.
- Establishes: Albrecht's reaction — he hears the accent, comes to the doorway, says nothing to Hannah, then at dinner breaks a year of German silence at the table.
- Karl's German sentence (*Das hält keinen Winter mehr*) should be italicized, with the context carrying the meaning.
- Lonzo is present on the property but not in this scene — he is in his truck or on the other side of the yard. His absence from this exchange is confirmed in Ch 10 when he reveals he *did* see it from the truck window.

---

## Notes for Drafter

- The first exchange should happen fast — it's over in thirty seconds. Neither Hannah nor Karl prepared for it. The speed matters; it should not feel like a scene building to a revelation.
- *Das hält keinen Winter mehr* — Karl is talking to a fence post. Hannah answers him. The humor of this (two people talking about a fence post) is available to the drafter but should not be labored.
- Albrecht in the doorway: he appears and disappears. He does not speak to Hannah about it. The chapter should honor that silence.
- The dinner German sentence is the turn. It is one sentence. It is not theatrical. Magda passes the bread. They eat. End of scene.
- German phrases are italicized throughout Hannah's chapters. No phonetic accent. No translation in the prose.
