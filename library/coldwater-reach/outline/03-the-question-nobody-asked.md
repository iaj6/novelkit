# Chapter 3 — The Question Nobody Asked

**One-line summary:** Marcus hears about the breakwater body through school-community talk and privately forms a hypothesis he does not say aloud; that evening he plays chess with Henry and asks an oblique question.

**POV:** Dr. Marcus Klein  
**Tense:** Third-person limited past  
**Arc phase:** Setup

---

## Setting / Time

Coldwater Reach Consolidated School (Marcus's classroom, the hallways, the staff room) and the Crow house (kitchen, front room). Late October 1943 — after the body's arrival but before it has been resolved by the Coast Guard into a closed case. The school year is six weeks old. The furnace is being coaxed into its winter mode.

---

## Goal

Marcus's professional goal: teach the afternoon Latin section, return papers, manage the school board's ambient suspicion. His private goal: understand the town he lives in well enough to live in it without incident.

---

## Obstacle

The body on the breakwater has circulated through the school in the way that all Coldwater news circulates — through the students, who got it from their parents, who got it from the pharmacy or the post office. Someone has told someone the dead man "sounded foreign" based on his clothing. In the hallway, two teachers discuss this in voices not quite lowered. Marcus hears. He has a hypothesis. He does not say it.

---

## Action Beats

**Morning — hallway, before classes:**
- Marcus arrives at 7:45. He overhears the two other teachers — Mrs. Eldredge (history) and Wakefield, the principal — discussing the body. Wakefield says the Coast Guard took it. Mrs. Eldredge says she heard the man's clothes were European. Not what you'd get at Sears.
- Marcus continues to his classroom. He writes the day's Latin conjugations on the board. He thinks, in the interval between one chalk stroke and the next, about European clothing and what it means and says nothing.

**First period — Latin, with Hannah present:**
- The class is translating Caesar's *De Bello Gallico*, Book I. The standard text. Hannah is one of three students who have actually prepared.
- A student named Rolfe asks whether the Romans ever took prisoners. Marcus gives the correct historical answer. Another student makes a joke about the German prisoners in the county. Marcus does not react. He redirects to the text.
- He notices Hannah's stillness when the German prisoners are mentioned. Files it.

**Staff room — lunch:**
- The body is the subject again. Someone says the Coast Guard man told someone it was likely a merchant sailor. Marcus knows merchant sailors. Their hands are not like what Eira will have noted (though Marcus doesn't know Eira's observations). He thinks: the hypothesis holds. He eats his sandwich. He says: "Does anyone know the condition of the body?"
- Silence. Slight shift in the room's temperature. He adds: "Professionally speaking — the cause of death." Nobody knows. He finishes his sandwich.

**Afternoon — the school, after students leave:**
- Marcus stays late to grade. The furnace is ticking. The building is quiet. He sits in his classroom and thinks, precisely, about a category of European men who traveled without papers through Canada in 1943. He knows this category not from hearsay. He does not pursue the thought further in the narration — the prose edges to the Sherbrooke memory and then moves on to practical action.
- He locks his classroom. He walks home.

**Evening — chess with Henry:**
- Thursday chess. Henry has set up the board in the front room. Lillian is in the kitchen. The Zenith radio is on low — WBZ from Boston, a news program.
- They play. Marcus opens with the Sicilian. Henry responds as he always does — patiently, with attention to what his opponent is not doing.
- Marcus says, not looking up from the board: "Did you hear about the body they found on the breakwater?"
- Henry says: "Most everybody did."
- Marcus: "Do you know anything about it? Beyond what's in the paper."
- Henry: "Can't make much of a body they've already taken."
- A pause. They play. Marcus sacrifices a knight. Henry takes it and then realizes it was a mistake.
- Marcus says: "The clothing, apparently. The man's clothing was European."
- Henry looks at him. Says: "You thinking he was running?"
- Marcus says: "I don't know what I'm thinking."
- Henry takes a pawn. Says: "Probably better that way."

**End of chapter:**
- Marcus goes to his room. He has a photograph in one of his books — group of men, a courtyard, barbed wire. He does not look at it. He reads for an hour and turns off the light.

---

## Outcome / Turn

By the end of the chapter, Marcus has a private hypothesis — the dead man was a European refugee trying to reach America without papers — and has done one thing with it: said something oblique to Henry, who absorbed it without drama. He will not say it again, to anyone, for months. But it has been thought. The hypothesis is now in the room.

---

## New Question or Pressure Raised

- What does Marcus's hypothesis rest on — and what does it cost him to hold it privately?
- What did he see or experience that gives him the shape of this kind of story?
- What is in the photograph he didn't look at?
- What does Henry know, or suspect, that he's keeping equally quiet?

## Notes for Drafter

- Marcus's hypothesis about the dead man should be conveyed through the structure of his thinking, not stated directly. He makes a list of what he knows (European clothing, no papers, body after a blow) and the reader can see where the list is going even if Marcus doesn't say it.
- The chess scene with Henry is the chapter's load-bearing scene. Henry's response — "Probably better that way" — is not dismissive. It is the response of a man who also knows what it means to understand more than it's safe to say.
- The photograph is planted here for Ch 23 (Henry finds it). The drafter should include it in the room's description without drawing attention to it.
- Marcus's register: precise, slightly formal, interior monologue structured as proposition/qualification. He does not let himself become emotional; when he approaches the Sherbrooke territory the prose should cut to action (he reads, he turns off the light).
