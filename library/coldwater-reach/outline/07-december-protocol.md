# Chapter 7 — December Protocol

**One-line summary:** A Naval Intelligence officer from Portland comes to the harbor asking questions; Eira gives him nothing new and catalogs what she saw about the body; afterward she prepares the harbor for winter by Owen's old protocols.

**POV:** Eira Bowman  
**Tense:** Third-person limited past  
**Arc phase:** Setup

---

## Setting / Time

Harbormaster's office and the harbor. Early December 1943. The first serious cold has settled in. The harbor is in its winter configuration — fewer boats, the transients gone, only the winter lobstermen and a few draggers. Ice on the walkways in the morning.

---

## Goal

Eira's goal: get the harbor through its winter transition without incident. The Naval Intelligence visit is an interruption of that work, not the chapter's main event — which is the winter protocol itself, Owen's legacy.

---

## Obstacle

The Naval Intelligence man is harder to manage than Lt. Farris. He is not from the Coast Guard; he is from a different branch of the apparatus, and his interest in the body suggests the case has attracted attention for reasons the Coast Guard hasn't told her. She cannot read what he wants, which means she cannot calibrate her answers as precisely as she did with Farris.

---

## Action Beats

**Morning — harbor winterization work:**
- Eira is at the harbor at 6 a.m. removing the summer mooring field markers and replacing them with the winter set. She has two dockhands helping — Ames, who is sixty-four, and a boy named Shep who is fifteen and working for school credit. The cold is serious; her breath is visible.
- The work is methodical and physical. She narrates it internally with technical precision: which buoys come out, which moorings stay for the winter lobstermen, what the ice formation sequence is in a Coldwater winter, how Owen used to mark the vulnerable pilings with orange paint in the first week of December.
- She marks the pilings with orange paint. It is December. She does it because it is correct.

**The Naval Intelligence man arrives — midday:**
- He arrives in a government vehicle that doesn't belong to the Coast Guard — a different plate, a different build. He introduces himself as Mr. Hale, Naval Intelligence, Portland district. He has a card but pockets it before she can read it clearly.
- He is younger than Farris, smooth, asks questions in the way of someone who has been trained to ask questions: opening wide, narrowing.
- He asks about the October body. He confirms what Farris told her about the clothing. He adds: they have a preliminary theory that the man may have crossed from the Canadian Maritime Provinces by sea. He asks whether Eira saw any traffic she couldn't account for.
- Eira says: she maintains the harbor log. All vessel traffic is registered. She shows him the October log.
- He reads the October 11 entry. He reads the October 14 entry (body). He reads the entries in between. He puts the log down.
- He asks: does she do rounds after storms?
- She says: yes. Standard protocol.
- He asks: did she see anything on those rounds that she didn't record?
- A pause. She says: "I record what I find. If I'd found something significant, it would be in the log."
- This is true in the way she has arranged it.
- Hale looks at her for a moment. He closes his notebook. He says he may be back. He leaves.

**After Hale leaves:**
- Eira sits at her desk. The BC-1206 is on — Portland, weather channel. Northeast wind, clearing by evening.
- She thinks about what Hale said: the man may have crossed from the Canadian Maritime Provinces by sea. This is consistent with what she saw — a man who was not from Coldwater, not from the road, not from a registered vessel. It is consistent with a man coming across water at night in October.
- She does not revise her decision about October 11. She sits with the new information.

**Afternoon — returning to the protocol:**
- She goes back to the harbor. Ames and Shep are finishing the mooring work. She checks it, notes what's wrong with Ames's float placement, corrects it herself.
- The winter protocol is Owen's document — four pages in his handwriting in the back of the first log she inherited. He was methodical. She follows the document every December. She has never changed it.
- She works until four. The light is gone by then. She locks the office. She drives home.

---

## Outcome / Turn

Hale has told her something important — the theory of a crossing from Canada — and she has given him nothing. The chapter turns on her sitting with Hale's information and not revising her decision. Her silence is not passive; she actively chooses not to speak, again. The winter protocol at the end anchors her in Owen's routines — which is both a comfort and a cost.

---

## New Question or Pressure Raised

- Who is Hale, and why is Naval Intelligence interested in what the Coast Guard classified as an accidental drowning?
- What does "crossed from the Canadian Maritime Provinces" mean — who else knows about this?
- Is the case going to come back to her in a form she can't deflect?
- What would Owen have done?

---

## Continuity Notes

- Establishes: Mr. Hale of Naval Intelligence, Portland district, as a second investigating authority.
- Establishes: The Naval Intelligence theory — the man crossed from the Canadian Maritime Provinces by sea. This is the most significant piece of external confirmation of Eira's private hypothesis so far.
- Establishes: Owen's four-page winter protocol document in the first log — this is a recurring touchstone for Eira's relationship with Owen's legacy.
- The orange paint on the pilings is a specific December detail that can recur in later winter harbor scenes.
- Hale's question — "did she see anything she didn't record?" — and her answer should be rendered precisely. She says: "I record what I find. If I'd found something significant, it would be in the log." This is the chapter's moral hinge.
- Hale may return (implied). In fact he does not appear again — the investigation moves to Portland, not back to Coldwater. The reader doesn't know this yet.

---

## Notes for Drafter

- Open with physical work — Eira at the harbor before dawn. The orange paint on the pilings. Establish Owen's protocol before Hale arrives; Hale is the interruption, not the point.
- Hale should be rendered as professionally competent, not sinister. He is doing his job. The moment when he reads the October 11 log entry and pauses is the scene's pressure point — handle with restraint.
- Eira's answer to his final question is the chapter's moral hinge. Write it as a statement, not as a performance. She is not lying; she is rendering the truth in the shape she has chosen.
- The afternoon return to the protocol — following Owen's document — should feel like work, not elegy. She is doing the job. The fact that it is Owen's document and she follows it every December is simply true.
- End on the drive home, or on the locked office. Not on a revelation. She has made her decision; nothing more is required of this chapter.
