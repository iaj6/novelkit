# Chapter 10 — Lonzo's War

**One-line summary:** Lonzo Reed, escorting the Pfeiffer farm POW detail on a February day, observes Hannah and Karl's fence conversations, considers reporting them, and decides not to — this is not his war anymore.

**POV:** Lonzo Reed (exceptional 4th-POV chapter)  
**Tense:** Third-person limited past  
**Arc phase:** Rising Action  
**Target length:** ~1,600–1,800 words (short, exceptional chapter)

---

## Setting / Time

Pfeiffer farm and the road to and from Camp Houlton's county satellite facility. February 1944. A cold, clear day after a week of snow. The fields are white. The barn work is mostly indoor. Lonzo's feet hurt.

---

## Goal

Lonzo's goal: get through a day's escort duty without incident and be home by five. He has no investigative goal; he is not looking for Hannah and Karl's conversations. He sees them because it is his job to watch, and watching is the only job he has.

---

## Obstacle

What he sees (Hannah and Karl talking through the fence in German) puts him in a position that requires a decision he does not want to make. The regulations are not explicit about whether a farmer's daughter may speak to the prisoners; the spirit of the arrangement is clearly against it; reporting it would change things in ways Lonzo can see clearly and does not want. The obstacle is the decision itself.

---

## Action Beats

**Morning pickup:**
- Lonzo's day begins at 6:30 at his rented room above the hardware store. His feet from the moment his boots go on. He drives the county road to the farm satellite in the dark.
- The four prisoners climb in the truck. He knows them by number and by the way they move: Bremer (the oldest, a sergeant, dignified and slow), Wicke (stocky, always cold), Fischer (who laughs too easily at the wrong things), and the young one — Reinmar — who says almost nothing to Lonzo and almost nothing to the others.
- He drives to the farm. He thinks about nothing in particular. He has learned to do this — not think about things in particular. France was a long time ago and he has arranged his mind around not visiting it.

**The day's work:**
- The prisoners go to the barn. Albrecht Pfeiffer is already there, directing by gesture. Lonzo sits in his truck with the engine off and a blanket over his lap and a cup of coffee from the thermos his daughter packed him the last time she visited — that was three weeks ago; the thermos has been refilled with inferior coffee since then, but the thermos is hers.
- He watches. The prisoners work. Bremer and Wicke are in the barn. Fischer is splitting wood at the block. Reinmar is at the east fence, doing repair work — the post that was split in the frost.

**Lonzo sees the conversation:**
- Hannah comes out of the house at mid-morning with a pail and crosses the yard. She stops at the east fence where Reinmar is working.
- They speak. Lonzo can see this but cannot hear it. He watches for thirty seconds, then a minute. The girl is not flirting; she is not frightened; she is just talking. The young man is talking back. Their manner is — what's the word — he tries to find it — *ordinary*. Like two people who have found a way to speak.
- They speak for maybe four minutes. Then she picks up her pail and goes to the barn.
- Lonzo has seen this happen before — maybe six times over the past three months, though never longer than a few minutes. He has not reported it.

**The deliberation:**
- He thinks about reporting it. He runs through what he'd say: *The Pfeiffer girl has been talking to Reinmar at the fence. In German.* Who does he report this to? The county man, Aldrich. What would Aldrich do? Pull the detail, or reprimand the Pfeiffers, or both. What would happen to the farm? Albrecht is not managing well enough without them. The farm would fall behind. And for what?
- He thinks about what war is for, which he knows something about. His WWI memory surfaces as a smell — not mud, not blood, but canvas and lye soap and the specific smell of a field hospital tent — and he pushes it back.
- He thinks: the girl is not giving the prisoner military information. The prisoner is not giving the girl anything. They are two young people who share a language and have found a fence.
- He makes his decision.

**He doesn't report it:**
- He finishes his thermos. He watches the yard. Fischer finishes the wood and starts stacking it. The afternoon goes.
- He picks up the prisoners at three. Reinmar is the last one to the truck. He does not look at Lonzo's face.
- Lonzo drives back. The road is icy in one stretch and he slows down. His feet hurt. His daughter is in Augusta, married to a man who sells insurance. She called last week to say she might visit in March. He hopes she does.
- He pulls up at the satellite facility, counts the prisoners out, signs the escort log. He drives home. He does not write anything additional in the log.

**End:**
- In his room, he takes off his boots. The relief of this. He heats a can of soup on the hot plate. He thinks, once, about the girl and the fence and what they were saying. He doesn't know any German. He never did. He thinks: it probably doesn't matter what they were saying. He eats his soup.

---

## Outcome / Turn

Lonzo does not report the conversation. His inaction is the chapter's event — a decision made by the oldest man in the story, from a position of accumulated weariness and long experience of what wars do and don't require of people. The chapter turns on his word: *ordinary*. He uses it to describe Hannah and Karl's manner at the fence, and it is the right word, and it is the reason he does nothing.

---

## New Question or Pressure Raised

- How long will Lonzo's silence hold?
- Does Lonzo think about this again? (He does, when something larger happens — but not in a way that leads him to report.)
- What does Karl's silence toward Lonzo mean — is he aware that Lonzo has seen?
- The France memory surfaces and is pushed back. What happened to Lonzo in France?

---

## Continuity Notes

- Establishes: The four prisoners by type — Bremer (sergeant, oldest), Wicke (stocky), Fischer (laughs too easily), Reinmar (young, silent toward Lonzo).
- Establishes: Lonzo has seen Hannah and Karl speak at the fence approximately six times before this chapter. He has not reported it each time.
- Lonzo's daughter lives in Augusta; she is married, sells insurance through her husband. She visited three weeks ago.
- The France memory: surfaces as smell (canvas, lye soap, field hospital tent). WWI. Not elaborated. This is Lonzo's Sherbrooke — he doesn't go in.
- Lonzo signs the escort log but writes nothing additional. This is important for the official record — the conversations do not appear in any documentation.

---

## Notes for Drafter

- This is a short, exceptional chapter. It should feel different from the three main POVs — less interiority, more observation. Lonzo's narration is laconic, accumulative, organized around what he sees rather than what he thinks.
- The deliberation scene is the chapter's core. It should be dry, practical, unsentimental. He is not performing moral courage; he is making a cost-benefit assessment that happens to come out on the side of mercy.
- The thermos from his daughter: a small, load-bearing detail. She packed it; he keeps using it with worse coffee. This is what domestic love looks like at sixty from the outside.
- The France memory: one sentence of smell, then pushed back. Do not elaborate. The restraint is the point.
- End on the soup. Not on reflection — on the specific relief of hot food in a cold room at the end of a long day. Let that be enough.
