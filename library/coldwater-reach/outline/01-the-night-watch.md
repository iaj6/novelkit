# Chapter 1 — The Night Watch

**One-line summary:** Eira sees an unidentified man on the north wharf at 2 a.m. on October 11; three days later his body washes onto the breakwater and the Coast Guard takes everything.

**POV:** Eira Bowman  
**Tense:** Third-person limited past  
**Arc phase:** Setup

---

## Setting / Time

Coldwater Reach harbor and the harbormaster's office. Night of October 11, then the breakwater and office on October 14, 1943. A three-day blow has just ended. The air is cold salt and diesel. The tide at discovery is running out — low water will be at 9:40 a.m.

---

## Goal

Eira's immediate goal in the night-scene: finish the 2 a.m. rounds and get back inside before the temperature drops further. Her goal at the body's discovery: do her job — document, notify, maintain harbor function.

---

## Obstacle

The man on the dock is not doing anything she can name as a violation. She is not certain enough of what she sees in the dark to hail a stranger. The chapter's real obstacle is internal: she doesn't trust herself to know what she saw, so she says nothing — and then the decision fossilizes.

---

## Action Beats

**Night rounds, October 11 (chapter opens here):**
- Eira walks the wharf at 2 a.m. — this is her routine after a blow, checking mooring lines and dock cleats.
- She notices a figure on the north side of the wharf, near the far mooring bollard. He is not working on the lines; he is standing near them, or appearing to check them.
- She stops. Wind NW 12 knots. She cannot see his face. His coat is wrong — not a fisherman's oilskin.
- She makes a decision: not enough to hail a stranger in the dark. She continues her rounds. When she passes back, the north side is empty.
- She writes in the log: *Mooring lines north wharf — all secure. Wind NW 12 knots.* Nothing about the man. She goes inside.

**Three days' gap (rendered in a brief passage — no scenes, just the harbor's routine):**
- The blow continues. Boats stay in. Eira monitors the shortwave. She does not think about the man. Or tells herself she doesn't.

**The body, October 14:**
- Dakin, a lobsterman, finds it on the breakwater at first light. He comes to the harbormaster's office. He is shaken but manages the information.
- Eira goes to the breakwater with him. She looks at the body before the Coast Guard arrives. She notes: European-cut coat (not a fisherman's oilskin, not American workwear), hands not a laborer's hands — soft at the knuckles, ink or something dark under two fingernails. Age perhaps 40 to 55. No papers in the outer pockets. Face has been in the water.
- She does not touch the body. She notes the position — north side of the breakwater, near the high-tide mark — and the condition of the granite around him (kelp, no visible injury, nothing to explain it).
- The Coast Guard station out of Rockland sends two men. She gives them what she has. She does not mention October 11.
- They take the body. They say they'll follow up.
- She stands on the breakwater after they leave. The wind has shifted to the east. She writes in the log: *Body discovered on north breakwater, 0612. USCG notified and present 0740. Body removed 0830. Breakwater clear.*

---

## Outcome / Turn

The body is gone before Eira has fully processed what she saw. The chapter ends with her standing on the empty breakwater — not in grief, not in fear, but in the specific stillness of a person who knows she has left something out of the record. The silence about October 11 has hardened into fact before she made a decision to harden it.

---

## New Question or Pressure Raised

- Who was the man? Not local, not American in dress, not a fisherman.
- Why didn't she hail him on October 11?
- Why didn't she tell the Coast Guard?
- What is the connection, if any, between the man she saw and the body?

---

## Continuity Notes

- Establishes: Eira's 2 a.m. rounds as a post-storm routine (this should be consistent in later harbor chapters).
- Establishes: The exact log entry text for October 11 (*Mooring lines north wharf — all secure. Wind NW 12 knots.*) — this will be quoted again in Ch 17.
- Establishes: The body's physical details as Eira observed them — European coat, not-laborer hands, no papers in outer pockets. The Coast Guard takes the body; she does not get another look.
- Establishes: Dakin as the lobsterman who found the body (he will provide the dory information in Ch 14).
- The man's face is seen by Eira only at distance and briefly — she cannot confirm later whether the dock-figure and the body are the same person. She believes they are. She cannot prove it.
- Date of discovery: October 14, 1943. Confirmed per continuity.md §6.

---

## Notes for Drafter

- Open on the routine of the 2 a.m. rounds — establish it as ordinary before the figure appears. The harbor at night in October should feel workmanlike, not ominous.
- The figure should be handled with restraint: she sees him, she processes, she decides not to act. Do not signal whether this is the right decision. It is simply what she does.
- When she stands on the breakwater at the end: no interior comment on her guilt or omission. She is looking at the water. The tide is running out. She makes the log entry. End there.
- Eira does not think in metaphors. The sea is not her grief. The empty breakwater is an empty breakwater.
