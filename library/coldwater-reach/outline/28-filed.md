# Chapter 28 — Filed

**One-line summary:** A letter from the Portland district office formally closes the October 1943 case; Eira files it, makes the day's entry in the log, and prepares the harbor for a coming front.

**POV:** Eira Bowman  
**Tense:** Third-person limited past  
**Arc phase:** Coda

---

## Setting / Time

Harbormaster's office. Late January to early February 1945. Deep winter. The harbor is at its quietest — the lobstermen are in, the draggers are laid up. Ice in the inner harbor. The shortwave catches Portland and New York on clear nights. The Battle of the Bulge is over; the Allied advance has resumed; the end of the war in Europe is now a question of months.

---

## Goal

Eira's goal: manage the winter harbor. Read the letter. File it. Keep working.

---

## Obstacle

There is no significant obstacle in this chapter. The letter is a bureaucratic closure; it asks nothing of Eira. The chapter's movement is entirely interior — what it means to receive a formal ending to something that was never formally opened.

---

## Action Beats

**The letter arrives:**
- A standard government envelope, Portland district address. She opens it with the letter opener she's had since Owen's time — a brass handle, dull-edged.
- The letter is from the Portland district Coast Guard office, referencing the case number from October 1943. It says:

  *Case No. [USCG-PD-43-2291]: Unidentified Deceased, Coldwater Reach Breakwater, October 14, 1943.*

  *The above-referenced case has been formally closed without identification of the deceased. Cause of death: accidental drowning, circumstances undetermined. Classification: European national, country of origin unknown, entry status undetermined. No further investigation is planned.*

  *This letter serves as official notification to the Coldwater Reach Harbor Office.*

- She reads it. She reads it again.
- She opens her filing cabinet — the second drawer, where she keeps official correspondence — and files it behind the tab labeled *Coast Guard — Communications, 1944–1945.*
- She closes the drawer.

**The log:**
- She opens the harbor log to today's date. She writes: *Received official USCG close notification for Case No. 43-2291 (October 1943 body, breakwater). Filed.* That's all. The entry is there, in the record, correct, complete, three words after the case number.
- She turns the page. She makes the day's other entries: weather (northeast wind, fourteen knots, dropping), harbor ice condition (inner harbor frozen to six inches; outer harbor clear), the one lobsterman who came in with a mechanical problem (Ames's nephew; she called the Rockland yard for the parts), the shortwave traffic.

**Interior — what it means:**
- She sits back. The BC-1206 is pulling in Portland. Weather channel: a front coming in from the northeast, expected Thursday, wind to twenty knots, some snow possible.
- She thinks about the letter. *Accidental drowning, circumstances undetermined. European national, country of origin unknown, entry status undetermined.* This is what the official record holds. It is not wrong, in the way that not-wrong can coexist with incomplete.
- She thinks about Werner Kahl and his photograph. She thinks about Marcus Klein's hypothesis. She thinks about the dory on the north beach and the canvas bag under the bow thwart. She thinks about the man on the north wharf at 2 a.m.
- None of this is in the letter. None of it is in her entries. It is in her, the way the harbor log holds what she writes and doesn't hold what she doesn't write. The case is closed. The harbor is not.

**Preparing for the front:**
- She stands up. She puts on her coat. She goes out to the harbor.
- She spends the afternoon preparing for Thursday's front: securing the mooring floats that could take wave damage, checking the pilings where the ice is stressing the fenders, telling Ames's nephew to keep his boat on the inner side.
- The work is cold and methodical. She does it as she has done it for eight winters.
- She comes back to the office at four. The light is gone. The shortwave is still on — weather channel, the front confirmed, northeast, Thursday.
- She writes the last entry in the day's log: *Prepared harbor for incoming front, Thursday. Inner harbor secure.*
- She locks the office. She drives home.

**The house:**
- At home, she makes dinner. She sits at the table. Owen's chair is in the corner.
- She thinks: it is done, in the official sense. The case is closed. The man has a file number and a classification and no name.
- She thinks about what Marcus said on the harbor dock: *What happened to him was not anyone's fault. Or not anyone here.* She has held this for eight months. She has not decided whether it is true. She has decided it is useful — which is not the same thing, and which is, for Eira, enough.

---

## Outcome / Turn

The chapter's turn is the filing of the letter. Not a dramatic moment — she opens the drawer, she puts the letter in, she closes the drawer. The case is filed. Her entry in the harbor log — *Filed* — is the three-word notation of something that cost an enormous amount to arrive at and is now, officially, over. The coda of Eira's arc begins here: the weight she has been carrying has not disappeared, but its official container is closed.

---

## New Question or Pressure Raised

- The one thing still open: Werner Kahl and the photograph. The letter doesn't mention Kahl; the Coast Guard doesn't know he visited. He may come back. (He does — sort of. In Ch 30, a woman comes, which may or may not be related to Kahl.)
- The canvas bag: still unresolved. Filed with everything else.
- What does Eira do now with the harbor, the log, the ordinary work? (She continues it — Ch 30 answers this.)
- Marcus's statement — *not anyone's fault. Or not anyone here.* Eira has held it for eight months. She has decided it is useful, not necessarily true. This is an honest distinction and worth marking.

## Continuity Notes

- Establishes: The official close of USCG Case No. 43-2291. Filed in the harbor office. The case number is invented; the letter format is period-accurate.
- Eira's log entry: *Received official USCG close notification for Case No. 43-2291 (October 1943 body, breakwater). Filed.* Three words after the case number: *Filed.* This is the chapter's title and its whole event.
- The front coming Thursday: northeast, wind to twenty, some snow. Eira prepares for it. The harbor has a future.
- Marcus's line from Ch 16 — *What happened to him was not anyone's fault. Or not anyone here* — is recalled here. This is the second time it has appeared in Eira's mind. The first was in Ch 17 (indirect). Here it is direct.
- Owen's chair: it is in the corner. She does not look at it directly, as established in Ch 17.

## Notes for Drafter

- The letter: render it in full, or nearly in full. The bureaucratic language is the point — *circumstances undetermined, entry status undetermined.* This is what the official record calls a man.
- The filing: she opens the drawer, puts the letter in the correct tab, closes the drawer. Two sentences. This is the whole event.
- The log entry: three words. *Filed.* Let it sit on the page with that brevity.
- The afternoon preparation for the front: this is Eira doing her job. The physical detail of securing floats, checking pilings, talking to Ames's nephew should feel like genuine harbor work, not symbolic action.
- The house: Owen's chair. She doesn't look at it directly. She thinks about Marcus's line. She decides it is useful, not necessarily true. End near here.
