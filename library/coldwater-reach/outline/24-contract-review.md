# Chapter 24 — Contract Review

**One-line summary:** The school board calls for an early contract review; Marcus prepares his case with precision; a letter from Cambridge offers a return to an academic post; he puts it in the drawer.

**POV:** Dr. Marcus Klein  
**Tense:** Third-person limited past  
**Arc phase:** Turn

---

## Setting / Time

Coldwater Reach Consolidated School and the Crow house. September into October 1944. The new school year has begun. The war news is good — the Allied advance in France, the liberation of Belgium. The furnace needs its annual attention.

---

## Goal

Marcus's goal: survive the contract review with his position intact. His private goal, which he doesn't yet fully articulate: decide what he wants, which is harder.

---

## Obstacle

The contract review is two years early — Marcus's contract runs through June 1946, and a mid-term review is irregular. Wakefield tells him this is a "wartime staffing reassessment." Alderman Marsh is behind it. The obstacle is not that Marcus will be fired — he is the only qualified German instructor available — but that the process is designed to remind him his position is conditional, not permanent.

---

## Action Beats

**Wakefield delivers the news — September:**
- Wakefield stops Marcus after the faculty meeting. He says the school board has asked for a contract review of all personnel hired under wartime conditions. This includes Marcus. The review will take place in October.
- Marcus says: "My contract is not due for renewal until June 1946."
- Wakefield says: "This is a review, not a renewal. A formality."
- Marcus looks at him. He says: "What criteria will the board be using?"
- Wakefield says: "Performance. Student outcomes. Community standing." He pauses. "That sort of thing."
- Marcus says: "I'll prepare a statement."
- Wakefield says: "That won't be necessary." A pause. "But it might help."

**Preparing the case — evenings:**
- Over three weeks, Marcus prepares. He does this with the precision of a man who has appeared before institutional bodies before and knows that the preparation is the whole of what he controls.
- He assembles: his teaching record (student pass rates, examination scores, the fact that Coldwater students have the highest Latin scores in the county, which is not irrelevant), his credentials (Vienna, the Emergency Committee sponsorship, his appointment paperwork), documentation of the English-only instruction compliance, letters of support from two parents of students (he asked them; they complied without understanding why).
- He prepares his answers to the questions he anticipates: about his background, about his loyalty, about why he is here.
- He does not prepare a speech. He prepares facts.

**The Cambridge letter — October:**
- The letter arrives in early October. From Ellenbogen. A position has opened at Cambridge (Massachusetts) — a junior appointment at the Extension School, German and Latin, starting in January. The Emergency Committee has recommended him. The salary is better than Coldwater's. The position is more prestigious. He can reply by November 1.
- He reads the letter. He reads it again. He puts it in the desk drawer. He does not tell Henry.
- For three days he does not open the drawer.
- On the fourth day he opens it and reads the letter a third time. He thinks about what Cambridge offers: a return to academic life, colleagues who share his frame of reference, a city, the possibility of eventually returning to Europe with institutional backing.
- He thinks about what Coldwater offers. He makes this list in his mind. The list is different from what he would have said two years ago, or even one year ago.
- He closes the drawer.

**The contract review — October:**
- Marcus appears before three selectmen and Wakefield in the school board room. Alderman Marsh is there; two others he knows by name.
- He presents his documentation. He answers questions with precision. When Marsh asks whether he has any connection to European political organizations, he says: "The Emergency Committee in Aid of Displaced Foreign Scholars is the only organization to which I have belonged since leaving Vienna. Its purpose is the assistance of scholars displaced by the Nazi government. I was displaced by the Nazi government. The connection is accurate."
- Marsh asks whether Marcus plans to return to Europe when the war ends.
- Marcus says: "I have not made that decision."
- Marsh says: "If you returned to Europe, the school would be left without a German instructor."
- Marcus says: "If I returned to Europe, yes. The school would need to hire someone."
- A pause. Marsh says: "That's all we need."
- Wakefield thanks Marcus and he leaves.

**The outcome — and the drawer:**
- Two weeks later, Wakefield tells Marcus the review found his performance satisfactory. His contract will continue.
- Wakefield shakes his hand. The handshake is genuine in the way a handshake can be genuine between two people who have just survived an awkward situation together.
- Marcus goes home. He opens the desk drawer. He takes out the Cambridge letter. He reads it one more time. He puts it back. He does not respond to it tonight. He will decide later. (He will decline it in Ch 27.)
- He tells Henry the review came out. Henry says: "Did you think it wouldn't?"
- Marcus says: "I wasn't certain."
- Henry: "That's different from thinking."
- They eat dinner.

---

## Outcome / Turn

The chapter turns on two things happening simultaneously: Marcus survives the review, and the Cambridge letter sits in the drawer unanswered. The review's survival is a conditional victory — his position is secure enough to keep, not secure enough to trust. The Cambridge letter is the real turn: he has read it three times and put it back three times. The not-answering is already an answer, though he hasn't admitted it yet.

---

## New Question or Pressure Raised

- Will Marcus answer the Cambridge letter — and what does his answer mean for his arc?
- Marsh's question about returning to Europe: Marcus says he hasn't decided. Has he?
- What does the school board know about Marcus that they are using as leverage? (His Jewish identity — they haven't asked, he hasn't said — remains the subtext of every board interaction.)
- Henry's distinction between *not being certain* and *thinking it won't*: what does Henry mean?

---

## Continuity Notes

- Establishes: The contract review as a specific institutional event, not a rumor. Three selectmen plus Wakefield, Alderman Marsh present.
- Establishes: The Cambridge letter — a junior appointment at Cambridge Extension School, German and Latin, starting January 1945. Deadline November 1. Salary better than Coldwater's. He puts it in the drawer unanswered.
- Marcus's answer to Marsh's Europe question — "I have not made that decision" — is literally true in October 1944. It will cease to be true in November (Ch 27).
- The documentation Marcus prepares: county Latin scores, parent letters, Emergency Committee credentials. This preparation is consistent with Marcus's character.
- Henry's *That's different from thinking*: this is Henry's sharpest line in the novel. It should land without elaboration.

---

## Notes for Drafter

- The preparation scenes are Marcus at his best — precise, methodical, without drama. He does what he can control. The prose should reflect this: lists, criteria, the specific act of asking parents for letters.
- The contract review scene: Marcus is in his element here. He has been interrogated by actual governments. A three-selectman school board is not a threat he cannot manage. He is composed because he has prepared and because he has been through worse. Let this show.
- Marsh's questions are the most direct the novel gets about Marcus's situation. His answers are calm and complete. "I have not made that decision" — said without hedging, without appeasing. Just the fact.
- The Cambridge letter: do not dramatize the decision not to decide. He reads it, puts it back. Three times. That's all.
- Henry's line: say it once, don't gloss it. They eat dinner. End there.
