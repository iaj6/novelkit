# Chapter 25 — One Year

**One-line summary:** One year after the body; Eira goes to the breakwater alone and assembles what she knows; she makes her decision — permanent silence — and it is a decision, not a lapse.

**POV:** Eira Bowman  
**Tense:** Third-person limited past  
**Arc phase:** Climax

---

## Setting / Time

The breakwater and Eira's office. October 1944. One year after the body's discovery. The harbor is in its autumn wind-down. Cold. The leaves have turned inland; the harbor is stripped and gray. The breakwater is wet with October's heavy mist.

---

## Goal

Eira's goal: note the anniversary and determine what, if anything, she still intends to do. She has been carrying this for a year. The carrying is the question.

---

## Obstacle

The obstacle is that Eira is a meticulous woman who keeps precise records, and she has kept one precise gap in the record for a year. The obstacle is not external — nobody is asking her anything new. The obstacle is her own accounting habit. She has been doing the math on what she knows, and the math now adds up to a conclusion she has to name for herself before she can put it down.

---

## Action Beats

**October 14 — the anniversary in the log:**
- Eira opens the harbor log in the morning. October 14. She notes the weather — northeast wind, twelve knots, sea state moderate — and the harbor's routine.
- She does not note the anniversary. It is not a harbor event. It was a harbor event a year ago; now it is something she carries.

**The breakwater at low tide — mid-morning:**
- She goes out to the breakwater at the mid-morning low tide. She does this alone, without telling Ames where she's going.
- The breakwater in October: the same kelp at the tide line, the same orange-brown granite, the same smell of salt and cold. She walks to the north end, where the body was found. The granite there looks the same as everywhere else on the breakwater. There is no mark.
- She stands where she estimates the body lay. She looks at the water.

**Assembling what she knows:**
- She does this methodically, the way she makes lists when she is uncertain — though she is not uncertain now. She is assembling for the purpose of knowing that she has assembled.
- What she knows: A man, European-dressed, crossed from the Canadian Maritime Provinces in a small dory (foreign build) sometime around October 10–11. He pulled the dory above the tide line on the north beach. He came up to the north wharf at 2 a.m. She saw him. She didn't hail him. He didn't speak — she understands now that he may have been afraid to speak, afraid of what she was.
- Over the next three days — during the blow — he was somewhere. Not in the dory (it was gone when she looked later). Sheltering, perhaps. Cold and in the wrong season for this and without help.
- He died — of exposure, she believes, or of a combination of exposure and something else — and his body came in on the breakwater. The Coast Guard took him. The case was formally closed.
- Werner Kahl came looking for him. Kahl had a photograph. She could not confirm.
- Marcus Klein told her about the sponsored-passage network. She believes the man was part of it — or was supposed to be, and wasn't met.
- What she does not know: his name. Who he was supposed to meet. What was in the canvas bag. Whether the photograph was his.

**The decision:**
- She makes it on the breakwater, standing at the place where the body was.
- She is not going to tell what she saw on October 11. She has considered the consequences: the case is closed; reopening it with her testimony would accomplish nothing for the dead man and might create official interest in the network that moved him — interest that would fall on people who tried to help, not on people who did harm.
- She is also aware that this argument is a good argument for a bad reason. The bad reason is that she did not speak in October and has not spoken since, and speaking now would require her to explain why she didn't speak then.
- She holds both of these truths: the utilitarian argument and the self-protective one. She cannot separate them cleanly. She has stopped trying.
- Her decision is not: *I am protecting the network.* Her decision is: *The man is dead. The case is closed. What I know will not change either of those facts.* This is the closest she can get to an honest accounting.
- She turns and walks back along the breakwater toward the harbor.

**Return to the office:**
- She sits at her desk. She picks up the harbor log and makes the afternoon entry. Wind northeast, twelve to fifteen knots, building. The *Sainte-Marguerite* is due in two days. The winter mooring schedule begins in three weeks.
- She works through the afternoon. She does not write anything in the log about what she decided on the breakwater.
- She doesn't need to. It is decided.

---

## Outcome / Turn

This is the climax of Eira's arc. She has been carrying the October 11 information for a year, and she puts it down — not by forgetting it or by confessing it, but by naming it to herself as a permanent decision. The chapter turns on the distinction between the good argument and the self-protective one: she holds both, cannot separate them, has stopped trying. Her decision is not clean. It is honest about being not clean. And she makes it anyway, and goes back to work.

---

## New Question or Pressure Raised

- Is the decision truly final? (It will be tested in Ch 28, when the Portland letter arrives, and again in Ch 30, when Kahl's relative appears. Each time Eira's decision holds.)
- What does she do with the knowledge that he may have been afraid of her — that she was the last person who might have helped him, and he didn't know she could?
- The canvas bag: she will not know what was in it. This is the novel's most durable unresolved thread.
- Does the decision make her feel better or worse? (Neither. It makes her feel like someone who has finished a piece of work.)

---

## Continuity Notes

- Establishes: October 14, 1944 as the one-year mark. Eira's explicit, private decision to maintain her silence permanently.
- This is the first chapter in which Eira fully assembles what she knows in a single, ordered sequence. The reader has been accumulating this picture chapter by chapter; Eira now assembles it in the same chapter.
- Eira's self-assessment of her decision: she holds the utilitarian argument and the self-protective argument together and cannot separate them. This is the novel's honest stance — she is not a villain and not a saint.
- The *Sainte-Marguerite* is mentioned as due in two days — continuity with Ch 17.
- The canvas bag: Eira notes it as something she does not know. It remains unresolved.

---

## Notes for Drafter

- The breakwater in October should be rendered practically — granite, kelp, cold, mist. Not a stage for drama. She is standing on a workplace.
- The assembling of what she knows: this should feel like bookkeeping. She goes through the columns. The prose can be slightly more ordered here than usual — this is Eira at her most deliberate.
- The decision: it comes in the middle of the chapter, not at the end. She makes it. Then she goes back to work. The rest of the chapter is the harbor, the log, the afternoon. The decision is embedded in the ordinary.
- The honest accounting of the two arguments — utilitarian and self-protective — is the chapter's moral center. Do not let it become a speech. Let it be brief and exact, the way Eira's interior always is.
- She does not write anything about the decision in the log. She doesn't need to.
