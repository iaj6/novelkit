# Chapter 26 — Last Day

**One-line summary:** The POW farm labor detail ends for the season; Karl's last day on the Pfeiffer farm; Hannah goes to the fence at the end of the day and says, in German, what she can say.

**POV:** Hannah Pfeiffer  
**Tense:** Third-person limited past  
**Arc phase:** Climax

---

## Setting / Time

Pfeiffer farm. Late October into early November 1944. The fall work is done; the farm goes into its winter mode. The county has reassigned the POW detail to a cannery in Rockland for the winter months — the farm's seasonal work doesn't require them through December–March. They will be transported back to Camp Houlton and redistributed.

---

## Goal

Hannah's goal on the last day: finish the fall work correctly. She has known the end of the season was coming for two weeks. She has arranged herself to get through the day without making it something it isn't.

---

## Obstacle

The obstacle is that the last day is the last day and she has known Karl for over a year. The conversations at the fence have been the only thing in her daily life that has belonged entirely to her — not to her father's silence, not to her mother's withdrawal, not to the war, not to the farm's demands. The ending of that is real. The obstacle is receiving the ending correctly.

---

## Action Beats

**Two weeks before — receiving the news:**
- Albrecht tells Hannah that the county has confirmed the detail will end in the first week of November. He tells her practically, without emotion. She nods. She goes out to the barn.
- She does not go to the fence that afternoon to tell Karl. She assumes Lonzo has been told and will tell them.

**The days before the last:**
- The remaining days of October are ordinary — fall cleanup work, final fence repairs, putting away the summer equipment. Hannah works alongside the prisoners and alongside her father.
- She and Karl speak at the fence twice more. Neither acknowledges the approaching end directly. He asks about the east fence's condition for the winter — will anyone maintain it? She says she and her father will manage. He nods.
- She thinks, during the nights: she has not made plans. She has not given him her address, or asked for his. There is nothing to exchange that would serve any purpose; he is a prisoner and will be returned to Germany when the war ends, and she is eighteen and in Maine, and these facts are complete.

**The last day — the work:**
- The last day is a Thursday. Clear and cold. They are stacking the remaining bales in the far barn — winter feed, everything secured.
- The work goes normally. Hannah works inside the barn while the prisoners handle the outdoor equipment.
- At two-thirty the county truck arrives an hour early — someone's schedule shifted. Lonzo comes to get the prisoners.

**The fence:**
- There is no time for the usual end-of-day gathering. The prisoners are collecting their few things from the icehouse. Karl comes out last — he is always last.
- Hannah is in the yard. She crosses the yard to the east fence. She does not go to the icehouse side — she stands at the farm side of the fence and waits.
- Karl comes out of the icehouse and crosses to the fence.
- They stand on opposite sides.
- She says, in German: *Ich hoffe, deine Schwester ist in Ordnung.* (I hope your sister is all right.)
- He says: *Ich auch.* (Me too.) A pause. He says: *Ich hoffe, dein Bruder kommt heim.* (I hope your brother comes home.)
- She says: *Das wird er.* (He will.) She is not certain of this. But she says it.
- He says: *Das glaube ich auch.* (I believe it too.)
- Lonzo calls from the truck. Karl turns. He does not look back.
- She watches the truck until it goes around the bend in the road. She goes inside.

**Evening — the icehouse:**
- After supper she walks to the icehouse. The door is not locked — the county will come to clear it out later in the week. She opens it and goes in.
- The smell of old sawdust and cold stove ash. The four cots, stripped. The single bulb on its cord. Nothing left — the prisoners took nothing and left nothing. Four empty cots. A cold stove.
- She stands in the middle of the room for a minute. She is not performing grief. She is marking the space.
- She goes back to the house.

**The next morning — the east fence:**
- The next morning she walks the east fence line for the first time alone. She checks the posts — there are four that Karl repaired over the past year, identifiable by the new wood and tighter wire. The work is good. It will hold through the winter.
- She notes this.

---

## Outcome / Turn

The chapter turns at the fence — the exchange between Hannah and Karl is eighteen months of careful conversation compressed into four sentences. *I hope your sister is all right. Me too. I hope your brother comes home. He will.* This is the grammar of their connection: practical, affectionate, honest about uncertainty. Hannah says *Das wird er* (He will) and is not certain she believes it. This is the small choice that defines her: she says it because she cannot not say it, and because he needs to hear it, and because it might be true. This is who she is becoming.

## Notes for Drafter

- The two-week lead-up: render it as ordinary work. She doesn't perform anticipation. Neither does Karl in their last two fence exchanges — they don't acknowledge the ending. This restraint is true to both characters.
- The last day's truck arriving early: the timing is slightly wrong, slightly off what she expected. This is good — the ending should have an irregular edge, not a ceremonial one.
- The fence scene: four sentences. In German. No tears, no last looks. Karl is called and he goes. She watches until the bend in the road takes the truck. She goes inside. That's the scene.
- The icehouse: she goes in alone in the evening. The cold stove, stripped cots, sawdust smell. She stands in the middle and marks it. One paragraph. She doesn't cry.
- The morning after: she walks the east fence line and checks Karl's repair work. The work is good. She notes it. End here, or very close.
