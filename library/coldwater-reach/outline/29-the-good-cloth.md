# Chapter 29 — The Good Cloth

**One-line summary:** Will comes home alive and changed; Albrecht speaks English at the dinner table for the first time in years; Magda sets the good cloth; Hannah thinks of Karl without grief.

**POV:** Hannah Pfeiffer  
**Tense:** Third-person limited past  
**Arc phase:** Coda

---

## Setting / Time

Pfeiffer farm. March into April 1945. The war in Europe is nearly over; in the Pacific, the battles for Iwo Jima and Okinawa are underway. The farm is coming out of winter. The ground is soft; the first field work is a few weeks away. Will comes home in mid-March, earlier than expected — the Army is rotating men back who have served through the Pacific campaigns.

---

## Goal

Hannah's goal: be present for Will's return without performing a version of it that isn't true. She has been carrying his absence for over two years; the arrival is as strange as the absence was, and the strangeness is its own kind of challenge.

---

## Obstacle

Will is alive but different. The tremor in his left hand. The way he takes up space differently than he used to — more carefully, more aware of where the exits are. The farm is the same; the person who left it is not. Hannah has to receive the person who came back, not the one she held in her mind through the letters.

---

## Action Beats

**The notification — March:**
- A telegram from the War Department: Sergeant Wilhelm Pfeiffer will be rotated stateside in late February/early March; report to separation center in Boston; expected home by mid-March.
- Albrecht reads it twice. He gives it to Magda. She reads it. She puts it on the kitchen table. She goes to the pantry and comes back with the good cloth — the white linen tablecloth that has not been on the table since before Pearl Harbor. She sets it on the table without unfolding it. Just puts it there.
- Hannah understands: when he comes, the cloth goes on the table.

**Will arrives:**
- He comes on a Tuesday in mid-March, on the bus from Rockland. Hannah drives the Ford to pick him up — she has learned to drive in the past year, one of the farm's practical necessities.
- She sees him at the bus stop before he sees her. He is thinner. He is standing with his kit bag on the sidewalk, looking at the street as though calculating something. His left hand is in his coat pocket.
- She calls his name. He turns. He walks toward her. He says: "You drive now."
- She says: "I do."
- He gets in. She drives. He looks out the window at the road back toward the farm. He says: "It looks the same."
- She says: "It mostly is."
- He says: "Good."

**The house:**
- Albrecht is in the kitchen. He stands when Will comes through the door. He is not a man who demonstrates easily; he does not embrace his son. He puts his hand on Will's shoulder and holds it there for a moment.
- Will says: "Pop."
- Albrecht says, in English — the first English he has chosen for the family table in over two years: "I'm glad you're home, Will."
- Will says: "Me too, Pop."
- Magda comes from the other room. She takes Will's face in her hands. She says, in German, very quietly: *Mein Kind.* (My child.) That's all.

**Dinner — the good cloth:**
- Magda unfolds the linen cloth and sets it on the table. The family sits down together. Albrecht says grace — *Komm, Herr Jesu* — and then he prays in English, a second sentence that he has added: *And thank you for bringing William home.* His English is imperfect. The grammar is approximate. It is the most important English sentence of his life.
- They eat. Albrecht asks Will questions in English — what he wants to do, whether he's hungry, whether the bus was long. Will answers. His answers are shorter than they used to be. He is watching the room as he eats.
- The tremor in his left hand: he keeps it below the table mostly, but Hannah sees it when he reaches for the bread.
- She doesn't name it. Nobody names it. It is there and they eat around it, the way families eat around things.

**After dinner — Hannah and Will:**
- They walk out to the barn after dinner. Old habit — Will always went to the barn after dinner when he was home.
- He looks around the barn. He says: "The east fence is new."
- She says: "The POW labor. They repaired it."
- He says: "Prisoners? Here?"
- She says: "Last year. German prisoners, from Houlton. Four of them. For the spring through fall."
- He says: "What were they like?"
- A pause. She says: "Workers. They knew what they were doing."
- He nods. He doesn't ask more. He picks up a piece of baling wire from the floor and examines it. He says: "I used to do this. Pick up stuff."
- She says: "You still do, I guess."
- He says: "Yeah."

**Hannah's thought — Karl:**
- Later, alone in her room, Hannah thinks about Karl. She does this without guilt or romance or the need for narrative. She thinks: he was at the fence for a year. He repaired the east fence. He placed a columbine on a post. He told her something true about himself. She received it. They said goodbye.
- She thinks about this as something that happened and was complete, not as something with a broken end.
- She goes to her father's shelf. The *Duden* is there. She opens it to the page. The columbine flower is pressed flat, dry, brown. Still there.
- She closes the dictionary.

---

## Outcome / Turn

The chapter turns on two things happening at once: Will's return and what it reveals about change (the tremor, the careful spatial awareness, the shorter answers), and Hannah's ability to hold Karl's memory without grief. The closing of the dictionary is the chapter's final gesture — the columbine is still there, dry and brown, preserved exactly. This is not a tragedy. It is what happened.

---

## New Question or Pressure Raised

- Will's tremor: unnamed, unaddressed. The family eats around it. Will it be dealt with? (The novel does not answer this — it is one of the deliberately unresolved threads.)
- Albrecht's English prayer: the most important English sentence of his life. What does it mean for him going forward — does this begin a return to English?
- Magda's *Mein Kind*: first time in the novel she speaks voluntarily, without being spoken to. Is this the beginning of her return?
- Karl: wherever he is. Germany, whatever it is becoming.

---

## Continuity Notes

- Establishes: Will's return, mid-March 1945. He is a sergeant. He takes the bus from Rockland. He has a tremor in his left hand (not named, not explained).
- Establishes: Albrecht speaking English at the dinner table — *I'm glad you're home, Will.* This is the formal break in his English silence, which began sometime in 1941–42.
- Establishes: Magda speaking voluntarily — *Mein Kind.* In German.
- Establishes: The good cloth — the white linen tablecloth that Magda sets when Will comes.
- Establishes: The columbine in the *Duden* dictionary — still there, dry and brown. Hannah closes the book.
- Will asks about the east fence. Hannah tells him about the POW labor without elaboration. He doesn't ask more. She says: *Workers. They knew what they were doing.* This is her accurate, economical account.

---

## Notes for Drafter

- Will at the bus stop: render him practically. He is thin, he is careful, his left hand is in his pocket. Hannah sees him before he sees her. Let the space between them close naturally.
- The "It looks the same / It mostly is / Good" exchange: three short lines, three full sentences of the chapter's emotional content. Let them carry it.
- Albrecht's English prayer: he adds a second sentence in English. His English is imperfect — the grammar should be slightly off. *And thank you for bringing William home* is exactly the English of a man who knows the words but not the idiom. Let it stand without correction.
- The tremor: never named. Render it once (when Will reaches for the bread) and let it be. The family's management of it — eating around it — is the model.
- The columbine: Hannah opens the dictionary, sees it dry and brown, closes the book. End there, or within a sentence of there.
