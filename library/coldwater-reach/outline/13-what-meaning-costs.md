# Chapter 13 — What Meaning Costs

**One-line summary:** Hannah's remarkable essay on translation prompts Marcus to request a meeting after class; their exchange is careful and inconclusive; separately, a letter from Cambridge mentions European nationals who passed through the Maritime Provinces in 1943.

**POV:** Dr. Marcus Klein  
**Tense:** Third-person limited past  
**Arc phase:** Rising Action

---

## Setting / Time

Coldwater Reach Consolidated School (Marcus's classroom). The Crow house. March 1944. The first signs of spring — mud at the road edges, longer light in the afternoons, the furnace running less at night.

---

## Goal

Marcus's professional goal: give Hannah an adequate response to an essay that exceeds what a standard comment can hold. His private goal: understand her situation well enough to be useful to her without overstepping.

---

## Obstacle

A teacher meeting with a student about work requires a certain distance — the professional relation is the container. But what Hannah has written exceeds the container. Marcus has to find a way to respond that opens without exposing, that is honest without being intimate in a way that would be wrong. His own position adds a layer: what he sees in Hannah's essay about living on a border of two languages is what he knows from the inside, and he cannot say this directly.

---

## Action Beats

**Reading the essay — marked and remarked:**
- Marcus has Hannah's essay on his desk. The assignment: translate a German poem (Mörike's "Auf ein altes Bild" — a small, precise poem about a landscape and the Fall) into English, then write a page on what was lost and what, unexpectedly, survived.
- Hannah's essay: she translated the poem correctly. Then she wrote: that what survived the crossing was not any word but the *relationship between words* — specifically, the way German holds together a compound noun (*Sommerbild*, summer-image) that English has to unpack into two words and thereby makes less than it was. She wrote: "When you translate you do not move a thing from one room to another. You describe the thing to someone who has never been in the room."
- Marcus has read this four times. He has written in the margin: *Yes.* Then: *This is not the assignment.* Then: *See me.*
- He crosses out *This is not the assignment.*

**The after-class meeting:**
- Students leave. Hannah stays. She stands at her desk, not sitting, not moving toward him — she is waiting to be asked.
- He says: "Sit down, Miss Pfeiffer."
- She sits. He is holding her paper. He says: "Where did this come from?"
- She says: "The poem. The compound word."
- He says: "That's what you wrote about. I'm asking where the idea came from."
- A pause. She says: "My father speaks German at home. And some things in German I can't think in English. And when I try, the thing becomes smaller." She stops. She says: "Is that wrong?"
- He says: "No. It's a correct description of a real problem." He puts the paper on the desk between them. He says: "You understand that this isn't the translation exercise I assigned."
- She says: "I did the translation first."
- He says: "I know. I mean: this other thing, the observation — where did you learn to do this?"
- She looks at him. She says: "I don't know. I've been thinking about it since we started doing German."
- He says: "Have you thought about what you'll do after school?"
- She says: "My father needs help on the farm."
- He says nothing. He writes something on the paper — a note — and slides it back to her. She picks it up. She reads it. She puts it in her bag.
- The note says: *There are programs for students like you. Write to the address below.* Below it he has written the admissions office of the University of Maine at Orono.

**The Cambridge letter:**
- After Hannah leaves, Marcus finds in his afternoon's mail a letter from Professor Ellenbogen, still on the Emergency Committee rolls in Cambridge. Ellenbogen writes that the committee has been assisting in tracking the fates of European nationals who may have passed through the Canadian Maritime Provinces in 1943, under the informal sponsored-passage program. He asks if Marcus knows anything about such cases near his location.
- Marcus reads this twice. He sits with it.
- *Sponsored-passage program.* He knows this category. The program was informal, unofficial, carried out by church networks and labor organizations — a way to move European refugees from Canada into the United States without the full weight of documentation that the official channels required. The program moved people who had no other route.
- He thinks about the body on the breakwater. He thinks about European clothing and night crossings and small craft.
- He thinks: this is what I thought it was.
- He does not write back to Ellenbogen that evening. He puts the letter in the desk drawer — the same drawer where, later, the Cambridge job offer will live.

**Evening — the Crow house:**
- He tells Henry nothing about the letter. At dinner he asks Lillian how her week at the canning plant has been. She tells him. He listens. He thinks: this is what it costs to know something and not say it. The weight of the knowledge is not extraordinary — it is the ordinary weight of having sorted a fact into the category of things you carry rather than things you share.

---

## Outcome / Turn

Two things happen: Marcus gives Hannah an actual opening — the University of Maine address — and Marcus receives a letter that confirms, informally, what he has been carrying since October. Both turn the chapter from receiving to acting. The letter does not change what he does, but it moves the body's probable identity from private hypothesis to near-confirmed fact.

---

## New Question or Pressure Raised

- Will Hannah write to the University of Maine? (She will not, in this chapter — but the address is now in her bag.)
- What does Marcus do with the Ellenbogen letter? (He puts it in a drawer. He will not respond this chapter.)
- Is the sponsored-passage program something that could implicate people Eira knows, or people in Coldwater?
- What did Marcus see or experience with the sponsored-passage program in his own journey? (He didn't use it — he had the Emergency Committee. But he knows people who did.)

---

## Continuity Notes

- Establishes: Professor Ellenbogen of the Emergency Committee in Cambridge — Marcus's connection to his pre-Coldwater life.
- Establishes: The "sponsored-passage program" — informal church/labor network moving European refugees from Canada into the US without full documentation. This is the structural explanation for the dead man's crossing, though it is never stated as certainty in the narrative.
- Establishes: University of Maine, Orono, as a possibility for Hannah. Marcus writes the address in his note.
- Mörike's "Auf ein altes Bild" — a real German poem by Eduard Mörike (1804–1875). Short, precise, appropriate for a high-school translation exercise.
- Hannah's essay observation: *"When you translate you do not move a thing from one room to another. You describe the thing to someone who has never been in the room."* This line should appear verbatim in the chapter.
- Marcus puts the Ellenbogen letter in the desk drawer — the same drawer that will hold the Cambridge job offer in Ch 24.

---

## Notes for Drafter

- The essay-reading opening should be in Marcus's precise, controlled register. He reads it, re-reads it, counts the ways it is correct. *Yes.* Then: *This is not the assignment.* Then: *See me.* The crossing-out of *This is not the assignment* is the turn in his thinking.
- The Hannah meeting: both characters are being careful. Marcus doesn't tell her what he sees in her essay fully; she doesn't explain more than she does. The note with the Orono address is a specific, practical act — the most Marcus-appropriate way to say *you are wasted here*.
- The Ellenbogen letter: render it in Marcus's translation. He reads it, and then his interior moves through the sponsored-passage program, and then to the body. Do not name the body as the dead man on the breakwater in this passage — let the association form in the reader's mind without the prose labeling it.
- The dinner with Lillian: end here. He asks about the canning plant. She tells him. He listens. The domestic warmth is real and he is grateful for it and carrying something it can't touch.
