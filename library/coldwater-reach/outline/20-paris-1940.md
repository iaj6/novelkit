# Chapter 20 — Paris, 1940

**One-line summary:** D-Day empties the school; Marcus sits alone in his classroom approaching the memory of Paris without entering it; Henry comes to find him at three in the afternoon and takes him home.

**POV:** Dr. Marcus Klein  
**Tense:** Third-person limited past  
**Arc phase:** Turn

---

## Setting / Time

Coldwater Reach Consolidated School (Marcus's classroom) and the Crow house. June 6, 1944 — D-Day. The school is officially dismissed when the news breaks at mid-morning; the teachers are released early; the building empties by noon. Marcus stays.

---

## Goal

Marcus's goal for D-Day is simply to keep his bearing. He has had practice at keeping his bearing. Today the practice is harder than usual.

---

## Obstacle

D-Day brings the war from a pressure to an event. For four years, since June 1940, Europe has been locked. The landing at Normandy is the beginning of its unlocking — and for Marcus, who has been living in a careful suspension between what he was displaced from and whether he might ever return to something, this is not simple liberation. It is a door opening on a room he left and doesn't know what is still inside.

---

## Action Beats

**The morning — school:**
- June 6. Marcus's morning class is Latin — Book V of the Aeneid. He assigns the passage (Aeneas descending into the underworld) and writes the questions on the board.
- Hannah is not in class. (Her birthday was four days ago; she is at the farm today — Albrecht has kept her home, which is unusual for a school day.)
- The news breaks at 10:15. Wakefield's voice on the PA: classes are suspended, the school is dismissed, the students should go home. Marcus hears the students leave from the corridor.

**The empty classroom:**
- He stays. The other teachers leave. Wakefield looks in and says Marcus can go. Marcus says he'll stay and prepare tomorrow's lessons. Wakefield nods and leaves.
- The school empties. The furnace runs. The building is quiet in a way it never is when the students are there — a larger quiet, the echo-quality of an empty institutional building.
- Marcus sits at his desk. He is not preparing lessons. He is holding still.

**Approaching Paris:**
- He thinks about June 1940. He does not enter the memory; he circles it. The prose approaches the edge — the road south out of Paris, the specific quality of the light that June (clear, early summer), the weight of the bag he carried, the people on the road — and stops before entering.
- What the reader knows without the prose saying it: he was one of the people on those roads. He left Paris by train or by road — he doesn't narrate which — and went south. Then England. Then Sherbrooke. He does not go to Sherbrooke either.
- What he does instead: he opens the desk drawer and takes out two things — the Ellenbogen letter from Cambridge (the one about the sponsored-passage program) and the permit receipt from the harbor (the bureaucratic record of the school boats' spring registration). He looks at both of them. He puts them back.

**The practical action:**
- He opens his Latin gradebook and enters the marks from last week's quiz. He does this methodically. His handwriting is precise. He enters seventeen marks. He closes the gradebook.
- He looks at the door to the corridor. He looks at the window — the playing field, empty, the elms at the edge, the sky.

**Henry arrives:**
- Henry appears in the doorway at three in the afternoon. He is carrying his toolbox — he has been in the building all day doing maintenance. He looks at Marcus for a moment.
- He says: "You've been here all day."
- Marcus says: "I had lessons to prepare."
- Henry says: "Lillian's making something with the chicken she put down this morning. Come home."
- A pause. Marcus says: "I'll be a few more minutes."
- Henry says: "Five."
- He leaves. Marcus sits for five minutes. He thinks about Elsa — no, not Elsa; he thinks about the word *Europa*, and whether the word will mean something different in a year. He has been waiting, in one sense, for four years for this day. He did not know waiting would look like sitting in an empty classroom in coastal Maine.
- He gets up. He locks the classroom. He walks home with Henry.

**The Crow house — evening:**
- Lillian has made chicken stew. The radio is on — WBZ, a special broadcast about the landing. They listen.
- Marcus eats. He thinks: Europe is being reclaimed. He does not know, yet, what reclaimed means for him. Whether it means a return, or simply a change in the tense of his exile — from permanent to temporary, with the past that much more reachable.
- Henry pours more from the pot. Marcus says, unexpectedly, to both of them: "Thank you. For — this." He gestures vaguely at the kitchen, the food, the house. He stops. He eats.
- Lillian says: "It's chicken stew." She says it without irony and without pity. It is chicken stew. This is all it needs to be.

---

## Outcome / Turn

The chapter turns on Henry's arrival — the practical summons home: *Five.* And on Lillian's response to Marcus's thanks: *It's chicken stew.* Both are acts of unremarkable domestic grace that Marcus is, as the brief puts it, "faintly undone by." The turn is that D-Day does not resolve his displacement; it makes it more complex. The exile that felt permanent might be temporary. The temporary — Coldwater, the Crow house, chicken stew — turns out to have been real all along.

---

## New Question or Pressure Raised

- Will Marcus return to Europe when the war ends? (His decision to stay will come in Ch 27, but D-Day is the first moment he thinks about it as a live question.)
- What does the Aeneid passage (Aeneas descending into the underworld) mean to Marcus today? He was teaching it this morning. It is in the room with him.
- What does Lillian know about Marcus that she doesn't say?
- Henry's *Five* — this is the whole of his emotional provision for the day. Is it enough?

---

## Continuity Notes

- Establishes: D-Day as a structural pivot in Marcus's arc. Before D-Day, his return to Europe is unthinkable. After D-Day, it is possible but not wanted.
- The Ellenbogen letter from the Cambridge drawer is taken out and replaced — Marcus looks at it but does not act on it. This is consistent with his established management of that correspondence.
- Hannah is absent from school on June 6 — she is at the farm. Marcus notices her absence. (Her chapter, Ch 19-adjacent, is Karl's POV; Hannah doesn't have a D-Day chapter. Her D-Day experience is off-page but present through her absence from Marcus's classroom.)
- The Aeneid passage: Book V, or Book VI (the underworld descent is Book VI). Book VI is more appropriate — Aeneas descending to the dead to speak with his father. The drafter may choose exactly which passage.
- Marcus's habitual address to both Crows together — he says "thank you" to both of them. He lives in their house; they are both present to him in a way that is real.

---

## Notes for Drafter

- The empty school should feel large and quiet — not oppressive, just empty. The furnace runs. This is a building Marcus knows well now; its emptiness is notable against his knowledge of its usual sound.
- The Paris approach: the prose should come to the edge of the memory and stop. A white space, or a cut to practical action (opening the gradebook). Do not enter Paris. Do not enter Sherbrooke. What the reader understands is more than what is said.
- Henry's arrival: entirely practical. He has been in the building all day fixing something. He is not performing emotional support; he is telling Marcus dinner is ready and giving him a time limit.
- Lillian's line: *It's chicken stew.* This is the chapter's best line. Let it land last, or nearly last.
- Marcus's thanks should be brief and slightly garbled — unusual for him, the precise man with words. This is the tell: he is undone by chicken stew in a warm kitchen.
