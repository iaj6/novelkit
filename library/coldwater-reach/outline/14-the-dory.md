# Chapter 14 — The Dory

**One-line summary:** Dakin mentions offhandedly that he saw a foreign dory on the north breakwater beach in early October; Eira goes to look at the place; the picture acquires a new piece.

**POV:** Eira Bowman  
**Tense:** Third-person limited past  
**Arc phase:** Rising Action

---

## Setting / Time

The harbor and breakwater, Coldwater Reach. Late March into early April 1944. The winter is beginning to release. The harbor ice has broken up; the lobstermen are starting to set gear again. The breakwater is accessible at low tide. The days are cold but getting longer.

---

## Goal

Eira's goal: get the harbor ready for the spring season's increase in activity — register transient vessels, update the mooring chart, do the maintenance deferred through winter. The Dakin information arrives unsought and must be folded into what she already carries.

---

## Obstacle

The dory information is six months old. The beach is tidal — anything that was there in early October has been moved, scoured, dissolved by weather and sea. There is nothing to find. The obstacle is not the evidence (there is none) but the fact that this piece — a foreign dory on the north beach — means the man came from the water, not the road, and that Eira now has a more complete picture of what she didn't report.

---

## Action Beats

**Spring harbor work:**
- Eira is updating the mooring registry for the spring season — a desk task, cross-referencing the winter lay-up log with the returning boats. Dakin has come in to re-register his dragger.
- He pays his mooring fee (correct, to the cent — Dakin is meticulous). He is about to leave. He stops at the door.
- He says: "I keep thinking I should have said something. About the thing I saw in October."
- She puts down her pen. She says: "Say it now."
- Dakin says: back in early October — before the body, before the blow — he was hauling gear off the north beach at low tide. There was a dory pulled up on the beach rocks, above the tide line. Not one of his, not one he knew. No markings he recognized. It was a small dory, built differently than Maine boats — the planking was wrong, the thwarts were in the wrong place. He had thought someone was sheltering. He hadn't thought much of it.
- He says: "I didn't put it together until later. I thought I should say something but then the Coast Guard took the body and closed it."
- Eira says: "When exactly in October?"
- He says: around the tenth. Maybe the eleventh. He isn't certain.
- She says: "Did the dory have any gear in it? Lines, anything?"
- He says: a canvas bag under the bow thwart. He didn't look inside. He didn't touch it.
- She says: "Thank you, Dakin."
- He leaves.

**Going to the beach:**
- Eira closes the office and walks to the north side of the breakwater. She takes the long way around to the beach — a hundred yards of rocky strand that's only accessible at low tide. The tide is at its low now; she has two hours.
- The beach is clean. Six months of tide have left nothing from October. She stands where the dory would have been pulled up. She looks at the water, and then at the breakwater above her, and then at the harbor.
- This is the geometry: a man crosses from Canada in a dory. He finds the north breakwater beach at night — accessible at low tide, out of sight of the harbor proper. He pulls the boat above the tide line. He comes up to the harbor. He appears on the north wharf at 2 a.m., near the mooring bollard. She sees him.
- Then: three days, a blow, and his body on the breakwater.
- She does not know what happened in those three days. Did he shelter somewhere? Was he in the dory during the blow? Did something happen to him — exposure, an accident — that killed him during or after the storm?
- She stands on the beach for several minutes. She is not performing grief or detection. She is looking at the geometry of it and holding it.

**The canvas bag:**
- Dakin mentioned a canvas bag under the bow thwart. She has been thinking about this since he left.
- She does not know where the dory is now. The Coast Guard did not mention a dory — they either didn't find it or it was taken by the weather. She makes a note to herself to ask Dakin if he knows what happened to it. She does not go to the Coast Guard.

**Return to the office:**
- Back at her desk. She makes an entry in the harbor log: *Dakin reports non-registered small craft (dory, foreign build) observed on north beach approx. Oct 10–11. Not reported at time. No remains present on inspection today.* She logs it. It is in the record now — months late, second-hand, but in.
- This is new: she has written something in the log that refers, however obliquely, to what was on the north side of the harbor in early October.
- She sits back. She thinks: the bag. What was in the bag?

---

## Outcome / Turn

The dory closes the geographic picture: the man came from the water, not the road, not from a registered vessel. The dory was non-Maine in construction — which is consistent with the Canadian Maritime Provinces or further. The canvas bag is the new loose thread. The chapter turns on Eira writing the Dakin information into the log — she has logged something connected to the October event for the first time since the body was discovered.

---

## New Question or Pressure Raised

- What was in the canvas bag under the bow thwart?
- What happened to the dory after the body was found?
- If the dory was above the tide line, it would have survived the blow — so why didn't the Coast Guard find it? (Possible answers: it was taken by the weather after all; someone removed it; the Coast Guard found it and didn't tell her.)
- Eira has now put Dakin's information in the log. Does this create a new exposure? (It is late and oblique, but it is there.)

---

## Continuity Notes

- Establishes: The dory. Non-Maine-built, pulled above the tide line on the north beach, observed by Dakin around October 10–11, before the blow. A canvas bag under the bow thwart.
- Establishes: The geometry of the crossing — north breakwater beach as the landing site, north wharf as Eira's observation point, breakwater as the death site. These are all on the same side of the harbor.
- Eira writes the Dakin information in the log — first log entry referring to the October circumstance beyond the body discovery itself.
- The canvas bag is established but not resolved. It will not be resolved in the novel — it is one of the deliberately unresolved threads.
- Dakin: established in Ch 1 (found the body), appears in Ch 4 (briefly), now gives the central new information. He will not appear again as a significant actor.

---

## Notes for Drafter

- The Dakin scene should be quick — he is not a man who talks for long, and this information has been sitting in him for months. He says it straight. Eira receives it straight.
- The beach scene: she is doing geometry, not grief. She is looking at the physical relationships between positions on the harbor map. The prose should feel like this — spatial, practical, without pathetic fallacy.
- The canvas bag: mentioned, returned to in thought, not resolved. Let it hang.
- The log entry: she writes it in. This is the turn. She has been keeping things out of the log; now she puts one in. It is oblique but it is there.
- Do not allow the beach scene to become symbolic. The breakwater is granite with kelp at the tide line. The beach is beach rocks. She stands on them, looks at the geometry, goes back to work.
