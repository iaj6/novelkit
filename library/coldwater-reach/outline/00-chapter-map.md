## Arc Shape

### SETUP — Chapters 1–9
*The world is made. The body arrives. Three people begin carrying it.*

The body on the Coldwater breakwater is the inciting object. Within the first nine chapters all three POV worlds are established (harbor, farm, school), the body arrives, the POWs arrive on the Pfeiffer farm, and each character's central predicament is defined. The body has not yet become each person's private problem; it is still a public event. The setup ends when that changes.

### RISING ACTION — Chapters 10–18
*Each character moves toward the body's meaning by a private route. Weight accumulates.*

Through a Maine winter and into spring 1944, the three carry what they know and don't say separately. Relationships deepen: Hannah and Karl through the fence; Marcus and Eira through a careful professional encounter; Marcus and Henry through chess and the long rhythm of the Crow household. Information trickles in — a dory, a stranger in Portland, a letter from Cambridge — without resolving. The body's probable identity as a European refugee trying to reach America begins to form in shadow.

### TURN — Chapters 19–24
*D-Day (June 6, 1944) and its aftermath. Each arc reaches its crisis point.*

D-Day breaks the long suspension of the war-as-pressure and makes the ending imaginable. For each character this triggers the question they have been avoiding: What does Eira do with what she knows? What does Karl's past actually mean to Hannah? What does Coldwater mean to Marcus — and does he stay? The three exceptional 4th-POV chapters (Lonzo, Karl, Henry) are placed in this block because outsider perspectives are most clarifying at the crisis point.

### CLIMAX — Chapters 25–27
*The decisive choices are made. One year after the body.*

In October 1944 — one year after the body — Eira makes her choice: deliberate silence, not omission. Hannah says goodbye to Karl at the end of the farm season with the specific clarity of something that was exactly what it was. Marcus declines a return to Cambridge, chooses Coldwater, and stops waiting.

### CODA — Chapters 28–30
*Spring 1945. Resolutions smaller and older than the war's ending.*

The war ends in the background. Will comes home changed. The official case closes on paper. A woman with a photograph comes to Eira's office looking for someone; Eira tells her the truth as she has arranged it. The harbor opens for spring. The story ends where Eira is standing, at the water, the log open, the pen down.
