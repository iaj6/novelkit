# Chapter 19 — Which End

**One-line summary:** D-Day, June 6, 1944, through Karl Reinmar's single day on the farm; he hears the news on the radio through the farmhouse wall; that night a fellow prisoner asks what it means for them.

**POV:** Karl Reinmar (exceptional 4th-POV chapter)  
**Tense:** Third-person limited past  
**Arc phase:** Turn  
**Target length:** ~1,600–1,800 words (short, exceptional chapter)

---

## Setting / Time

Pfeiffer farm. June 6–7, 1944. D-Day. A clear day — cold in the morning, warming by noon. The farm is in full spring work. The icehouse at night.

---

## Goal

Karl has no special goal on this day. He is working. He hears the news. He thinks about it the only way he can.

---

## Obstacle

Karl's English is minimal. He hears the WBZ news program from the farmhouse radio — he can hear it but not understand everything. He understands enough. The obstacle is what to do with understanding: how to hold a fact whose implications run in every direction.

---

## Action Beats

**Morning — the ride in:**
- Lonzo's truck. The four prisoners. The routine. Karl looks at the road, the fields, the tree line. He has been in Coldwater for eight months. He knows the names of most of the road features now — not the official names, but his names for them: the bent pine, the stone wall that stops and then starts again.
- Lonzo has the truck radio on. A morning program. Music and then news at the top of the hour. Karl catches words: *Normandy*, *invasion*, *Allied forces*.
- He does not react. He looks at the bent pine as they pass it.

**The day's work:**
- The farm. The morning assignment: breaking up the compacted soil in the west field, preparing it for a late crop. Physical work, sun warming, the sound of the horses and the earth.
- He works. He thinks, in his working, about what *Normandy* means. He knows where Normandy is. He was briefly near it — not in combat, but passing through on transport. He knows the coast. He knows what a landing on that coast would require.
- He thinks about what this means for the war. He thinks about his sister in Kirchheim. He thinks about his father's hardware store, which may or may not still be there.
- He does not think about the girl (Hannah). Today is not a day for that thought. He thinks about his sister's name: Elsa.

**Mid-day — the farmhouse radio:**
- At noon they come in from the field. Lunch is brought out by the Pfeiffer household — or rather, by Hannah, who brings a pot of soup and bread to the yard and sets it on the fence post nearest the barn.
- Karl takes his portion. Hannah does not speak to him today; her face is elsewhere.
- The farmhouse radio is audible through the open kitchen window. *Allied invasion forces have established beachheads along a fifty-mile front.* Karl hears this clearly. He eats his soup.
- He looks at the fence where the columbine used to be. It is gone. He knows she took it.

**Afternoon — the field:**
- They go back to the field. Bremer works beside him. Bremer is the oldest, a sergeant, from Hamburg. They rarely speak.
- Bremer says, in German, without looking up from the ground: "Did you hear the radio?"
- Karl says: "Yes."
- Bremer says nothing else. They work.
- Fischer is in a worse mood than usual — he keeps stopping, looking at the sky, starting again. Wicke works with his head down, efficiently, as though the day has not changed.

**Evening — the icehouse:**
- Back in the icehouse by three-thirty. (The farmer sent them in early — Albrecht, who knows what the news means, has sent them inside without explanation.) Lonzo drives them home by four.
- In the icehouse that night, after the light is out, Wicke asks: "What does it mean. For us."
- Bremer says: "It means the war gets shorter."
- Wicke says: "Which end?"
- Silence. Karl is on his cot, looking at the ceiling. The single bulb is off. The stove has been lit; it ticks and settles.
- Karl says: "Both."
- No one speaks after that. After a while Wicke sleeps. Then Fischer. Then Bremer.
- Karl does not sleep immediately. He thinks about Elsa. He thinks about the hardware store. He thinks about a coast he passed through on transport and what it must look like now. He thinks: the war ends. He does not know, yet, in what direction home is. He is not sure home is a direction you travel in anymore; it may be something you only know by arriving, if you arrive.

---

## Outcome / Turn

The chapter turns on Karl's answer to Wicke's question: *Which end?* — *Both.* He is not being defiant or clever. Both ends of the war are ending — the side that takes prisoners and the side that loses them. What the ending looks like from his cot in a Maine icehouse is impossible to imagine clearly. The chapter ends with him not sleeping, thinking about Elsa, thinking about direction, which is the closest this novel gets to a character in free reflection.

---

## New Question or Pressure Raised

- What is happening to Kirchheim? What is happening to Elsa and his father's store?
- What will happen to the prisoners when the war ends? (They will go back — but to what?)
- Karl sees the fence post where the columbine was. It is gone. He knows she took it. What does he think about this?
- What does Albrecht's sending the prisoners in early — without explanation — mean? He knew what D-Day meant. He acted. Karl noticed.

---

## Continuity Notes

- Establishes: D-Day, June 6, 1944, as heard by Karl through the WBZ radio from the farmhouse.
- Establishes: Karl's sister is named Elsa. His father's hardware store in Kirchheim.
- Establishes: The icehouse atmosphere — four men, darkness, stove ticking. Bremer, Wicke, Fischer as personality types.
- Karl notices the columbine is gone from the fence post. He knows she took it. This is confirmed without dialogue.
- Albrecht sends the prisoners in early. He gives no explanation. Karl notices this.
- Karl's answer *Both* — to Wicke's question *Which end?* — should be the chapter's most resonant line.

---

## Notes for Drafter

- This chapter is in Karl's POV — his English is minimal, so the narration is in simple declarative German translated to English. The prose should feel different from the three main POVs: less interiority, more sensory immediacy, simpler syntax.
- Karl is not a Nazi ideologue and not innocent of the war's logic. He was there; he was part of it; he doesn't fully examine this because he is nineteen and doesn't yet have the vocabulary. The prose should not judge him and should not absolve him.
- The radio: Karl hears it through the wall. He catches words, not a full broadcast. Render it this way — fragments, not exposition.
- The icehouse at night: four men, one stove, the dark. Wicke's question is the chapter's hinge. Give it the simplicity it deserves.
- Karl's final thought — *home may not be a direction you travel in anymore; it may be something you only know by arriving* — is the closest thing to interiority in this chapter. Keep it spare and do not let it become a speech.
