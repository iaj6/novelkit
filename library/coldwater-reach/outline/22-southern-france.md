# Chapter 22 — Southern France

**One-line summary:** Karl, unprompted, tells Hannah something about his posting in southern France before capture; she understands what he means; she does not come back to the fence that week.

**POV:** Hannah Pfeiffer  
**Tense:** Third-person limited past  
**Arc phase:** Turn

---

## Setting / Time

Pfeiffer farm. July into August 1944. High summer. The fields are in full growth. The prisoners are working long days — haying, the summer's heaviest labor. Hot. Dust and chaff in the air. The war news coming through the radio — the advance in Normandy, the liberation of Paris in late August — is close enough now to feel real.

---

## Goal

Hannah's goal: manage the haying with the prisoners and her father. Stay on top of the farm documentation. Keep the conversations at the fence at their established register.

---

## Obstacle

Karl breaks the register. He says something he hasn't said before — not about himself or his family or what he misses, but about where he was and what he saw. This is a different kind of disclosure. Hannah has to decide, within the conversation, how to receive it — and then has to decide what to do with it afterward.

---

## Action Beats

**Haying — the long days:**
- July and August on the Pfeiffer farm are haying months. The prisoners work from seven to six with a midday break. The work is physical and relentless — cutting, turning, baling, stacking. Hannah is in the field alongside them under Albrecht's direction. This is the closest physical proximity she has had to the prisoners as a work group.
- Karl works near her some days, on different parts of the field other days. The fence conversations happen in the breaks.

**The Paris liberation — radio:**
- On the day of the Paris liberation (August 25), Albrecht brings the portable radio to the field. He sets it on a fence post and they all listen — prisoners included. The announcer's voice. Paris is free. Nobody speaks.
- Albrecht stands with his arms at his sides. He does not weep. He says nothing. He picks up the radio and walks back to the barn.
- Hannah watches him go. She watches the prisoners. Bremer has turned away. Fischer says something in German too low to hear. Karl is looking at the ground.

**The conversation — the following day:**
- The day after Paris. They are stacking hay at the east end of the field. Lonzo is at the far end, watching. Hannah and Karl are near enough to speak.
- Karl, without preamble, says in German: "Before Africa, I was in a posting in the south of France. Administrative. We were assisting with transportation." He pauses. "Transportation of people."
- She understands, from the way he says it, what he means. He is using the bureaucratic language because there is no other language for this that he can bring himself to use.
- He says: "I did not drive the trucks. I wrote in a ledger." He pauses again. "I wrote in a ledger."
- She says nothing. She keeps stacking hay.
- He says: "I wanted you to know. Because you asked me once why I was there and I said I was conscripted. That's true. But it's not all of it."
- She says: "What else is it?"
- He says: "I didn't ask where the people in the ledger were going. I knew what I was writing. I didn't ask."
- She says nothing. After a moment she says: "I know." She doesn't mean she already knew — she means: she has heard him.

**The following week — she doesn't come back:**
- She does not go to the east fence during the break for the rest of that week. She is not avoiding the field; she is in the field every day. She just doesn't go near the east fence.
- She is not angry. She is not condemning him. She is sitting with something that needs more space than the fence allows.
- She thinks about what it means to write in a ledger. She keeps the county feed ledger for the farm. She knows what it means to enter a number in a column — it is one of the most ordinary acts. She knows what it means to enter it knowing what it represents and not asking.
- She thinks: he told me. He didn't have to tell me. He chose to tell me before the war ended and the ledger closed.
- By the end of the week she goes back to the east fence break.

**Return — brief:**
- She is at the fence on a Friday afternoon. Karl is working the post again — the east fence needs constant attention. She says, in German: "You didn't have to tell me."
- He says: "I know."
- That is all that passes between them. They go back to work.

---

## Outcome / Turn

The chapter turns on Hannah's week of distance — not rejection, not condemnation, but the honest requirement of time. She returns. What she returns to is not exactly what she left, but it is still there. The chapter ends with the smallest possible exchange: *You didn't have to tell me. I know.* This is the moral register of the novel: not absolution, not judgment. Acknowledgment. And then work.

---

## New Question or Pressure Raised

- What does Hannah's reception of Karl's disclosure mean for her arc? She has received a piece of information that removes the distance between admiring Karl's ordinariness and knowing what his ordinary participation looked like.
- Does she tell anyone? (No. She tells no one.)
- Is Karl's confession a form of seeking absolution? (He says he doesn't need to tell her. He tells her anyway.)
- The ledger: Hannah keeps the county feed ledger. Karl wrote in a ledger. This parallel is available to the reader — do not name it in the prose.

---

## Continuity Notes

- Establishes: Karl's posting in southern France — administrative, transportation of people. He wrote in a ledger. He did not ask where the people were going.
- Establishes: Paris liberation date — August 25, 1944. Albrecht brings the radio to the field.
- Establishes: Hannah's weeklong withdrawal from the east fence, and her return.
- The ledger parallel: Hannah's feed ledger / Karl's transportation ledger. This is structural irony — do not label it. Let it exist.
- Karl's disclosure is not a full confession and is not a full accounting. It is what he can say. He is nineteen. He has named the shape of what he did.
- Hannah says *I know* in response to *I know.* Both sentences carry the same two words with different grammatical subjects. This is the dialogue's compression.

---

## Notes for Drafter

- The Paris liberation scene: Albrecht brings the radio, they all listen, silence. He picks it up and goes back to the barn. Do not explain his reaction. He has lived in two countries; Paris's liberation means something to him that is private and unspoken.
- Karl's disclosure: he does not confess in the language of confession. He uses the bureaucratic language — *transportation of people, wrote in a ledger* — because that is what he has. The horror is in the language's adequacy to what it describes.
- Hannah's response during the conversation: she says *I know* (I heard you) and she keeps stacking hay. She does not stop working. The continuity of physical work is the scene's container.
- The week of distance: render it as absence rather than avoidance. She is in the field. She just doesn't go near the east fence.
- *You didn't have to tell me. I know.* This is the chapter's last significant dialogue. End close to here.
