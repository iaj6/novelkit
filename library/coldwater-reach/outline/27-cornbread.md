# Chapter 27 — Cornbread

**One-line summary:** Marcus writes the letter declining the Cambridge appointment, survives the contract review, tells Henry and Lillian he is staying; he eats Lillian's cornbread and cannot find the words he needs.

**POV:** Dr. Marcus Klein  
**Tense:** Third-person limited past  
**Arc phase:** Climax

---

## Setting / Time

The Crow house and, briefly, the school. November into December 1944. The war in Europe is moving; the German winter counteroffensive (the Battle of the Bulge) begins December 16, slowing the Allied advance. The days are very short. The Crow house is warm.

---

## Goal

Marcus's goal: write the Cambridge letter. He has been putting it off for six weeks. The November 1 deadline has passed; Ellenbogen has sent a follow-up note asking if Marcus received the first letter. He needs to respond.

---

## Obstacle

The obstacle is that declining Cambridge requires him to know why he is declining it. He has been avoiding the answer for six weeks because he is a man who uses precision as a form of protection, and the precise answer to this question is a form of vulnerability he is not accustomed to.

---

## Action Beats

**The follow-up from Ellenbogen:**
- A second letter from Ellenbogen. He asks, gently, if Marcus received his October letter. He says the position needs to be confirmed or released by December 1. He says he hopes Marcus is well. He says Cambridge would be glad to have him.
- Marcus reads it at his desk at school. He puts it with the Cambridge letter in his jacket pocket. He brings both home.

**The evening — the letter:**
- After dinner, Marcus takes out both letters. He reads them both. He is in his room; Henry is downstairs; Lillian is in the kitchen.
- He takes a piece of stationery — the school stationery, which he uses for professional correspondence. He writes:

  *Dear Professor Ellenbogen,*
  *Thank you for your letter of October and for your patience in awaiting a response. I have given the Cambridge appointment considerable thought. I am grateful for the Committee's continued support, and for this specific opportunity.*
  *I will not be accepting the position. I have made a commitment to this school and to this community that I do not wish to leave prematurely. I believe my work here is not finished.*
  *With warm regard and gratitude,*
  *Marcus Klein*

- He reads what he wrote. The sentence that cost him: *I believe my work here is not finished.* This is true. It is also not the whole truth. The whole truth — that Coldwater has become, by some slow and unremarkable process, the place he lives rather than the place he is waiting in — is harder to put in a letter to a scholar who knew him in another context.
- He puts the letter in an envelope. He addresses it. He will mail it in the morning.

**Telling Henry:**
- He goes downstairs. Henry is at the chess table, but not playing — he is reading a newspaper. He looks up.
- Marcus says: "I've decided to stay."
- Henry says: "Did you think you weren't?"
- A pause. Marcus says: "Yes."
- Henry: "When?"
- Marcus: "Since October, probably. Possibly longer."
- Henry looks at him for a moment. He goes back to his newspaper. He says: "Lillian made cornbread."
- Marcus goes to the kitchen.

**Telling Lillian — the cornbread:**
- Lillian is at the kitchen table with her mending. The cornbread is on the counter, wrapped in a cloth.
- Marcus says: "I've decided to stay in Coldwater. I turned down a position in Cambridge."
- Lillian says: "You've been here two and a half years."
- Marcus says: "Yes."
- She gets up and cuts a piece of cornbread and puts it on a plate and sets it on the table in front of him.
- He sits down. He thinks: he would like to say something adequate to the fact that this kitchen, and this household, and this woman's careful and uncalculated kindness, are among the reasons he is staying. He would like to say this with the precision he gives to everything else. He finds he cannot. The precision is not available.
- He eats the cornbread.
- Lillian sits back down with her mending. She says nothing more. Neither does he. The kitchen is warm.

**The Battle of the Bulge — December:**
- The German counteroffensive begins December 16. The news is alarming — the Allied advance stalled, American casualties. Marcus hears it on the Zenith radio in his room.
- He thinks, for the first time since D-Day, about whether the war might last longer than anyone hoped. He thinks about what a longer war means for the question of return. He sits with this. He does not revise his letter to Ellenbogen.
- He goes downstairs. Henry has the radio on too. They listen together.
- After a while Henry says: "It'll hold."
- Marcus says: "Do you think so?"
- Henry says: "I don't know. But it'll hold."
- They listen.

---

## Outcome / Turn

The chapter's climax is not the letter — it is the cornbread. Marcus has been precise about everything for six years: every city, every permit, every institutional presentation. In Lillian's kitchen, with a piece of cornbread on a plate in front of him, he cannot find the words. He eats the cornbread. This is the chapter's turn — the moment when Marcus's relationship to language, which has been his armor, shows its seam. And he eats the cornbread anyway, without words, and it is enough.

---

## New Question or Pressure Raised

- Henry's *Did you think you weren't?* — what does Henry know about when Marcus made this decision? Has he been watching it form?
- The Battle of the Bulge: Marcus chose to stay, and then the war doesn't end on schedule. Does he second-guess himself? (No. But he sits with it.)
- What does Marcus's work that is "not finished" actually consist of? (Hannah's arc, the school, the Crows, the harbor — all of it.)
- Will Marcus eventually return to Europe? (Post-novel question. The novel ends before that's answered.)

---

## Continuity Notes

- Establishes: Marcus's letter to Ellenbogen declining the Cambridge appointment. It is written, addressed, and will be mailed.
- Establishes: The moment Marcus tells Henry and Lillian. Henry's response: *Did you think you weren't?* Lillian's response: cornbread.
- The Battle of the Bulge: December 16, 1944. Marcus hears about it on his Zenith radio. He does not revise the Cambridge letter.
- Henry's *It'll hold* — said about the war and about everything else too. This line should carry its ambiguity.
- The cornbread is Lillian's. She makes it in the way the brief describes: she feeds Marcus, and feeds him well, and asks no questions. The cornbread is the most concrete form of this.
- Marcus's sentence in the letter: *I believe my work here is not finished.* This is the most direct statement of his decision's real content.

---

## Notes for Drafter

- The letter itself: render it in full, or nearly in full. It is a precise document from a precise man. The sentence that cost him should be visible: *I believe my work here is not finished.*
- The Henry exchange should be brief and exactly what it is: Henry asks if Marcus thought he wasn't staying. Marcus says yes. Henry says Lillian made cornbread. That's the whole exchange. Let it be.
- The cornbread scene: Marcus cannot find words. This should be clear without the prose saying *he could not find words.* He sits down. He tries. He eats. Lillian says nothing. The kitchen is warm. End of scene.
- The Battle of the Bulge: a paragraph at the end of the chapter. He hears the news. He doesn't revise the letter. Henry says it'll hold. They listen. End there.
- Marcus's register: even in his most interior moments, his thinking is structured. But here the structure shows its limits. He approaches precision and finds it unavailable. This is the tell.
