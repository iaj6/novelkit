# Reader guidance — Thornwick (working title)

## Intended audience

Teen readers, ages 13–15, who have read and enjoyed Leigh Bardugo's Six of Crows, Naomi Novik's Scholomance, or similar YA fantasy with:
- A clearly anchored protagonist (not an ensemble of equals)
- Fast-paced dialogue-driven chapters
- Real stakes handled without graphic content
- Humor that coexists with danger rather than undermining it

This is not an adult literary fantasy reader. This is a fourteen-year-old who will put the book down if the first chapter drags, and who reads the last line of every chapter before deciding whether to keep going. That reader is the standard.

## Brief-adherence checks to perform

The Reader must answer all of these yes/no questions explicitly in its review:

1. **Would a 13-year-old finish Chapter 1 and immediately start Chapter 2?** If the chapter requires patience, abstraction, or tolerance for atmospheric delay, the answer is no, and that is a problem.

2. **Is Mira unambiguously the anchor?** The reader should never be confused about whose story this is. Emric and Cass are vivid but they are seen through Mira's eyes throughout. If a chapter reads as an ensemble story or if Emric/Cass get interiority, flag it.

3. **Do all three chapters end on cliffhangers or mid-action?** This is a hard requirement from the brief. Check each chapter ending explicitly. A quiet, literary, or emotionally-resolved chapter ending is a brief violation, not a stylistic choice.

4. **Is the discovery of the Registry (or equivalent) the structural turning point of Chapter 2?** The heist's object changes mid-story. If this pivot doesn't feel like a genuine gear-shift — if the reader isn't more unsettled after Chapter 2 than at the start of it — the story hasn't delivered its premise.

5. **Does Chapter 3 end on an unresolved decision?** Not a resolution with ambiguity around it. A genuine open ending — the decision is made, the consequences are not shown. If the drafter has softened this into a "and then they walked away, unsure what came next" literary fade, name it as a brief violation.

6. **Is the banter doing triple duty?** Dialogue should characterize all three (Mira/Emric/Cass sound different from each other), advance the heist mechanics, and carry emotional weight simultaneously. If dialogue is only doing one of these things, it needs work.

7. **Is the plan specific and does it fail in a specific, character-driven way?** Generic heist-goes-wrong is not enough. The failure should be a consequence of who these people are.

## What "good" looks like for this book

**Good Chapter 1** reads like the Six of Crows opening: a situation in motion from the first sentence, characters differentiated through dialogue within the first page, stakes clear within the first 300 words, ending on forward momentum. The reader finishes it knowing exactly who Mira, Emric, and Cass are and exactly what is about to happen.

**Good Chapter 2** reads like the Scholomance voice applied to a heist: a bold, slightly reckless forward motion disrupted by something worse than expected. The discovery of the Registry should feel like a cold hand on the shoulder, not a dramatic reveal speech. Mira finds it. She reads it. The chapter ends before she has to explain it.

**Good Chapter 3** is the tightest chapter in the book. No wasted beats. The extraction is physical danger, the knowledge is emotional danger, and Mira is running both tracks simultaneously. The open ending should feel earned — the reader should finish it understanding exactly what Mira decided and why, even though they don't see what happens next.

The three voices must be audible on the page without attribution. If you could remove the dialogue tags and still know who said what, the characterization is working.

## What to flag explicitly if the manuscript drifts

**Name these patterns explicitly, even if the prose is well-written in a craft sense:**

- **Drift toward adult-literary register:** Long sentences with nested clauses, abstract scene-setting paragraphs, introspective passages where the protagonist meditates on her situation for more than a paragraph, restrained chapter endings that prioritize mood over forward motion. These are the literary attractor's default output. They are wrong for this book regardless of their craft quality.

- **Ensemble POV instinct:** Any moment where the narration enters Emric's or Cass's interiority directly (not as Mira's inference). "Emric felt—" / "Cass knew—" without explicit attribution to Mira's perception is a POV violation. Flag every instance.

- **Explained humor:** If a joke or a funny beat is followed by a sentence that explains why it was funny (or that tags the emotional response to it), name it. The Bardugo-Novik register does not explain its jokes.

- **Softened endings:** A Chapter 3 that resolves even slightly — that gives the reader a sense that Mira knows what comes next, that the characters are okay, that the decision will probably be fine — is not the open ending the brief requires. The reader should finish this book with the decision ringing in their ears, not reassured.

- **Thesis sentences:** Any sentence that abstracts the story's meaning and states it directly ("she understood now that institutions didn't lie, exactly; they just—"). These are failure modes for this register. Name and cut.

## What to applaud

**Defend these aggressively in the "what to leave alone" section if the manuscript hits them:**

1. **Banter that characterizes without over-signaling.** If the three voices are audibly distinct and the humor coexists with the stakes without undercutting them, this is the brief being fulfilled. Do not recommend toning down the comedy in the name of gravity.

2. **Hard chapter endings.** If a chapter ends mid-action with no softening, and it feels abrupt, and a 14-year-old would immediately turn the page — that is correct. Do not recommend adding a resolving beat.

3. **The open ending of Chapter 3.** If Mira's decision lands without aftermath and the book simply ends, this is what the brief requires. Do not recommend closure.

4. **Sparse, physical dialogue tags.** If the drafter has replaced adverb tags with action beats (*He grabbed her wrist. "Wait."*) and tags are mostly *said/asked*, this is right. Do not recommend more expressive tagging.
