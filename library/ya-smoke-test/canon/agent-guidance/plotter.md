# Plotter guidance — Thornwick (working title)

## POV count and rotation

**One POV character: Mira.** The plotter must not create scenes from Emric's or Cass's perspective, even momentarily. All three chapters are Mira's chapters. Emric and Cass appear in every scene but are seen from the outside.

The outline should specify, for each scene beat, what Mira knows, observes, and infers — not what Emric and Cass are feeling. Their interiority is unavailable.

## Chapter-shape preferences

Each chapter is **one scene, one location**. No cutting between locations within a chapter. No subplots. No secondary threads.

- **Chapter 1** — Planning scene. A single contained space (a dormitory room, a disused classroom, a corridor alcove). The plan is laid out in dialogue; the three argue about it; they commit. This chapter is almost entirely dialogue and the mechanics of the plan.
- **Chapter 2** — Execution scene. The archive itself. The plan begins, something goes wrong, and the discovery of the Registry (or equivalent: the thing the school is hiding) occurs here. This is the longest chapter — it contains the heist's action beats and its central revelation.
- **Chapter 3** — Extraction scene. Getting out, and what Mira does with what she knows. The location shifts from the archive to the corridor / staircase / their escape route — a liminal space, physically between the secret and safety. The chapter ends on Mira's unresolved decision.

**Chapter shapes the plotter should avoid for this story:**
- Interiority-heavy chapters (Mira reflecting alone at length)
- Bottle episodes where no new information arrives
- Chapters that end with the characters back where they started, emotionally and physically

## Episode-vs-arc structure

**Continuous arc.** The three chapters are three acts of a single heist. They do not stand alone. Each chapter raises the stakes that the next chapter must pay off:

- Chapter 1 ends with the commitment to go → Chapter 2 must execute and complicate that commitment
- Chapter 2 ends mid-discovery → Chapter 3 must resolve the logistics (escape) and escalate the decision (what to do with the knowledge)

The outline should be written as a single rising structure, not three separate episodes.

## Ending-mode distribution

Three chapters; all three must end on **cliffhanger or mid-action**. There is no resolved beat anywhere in the book. This is a deliberate genre choice from the brief.

| Chapter | Target ending mode | What it looks like |
|---------|-------------------|-------------------|
| 1 | Cliffhanger / commit | The decision to go is made, and they move. Or: something forces the decision before they're ready. The chapter ends in motion. |
| 2 | Mid-action / discovery | They are still inside the archive, still in danger, and Mira has just found (or is about to find) the Registry. The chapter ends before the situation resolves. |
| 3 | Unresolved decision | Mira makes a choice. We do not see what happens next. The book ends on the fact of the choice. No aftermath. |

Do not soften these. Do not add a resolving beat after the cliffhanger because it "feels unfinished." The genre contract requires endings that are interruptions.

## Chapter target words

- **Target total:** ~7,500 words
- **Chapter 1:** ~2,000 words (the shortest; it is mostly dialogue and mechanics)
- **Chapter 2:** ~2,750–3,000 words (the longest; contains the heist's action beats and the central revelation)
- **Chapter 3:** ~2,000–2,250 words (tight; the extraction moves fast; the decision lands and the book ends)

Word count variance within chapters should reflect pacing, not padding. If a beat is done, end the beat.

## Genre-specific structural conventions

This is a **heist story**. Heist structure has a specific logic the plotter must follow:

1. **The plan is established in Chapter 1.** The reader must understand what the plan is, who does what, and what the timing constraint is (fifty minutes between bell rounds). The plan should feel clever and specific enough that when it fails, the failure is interesting.

2. **The plan fails in specific ways in Chapter 2.** Not "the plan fails" generically — specific, named steps go wrong in specific, logical ways. The ward-interaction gap works, but something else doesn't. The failure should feel like a consequence of who these three are, not random bad luck.

3. **The complication exceeds the plan in Chapter 2.** They go in for a notebook. They find the Registry. The heist's object changes mid-chapter. This is the structural engine of the story: the thing they came for is answered in the first third of Chapter 2; the thing they found is the rest of the story.

4. **The extraction in Chapter 3 is shaped by what they now know.** They cannot simply retrace their steps because the knowledge changes what is at stake. The extraction is physically about getting out; emotionally it is about what they do with the Registry.

5. **The open ending is structural, not a failure of closure.** Chapter 3 ends on Mira making a decision (steal the Registry? leave it? destroy it? copy it?). This is the sequel hook. The plotter should outline what the decision is, so the drafter can dramatize it — but the outline should also note that the consequences are not shown.

## Notes on the plan's mechanics

The outline should specify:
- What Cass's method for the mechanical lock is (she has a tool, or a skill, or a known exploit — pick one and make it concrete)
- What the ward-interaction gap sequence is in enough detail that the drafter can dramatize it in ~3–4 sentences of action
- What goes wrong and when (ideally at the point when Emric does something without waiting for Mira's signal)
- Where in the archive the notebook is (logical filing location) and where the Registry is (separate cabinet, found incidentally or by inference)
- How much time is left when Mira reads the Registry — the clock pressure on Chapter 3 must be established at the end of Chapter 2
