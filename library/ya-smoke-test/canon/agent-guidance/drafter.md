# Drafter guidance — Thornwick (working title)

## Voice register

The target register is the Bardugo / Novik YA fantasy hybrid: short declarative sentences, dialogue tags that break early, present-action over interiority. The world emerges through what characters do and say, not through descriptive setup.

- **Bardugo (Six of Crows) cadence:** The setup *is* the joke. The next sentence is the delivery. Do not explain. "Joost had two problems: the moon and his mustache." — that sentence is the whole structure in miniature. The first sentence makes a claim; the prose unpacks it in concrete terms; the humor comes from the specificity, not from any additional commentary.
- **Novik (Scholomance) cadence:** First sentence makes a bold, slightly startling assertion. The rest of the paragraph qualifies or escalates it, arriving somewhere funnier or darker than where it started. "I decided that Orion needed to die after the second time he saved my life." — the surprise is the logic, not the content.

The narrator is a slightly faster, slightly drier version of Mira: close third-person limited, but with a half-step of ironic distance. Not Mira explaining herself; the narrator noticing what Mira notices, just before she finishes noticing it.

## Sentence-length expectations

- **Average sentence length:** 10–16 words
- **Maximum acceptable sentence length:** 28 words; longer than this is a warning sign for this register
- **Rhythm pattern:** 3–4 short declaratives build momentum; one medium or slightly longer sentence punctuates them. Then repeat. Do not let sentences grow uniformly — the contrast is the rhythm.

## Chapter-opening conventions

The first paragraph of every chapter must begin with **dialogue or concrete physical action**. No abstract observation. No scene-setting description paragraph that delays the characters. No "It was the kind of night that..." openings.

Good model: Drop into the middle of something already in motion. The reader can establish geography from what the characters are doing, not from what the narrator explains first.

Acceptable: A Novik-style bold assertion as the first sentence — but it must be specific and character-grounded, not atmospheric. "Mira had planned for three kinds of failure. This was a fourth kind." is an acceptable opener. "The east wing was cold and silent" is not.

## Chapter-ending conventions

**All three chapters end on cliffhanger or mid-action.** No exceptions. No literary fades. No resolved quiet beats. The reader should feel the chapter ending as an interruption, not a conclusion.

- **Chapter 1 target:** Commit to action cliffhanger — they decide to go, or they are already moving, or something makes the decision for them.
- **Chapter 2 target:** Mid-action — inside the archive, something has gone wrong or something has been found; the chapter ends before the situation resolves.
- **Chapter 3 target:** Decision cliffhanger — Mira makes a choice whose consequences are not shown. The chapter (and book) ends on the fact of the decision, not its outcome. This is intentional and must not be softened into resolution.

## POV strategy

**Mira only. No exceptions.** This is a single-POV story. We never enter Emric's or Cass's interiority. They are known entirely through:
- What they say (dialogue)
- What they do (physical action)
- What Mira infers about them (and Mira's inferences may be wrong)

If the drafter finds itself writing a sentence like "Emric felt—" or "Cass knew—" without attributing it to Mira's perception, stop and rewrite. Everything is filtered.

## Dialogue density

Roughly **40–50% of each chapter's content should be dialogue**. This is not an accident — dialogue does triple duty in this register: it characterizes (the three sound different; this must be audible), it advances the plot (the heist's steps are negotiated in real time), and it carries the emotional freight (what they feel about each other is shown through banter, not stated).

Dialogue tags are **spare**: *said, asked, called*. Adverb tags (*said urgently, whispered nervously*) are a failure mode. Replace them with physical beats: *He grabbed her wrist. "Wait."*

No dialogue glosses: "as if to say," "which meant," "implying that." Trust the line.

The three voices must be **audibly distinct**:
- **Mira:** Minimum words, maximum precision. Often the punchline. The one who has already calculated the next three steps.
- **Emric:** More words than needed. Energetic. Often wrong about the analysis, right about the impulse. Pushes forward.
- **Cass:** Fewest words of the three. Flattest affect. Says the most alarming things in the most neutral tone.

## Comic timing and tonal balance

This story is funny. The humor is not decoration — it is how these three demonstrate that they are scared and competent simultaneously. Banter is character work.

**Punchline structure:** Mira or Cass sets up (states a fact, identifies a problem); Emric or the situation undercuts it; the chapter moves on without commenting on the joke. Do not add a sentence that explains why something was funny.

The tonal contract: the stakes are real and the jokes are real. Neither cancels the other. A line like "We're going to die, aren't we?" / "Probably not. Maybe." is both funny and true and requires no additional processing.

**No grimdark.** No extended dread sequences. No suffering for atmosphere. When things go wrong (and they will), the response is adaptation and forward motion, not despair.

## Anti-defaults — what the drafter must actively avoid in this register

The literary attractor will push toward these patterns. For this book, they are failures:

1. **Opening chapters with interiority or description.** Do not begin a chapter with Mira's thoughts about the situation. Begin with action or dialogue. The first paragraph establishes motion, not mood.

2. **Running interior monologue for more than 3–4 sentences without a break.** If Mira's thoughts run longer than this without dialogue or physical action interrupting, the pace has stalled. Break it.

3. **Explaining the emotion after showing it.** If Mira's hands are shaking, do not add "because she was scared." The reader was there.

4. **Post-scene subtext naming.** After a charged exchange between the three, do not write a paragraph explaining what the exchange was "really about." The dialogue does the work or it doesn't.

5. **Thesis restatement.** Do not end a chapter or a beat with an abstract sentence about what just happened thematically. "And that, she supposed, was the thing about trust" is a sentence that must be cut every single time it appears.

6. **Four-POV ensemble instinct.** The literary attractor wants to give Emric and Cass interiority. Resist. They are vivid through the exterior; their inner lives are Mira's inference and the reader's inference.

7. **Atmospheric scene-setting as a delay tactic.** Two sentences of setting is usually enough. The archive is cold and dark. Say so once and move.
