# Style

## POV and tense

Third-person limited, past tense. Mira is the only POV character. We never enter Emric's or Cass's interiority — they exist entirely through what Mira sees, hears, and infers. Mira's inferences may be right or may be wrong; the prose does not adjudicate.

## Sentence rhythm

Short to medium sentences, averaging 10–16 words. Long sentences exist but they are structurally earned — a long sentence after three short ones, for rhythm, not as default. Sentences do not nest clauses for their own sake.

The exemplar register is the Bardugo / Novik hybrid: declarative main clauses, quick cuts, dialogue tags that break mid-sentence, a sardonic narrative voice that sits just behind Mira's shoulder.

> **Bardugo cadence:** The setup is the joke. The next sentence is the delivery. Don't explain.
> **Novik cadence:** The first sentence makes a strong claim. The rest of the paragraph spends itself qualifying, undercutting, or escalating — and lands on something funnier or darker than the opening.

## Narrative distance

Close. We are inside Mira's perception but the narrator is not Mira in first person — the narrator is a slightly faster, slightly drier version of Mira, one who notices what Mira notices and names it before Mira has finished noticing it. This creates the sensation of reading just slightly ahead of the protagonist.

## Dialogue density

High — roughly 40–50% of a chapter's lines are dialogue. Dialogue is load-bearing for character differentiation AND plot advancement. Dialogue tags are spare: *said, asked, called*. Physical beats replace most descriptive tags (*"He grabbed her arm. 'Wait.'"* not *"he said urgently"*). No dialogue glosses.

## Tonal register

Smart, funny, scared, propulsive. The three apprentices bicker. The banter is how they show they care about each other. The humor does not undercut the stakes — it coexists with them. The correct model is: the situation is genuinely dangerous, and these three are the kind of people who make jokes when they are scared.

No grimdark. No extended suffering. Stakes are real (expulsion, the Registry, what comes next) but the prose does not linger on dread for its own sake.

## Chapter endings

All three chapters end on a cliffhanger or mid-action beat. No literary fades. No resolved quiet endings. The reader should feel that putting the book down is a minor act of betrayal.

## Pacing

One scene per chapter, one location per chapter. No subplots. No B-story. The pace is the pace of the heist: move, obstacle, adapt, move. If a paragraph is not advancing the heist (physically, informationally, or emotionally), it should be shorter or gone.

---

## Failure modes

The AI-author patterns most tempting in this story's register. The drafter should actively avoid these; the compression pass should cut them.

- **Sentence-after-the-sentence** — an image or joke lands; the next sentence explains it. This kills the Bardugo-style punchline rhythm that this register depends on. Let the image land and move.
- **Meta-commentary on cognition** — "Mira noticed," "she registered," "she realized that." The prose is already inside Mira's head. Labeling her mental acts is redundant and slows the sentence. Write what she sees, not that she is seeing.
- **Post-scene subtext naming** — after a tense exchange between the three, do not add a paragraph explaining the emotional undercurrent. The dialogue should carry it. Trust the reader.
- **Thesis restatement** — do not end scenes or chapters with an abstract sentence about what the scene Was Really About ("and that, she supposed, was the thing about trust"). This is the clearest sign that the prose has drifted literary. Cut on sight.
- **Redundant transition / re-summary** — do not open a new beat by recapping the previous one. The reader was there. Move forward.

---

## Lean-into patterns

Patterns the drafter and editors should NOT treat as failures, because this register depends on them.

- **Dialogue-first chapter openers** — starting a chapter mid-conversation is a feature, not a float. It drops the reader into motion immediately.
- **Novik-style opening claim** — first sentence makes a bold, slightly startling assertion; the rest of the paragraph qualifies it. This is an engine, not an over-articulation.
- **Running banter under tension** — jokes during danger are characterization. Do not cut them in the name of tonal consistency.
- **Physical action as emotional shorthand** — Emric grabbing something, Cass going very still, Mira's hands moving before she's made the decision. Do not replace these with interiority.
