# Threads

Five threads braid across the three chapters of this story. They are not the spine (the heist, the Registry, the escape) — they are the tensions the reader tracks around the spine. Three are deliberately unresolved. One is purely internal. None are generic.

---

## Thread 1: The Registry Decision

- **Question**: Now that Mira has the Conclave Registry, what will she do with it — and will the decision she makes alone be the right one?
- **Enters**: `02-the-archive` — Mira reads the Registry, understands it, takes it without telling Emric or Cass, and tells them only "we found more than the notebook."
- **Advanced in**:
  - `02-the-archive` — She pockets the ledger unilaterally while Cass is watching the door; she withholds its contents until they're out.
  - `03-the-passage` — The three argue in the service passage: Emric wants to burn it; Mira argues it's the only record of forty years of students; Cass declines to vote and cites the clock. Emric transfers the decision to Mira. She folds the Registry and puts it inside her coat.
- **Resolves in**: Deliberately unresolved. We see the decision (she keeps it) but never the consequence. What she does with it before the hearing at first bell, whether anyone believes her, what it means for the next cohort — none of this is shown. The book ends mid-step on the dormitory staircase.
- **What it's about underneath**: This thread carries the book's central ethical weight: the act of knowing obligates. Mira cannot unknow the Registry, cannot leave it on the shelf, cannot pretend the other forty years of names didn't exist. The thread asks whether moral clarity is the same as moral wisdom, and declines to answer. Mira is sixteen. She does what she can do with what she has. The reader is left to judge.

## Thread 2: The Signal Rule

- **Question**: Will Emric actually wait for Mira's signal during the ward-gap sequence — and if he doesn't, what will it cost them?
- **Enters**: `01-the-eleven-bell` — Mira makes Emric say the rule out loud ("nobody moves without my signal") while she looks at the candle instead of his face. She does not say she doesn't believe him. Both of them know she doesn't believe him.
- **Advanced in**:
  - `02-the-archive` — Emric counts to forty-five instead of sixty and steps forward to check Cass's progress with the lock. His foot crosses the trip-wire ward's secondary sweep. A pulse of pale blue at ankle height. Mira says "don't move." They wait four seconds. The pulse fades. They proceed. Nobody names what just happened.
  - `02-the-archive` (late) — They do not know a query pulse was dispatched to the patrol system, and that Prefect Hale has been sent on a precautionary sweep of the east wing.
- **Resolves in**: `03-the-passage` — Hale's lantern chain appears in the east wing one floor above, twenty-six minutes before the midnight bell. She is early. The three cannot use the direct route back to the dormitories. Mira calls the service passage. They lose time but survive. The cost was real, measurable, and paid. The thread resolves: yes, he broke it; yes, it mattered; yes, they managed it — barely.
- **What it's about underneath**: This thread is the externalized form of Mira's leadership problem: she can plan for everything she can calculate, and she cannot calculate Emric. The breach doesn't destroy them, which is almost the point — it just forces the improvisation she is better at than anyone and hates needing. It also shows Cass's function: she sees the secondary sweep before anyone names it, goes still, doesn't speak about it after. The three have different relationships to rules and to each other's failures.

## Thread 3: Cass's Before

- **Question**: Where did Cass come from — and what does expulsion actually mean for her, compared to what it means for the others?
- **Enters**: `01-the-eleven-bell` — Cass names her stakes asymmetry aloud in flat, exact language: her provisional scholarship has a conduct clause, and a single formal disciplinary finding triggers automatic review. She does not say what review means. She does not say where she would go. "Wherever she came from" is the phrase she uses, and she does not complete the sentence. She says this not to back out but to force Mira and Emric to acknowledge it. Then she says she's in.
- **Advanced in**:
  - `02-the-archive` — She is the one who executes most cleanly: she holds the trip-wire plate, she works the padlock, she relocks on exit. Her competence is her way of controlling what she can. Every complication deepens her jeopardy without her showing it. When Mira pulls out a pick to open the cabinet, Cass hands over the pin without comment and turns to watch the door. This is Cass saying: I trust you; but I'm managing my exposure.
  - `03-the-passage` — When the argument about what to do with the Registry runs out of time, Cass says: "Twelve minutes." She doesn't weigh in on burn-it versus keep-it. Her only other spoken line in the chapter is "Keep walking" to Emric. The routing call (service passage over east wing) protects her most specifically and Mira makes it without being asked.
- **Resolves in**: Deliberately unresolved. "Wherever she came from" is never named. Whether they make it back before Hale completes her sweep is not confirmed. Whether Cass's scholarship survives the night is not shown. The thread is closed by silence, not by answer — which is Cass's register throughout.
- **What it's about underneath**: Cass is the character the story withholds most. She is not mysterious in the gothic sense; she is private in the specific sense of someone who has learned that showing what she wants makes it easier to take. Her unnamed past is not backstory the story is saving — it is a deliberate gap. The reader is meant to feel the weight of a life that fits inside a provisional scholarship and a pair of modified iron pins. She is the one with the most to lose and the fewest words about it.

## Thread 4: Smartest ≠ Right

*(Internal thread — a question about Mira, not a plot question.)*

- **Question**: Is Mira's speed in understanding the Registry an advantage — or does being first to know simply mean she has to carry the weight of the decision alone, before she's ready?
- **Enters**: `01-the-eleven-bell` — Mira is already there when the chapter starts. She planned the ward-interaction gap from the maintenance logs. She assigns the roles, counts the steps, names the time. She calculates that Emric won't keep the signal and decides not to say so. She is correct about almost everything. The chapter establishes this as a fact, not a compliment: she is the sharpest analytical mind in the cohort, and she knows it, and this is both true and insufficient.
- **Advanced in**:
  - `02-the-archive` — She reads the Registry twice before Emric and Cass know she's found anything significant. She understands what "Collection" means from context before they do. She does not share this. She takes the ledger unilaterally and supplies the minimum: "We found more than the notebook." This is not deception; it is triage. But the reader should feel the asymmetry of it: she has moved ahead of them without asking if they want to follow.
  - `03-the-passage` — The argument. Emric's case for burning it is coherent. Mira's case for keeping it is coherent. Neither is obviously right. When Emric says "whatever you decide, Mira," he is not giving her permission — he is transferring moral weight to the person who will carry it alone because she is the person who always does. She makes the decision. She folds the Registry with the precision of someone who folds things she means to keep.
- **Resolves in**: Deliberately unresolved. We see that she decided. We cannot know if she was right. She is sixteen. She is the smartest person in the room. This has always felt like enough, and now it isn't clear that it is, and the book ends before it becomes clear. The thread is refused, not dropped.
- **What it's about underneath**: This is the book's core internal engine, stated in `themes.md`: intelligence reads patterns; wisdom decides what patterns mean. Mira is at the exact moment where those two things split apart. She is right about the facts. She may be right about what to do. But "may be" is new to her, and the story ends in the middle of that newness. The reader is the only one who watches her walk upstairs without knowing whether she's walking toward something or away from it.

## Thread 5: The Unequal Compact

- **Question**: Does "we go in together, we come out together" hold when the information they find lands differently — and when one of them understands it first and decides alone?
- **Enters**: `01-the-eleven-bell` — The plan requires three people in three specific roles. Cass names the asymmetry of what they're risking. Emric names what he needs. Mira stops calculating and decides. They go. The compact is implicit: whatever they find, they face it together.
- **Advanced in**:
  - `02-the-archive` — Mira reads the Registry alone. She takes it alone. She tells the others the minimum. The compact survives this — they follow her out — but the group's shared knowledge is now unequal in a way it wasn't when they entered. She is carrying something they don't yet have access to. This is not a betrayal; it is the beginning of an asymmetry the thread will spend Chapter 3 testing.
  - `03-the-passage` — They disagree. Emric: burn it. Mira: keep it. Cass: neither — twelve minutes. The argument makes visible what the compact meant and what it costs: they built the plan on trusting each other's roles, but nobody assigned the role of "decides what to do when the plan exceeds itself." Emric gives that role to Mira with one sentence. Cass never takes it. The compact holds — no one leaves, no one refuses — but it holds in the shape of one person deciding and two people walking behind her.
- **Resolves in**: `03-the-passage` — The compact holds, but not evenly. Mira makes the decision. They walk upstairs. The resolution is that they come out together, as promised, and the Registry came with them, and the cost of that is distributed unequally by what each of them now knows they owe the other. This is not a clean resolution; it is a held resolution, the way a knot holds.
- **What it's about underneath**: Three people planned a heist and made an implicit promise about what kind of friends they were. The heist gave them something they didn't plan for, and the promise had to stretch to cover it. The thread tests whether loyalty is a condition of equality or whether it can survive asymmetry — of stakes, of knowledge, of nerve. It mostly can. "Mostly" is where the sequel lives.
