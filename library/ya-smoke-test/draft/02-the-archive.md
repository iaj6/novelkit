# The Archive

Two flights down, the cold changed.

Not wind, not draft — just the accumulated chill of stone that had never seen light and had decided not to start. The smell changed too: less lamp oil, more stone and standing water, and something underneath both of those that was older than the building and had no interest in being identified. The passage narrowed to one person wide and ran east. Cass kept the lantern half-shuttered. Mira counted Cass's footsteps and walked in the same places.

The tapestry was where Cass had said it would be.

That was the thing about Cass's information: it arrived at exactly the coordinates she'd given, not approximately. Not *near* the end of the sub-basement passage. There, at the terminal wall, under the last bracket lamp — which was unlit, had been unlit for some time, a cold iron ring with a dried wick and no reservoir. The tapestry hung below it: a sea-battle in grey and brown thread, the ships indistinguishable now from the water below them, the hem fraying thin against the stone floor.

Cass pushed it aside and held it.

The archive door was behind it: old iron, black-painted, close enough to touch. The lantern light caught the seam of the frame and found it — three separate shimmers, layered, barely visible. Not quite invisible.

"There they are," Emric said, quietly.

"They've always been there," Mira said.

A small breath from him. "I know. I've just never seen them."

The cold pressed up through the stone floor into Mira's boots. Sub-basement cold — a different temperature than the corridors above, the kind of cold that had been living in the rock for a century and had no particular plans to leave. She counted. Thirty-one seconds since the staircase. On schedule.

"Step one," she said.

She reached forward. The lock-ward node was an iron boss at eye height, flush with the door's face, tarnished to near-black. Her fingers found it without looking — she had pictured this moment often enough that her hand knew the distance before she did. Three taps: deliberate, spaced. She felt the ward shift under her fingertips, a faint vibration like pressing a thumb against a turning fork. The shimmer in the door seam brightened slightly.

Ninety seconds. Starting now.

She stepped back. Cass was already crouching. The trip-wire ward's sensor plate was a small iron disk flush with the floor at the door's base — the kind of detail you found only if you'd traced this passage with your hands and not just your eyes. Cass pressed it flat-palmed, full contact, holding the signal in a static triggered state.

The door seam flared. Brief and clean: a soft orange light, the color of a candle snuffing. The erasure ward discharging at nothing, at empty air.

Then quiet.

Mira began the count. One. She breathed steadily. Emric made no sound at all, which meant he was counting too. Ten. Twenty.

She reached forty-two.

Emric moved.

Not much. Half a step forward, angling to see past Cass to the padlock — just checking, just looking. His foot crossed the line Mira hadn't known to calculate: a pale blue wash at ankle height, barely there, activated only after the primary discharge. The secondary sweep.

The pulse touched his boot.

Stopped.

"Don't move," Mira said.

Very quietly. She didn't move herself. Cass had gone absolutely still, palm flat against the sensor plate, not looking at Emric, not looking at Mira — looking at the door, at the seam, waiting to see what it did next.

One second. Two. Three. Four.

The blue faded.

Mira counted the remaining sixteen seconds in silence. She heard them all breathing. She didn't know what the pulse had sent, or if it had sent anything. She kept counting.

Sixty. She nodded once.

Cass moved. Left hand still on the sensor plate; right hand to the padlock. The two modified pins had been in her coat pocket since the stairs — she'd had them ready the whole descent and nobody had asked about this because it was simply how Cass operated. She worked by feel. Eyes almost closed. The faint sound of metal finding metal, a lever lifting, setting, holding. Then another.

Emric watched the passage behind them.

Mira watched Cass.

One minute. The padlock sat there. Ten seconds more. Then: a soft, final click. The shackle lifted. Cass caught it with two fingers before it could swing against the door.

She looked at Mira. Mira pushed the door open.

---

The cold was the first thing — a wall of it, the kind that had been building since before any of them were born.

Mira inhaled. Cedar oil. Dust. Chalk underneath both, deep and dry, the chalk of the headland itself. Then the dark. Their lantern pushed it back eight feet in every direction. Beyond that: iron shelves, floor-to-ceiling, disappearing into the room's far corners. The silence of a room that hadn't heard voices in a long time.

Cass caught the door with her heel. She didn't latch it.

"Left wall," Emric said, and went.

He didn't need to be told twice; he'd spent two weeks in the outer reading room for exactly this moment, so that when he was inside he wouldn't waste a second being uncertain. Left wall: administrative records, organized by year. Right wall: academic — coursework, assessments, disciplinary materials. Student Materials, Misconduct Evidence, current year and term. Third shelf from the floor.

Mira stayed near the door, holding the lantern steady.

She was looking at the back wall.

Four cabinets. Dark wood. Floor to ceiling. Set against the stone in a row, evenly spaced. The rest of the archive's storage was iron shelving — open, organized, everything legible from a distance. The cabinets were the only closed furniture in the room.

They had padlocks. Old iron, not arcane, not ward plates — mechanical, the same family as the door's fitting but smaller. The second one from the left was worn smooth on the shackle. Not from age.

From use.

"Got it," Emric said.

He was at the third shelf from the floor, a wooden box open in both hands. Three items inside. He pulled one: a notebook, thick, soft-covered, the edges worn soft from months in a coat pocket. He opened it to the middle. Read three lines. Turned to the back. He exhaled — not a relieved sound exactly, more like something that had been very tightly held choosing, briefly, to let go.

He held the notebook up in the lantern light.

Mira allowed herself one second of something uncomplicated.

"Still twenty-eight minutes," Cass said, from near the door, without taking her eyes off the passage.

Emric laughed under his breath. He tucked the notebook inside his coat.

They had what they came for. The door was eight feet away. The plan, two hours ago in Classroom 2-F, had called for exactly this moment and then an immediate exit. Mira looked at the second cabinet and its worn padlock, and the plan no longer applied.

"I need one of your pins," she said.

Cass looked at her. Then at the cabinets. She reached into her pocket without asking anything and held a pin out.

Mira crossed the room and took it.

"Mira," Emric said. "We have what we came for."

"Thirty seconds."

"We have twenty-eight minutes and what we came for."

"Thirty seconds," she said again.

She set the pin into the keyway of the second cabinet's padlock and found the first lever. These were simpler mechanisms — fewer parts, less to account for. First lever set. Second. She counted. Forty. Third lever, just past forty-five.

The padlock opened.

Emric said nothing. She could feel him deciding not to.

She swung the door open.

---

Inside: a shelf packed with ledgers standing upright. Red cloth bindings, identical, spines labeled in careful block letters with year ranges. She counted them. Eleven. The earliest sat at the far left; they advanced toward the right in chronological order. The rightmost spine read the current year in small, neat letters.

She pulled it out.

She opened it to the current year's page.

The page was laid out in two columns. The left side held a printed heading in block text: *CONCLAVE REGISTRY — YEAR 231 — IDENTIFIED FOR COLLECTION.* Below the heading, three names, handwritten, in a neat administrative script.

Her name was first.

She read the rest. Emric's name. Cass's name. Below those, a date — three months from now, near the end-of-year ceremony. Below that, in a different hand, smaller and more slanted: *Confirmed to Conclave, per standing agreement.*

She turned to the facing page. A printed form. Pre-filled. Thornwick's seal in the upper left corner. The Conclave's seal in the upper right — the same seal she had seen on official placement notices, the ones that were supposed to mean an honor. The form was completed. Every line. The final entry read: *Transmitted: Autumn Term, Year 231.*

She had been named, documented, confirmed, and sent, and she had not known any of it.

She turned to the front of the ledger. The current year took two pages. She turned back further — one year, five years, ten. Every cohort. Three names per year, without exception, in the same neat administrative hand, confirmed in the same slanted note throughout. The form repeated, pre-filled, in the facing column. Forty years of it. The same arrangement, the same language, without interruption.

She read the current year's page again.

She closed the ledger.

---

The cold had not changed. The cedar oil had not changed. The shelves were still iron, the dark still pressed in from the far corners. None of the physical facts of the archive had moved.

"Mira," Emric said.

She held the closed ledger in both hands.

"Mira. We need to move."

She looked at the spine. The year in small, careful letters.

She tucked the ledger under her arm.

She closed the cabinet door and worked the padlock shut. Pressed the shackle to confirm it had caught. Then she crossed the room and held the pin out to Cass.

Cass took it. She looked at Mira's face. Then at the ledger. Then at the door.

"We found more than the notebook," Mira said.

"What is it?" Emric said.

"I'll tell you when we're out."

He started to say something else. Cass moved to the door. She wrapped one hand around the edge and eased it open without scraping the frame, checked the passage — two seconds, silence, a nod — and stepped through.

Emric looked at Mira. He shut his mouth. He followed.

Mira went last. Lantern in one hand, ledger under the other arm. She didn't look back at the cabinets. She stepped into the passage.

---

Cass relocked the padlock on the door. She couldn't leave it undone — an open padlock was evidence; a closed one was furniture — but Mira also knew she would have relocked it regardless, because that was how Cass was made. Leave things as you found them. Better, if possible.

She worked the shackle in. Pressed it. Checked. Pressed again. The faint, final click of the mechanism seating.

The tapestry fell back into place. The grey and brown ships dissolved into the grey and brown water. The archive disappeared behind them.

"Twenty-four minutes," Mira said.

More than enough. The route back to the dormitories was ten minutes if they walked well. They had what they came for, the door was relocked, the passage above should be clear. Twenty-four minutes was enough for almost anything.

Then, from the corridor one floor above — from the east wing, moving through it north to south — a sound.

Soft. Regular. Rhythmic. A chain tapping against a lantern handle as someone walked.

Cass went still.

The sound moved slowly. Not the fluid pace of a timed patrol circuit — the considered pace of someone checking rooms, listening at doors. Methodical. The midnight bell was twenty-four minutes away.

This wasn't the midnight patrol.
