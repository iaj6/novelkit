# The Eleven-Bell

Emric was three minutes late. Mira knew this because she had been counting, and counting was the kind of thing she did when there was nothing useful to do and also when there was.

Classroom 2-F had no working lamp. Emric had left a stub of candle on the front desk before going off to collect information — a cold yellow stub that threw a circle of light about three feet across and gave everything outside it an approximate quality. The east-corridor windows admitted nothing. November. The sky was solid and the North Sea two hundred feet below was not inclined to provide supplementary lighting. The room smelled of old chalk, cold glass, and the specific kind of dust that accumulates in places nobody has used for a full term.

Cass sat on the nearest desk with her feet on the chair, arms crossed, face doing nothing in particular. She had been there when Mira arrived, which had never happened before. Mira was always first. Cass had looked up and said "you're later than I expected" and Mira had not answered because it wasn't worth answering.

"He said eleven," Cass said now.

"He'll be here."

"I know. I'm noting it."

The door opened. Emric came through it sideways, the way he moved through doorways when he was excited — some part of him already past the threshold while the rest caught up. He was breathing harder than walking required.

"The hearing's been moved," he said.

Neither of them spoke.

"First bell. Six in the morning, not mid-morning. Six."

Mira looked at the candle. It had burned to about three-quarters. The wax pool at the center had widened.

"How sure?" she said.

"Prefect Solen was talking to Aldren's assistant outside the ward records room. I was in the alcove. I heard correctly."

"Sit down," Cass said.

"I know the plan."

"Emric." Cass looked at him. "Sit down."

He sat on the desk next to her. He still had the breathing of someone who had walked fast and hadn't quite decided to stop being alarmed yet. He looked at the candle. Mira could see him doing the arithmetic on time. She'd already done it.

"The ward-interaction gap," she said. She stayed at the front of the room with the candle between her and them — she could see their faces clearly, and they got the dark half of hers. "Step one. An unauthorized attempt on the lock-ward starts its reset cycle. Ninety seconds. Step two. In that ninety-second window, before the lock-ward resets, you trigger the trip-wire ward from the outside. Now both wards are active. They cancel each other's alert signal to the erasure ward — the interference makes the erasure ward read it as a false positive. Step three. The erasure ward fires. At nothing. At empty air." She paused. "And then the trip-wire ward has to reset because it just fired. That takes approximately four minutes."

"Four minutes," Emric said. "That's the gap."

"That's the gap. Nobody moves on forty-nine seconds. Nobody moves on fifty-eight." She looked at him. "Sixty. I signal. We move."

"And you calculated this from the ward maintenance logs."

"First-year monitor access."

"You read the ward maintenance logs."

"They were available."

He started to say something. She said "don't" and he stopped.

"Cass," Mira said. "The lock."

Cass reached into her left coat pocket and produced two thin iron pins, their tips bent at a slight angle. She held them flat on her palm, long enough to see. Then she put them away.

"The archive door has an old mechanical fitting," she said. "Five-lever padlock, Hessian pattern. About thirty years older than the ward system. It can be read by feel." A brief pause. "Whoever installed the wards didn't replace it because they assumed anyone breaking in would go for the arcane door."

"Nobody does the mechanical," Emric said.

"Nobody does the mechanical," Cass agreed.

"When did you find this?"

"Building survey. Infrastructure practicum, two months ago. I was mapping the lower east wing. The padlock was interesting, so I noted it." She looked at the pocket where she'd put the pins rather than at either of them. "I found a similar lock in the maintenance stores. Modified those two pins and practiced. Twelve times in the last three weeks. My average is one minute forty-seven."

A silence.

"Cass," Emric said. "Have you been training to break into the archive since October."

"I noticed something that might be useful someday."

"That's not an answer."

"Yes," Cass said. "I have. Two minutes to be safe. Are we doing the window math?"

Emric looked at Mira. Mira did not say anything because there was nothing to say. Cass had, approximately two months ago and for no particular reason, begun preparing for tonight. This was deeply useful. It was also completely normal for Cass, which Mira had long ago decided not to interrogate.

"Has anyone told you you're slightly frightening?" Emric said.

"Multiple people," Cass said, without affect.

"The retrieval," Mira said. "Emric."

"Five minutes." He straightened up. "Filing logic: year ledgers on the left wall, category on the right. Student Materials, Misconduct Evidence, current year — third or fourth shelf from the top, based on how the ward ledgers are organized. I know the pattern. I spent two weeks in the outer reading room at the start of term."

"Reading administrative filing ledgers," Mira said.

"To understand the system. They were interesting." He didn't sound defensive. "Five minutes to find it and be back at the door."

"The math," Cass said.

Mira did it aloud. "Four-minute gap. Two for the lock. Two remaining. Emric needs five. Seven minus four is three minutes short."

"I can go faster."

"Or," Cass said, in the tone she used when she was about to reorganize a problem entirely, "the four-minute gap is the exterior reset timer. If Emric's inside when it closes, the alert logs as an internal origin. Different response protocol."

Mira looked at her. "Twelve minutes if the duty prefect is in the common room."

"Correct."

"That's — yes. That's enough."

"Assuming the duty prefect isn't already in the east wing," Cass said.

"They won't be. Not at eleven-forty."

"Hale does the midnight patrol," Cass said.

"Hale doesn't run," Emric said. "Hale walks fast and checks every door handle."

"Four or five minutes if she's already in the wing," Mira said. "Which she won't be." She looked at the candle. "Twelve-minute window, good conditions. Nine if something goes wrong early. Thirty-six minutes before the midnight bell, minus travel."

They were all looking at the candle. The flame sat perfectly still. No draft reached Classroom 2-F in November; the east corridor was too well sealed.

"It works," Emric said.

"It works if nothing breaks."

"When do your plans break?"

She didn't answer that. It wasn't the kind of question that needed an answer.

Cass said, in the same register she'd used for every other fact in the last ten minutes: "My provisional scholarship has a conduct clause."

Neither of them moved.

"A formal disciplinary finding," she said. "Not expulsion. Not even suspension — just a finding. A written citation on record. That's enough to trigger automatic review." She looked at her hands. Then at the candle. "I'm not backing out. I want you to say, out loud, that you understand what you're asking me to risk."

Emric said: "Cass."

She looked at him.

"I know," she said. "I just wanted you to say it."

"We know what we're asking you to risk," he said.

She looked at Mira.

"We know," Mira said.

Cass nodded once. She didn't complete what she'd left open — the somewhere she'd be sent, the before she'd never named. She didn't explain what automatic review meant or where it ended. She said "I'm in," and the words landed with the same flat weight as all her other facts, and that was all.

Emric set his hands flat on the desk.

"The notebook," he said. "I need you to understand what it is."

He stopped. Started again.

"It's not coursework. I know it looks like coursework — school binding, standard paper. But I've been doing original research into arcane resonance layering. What happens to ward structures when you layer at specific intervals on the same object. The interference patterns, the amplification effects. It's not in any curriculum. It might not be in any published text." He looked at the flame. "I don't know if Aldren took it because he didn't understand what it was. Or because he understood it exactly." He didn't say which possibility worried him more. "Tomorrow morning at first bell I go in front of a panel without it. I know what I'm asking." He stopped. "I just needed to say it."

Mira looked at the candle. The wax pool had widened another half inch.

"Nobody moves without my signal," she said. "Not when the ward gap fires. Not when Cass opens the lock. Not when we're inside and everything says go. My signal. Sixty seconds." She looked at Emric. "Say it back."

"Nobody moves without your signal."

He held her eyes when he said it. Took his time with it. She looked at the candle, because looking at the candle was easier than looking at his face while she made her assessment.

She didn't believe him. She didn't say so. He didn't ask.

Somewhere in the corridor outside, the east staircase lamp hissed faintly.

"Eight minutes before the eleven-bell patrol finishes this corridor," she said. "After that, the east wing is ours."

She leaned forward and blew out the candle.

Dark. Absolute and immediate, the kind that presses against the eyes. Two seconds of adjustment. Mira heard Cass slide off the desk and cross the floor — twelve steps, soft and exact, not a single one placed wrong. She'd counted the room at some point. Of course she had.

A thin line of amber appeared at floor level. Cass had the door open one careful inch. She held it there. One eye in the lamplight from the staircase at the far end of the corridor, forty meters away, burning low.

She tilted her head. Listened.

She nodded.

Cass first. Then Mira. Then Emric. The corridor was cold the way corridors near the sea are cold in November: it came through the casements and pooled at ankle height and didn't go anywhere. The staircase lamp guttered once and held. Nothing else moved.

The classroom door swung shut behind them. The handle caught — a small, clean click that didn't travel, that Mira felt in her teeth because she was listening for it.

She counted. One. Two. Three. Four.

Nothing moved.

She started walking.
