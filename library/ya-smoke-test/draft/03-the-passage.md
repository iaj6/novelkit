# The Passage

"Service passage," Mira said.

No vote. She was already walking — back past the tapestry, east passage, south at the junction before the unlit bracket lamp. Cass fell in behind her. After a beat, so did Emric.

The lantern chain above them was still moving. Slow. South. Getting no further away.

The direct route — east wing staircase, second floor corridor, dormitories — put them on the same floor as Hale before she finished her sweep. The service passage added time and took the east wing off the board entirely. Twenty-four minutes remaining. The passage ran sixty feet, exited in the ground-floor linen corridor, connected to the dormitory staircase. Long way around. Still possible.

Mira ran the numbers twice. Then she stopped running them.

"Tell me now," Emric said, behind her. Quiet, but carrying it since the archive door. "What is it."

"When we're up a floor," she said.

A pause. "We are out of the sub-basement."

"Up a floor."

The passage branched at the south wall. East ran back toward the archive. West toward the main stairwell, which they weren't taking. South was narrow — barely a shoulder's width — running toward the maintenance stores and past them, to the iron door Cass had left unlocked three months ago during the building survey, for no reason she could have articulated at the time.

Cass reached the door first. The handle turned without resistance.

The cold that came through the gap was different from sub-basement cold — drainage-era cold, the cold of stone that had been built to carry running water and was still trying to do that job by other means. Cass went through. Emric angled his shoulders to fit the frame. Mira pulled the door shut behind them and heard it latch.

Sixty feet of narrow stone. No lamp brackets. The ceiling low enough that Emric had to angle his head as well as his shoulders. The smell was mortar and drainage and very old clay. Their lantern was the only light, the brass housing still warm from the archive, the oil level fine. Another two hours in it, easy.

Above them — somewhere — the lantern chain stopped moving.

Mira kept walking.

"She stopped," Emric said.

"I know."

"That means—"

"She's in a room. We don't know which one. We keep moving."

He was quiet for three steps. "The ledger," he said. "Tell me."

The service passage had a narrow stone stair cut into its eastern wall, seven steps up to ground-floor level. The ceiling dropped at the first step. Emric bent at the neck and kept climbing.

"There's a ledger," Mira said. "Red binding. Back cabinet — the second one." She kept her voice even. Counting stairs. One. Two. "Heading says: Conclave Registry. Year 231. Identified for Collection."

Emric was on step four. "Collection of what."

"Us."

She heard his boot stop on stone.

"Keep walking," Cass said.

He started walking.

Mira gave him the rest in four sentences, because four was what they had time for and because she'd been deciding how to say it since the archive cabinet door swung shut. The Registry covered forty years of cohorts. Three names per year, with a date near the end-of-year ceremony. Their names were on the current page. The Conclave's form had been pre-filled and transmitted in the autumn term — before any of them knew this was happening.

Emric was very quiet.

She could see the line of his jaw in the lantern light. Not his expression; just the line of the jaw and the tension in it. His hands were inside his coat. She didn't need to see them to know exactly where they were.

"That's why he took it," Emric said, after a moment. Still flat. "Aldren. My notebook. If someone at the Conclave understood what the research was—" He was working it through as he walked. "—if someone understood what you can do with resonance layering on layered wards — that's a reason to want me somewhere they can see me. That's a reason to move the hearing to six in the morning before anyone—"

"Emric."

He kept walking. Breath coming a little faster, but he kept walking.

They were past the stairs, on the ground-floor stretch now. Thirty feet of passage left to the linen corridor. The lantern made long, tilted shadows of all three of them against the stone.

"What do we do with it," Emric said.

Cass didn't look back. "What are we going to do with it."

Not the same question. Both of them were asking Mira. Only one of them was asking what she'd already decided.

"Burn it," Emric said. "Common room fireplace. Tonight, before first bell. There's no evidence."

"The Registry goes back forty years," Mira said. "Forty cohorts."

"That's not our problem right now."

"It was someone's problem," Cass said, still forward, still moving. "Every year. For forty years. Now it's ours."

Emric's voice went tighter. "If we take it, they know someone broke in. If we burn it, they know someone broke in. At least if we burn it, no one's caught carrying it."

"If we burn it," Mira said, "we are the only people who ever know what was in it."

They walked. The stone walls were close enough that she could have touched both sides at once if she'd spread her arms. She didn't.

"There's no version of this where we're not already in it," Emric said. "Our names are on that page. They already transmitted. Burning the ledger doesn't change that." A beat. "It only changes what we can be caught with."

"It changes what the next cohort has," Mira said.

The shape of the silence after that was different from the silence before it. He kept walking — his boots were quiet on the stone, quieter than she expected — but whatever he'd been about to say, he didn't say it.

"The forty prior cohorts," she said. "They didn't know either. Their names are still in the other eleven ledgers in that cabinet. We know what happened to them — or at least we know the shape of it. If we burn the current-year ledger, we burn the only record that this happened to anyone at all."

She said it quietly. Not as an argument. As a fact.

The thing she'd been holding since she closed the cabinet door and was only now putting down.

"Twelve minutes," Cass said.

No other opinion. No vote. Just the number: the remaining time, offered once, and that was all. The argument had run out of passage.

Emric walked in silence for ten feet. Then, without looking at her: "Whatever you decide, Mira."

Not permission. She knew what permission sounded like, and it didn't sound like that. It sounded like someone setting down something they'd been carrying and leaving it precisely on the person who was going to end up with it regardless.

She looked at the back of his head.

He reached into his coat and held up the notebook, briefly, in the lantern light. Then put it back.

---

The linen corridor was warmer than the service passage by enough to matter — sizing and lamp oil, the smell of a building that had people in it and intended to keep them. The low iron door swung open and the warmth came through and Mira stood in it for one second, just the one, before she remembered they were still moving.

The dormitory staircase was fifteen feet away. Emric was already at the foot of it, one hand on the banister, looking back. A wall bracket here, actually lit, oil in the reservoir, casting proper yellow light over the landing.

Eight minutes.

They were going to make it.

Mira stopped.

She didn't realize she'd stopped until she noticed Cass had stopped too — precisely when she had, without comment, without asking why. Emric stood on the landing and watched.

Mira held the Registry in both hands.

Three options. She held them the way she held a calculation: not feeling, not deciding yet, just seeing the whole thing at once before she moved.

She could leave it here. Set it on the corridor floor, walk upstairs, be in her dormitory room when the bell rang. Someone would find it in the morning. The school would retrieve it quietly, or wouldn't — she had no way to know. The safest option, if safe meant surviving the next twelve hours. The one that cost the most.

She could burn it. Common room fireplace, ash before dawn. No evidence in anyone's coat. No record of what it said. And no record of forty years of prior cohorts — all those names in the other eleven ledgers, in the locked cabinet, in the locked archive, in a sub-basement most people at Thornwick didn't know existed.

She could take it. Tuck it inside her coat. Walk upstairs. Spend the five hours before the hearing figuring out who to tell, and whether anyone would listen, and what it meant for the cohort after theirs and the cohort after that. With no guarantee that anyone would believe her. With no guarantee she was right about what to do with it. With no way to know whether she was making a decision or just making a gesture.

She knew two things.

It was the only copy of the current year's page. She was the one holding it.

Mira folded the Registry once across its spine — firmly, precisely, the way she folded anything she meant to keep — and put it inside her coat, flat against her ribs. She did up the top button.

She looked at Emric. He held up the notebook once, a small gesture, and said nothing.

She walked to the foot of the stairs. First step.

Second step.

Third step, and the midnight bell began — the gatehouse tower, one floor up and a hundred feet east, its clapper striking once —
