## 04-the-three-options.md

**Varga's radio address — "Halvorsen" vs. "Hartmann"**

`canon/characters.md` specifies: *"He calls Elena 'Hartmann' on the radio (protocol)"* and notes the asymmetry with Elena always calling him "Varga" as a meaningful character detail. In this chapter, Varga uses the station identifier "Halvorsen" throughout both radio exchanges — never "Hartmann":

- 0800 exchange: *"South Pole reads you, Halvorsen. Morning."* / *"All right. Until 1800, Halvorsen."*
- 1800 exchange: *"South Pole reads you, Halvorsen. You should see it tonight..."*

Using the station name for opening/closing calls may be intentional radio protocol, distinct from addressing her personally mid-exchange. But if the "Hartmann"/"Varga" asymmetry is meant to be present and felt in the radio scenes, neither exchange currently carries it. Writer should decide whether Varga should use "Hartmann" at some point in these conversations — particularly in the warmer 1800 exchange where the unspoken friendship is the point.

## 11-something-in-your-voice.md

**Two issues requiring writer judgment. Three word/clause fixes were applied directly (see below).**

---

### Applied fixes

1. **Generic "you" → third-person (fax section).**
   Original: `"the way you came to know all the station's sounds at their distance and their register"`
   Fixed: `"the way she had come to know all the station's sounds at their distance and their register"`
   Reason: POV slip into colloquial second-person; style guide specifies strict third-person limited.

2. **Generic "you" → third-person (radio reflection).**
   Original: `"the way you sometimes heard a thing the moment it left you and understood exactly how it would land"`
   Fixed: `"the way she sometimes heard a thing the moment it left her and understood exactly how it would land"`
   Same reason as above.

3. **Italics removed from remembered speech (Amsterdam memory).**
   Original: `he had said, without hesitation and without looking at anyone but her: *yes, and I think the corollary is this*`
   Fixed: italics removed; plain text, colon retained.
   Reason: Style guide prohibits special formatting (italics, em-dash brackets) for interior/recalled experience. The colon introduction is sufficient.

---

### Flagged — needs writer judgment

**A. Opening sentence fragment.**
Line: `"The afternoon she spent on the directional parameters."`
Issue: A sentence fragment used as an orienting/atmospheric opener. Style guide is explicit: "Do not use sentence fragments for atmospheric effect. The narration is not cinematic." If this is a recurring chapter-opening device across the novel, changing only chapter 11 would break series consistency — the writer needs to decide whether to normalize the pattern or remove it here.

**B. Hedged emotional naming.**
Line: `"Her first response to the possibility of more time was something uncomfortably close to relief."`
Issue: The construction `something uncomfortably close to [emotion]` is structurally parallel to `"a wave of something she couldn't name"` — which style.md lists as a specific example of what to avoid. The hedging (`uncomfortably close to`) is the problem: it names the emotion while pretending to withhold it. The correct approach per style.md would be to cut the sentence and let the action that follows (putting down the advisory, closing the log, returning to the receiver hut) carry the weight. However, this sentence is the emotional pivot of the weather-system section; cutting it changes the scene's meaning. Rephrasing it still names the same thing. Writer judgment needed on whether the pivot is necessary here or whether the action carries it without the named state.
