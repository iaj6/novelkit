# Chapter 13 — HS-910603

**One-line summary:** Elena writes the complete formal record into the official station log, labels the tape with the designation HS-910603, annotates the chart printout, and goes to sleep without transmitting — the documentation is done.

**POV:** Elena Hartmann  
**Tense:** Third-person limited past  
**Setting:** Receiver hut; main hut desk. Night of Day 11.  
**Target words:** ~2,800

---

## Goal
Elena wants to produce a document that will outlast her presence at this station — a record that any competent scientist could read, evaluate, and independently verify. She is doing the work properly, for the last time, because it is her work and she knows how.

## Obstacle
The work itself is not difficult; she has been preparing for it across the full eleven days. The obstacle is the irreversibility of it. Writing the finding into the official station log is not the same as writing it in her field log. The official log is a permanent institutional record. When she closes it, the decision is enacted. There is no drawer to put it in.

---

## Action Beats

- **She begins after the 1800 Varga call.** She has eaten. She has the official station log open on the main hut desk — a bound A4 logbook, the entries in different hands across the season, the last entry before her solo period in Brandt's handwriting. She opens her field log beside it for reference. She picks up the pen.

- **She writes the date and context.** *June [date], 2100 local. Solo operation period, Day 11 of 12. Below is the formal record of an anomalous signal event detected on the third night of the current polar darkness period.* She writes in her standard log hand — clear, upright, unhurried.

- **The signal entry.** She writes the full detection record into the official log: date, time (21:14:07), duration (47 seconds), frequency parameters, signal-to-noise ratio, bearing and sky-position coordinates, instrument configuration at time of detection. Everything a scientist would need to find and replicate the coordinates. Everything except the content of the signal. She writes around the content precisely — she names its duration, its internal structure (organized, non-random, internally coherent), what makes it inconsistent with any known source. She does not write what it says, or whether it says anything. The narration notes this absence without remarking on it.

- **The verification summary.** She writes a clean summary of the two full Hartmann Protocol verifications — Day 3 and Day 11 — with dates, methodology, and results. She writes the conclusions. She writes: *On the basis of full verification via the Hartmann Protocol (methodology: Hartmann 1979), the author concludes that the signal recorded at Halvorsen Station on [date] is of non-terrestrial, non-natural origin. This conclusion has been reached twice, independently, with identical results.* She signs: *E. Hartmann, Ph.D. [date].*

- **She goes to the receiver hut.** She takes the 9-track tape from the fireproof box. She pulls out the label she wrote on Day 1 — handwritten, with the date and time. She makes a new label, clean and formal, on a proper label form from the station supplies: *HS-910603. Halvorsen Station, 1991 June 03, 21:14:07 UTC. Non-standard signal recording. See station log, p. [page number]. Backup copy.* She applies the new label beside the original. She initials both. She puts the tape back in the fireproof box. She latches it.

- **The chart printout.** She takes the chart printout from the log binder. She annotates it by hand: coordinates, date, time, duration marks, a brief note in the margin: *Primary trace, 47s. HS-910603. Verified x2. E.H.* She numbers the chart pages and files them in order behind the cover sheet. She puts it back in the log binder and marks the section with a paper tab.

- **She returns to the main hut desk.** She checks the official log entry against her field log: it is complete and accurate. She closes the official log. She does not open the desk drawer where the two paper drafts are clipped together. She will leave those there for Brandt to find, if she opens the drawer — they are not the record; the record is in the log. They are evidence of what she considered.

- **She thinks about transmitting.** One beat — she picks up the handset from the radio room desk and holds it. The radio is off. She turns it on. The frequency hisses. She does not key the transmit button. She turns the radio off. She puts the handset down. She goes back to her bunk.

- **She lies awake for a while.** Not anxiously — with the particular stillness of a person who has completed a piece of work and is letting the completion settle. She thinks about Brandt stepping off the LC-130. She thinks about how she will say it — not what she will say, just how. Whether she will say it inside the Jamesway or on the walk from the landing strip. She decides: inside. After coffee. She wants Brandt sitting down.

- **She sleeps.** The narration notes it: she sleeps through to 0600 without waking. This is the first full night's sleep she has had since the signal arrived.

---

## Outcome / Turn
The documentation is complete. The official station log holds the full record. The tape is labeled. The printout is annotated and filed. The MicroVAX has the primary copy. All three copies are present, documented, and cross-referenced. When Brandt opens the station log, she will find everything she needs. Elena has slept. The work is done.

## New Question Raised
Now that the record exists in permanent form, does Elena feel differently about what she has? Does the fact of the documentation change the weight of what she carries into tomorrow?
