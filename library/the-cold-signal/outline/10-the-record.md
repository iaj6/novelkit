# Chapter 10 — The Record

**One-line summary:** Elena sits through a long night thinking about what her career record will say under each remaining option; she separates the question of legacy from the question of what to do and, separated, sees both more clearly.

**POV:** Elena Hartmann  
**Tense:** Third-person limited past  
**Setting:** Main hut desk; receiver hut (briefly for instrument checks); main hut galley. Night through early morning, Days 9–10.  
**Target words:** ~2,700

---

## Goal
Elena cannot sleep. She does not fight it — she gets up, makes coffee, and sits with the question she has been resisting for nine days: what does she want? Not what the protocol requires. Not what Brandt will expect. What does Elena Hartmann, at sixty-four, with nine months left on her last contract, want?

## Obstacle
Every time she approaches this question from the direction of her career, it turns into a calculation about legacy — and the calculation is not helpful, because the signal makes every legacy calculation absurd. You cannot weigh thirty years of methodological contributions against this. The scales are wrong. She has to find a different axis.

---

## Action Beats

- **She wakes at 0248.** The receiver hut instruments are set to alarm if anything notable happens; the alarm has not sounded. She is awake anyway. She lies in her bunk for twenty minutes, then gets up. Pulls on her station clothes over her long underwear. Puts the kettle on.

- **She thinks about her career.** Standing in the galley with the coffee, she thinks about the professional record in the way she might review a long instrument log: event by event, year by year. The big moments: the false positive she eliminated in 1974 that saved a colleague from an embarrassing premature announcement. The methodology paper in 1979 — the one the Parkes preprint cited. The moment at the VLA in 1983 when she nearly called something, and didn't, and was right not to. The sessions at Parkes, the decade at the planetary survey, the five Antarctic seasons. A long, careful, skeptical record of what the universe is not doing.

- **What the record looks like under option 1.** If she transmits immediately — the NSF protocol plays out, the chain of command activates, the finding is logged and scheduled and claimed by the institutional process. Her name is on the original detection. The paper will credit her, correctly, as the discoverer. She will also be the retired person who handed it over, and the paper will be written by younger people who will spend the next thirty years on it. Her contribution to the science ends at the moment of transmission. She will be the person who found it and passed it on. This is honorable. It is also not what she wants.

- **What the record looks like under option 2.** If she holds for orderly publication — she writes the paper herself, sole author or with a small co-author list she chooses, submits through peer review, publishes under her own name before she retires. This is the shape of a career that ends on its own terms. It is also the slowest path to the finding reaching the scientific community — six months, a year, two years of review. She would be holding the largest finding in the history of science back from the world for publication credit. The thought of that, stated plainly, is uncomfortable in a way she does not brush past.

- **What is actually left.** She clears away the career calculation — sets it to one side in her mind the way she would set aside a line of analysis that is leading her wrong. The question underneath: what does the finding itself require? Not what is owed to Elena, but what the signal deserves. She thinks about this for a long time sitting at the main hut desk with the coffee going cold.

- **The insight.** She does not owe the signal a rapid transmission. She does not owe it a delayed publication. She owes it what she would owe any other finding she had made in thirty years of work: precise, rigorous, fully documented science, followed by responsible disclosure to a person she trusts, followed by the apparatus doing what the apparatus does. The apparatus she has been afraid of — the institutional claim, the protocol chain, the room she wouldn't be invited to — those things are real, but they come after the documentation. They have always come after. She has been confusing the fear of what comes next with the question of what to do now.

- **She writes in her field log.** 0530. Four pages of running thought, more discursive than anything else she has written in the log — less like a log entry and more like a letter to herself. The last paragraph: *The signal is not mine. It has not been mine since I made the copies on night one. What is mine — what has always been mine — is the work. Do the work. The rest follows.* She underlines *The rest follows.*

- **Instrument check.** 0600. She goes to the receiver hut and runs the standard check. Everything is nominal. The MicroVAX fan is running perfectly; she notices this and it feels like a small, mechanical assurance. She holds the labeled 9-track tape for a moment before putting it back in the fireproof box.

- **0800 call with Varga.** Brief. He says he was up late — the potential herbie that had been developing has dissipated; all clear. She says: good. He says: and you? She says: I was up late too. He says: *Working or worrying?* The question is gentle and precise. She says: *Thinking.* He says: *That's the same thing for you.* She says: *Yes.* The call ends normally, but she carries the exchange in her head for the rest of the morning.

---

## Outcome / Turn
Elena has separated the question of her career legacy from the question of what the data requires. The result of that separation is that the path ahead is clearer: she does not transmit immediately, she does not hold for personal publication credit. She documents the work with full scientific rigor and then she hands it to a person she trusts when that person arrives. The blank fourth line on the options page is nearly filled.

## New Question Raised
The path is clearer — but there are still two days before the LC-130. And Varga's question — *working or worrying?* — sits in her chest. What happens in the next call if he asks something more direct?
