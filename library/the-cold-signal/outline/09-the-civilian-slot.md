# Chapter 9 — The Civilian Slot

**One-line summary:** Elena calls Carol in Boulder during the scheduled civilian traffic window; their warm, elliptical conversation ends with the only lie Elena tells anyone — she says she is sleeping all right.

**POV:** Elena Hartmann  
**Tense:** Third-person limited past  
**Setting:** Main hut radio room; main hut galley after the call. Day 9.  
**Target words:** ~2,700

---

## Goal
Elena calls Carol because it is the scheduled civilian slot and she has been holding Carol in the back of her mind — a warmth she has deferred. She wants to hear her daughter's voice. She does not intend to say anything she shouldn't say. These two things are not in conflict, except that carrying this particular knowledge and talking to your daughter in an ordinary way turns out to be harder than she expected.

## Obstacle
Carol asks the right question. Not about the signal — Carol doesn't know there is a signal. She asks about sleep, about whether Elena is holding up in the solo stretch, with the particular attentive care of a nurse who is also a daughter. The question lands exactly where Elena is most exposed. Elena lies cleanly — *yes, I'm sleeping all right* — and the lie is instantly recognizable to her as a lie in a way her omissions with Varga have not been.

---

## Action Beats

- **The hours before the call.** Elena works through the morning monitoring, eats a reasonable lunch, goes to the receiver hut for instrument checks. The civilian slot is scheduled for 1400. She is aware of it the way you're aware of something you're looking forward to and slightly afraid of. At 1345 she goes to the main hut to prepare the radio.

- **Establishing the call protocol.** A brief beat showing the mechanics: scheduling through McMurdo's radio relay, the patch to the commercial phone network, the delay inherent in the system, the slightly unreal quality of the connection — Carol's voice in Boulder coming through the HF link like something traveling a long distance to reach her. Which it is.

- **The call opens.** Carol answers on the second ring. Her voice is warm and normal and immediately real. There is a small delay in both directions, which they've both learned to pause for. Carol says: *Mum.* (She uses Mum sometimes, not always — a marker of warmth.) Elena says: *Hello.* And for a moment that is enough.

- **What Carol talks about.** Carol is three shifts into a hard week at the hospital — a particular patient, an elderly man who kept asking where his wife was (his wife died six years ago, Carol doesn't know if he knows). She tells the story well. It is ordinary and human and full of the specific care and fatigue of nursing. Elena listens. She asks the right questions: *How old is he? What does the family say? Are you managing the shift load?* She is present. This is one of the things she knows how to be — a listener. She is better at it on the phone from Antarctica than she is in the same room.

- **Carol asks the questions.** After the patient story: *How are you, though? Are you eating?* Elena says: *I am.* *How's the work going?* Elena says: *The monitoring is running clean. I've been doing maintenance.* *And the dark — how's the polar dark treating you?* Elena says: *It's fine. I don't notice it much by now.* These are all substantially true. Then Carol says: *Are you sleeping all right?*

- **The lie.** Elena says: *Yes.* One syllable. She is not sleeping all right — she has been waking at 0300 most nights, which is when she replays the signal in her head, and the nights before the fan crisis she barely slept at all. The lie is immediate and clean and she knows exactly what she has done. The word *yes* is the only thing she says in the entire eleven days that is straightforwardly false.

- **The call continues.** Carol talks about the drive back from Boulder to her apartment. Normal things. Elena laughs once, genuinely. There is a moment, near the end of the call, when Carol says: *You sound — a little different. Not bad, just — like you're in the middle of something.* Elena says: *Winter solo does that. I'm fine, Carol.* Carol says: *Okay. I trust you.* There is a weight to this that may be nothing. Elena says: *I know.* They say goodbye with warmth; they say they love each other; Elena hangs up the handset.

- **After the call.** Elena sits in the radio room for three or four minutes. Then she goes to the galley and boils water and does not make anything with it. She stands at the galley window looking at nothing. She is thinking about the word *trust* and what it costs and what it is worth, and she is not thinking about the signal directly but the signal is in everything she is thinking.

- **She goes back to work.** Receiver hut, instrument check, log update. She does not write about the call in her field log. She writes: *Day 9. Instruments nominal. No unusual events.*

- **1800 Varga call.** Standard. Weather, status. He says: *How's the day been?* She says: *Quiet. Civilian slot this afternoon.* He says: *Carol?* She says: *Yes.* He says: *Good.* There is genuine warmth in his good — he knows Carol exists, knows what she means to Elena, has never made any fuss about it. The call ends simply.

---

## Outcome / Turn
Elena has made the only outright lie of the eleven days. Unlike her omissions with Varga — which are shaped by protocol and professional distance — this is a lie to her daughter, and it is about the most ordinary thing: sleep. The call reveals the weight she is carrying by the fact that she cannot carry it normally. She could not tell Carol the truth, and she could not tell Carol nothing, so she lied. She knows this.

## New Question Raised
What kind of person carries something like this for nine days? What kind of person calls her daughter and says she's sleeping fine? And is Elena now certain what kind of person she is, or is she still finding out?

- Carol says: *I trust you.* — a line the drafter should use; it carries weight without being explained
