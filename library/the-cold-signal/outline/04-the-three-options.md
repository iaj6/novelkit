# Chapter 4 — The Three Options

**One-line summary:** Elena writes the three options out in her logbook, drafts a complete formal transmission notification — then does not send it; the decision acquires physical weight.

**POV:** Elena Hartmann  
**Tense:** Third-person limited past  
**Setting:** Main hut desk (primary); receiver hut (briefly). Day 4. Afternoon and evening.  
**Target words:** ~2,600

---

## Goal
Elena wants to think clearly about what she is going to do. She approaches the decision the same way she approaches a technical problem: by naming the components explicitly and examining each one. She is not yet deciding. She is mapping.

## Obstacle
The options don't behave like clean technical alternatives. Each one has implications she cannot fully hold at once: institutional, professional, personal. And the act of writing them down makes them more real, not less — which was not what she expected.

---

## Action Beats

- **She begins at the main hut desk.** Morning light (none). She has her field log and a pen. She is deliberate about this: she puts the coffee within reach, she adjusts the desk lamp, she opens the log to a blank page. The formality of it.

- **She writes the three options.** In her log, numbered and labeled the way she would label sections of a methodology:
  1. *Transmit immediately — full data package, notification per NSF/USAP protocol, copy to Varga, copy to McMurdo, copy to institutional chain.*
  2. *Preserve for orderly publication — hold data, complete formal write-up, submit through standard peer-review channel under sole authorship before retirement.*
  3. *Destroy — erase MicroVAX disk, burn chart printout, degauss tape. No record. No one knows.*
  She stares at option 3 for a full minute. She does not cross it out.

- **She thinks through what option 1 requires physically.** She walks herself through the mechanical steps: compose a message, set up the data modem, schedule a satellite transmission window, key the Codan, send the notification to McMurdo, wait for acknowledgment. She goes to the receiver hut and looks at the Codan. She doesn't touch it. She goes back to the main hut.

- **The draft transmission.** At the desk: she writes out the formal notification as she would transmit it. She knows the format; she wrote a version of this notification once before, for a colleague, in the other direction — *this is why the signal is not what you think it is.* She writes it perfectly. Signal parameters. Verification summary. Methodology citation. Conclusion. It takes ninety minutes. She reads it back. It is exactly right.

- **She folds it and puts it in the desk drawer.** She does not tear it up. She does not transmit it. She puts it in the drawer and closes the drawer and sits for a moment looking at the surface of the desk.

- **The 0800 call (placed mid-chapter, before drafting).** Varga reports ionospheric conditions are degraded — they may lose some hours of the midday window. Elena says noted, she'll plan accordingly. He asks how the overnight data is running. She says: nominal. He says: good. The call is under three minutes.

- **She thinks about Brandt.** She knows exactly what Brandt will do when she arrives: log the instrument records, pull the MicroVAX archive, review the tape catalog. She will find anything Elena leaves intact; she will notice any gap Elena creates. Elena has worked with Brandt long enough to know this is not a threat — it is simply what Brandt is. The thought of Brandt's professional precision feels, in this moment, slightly like being watched from a distance.

- **She returns to the options.** She opens the log and reads what she wrote. She adds a fourth line: *4. Something else.* She leaves it blank. She stares at the blank line.

- **1800 Varga call.** Varga has aurora visible again. He describes it briefly — he knows she can't see it at her latitude from inside. She says she'll go out after the call and look for it. He says: you won't see it. She says: I know. There is a warmth between them in this exchange that neither names. She goes outside after the call. She looks up. The sky is clear, no aurora visible, but the Milky Way is absolute overhead. She is outside for four minutes. The cold settles in past her jacket. She goes back inside.

---

## Outcome / Turn
Elena has written the options down and given each one its full shape. She has written — and not sent — a perfect draft transmission. Option 4 exists on the page: a blank line she has not yet filled. The decision is no longer abstract.

## New Question Raised
What would option 4 be? What would it mean to do something the protocol doesn't account for?
