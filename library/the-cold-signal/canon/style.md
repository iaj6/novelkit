# Style

## POV and Tense

Third-person limited, past tense. The narration is bound tightly to Elena — no information reaches the reader that Elena does not have, observe, or think. The perspective should feel like her memory of each day, filtered through her trained precision. The narration is allowed to be slightly more reflective than Elena's real-time consciousness — it can observe her behavior from a half-step back — but this distance is never large enough to become editorial. We do not judge her. We watch her.

## Sentence Rhythm

The default sentence is medium-length, complete, and does its work before stopping. Longer sentences occur when Elena is reasoning through something: they may run through several subordinate clauses, each clause building on the last the way a calculation builds, before arriving somewhere. Shorter sentences mark observation or arrival — the moment she sees something, decides something, or stops. Two-word sentences are permitted only when they earn it.

**Do:** *The tape ran for forty-seven seconds. She played it back three times before she let herself write anything down.*  
**Don't:** *Her heart pounded as she realized what she was hearing.*

Do not use sentence fragments for atmospheric effect. The narration is not cinematic.

## Prose Register

Restrained but not cold. The prose notices things — the tick of pipes, the particular cold of a galley mug at 0300, the difference between station silence and the silence outside — because Elena notices things. These observations are not decoration; they are how Elena processes her environment. They are also, during the eleven days, displaced attention: she is thinking about the signal and noticing the temperature of her coffee.

Technical vocabulary appears where it is Elena's natural vocabulary. Never explain a technical term to the reader unless Elena is explaining it to herself. If she would know it without thinking, so does the reader.

Period detail (the sound a 1991 fax makes, the specific buttons on the Codan, the smell of 9-track tape) is used as texture, never indulged as nostalgia.

## Dialogue

Sparse. For the bulk of the story Elena is alone; dialogue exists only in radio exchanges and, once, a scheduled call to Boulder. Radio dialogue carries its own register:

- Slightly formal, with call signs at opening and close
- Clipped by protocol; pleasantries are short
- There are natural pauses and static; responses may be brief
- The medium makes long speeches impossible

Elena's radio voice is more careful than her internal voice. She selects words. This carefulness is visible to Varga, who knows her. The gap between what she says in these exchanges and what she is thinking is where the story's primary tension lives. Draft these scenes as two separate tracks — what comes through the handset, and what runs underneath — without announcing the split.

Do not use dialogue to convey exposition. Varga does not explain things to Elena to inform the reader.

## Narrative Distance

Close but not claustrophobic. The narration describes what Elena sees and does with physical precision. It does not always name her emotional state; the emotion is implied in what she chooses to notice, what she avoids, what she repeats in her log. When she is afraid, she checks the data again. When she is overwhelmed, she boils water and does not drink it. This is how the narration shows interiority: through action and attention, not through adjectives.

Avoid: *She felt afraid.* *She was suddenly overwhelmed.* *A wave of something she couldn't name.*

Use: the detail she focuses on when the alternative is acknowledging what she found.

## Internal Monologue

Woven into the narration without special formatting. No italics, no em-dash brackets, no announced shifts. The prose slides between external description and Elena's thought without announcement. The reader should not always be certain where observation ends and inference begins — that ambiguity is intentional.

## Pacing

Follows Elena's attention. She works slowly and deliberately; so does the prose. The Antarctic winter is a chamber that enforces slowness; the narrative inhabits that chamber.

When Elena is focused — running a verification, replaying the recording, drafting a log entry — the prose is precise and the scene is long relative to the elapsed time. When she is dissociated or avoidant, the narration can compress hours into a single paragraph, or skip from 0900 to 1600 without accounting for the middle.

Action, when it occurs (equipment fault, weather event, a technical problem with the radio), is rendered plainly and quickly. It is not elevated. Elena is a person who handles physical problems as part of the job.

## What to Avoid

- Epiphanic moments named as such ("She understood, suddenly, that—")
- Melodrama in any register — the material does not need it
- Rushing toward meaning; let the reader arrive
- Overexplaining Elena's emotional state
- Treating the signal's content as a mystery to be solved
- Treating Elena's decision as a moral problem with a discoverable answer
- Adjectives that substitute for observation ("the eerie silence," "the cold loneliness")
- Foreshadowing that winks at the reader
- The word "suddenly"
