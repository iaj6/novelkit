# Characters

## Dr. Elena Hartmann — Protagonist / POV

**Age:** 64  
**Role:** Senior astrobiologist; principal investigator for the USAP-affiliated radio survey at Halvorsen Station  
**Background:** Born in Heidelberg, 1927; emigrated to the United States in 1963; PhD in radio astronomy from Caltech, 1964. Thirty years of fieldwork: Arecibo, Parkes Observatory (Western Australia), the VLA in New Mexico, a decade at a mid-latitude planetary radio survey, and five Antarctic seasons beginning in the 1980s. This is her second-to-last season. She has retirement paperwork on her kitchen table in Pasadena.

**Career identity:** Elena Hartmann is what happens to a scientist who finds, repeatedly, that the exciting interpretation is wrong. Her career-defining contribution is not a discovery but a methodology — a protocol for eliminating false positives in narrow-band radio detection that is now standard practice at six observatories and carries her name on the second page of papers she had no involvement in. The institutional joke is that if Hartmann calls it, it's real. She has heard the joke enough times that it no longer lands.

**Voice:** Economical and precise. She does not fill silence. She uses technical vocabulary because it is accurate, not to perform expertise. Drily funny in a way that requires knowing her. In radio exchanges she is more formal than in her written log; in her written log she is more formal than in her thoughts. There is a version of Elena who is warmer than any of these — Carol, her daughter, has met her — but that version does not often travel to Antarctica.

**Inner drives:**  
Elena's deepest professional need is for precision — to be exactly right in ways that can be verified. This has served her career and cost her a marriage (David left in 1982, citing, accurately, that she was more present to the data than to anything else). She has made peace with this, more or less. What she has not made peace with is the suspicion, grown quietly over the last decade, that her famous skepticism has become a kind of shelter. She stopped believing contact was possible somewhere in her forties. This was not announced; she simply stopped. The signal forces her to reckon with the fact that she has spent twenty years not believing in something she has spent thirty years looking for.

The other pressure, smaller but persistent: this is her second-to-last season. After the data is processed and the papers are written, the long careful career ends. She has a sense of what that career will look like in the record — what it will be remembered as — and the signal makes that calculation both easier and impossible.

**Physical:** Small (5'4"), unhurried in movement. Gray hair cropped short for efficiency rather than style. She reads without glasses for distance; bifocals for close work, which she keeps on a cord around her neck and habitually loses anyway. Her hands are capable and quick; she learned early that Antarctic fieldwork rewards dexterity over strength.

**Relationships:**  
- Divorced (1982). David Hartmann lives in Tucson; they are not in contact.  
- Daughter Carol, 38, a nurse in Boulder. They love each other with the particular care of two people who know the shape of what is missing between them.  
- Tomás Varga: her closest remaining professional friend. One of four or five people whose judgment she fully trusts.  
- Annelie Brandt: her team leader, 15 years younger; Elena respects her competence and finds her occasionally exhausting.

## Dr. Tomás Varga — Secondary: Radio Contact

**Age:** 56  
**Role:** Radio astronomer at Amundsen-Scott South Pole Station; Elena's designated check-in contact for the solo period  
**Background:** Born in Budapest, 1935. Left Hungary in 1956, the year of the uprising. Completed his doctorate in the US; has spent most of his career in radio astronomy with an adjacent interest in SETI theory. He and Elena met at a SETI colloquium in Amsterdam in 1973 and have sustained their friendship across conferences, correspondence, and — since the early 1980s — shared Antarctic deployments. He is currently in his third South Pole winter.

**Voice:** Warm, careful, patient. He has a habit of long pauses before responding on the radio — not hesitation, but the pause of a person who is actually considering what to say next. He calls Elena "Hartmann" on the radio (protocol) and "Elena" in private correspondence. She calls him Varga, always. Both register this and neither has addressed it.

**Relationship to Elena:** Not romantic, but the longest active friendship of her adult life. He is one of the few people who can make her laugh, and one of the very few whose scientific judgment she trusts without reservation. He knows her patterns well enough to hear the difference between Elena-being-precise and Elena-withholding-something. The reader tracks him because Elena's silences in their conversations are where the pressure of the story lives. He does not know what she found. Whether he suspects something changes across the eleven days.

**His own stake:** Varga has spent his career believing contact was possible and not caring much whether it was. He is at peace with the searching in a way Elena never fully managed. This is visible to her and she has never told him it irritates her slightly.

## Dr. Annelie Brandt — Secondary: Absent Presence

**Age:** 49  
**Role:** Team leader for the full Halvorsen Station season; Elena's immediate operational supervisor  
**Background:** German-born glaciologist from Munich. Efficient, unsentimental, deeply practical. Has run two previous Antarctic seasons and knows how to manage a small station with a minimum of drama. She respects Elena's scientific record without being awed by it, which Elena finds restful.

**In the story:** Brandt appears almost entirely as an absence — as the person Elena imagines conversations with, the voice that will ask the right questions when she steps off the LC-130. Elena is under no illusions about Brandt: she will notice everything, ask about the log gaps, look at the tape archive. What Elena has or hasn't done with the data will be immediately visible to her.

**First physical appearance:** Final chapter, stepping off the LC-130.

## Carol Hartmann — Tertiary: One Radio Call

**Age:** 38  
**Role:** Elena's daughter; registered nurse at a hospital in Boulder, Colorado  
**Background:** Grew up partly around Elena's fieldwork and made a deliberate choice of a life with fixed hours, fixed geography, and people who needed her in ways that were immediate and physical. She and Elena love each other with the specific care of two people who understand why there is distance between them.

**In the story:** Elena calls her once during the eleven days on the scheduled civilian traffic slot. Their conversation is not about the signal. What Elena says and doesn't say in this call reveals the weight of what she's carrying without naming it. Carol asks if she's sleeping all right. Elena says yes. This is the only lie she tells anyone.
