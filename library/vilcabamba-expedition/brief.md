# Brief

## Premise

1938. Captain James Holroyd — a washed-out British army surveyor blacklisted from the Indian Survey for an incident he refuses to explain — is hired by the Royal Geographical Society for an off-the-books expedition into the eastern Cordillera Vilcabamba in the Peruvian Andes. The target is a city said to be sealed under the snow on the lee face of an unnamed peak: a place older than the Inca, mentioned in three independent Spanish missionary accounts from the 1570s and then in nothing for three hundred and sixty years. Holroyd is not the Society's first choice. Their first choice is dead. Their second declined. Holroyd is the man who has surveyed the eastern range and has nothing left to lose.

His party is small. Isobel Carrow, twenty-seven, daughter of a recently-dead Methodist missionary, speaks Quechua, Aymara, and broken Yagua, and is fluent in the way mountain people withhold information. Mariano Quispe, an arriero from the lower valleys, knows mules and weather and the trails as far as the snowline; he has been above it twice. They are racing — without saying so aloud — against a German team sponsored by the Ahnenerbe, led by a Munich-trained mountaineer named Klaus Riedel. Riedel has read the same Spanish accounts and reached different conclusions. He is closer than Holroyd fears.

The journey is hard. Mules die. A rope bridge gives way under the wrong man. Riedel's party closes on them above seventeen thousand feet. And the city, when at last they reach it, is exactly what the missionary accounts described and nothing they were prepared for.

## Length and shape

- Target length: ~20,000 words
- Number of chapters: 8

## Voice

Third-person limited past, mostly bound to Holroyd. The POV may shift to Isobel or Mariano for one chapter each where the story is theirs to carry — but no more than that, and not in the climax.

Propulsive prose — sentences that move. Dialogue is lively and character-specific. Holroyd is dry, reluctant, and observant; he speaks in declaratives and avoids questions whose answers would commit him. Isobel is direct in the way her father was direct — plain language, no theatricality, but the plainness is itself a kind of force. Mariano speaks rarely and seems to know more than he is saying; when he does speak, the others listen.

Action scenes are rendered concretely — the body's weight on a wet rope, the cold inside the lungs, the specific sound a bridge plank makes before it goes. Awe is allowed: when the city is finally revealed, the prose may breathe. Wonder and danger sit on the same shelf in this book; one does not replace the other.

## Constraints

- Real-world physics. No supernatural mechanism in the city. What is there is strange because it is old, and because the altitude and silence make everything strange. It does not do anything.
- No "ancient evil awakens" beat. The danger is mountains, weather, rival humans, and choices.
- Klaus Riedel is competent and dangerous, not a cartoon. He has read the same accounts as Holroyd and has reached at least one conclusion Holroyd was wrong about. He is wrong about other things in ways that matter.
- Mariano Quispe is not a sidekick. He is a competent person in his own right, with his own reasons for being there, and at least one of those reasons should land in the climax.
- At least three named set pieces across the eight chapters: a rope-bridge crossing that goes wrong, a weather event that kills someone (or nearly does), and a direct confrontation with Riedel's party at altitude.
- The ending is not the discovery of the city. The ending is what each of the three survivors chooses to take from it and what they choose to leave behind.
- Period detail should be specific (oil-and-canvas tents, hobnailed boots, primus stoves, Mauser rifles, a Leica camera, mules carrying loads in standard 80-pound ataditos) but the prose should not stop to admire it.
