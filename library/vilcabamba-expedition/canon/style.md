# Style Guide

## Point of View and Tense

**Third-person limited, past tense.** The default binding is James Holroyd. The narration sees what he sees, notices what he notices, and is shaped by his habits of mind — observational, assessing, reluctant to name feeling directly. It does not have access to other characters' inner lives; it reads them from behavior and speech.

**Two chapters shift POV:**  
- One chapter in Isobel Carrow's POV — the chapter where her relationship to her father's death and to Rititambo becomes the story's primary lens.  
- One chapter in Mariano Quispe's POV — the chapter where his reason for being on this expedition becomes visible and the terrain is primary.  

Both shifts are sustained through the full chapter. There is no alternating of POV within chapters. **The climax remains in Holroyd's POV.** No other POV is used.

---

## Sentence Rhythm

**Propulsive.** Sentences move. The default is medium-short: subject, verb, object, with few trailing clauses. When clauses stack, they earn their length by arriving somewhere that the shorter sentence could not.

The prose does not stop to admire things. It notices them and keeps moving. A rope bridge appears; it has specific characteristics; the character crosses or does not cross. Description is quick and concrete. The sentence that contains "the bridge moved" does not then spend three sentences describing how the bridge looked in the morning light.

**Sentence length varies with intensity:** In action scenes, sentences shorten. Paragraphs shorten. Cuts are fast. In the moment of the city's revelation, sentences are allowed to lengthen — but measured lengthening, not drift.

What to avoid:
- Strings of adjectives standing in for observation
- Sentences that explain what the previous sentence just showed
- Sentences that begin with "There was" or "It was"
- Participial phrase openings used more than once per scene

---

## Dialogue

**Lively, embedded, and character-specific.** Each major character has an identifiable speech pattern that a drafting agent should be able to reproduce without reference to the speaker tag.

**Holroyd:** Declarative. Terse. Avoids questions that commit him. Gives assessments, not opinions. Almost never uses the word "feel." His humor is dry and appears at moments of physical extremity, which startles people who don't know him. He does not elaborate unless pushed. He does not use exclamation marks.

> *"The bridge will hold one man. Not two."*  
> *"Riedel has the north approach. I had it wrong."*  
> *"We can argue about this in Cusco."*

**Isobel:** Direct, plain, untheatrical. Names problems without softening them. Her plainness is active, not flat. She will say the difficult thing because she has watched imprecision cause damage. Her translations are accurate as to meaning and occasionally pointed in register.

> *"He says the pass is closed. What he means is he won't take us."*  
> *"Your boot is wrong for this. You'll know it by tomorrow."*

**Mariano:** Rare and precise. Does not decorate. When he speaks in Quechua and Isobel renders it, the rendering is sometimes slightly off, or right in ways that sting. He states; he does not argue. He waits.

> *"Three days."* [pause] *"If the weather holds."* [longer pause] *"It won't."*

**Riedel:** Formal English with German grammatical exactness. Complete sentences. Uses courtesy as control. Acknowledges credit precisely and without warmth.

> *"Your surveying of this range is better than ours, Holroyd. I want you to know I acknowledge that."*  
> *"We will reach the bowl. The question is whether we reach it together or separately, and I think you understand which of those is safer for both parties."*

**Dialogue is embedded in action and bodies.** Characters do things while they talk. A character answers a question while relashing a pack, or delivers a piece of information while watching the ridge, not while standing face-to-face in a neutral space. Dialogue that floats in white space with no physical context should be revised.

---

## Narrative Distance

**Close but not claustrophobic.** The narrator has access to Holroyd's perceptions and assessments but does not luxuriate in interiority. What he feels is mostly legible from what he does and says. Introspective passages are rare and short; they are triggered by specific stimuli, not by general reflection.

In **Isobel's chapter:** slightly more interior access. She is actively working something through; the narration is allowed to follow the working. But it stays in observation and inference — it does not narrate feeling in abstract terms.

In **Mariano's chapter:** less interior than any other chapter. More attention to terrain, weather, and other people's behavior. What Mariano knows is held in the body and in the landscape. His chapter is the most externally observant, and in that externality it carries what it needs to carry.

---

## Action Scenes: Concrete Physical Rendering

Action is rendered through the body's specific experience. No vague gestures. Not *"he slipped"* but *"his right boot lost the hobnail purchase on the wet planking and his knee hit the edge of the bridge rail."* The reader should experience:

- The weight of a body on a rope at altitude
- The cold inside the lungs at seventeen thousand feet — not pain exactly, more like an awareness that the air is thin and what is coming in is not quite enough
- The specific sound a bridge plank makes before it separates
- The burn in the thighs on the fourth hour of ascent
- The sound of wind on canvas at three in the morning versus at noon — they are different sounds

**Pace mechanics:** Sentence length drops. Action verbs in active voice. No passive constructions during physical crisis. Cut quickly between moments; do not fill the gaps between consequential actions with transition sentences.

---

## The Moment of the City

When Rititambo is revealed, **the prose may breathe.** This is the one place where the narrative pace opens. Sentences may lengthen; observation may pause and stay. The awe is allowed.

But: wonder and danger share a shelf. The city is not safe. The exposure is real — they are above seventeen thousand feet, Riedel is somewhere on the mountain, the weather is unpredictable. The prose does not enter a separate mode when they reach the city; it opens, while the underlying tension remains. Awe does not suspend consequence.

---

## Period Detail

Present and specific but not paused over. The Leica camera is there; it is not described in a paragraph. The primus stove is pumped; the reader hears it; the prose does not explain how a primus stove works. The detail confirms period and physical reality without performing research. When period-specific objects appear, they appear as objects a character uses, not as objects the narrator wants credit for knowing about.

---

## What to Avoid

- **Anachronistic diction:** No contemporary psychological vocabulary. Not *trauma* as a noun, not *processing*, not *closure*, not *boundaries*. Characters do not have these words available to them.
- **Landscape as active metaphor the character is aware of.** The mountains are not commenting on the story. They are mountains.
- **Hindsight condescension toward the Germans.** Riedel is not a fool because we know how 1938 ends. He is a competent man operating in his present tense.
- **The word "suddenly."**
- **Explanatory dialogue.** Characters do not explain the situation to each other for the reader's benefit.
- **Any suggestion of supernatural agency in the city.** The city does nothing. The strangeness is material: age, altitude, preservation, proportion, silence.
- **Exclamation points in the narration.** In dialogue only, and sparingly.
