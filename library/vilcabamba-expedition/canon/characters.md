# Characters

---

## James Holroyd — POV Principal

**Role:** Expedition leader; British army surveyor, retired under a cloud.  
**Age:** 44 in 1938. Born 1894.

**Background:** Joined the Survey of India in 1918 following the war, in which he served as a Royal Engineers officer mapping the Mesopotamian front. Spent thirteen years producing meticulous topographic work — the Northwest Frontier, Assam, two seasons in the eastern Himalayas. Blacklisted in 1931 following an incident on the Assam-Burma frontier that he will not discuss. The record shows a disputed field report and the death of a surveying colleague. What the record does not show, or shows in a form Holroyd contests, is the substance of whatever decision was made. Since 1931 he has worked as a freelance surveyor in South America — Bolivia first, then Ecuador, then Peru. He has surveyed the eastern Cordillera Vilcabamba's approaches because the Peruvian government paid him to in 1934 and no one else bothered. He is the only non-Peruvian who has walked the eastern approaches of SM-7. He is not the Royal Geographical Society's first choice for this expedition, or their second.

**Inner drives:** Holroyd is not a man who wants glory. He arranged his pride into a smaller space some years ago, and he has made that space survivable. What he wants — what he will not name to himself — is to be on the right side of a field judgment. To have observed correctly, decided correctly, and to have it recorded as such. The India incident is the one time the record does not show this, and the weight of that — whether he was right and overruled, wrong and too proud to admit it, or something worse than either — is what he carries into the Vilcabamba. He took this assignment because Dr. Finch needed someone who had surveyed the eastern range, because the money covered three months of debts in Lima, and because Rititambo is the most plausible thing he has heard in seven years. He is not sentimental about it. He is here.

**Voice:** Dry and declarative. He avoids questions whose answers would commit him to something he's not prepared to defend. He speaks in assessments rather than opinions: *"The bridge will hold one man at a time, not two."* *"Riedel has better rope than us."* *"Friar Arce was a competent observer."* He does not say how he feels about things; he says what he sees. When his assessments are wrong — and occasionally they are — he acknowledges it in the same register: *"I was wrong about the north approach."* His dry humor surfaces in extremity, which tends to alarm people who are not used to him.

**Relationships:**  
- *With Isobel:* Professional respect neither will call friendship. She is more necessary to this expedition than he is, and he knows it without finding it troubling. He does not condescend to her; she does not defer to him except on surveying questions. They have developed, over the approach, a working economy of language.  
- *With Mariano:* Holroyd defers on terrain and logistics without comment or ceremony. He does not perform respect for Mariano; he extends it through behavior. Mariano notices this and says nothing about it, which Holroyd reads accurately.  
- *With Riedel:* A cold mutual recognition between two men who have read the same documents and reached different places. Holroyd does not exactly dislike Riedel. He dislikes what Riedel is working for, and he dislikes that Riedel has gotten one thing right that he missed.

## Isobel Carrow — Secondary POV (one chapter)

**Role:** Interpreter, cultural intermediary, de facto second-in-command.  
**Age:** 27 in 1938. Born 1911.

**Background:** Born in the Urubamba Valley, the only child of the Reverend Thomas Carrow, a Methodist missionary who spent thirty-one years in the Peruvian highlands. Her mother died of typhoid when Isobel was nine. She grew up between her father's posting at Pisac, a Catholic mission school in Cusco where she was the only non-Peruvian student, and two years at a women's college in Bristol (1929–1931) that her father could barely afford and that she found interesting in ways he had not anticipated. She returned to Peru at twenty-one and has not left since. She speaks Quechua fluently, Aymara at working competence, and broken Yagua — enough to establish trust in the upper Loreto region, not enough to be relied upon. She is fluent in the way mountain people withhold information: she recognizes the shape of what is not being said.

Her father, Reverend Thomas Carrow, died of a fever in Pisac six weeks before the expedition departed. He had spent the last years of his life becoming increasingly convinced that the three missionary accounts of Rititambo were genuine and that finding it would be — in his phrase — "a confirmation of what has always been known in this valley." Isobel spent years half-believing that this obsession was a symptom of displacement: a man who had given his life to a community that was kind to him and would always remain foreign to him, finding meaning in a mystery rather than facing that fact. She is not sure anymore. That uncertainty is part of why she is here.

**Inner drives:** She is there because she was hired for skills she genuinely has — the RGS had no one else. She is also there because she wants to know whether her father was right, and she wants to settle that question before it calcifies into something she can't get at. This is not grief exactly — grief is six weeks old and still too shapeless to navigate — but it is adjacent to grief: the desire to resolve an argument with someone who can no longer argue back. She has her own opinion about what Rititambo will be, and she is prepared to be wrong about it.

**Voice:** Direct and plain. She says what she means without decoration. She does not perform certainty she does not have; she does not soften news she is carrying; she does not allow courtesy to muddy a practical communication. *"That man will not tell us anything useful."* *"We're going the wrong way."* *"Mariano says three days; I think two if the weather holds."* Her plainness is active — the cultivated speech of someone who has watched people misunderstand each other across language barriers for her entire life and concluded that clarity is the only genuine kindness. Her translations are not quite neutral. She renders meaning rather than words, and occasionally the gap between the two becomes visible.

**Relationships:**  
- *With Holroyd:* She respects his precision and finds his emotional obliqueness legible without being interesting. He is good at his job. She is good at hers. They have reached an accommodation.  
- *With Mariano:* A working relationship predating this expedition — she has hired him twice before for interpretation fieldwork. There is genuine respect and occasional argument conducted at speed, in Quechua, that Holroyd cannot follow. He registers that they are arguing; she doesn't always translate the resolution.  
- *With her father's death:* She carries it the way she carries her pack — she doesn't talk about it, but it shapes the load distribution. The chapter in her POV is the chapter where this surfaces.

## Mariano Quispe — Secondary POV (one chapter)

**Role:** Arriero, mountain guide; the man who knows this terrain, and why he is really here.  
**Age:** 38 in 1938. Born 1900.

**Background:** Born in a village at the head of the Yanatile valley, the son of a muleteer. Has worked the eastern Cordillera for twenty years. Knows the trails as far as the snowline as well as anyone; has been above the snowline twice — once escorting a Peruvian geological survey in 1929, and once for reasons he has not shared with anyone on this expedition. He knows which bridges were rebuilt last season, which pastures hold water in dry-season August, where a mule can be left for three days and still be there when you return. He has four mules on this expedition; they trust him in the specific way working animals trust someone who understands their limits.

**Inner drives:** Mariano's oral tradition — the tradition of his valley community, preserved outside of writing — identifies Rititambo as the withdrawal place of the pre-Inca ancestors who refused to submit when the Inca expansion reached the eastern Cordillera. The city is not mythologized in his tradition as a sacred ruin or a lost paradise; it is understood as a *fact* — a place that exists, that was sealed by the people who entered it, and that has been known about since. What it has not had, in his tradition, is a witness of the right kind: someone who goes there, sees it, and makes an accounting — not for European archaeology or Inca patrimony, but for the people of the valley who have held the knowledge. He is that witness. He did not explain this when Isobel hired him, because it is not relevant to whether he can do the job, which he can. He does not intend to remove anything from the city, damage anything, or prevent Holroyd and Isobel from doing what they came to do. He intends to stand there and bear witness. This act, private and formally slight, lands with weight in the climax.

**Voice:** Spare. He speaks when he has something to say. In Spanish with Holroyd — correct, functional, unadorned — and in Quechua with Isobel, where he is more precise and occasionally more pointed. He does not argue; he states and waits. If ignored, he waits longer, and is usually found to have been right. When he is wrong he says so without preamble. When he is right and was not listened to, he does not note this; he deals with the situation. He has a habit of looking at the sky or the terrain while others are still talking. It reads as inattention. It is not.

**Relationships:**  
- *With Holroyd:* Functional and steady. Holroyd knows his business and does not waste Mariano's time with performance. That is enough.  
- *With Isobel:* The closer working relationship; she is the one who found and hired him, and her Quechua is good enough that he can say what he means. He volunteers information she did not know to ask for; she understands that this is a form of trust.  
- *With the other mule drivers (unnamed porters):* He hired two additional arrieros from the Yanatile valley for the lower approach. They turn back at the snowline, as agreed. Their names are Cleto and Rufino. Only Mariano goes above.

## Klaus Riedel — Antagonist

**Role:** Leader of the German Ahnenerbe expedition; rival for Rititambo.  
**Age:** 36 in 1938. Born 1902.

**Background:** Munich-born and educated at the University of Munich in geography and physical geology. A committed mountaineer since his twenties — summited six major Alpine routes, made two Himalayan attempts (the 1934 Karakoram expedition; survived but did not summit). He joined the Ahnenerbe in 1936 not from fervent ideology but from opportunism: they had equipment budgets and institutional access that no academic geography department could match, and he had an obsession with pre-Columbian altitude sites that needed funding. He has read the three Spanish missionary accounts of Rititambo in the original and in his own German translation. He has reached one conclusion that Holroyd missed: the friar Arce's account specifies a tree-line feature that places his northern approach route correctly, meaning Riedel has the right bearing and is, geographically, closer.

He is wrong about the other major question. He has come to believe — partly from the Ahnenerbe framing, partly from his own reading of the accounts' architectural descriptions, partly from wishful thinking that has crept up on him in the field — that Rititambo will show evidence of a non-Andean cultural origin. This belief distorts how he will interpret what he finds. He is not cruel. He is not a cartoon. He is a competent man who has allowed a borrowed conclusion to dress itself as discovery, and who is, by the time he and Holroyd meet at altitude, too far in to easily revise.

**Voice:** Formal English with German exactness. Complete sentences; precise vocabulary; the courtesy of someone who uses politeness as a form of control. He gives credit when it is due without warmth: *"Your surveying of this eastern range is better than ours, Holroyd. I want you to know I acknowledge that."* He does not beg or threaten. He does not explain himself unless he decides it serves a purpose. Under pressure, at altitude, he becomes not cruder but more rigid — the sentences stay formal, the demands become absolute.

**Relationships:**  
- *With Holroyd:* A rivalry that has taken personal shape despite their having met only once before this expedition. He has read Holroyd's published Survey work and considers it excellent. He does not quite respect Holroyd's employment situation — but he respects the work.  
- *With his own team:* Two German mountaineers (Fischer and Kern; secondary characters) and a Peruvian guide-interpreter named Agustín. He is not unkind to them. He is also, at altitude and under pressure, inflexible about the objective in a way that has put them in difficult positions.

## Minor Named Characters

**Reverend Thomas Carrow** (deceased) — Isobel's father. Methodist missionary, based in Pisac for thirty-one years. Died of fever six weeks before the expedition departed, in the house he had lived in for twenty years and never managed to make warm. His belief in the Rititambo accounts is part of Isobel's inheritance — the part she has not yet sorted.

**Dr. Aubrey Finch** — Secretary of the Royal Geographical Society, London. The man who hired Holroyd. Remains in London. He worked down a list of four names; Holroyd was fourth. He is not unsympathetic, but he made the conditions of the engagement clear: off-the-books, no public RGS endorsement unless the expedition produces something defensible. He will be waiting for a cable.

**Cleto and Rufino** — The two additional arrieros Mariano hired from the Yanatile valley. They work the lower approach, turn back at the snowline as agreed, and take the pack mules with them. Neither is in the story above fourteen thousand feet.

**Agustín** — The Peruvian guide-interpreter employed by Riedel's party. His role in Riedel's expedition parallels Isobel's in Holroyd's — and the parallel is not lost on Isobel.
