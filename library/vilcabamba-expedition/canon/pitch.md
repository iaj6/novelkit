# Pitch

In 1938, James Holroyd — a cashiered British army surveyor with a blacklisted name and no good reason to be careful with his life — is hired off the bottom of a short list by the Royal Geographical Society. The job: lead a small, deniable expedition into the eastern Cordillera Vilcabamba in Peru, find a city sealed under snow on the lee face of an unnamed peak, and return with a defensible account of what is there. The city appears in three Spanish missionary documents from the 1570s and nowhere after. Nobody official believes in it. An Ahnenerbe team led by a Munich-trained mountaineer named Klaus Riedel has read the same documents and believes.

Holroyd's party is three: himself; a missionary's daughter named Isobel Carrow, twenty-seven, who speaks Quechua and knows the mountain people's silences; and an arriero named Mariano Quispe, who knows the trails above the snowline and has his own reason for going — one he has told no one. The journey destroys equipment, kills mules, collapses a bridge, and closes Riedel's team to within a day's march at altitude. The city, when they reach it, is exactly as the missionaries described and nothing any of them were prepared for. The story ends not with discovery but with what each of the three survivors chooses to carry back down — and what they choose to leave.
