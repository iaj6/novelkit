# Chapter 3 — Puna

**One-line summary:** On the high puna a herder woman mentions Isobel's father; that night Isobel opens his annotated copy of Arce's account for the first time since he died and discovers his belief in Rititambo was not displacement but a decades-long act of testimony — and her grief, which has been shapeless, begins to find a form.

**POV:** Isobel Carrow — third-person limited, past tense. This is Isobel's chapter. The narration has slightly more interior access than in the Holroyd chapters, following her as she works something through; it stays in observation and inference and does not name feeling in abstract terms.

**Setting / Time:** The upper selva alta transitioning to the open puna; a herder encampment at approximately 13,000 feet. Late August, five days after the gorge crossing.

---

## Goal (Isobel's)

Move the party through the puna herder territory efficiently — read the social landscape correctly, get accurate information about the German party and the route above — and manage her own relationship to why she is here, which has been pressing on her since the village man asked her to tell her father what she finds.

## Obstacle

The herder woman gives information in the Andean oblique — accurate and incomplete, nothing withheld dishonestly, everything dependent on the quality of the question. The German party has been through and asked in the wrong way. Isobel will have to ask correctly, and then translate for Holroyd, and the gap between what the woman says and what Holroyd receives will be visible. The harder obstacle is the oilskin case in Isobel's pack — her father's annotated copy of Arce's account, which she has been carrying since Pisac and has not opened.

---

## Action Beats

**The treeline gives way:**
- The last ferns, the last mossy trunks, the last smell of wet earth. Then the open puna: tussock ichu grass to the horizon, the sky arriving like a door swung wide. The light is different up here — harder, the shadows shorter, ultraviolet beginning to work on the backs of hands. Isobel watches Holroyd's body shift slightly as the terrain opens. He is a man who functions better with a visible horizon. She files this, as she files everything about the people she works with.
- The wind is constant. The ichu bends and rights itself in long, slow waves. There are clouds building to the east over the Amazon basin, the daily convection cycle, but the sky to the west is the high Andean blue — thin and specific and reliable.

**The herder encampment:**
- Two low stone houses, walls thick enough to hide inside the wind shadow. An alpaca pen, stone-walled. Three children playing in the lee of the second house. One woman of perhaps sixty — grey hair, a wool hat pulled low, hands that have been at altitude all her life. The men are with the animals on the upper pastures. The woman offers them muña mint tea. It is very thin at this altitude but it is offered correctly and Isobel accepts correctly, which the woman registers.
- The woman asks, with the question's particular Quechua flatness that means she already knows the answer: where are they going? Isobel says: to the upper snowfields of SM-7. (She uses Holroyd's survey designation and then, on impulse, adds the old description from Arce: the lee face, the sealed place.) The woman is quiet for a moment. Then: the Germans came through nine days ago, she says. Not twelve. Nine. Four of them, heavily equipped, a Peruvian man among them. They asked about the northern pastures.
- Isobel: "What did you tell them?" The woman: "The pastures. Where the water stays in dry season. The snow on the northern face." She pauses. "They asked about the old places above. I told them what I tell those people." Isobel: "What is that?" The woman, with no particular edge: "Nothing worth knowing."
- Isobel translates for Holroyd, standing slightly back: "She confirms nine days. They asked about the old places. She says she didn't tell them anything useful." She does not translate the last phrase exactly — she renders it as: "She says she didn't give them topographical detail." This is accurate enough and leaves out what Holroyd doesn't need and would not know how to receive.

**The woman says something Isobel doesn't translate immediately:**
- The woman looks at Isobel with the particular attention of someone placing a face they've been expecting. She says, in Quechua: "Your father knew the right questions." Isobel goes very still. "He came through here once. Long ago. Asking about the sealed places — but not for himself." She pauses. "He asked in the way people ask when they are carrying a question for someone else."
- Isobel: "You knew him." The woman: "I knew of him. Word comes down the valley. He stayed for three days at the Tacana family's place, asking and writing. He told the right people what he found." She looks at Mariano, briefly, then back at Isobel. "That is a long work. Patient work."
- A silence. Then Isobel: "Did he reach the sealed place?" The woman: "No. But he knew where it was. The knowing was enough for what he needed to do."
- Mariano has been sitting at the edge of the group throughout this exchange. Isobel watches him from the corner of her eye. He has not moved, has not indicated he is following the Quechua, but he is following the Quechua. When the woman speaks of the right people who were told, Mariano's eyes settle briefly on the middle distance — not checked out, not absent. Present in a different way.
- Isobel does not translate this section for Holroyd.

**The camp — Isobel opens the oilskin case:**
- Holroyd has already entered the tent to review the route with Mariano; they are talking in their functional Spanish, mule counts and elevation estimates. The fire outside is low but warm.
- Isobel sits outside the tent with the oilskin case in her hands. She has been carrying it since the day she cleared her father's room in Pisac — six weeks ago, while his body was still cooling two rooms away and the women of the congregation were singing in the courtyard. She has not opened it because it seemed wrong somehow to open it there, and then it seemed too soon, and then the expedition preparation had taken over and the wrongness had become a habit.
- She opens it now. Inside: the friar Arce's 1573 account in a printed copy with a hand-sewn binding — her father's own. The margins are dense with his handwriting, in layers of ink that mark decades of return visits to the same text. Some in English (early, the hand of a young man in his thirties). Some in Spanish (the middle years — he started thinking in Spanish sometime in the 1910s, she knows this). Some in Quechua (the last decade, the hand slightly thicker, the pen held differently after the arthritis started).
- She reads the marginalia in the lamplight. Not Arce's account — she has read that; she knows it — but what her father wrote beside it.
- What she finds: he was cross-referencing Arce's descriptions against testimony he gathered over thirty years from the communities of the Urubamba valley, the Yanatile valley, the Apurímac headwaters. Dates and names in the margins she does not recognize. But the structure is unmistakable: he was building a concordance between what Arce saw and what the valley communities held in oral tradition. He was not trying to confirm his own faith in the accounts' authenticity. He was trying to make a record, for the communities, that connected what the missionaries had written to what they already knew. A kind of restitution — a document that the communities could use as corroboration in their own terms.
- In the last pages of the margin notes, written in the last year or two — she knows the hand, the stiffness — he lists twelve names of people who told him specific things about Rititambo. Beside each name, a location and a date. Beside each entry, the matching passage in Arce's account.
- The last entry: a name she doesn't know, followed by a single Quechua phrase she translates for herself: "the witness who goes and returns and makes it real."
- Isobel has been wrong about what her father was doing. She thought he was retreating from the fact of his life — that he was a man who had given thirty-one years to a community that was genuinely fond of him and would always remain, in some essential way, foreign to him, and that the Rititambo obsession was a way of finding meaning in mystery rather than facing that fact. This reading felt correct to her. It was wrong. He was not retreating; he was participating, in the only way he knew how, in something that was not his and was not about him.
- She closes the book. The grief has been shapeless since he died — she has been carrying it in the way she carries her pack, which means not talking about it but letting it shape the load distribution. Now it begins to have a shape. The shape is not simple. He was doing something she would respect. He did it for thirty years without telling her, because it was not hers to carry — it was for the people of the valley. She can be angry about that (she was the one who thought it was displacement) and it doesn't change what he was doing. She sits with this for a while, in the wind.

**Isobel and Mariano, briefly:**
- Mariano comes out of the tent to check the mule tethers before sleep. He passes close to Isobel. He does not look at the book she is holding — or does not obviously look. She says, in Quechua: "He listed twelve names. The last one says 'the witness who goes and returns.'" Mariano secures a tether. He says, in Quechua: "Your father was careful." He goes back inside. This is the most Mariano will say about it, and it is enough — Isobel understands that "careful" means something specific in this context: he was careful with the knowledge, which means he knew whose knowledge it was.

---

## Outcome / Turn

The gap to Riedel is nine days, not twelve — they are gaining less than they thought, but they are gaining. The herder woman's route information is solid. And Isobel's relationship to the expedition has shifted: she is no longer arriving to confirm or refute her father's belief, which was never really what he was doing. She is arriving to see what he spent thirty years trying to prepare a witness for. This is harder and more specific than her previous motive, and she is not entirely comfortable with it.

---

## New Question or Pressure Raised

The herder woman said Isobel's father told "the right people" what he found, and she looked at Mariano when she said it. Does Isobel understand what that means? She understands enough. Does she tell Holroyd? She does not — not because she is keeping a secret from him but because it is not his question to carry. The question of what Mariano is really doing here is beginning to take shape, and Isobel is letting it take shape rather than extracting it.
