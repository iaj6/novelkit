# Chapter 4 — Snowline

**One-line summary:** Cleto and Rufino turn back with the mules; Mariano leads the three above the snowline alone; he finds Riedel's fire ring at 16,500 feet, still holding warmth — the gap has collapsed from nine days to thirty-six hours, and the rock formation that marks the bowl entrance is visible above.

**POV:** Mariano Quispe — third-person limited, past tense. This is Mariano's chapter. The narration is the most externally observant in the book: less interior than any other chapter, more attention to terrain, weather, and other people's behavior. What Mariano knows is held in the body and in the landscape.

**Setting / Time:** The upper puna transitioning to permanent snowfield; the camp at 15,000 feet; the hard-snow ascent to 16,500 feet. First week of September 1938. The dry season above the snowline: wind, frost, no moisture, no cloud until afternoon.

---

## Goal (Mariano's)

Get the three people above the snowline safely — assess the terrain, maintain a pace that keeps altitude sickness manageable, and do not reveal the weight of what he is carrying toward. Not yet.

## Obstacle

The snowline itself is treacherous in dry-season conditions: hard, wind-scoured snow over rock, poor purchase for hobnailed boots on the steepest sections. The real difficulty is Mariano's own knowledge of where he is: this is the approach he failed once before, in 1933, alone, turned back by an ice face he couldn't cross without crampons. The unfinished obligation from that failure is present in every step. He is also managing the other two people — Holroyd's altitude headache (the man won't mention it); Isobel's quiet recalibration since the puna camp. Both are functional. Both are costing themselves something.

---

## Action Beats

**The handoff:**
- Camp at 15,000 feet, before dawn. The mules are restless; they can smell the altitude change even in the dark. Mariano checks each of them: the hip bones, the nostrils, the way each animal distributes its weight. They are at the edge of what altitude allows. He knew this when he planned the loads; the 80-lb ataditos were already lightened to 65 above 13,000. He says to Cleto: "If the Pampa Chica pass is closed when we come down, take the Yanatile south branch. Three days to the river." Cleto nods. He does not look at the mountain.
- Rufino's hands are rewrapped by Isobel — she has changed the dressing twice since the gorge. He shakes Mariano's hand with both of his, which is not Rufino's usual way. Mariano holds the grip for a moment, then releases. Rufino and Cleto begin leading the four mules back down toward the lower puna. They do not look back.
- Three people now. The simplification of this is real: no mule sound, no weight negotiation with pack animals, just the terrain and the wind and three sets of hobnailed boots.

**Above the snowline — the terrain as Mariano reads it:**
- The snow begins at 15,500 feet: wind-packed to the hardness of old wood in the flat sections, crystalline and deceptive on the angled faces. Mariano uses his ice axe as a probe before committing weight — not dramatically, as a matter of course, the way a man uses a stick on a dark trail. He cuts steps where the angle exceeds twenty degrees. The steps are small and regular; Holroyd follows them without comment, which Mariano reads as competence.
- The sky: cloudless overhead, which in dry-season terms is not reliable information. There is a high cirrus sheet advancing from the west — thin, barely visible, a smear across the blue rather than a cloud. It has been there since yesterday morning and it has been advancing. A weather system is coming. The question is how fast and how hard. Mariano looks at it and looks away. Not his to fix.
- Holroyd's headache: the man walks with his eyes slightly narrowed and occasionally stops to adjust his pack in a way that involves tipping his head down. Mariano has seen this enough times to read it correctly: altitude headache, at least day two, not serious yet but present. He says nothing. Isobel has already noticed and has been offering coca leaf at intervals, which Holroyd accepts.
- Isobel herself: she is quieter today than in the puna camps. She is chewing coca and walking well and her silence is not the silence of difficulty — it is the silence of a person who is thinking. She is working through whatever she found in her father's book. Mariano does not inquire.

**The 1933 attempt — held internally:**
- At 16,000 feet, on a ledge where the wind comes around a rock spur and hits hard from the northwest, Mariano pauses to let the others rest and breathes and does not look the way a man looks at a place he failed. The 1933 attempt was in March — the wrong season, the slopes wet and then frozen in layers, a crevassed ice face at 17,200 feet that he could not cross safely alone without crampons and could not solo in any case. He had turned back from fifty yards below the bowl entrance. He had gone back down without saying anything to anyone — not lying, simply not telling, because what would be the use. He had told the woman who keeps the knowledge in his valley that he had tried and had not reached it. She had said: "Then try again when the conditions allow." The conditions had not allowed for five years.
- He has crampons now. They are in his pack. He purchased them secondhand from a Peruvian climbing club in Cusco with his own money, three months before Isobel contracted him. He does not think of this as preparation. He thinks of it as correction.

**The witness-obligation, carried internally:**
- In his tradition, a witness is not a reporter or a documentarian. A witness stands in the fact and comes back changed, and the change is the testimony. The change is not measurable; it does not produce a document; it produces a person who has been somewhere and looked, and who can then say what was there to the people who need to know it. This is not mystical. It is a form of accountability — the specific form that exists before writing, and that writing has never fully replaced. Mariano is not certain his witnessing will be adequate. He does not know what adequate looks like from this side. He knows he has been carrying the obligation for five years and that the debt runs before him to a time before his father's father.
- He does not pray. He does not perform preparation. He walks.

**The fire ring:**
- At 16,500 feet, on a wind-scoured shelf below a rock step — a natural platform in the lee of a granite outcrop — Mariano stops. He is looking at the snow. Holroyd is twelve steps behind him. Mariano calls back: "Keep going — there is a route around the step to the left. Easier." He waits until both Holroyd and Isobel have moved off, then crouches and examines the snow.
- The impression: a boot print, partial — the toe and front third of a sole, pressed into snow that is harder than the snow below. The impression is still sharp-edged. Wind would have softened it within twenty-four hours at this altitude; at this temperature it would take six to eight hours to begin degrading. This print was made this morning, or last night.
- He follows the shelf twenty feet further. The hollow in the lee of the rock: three flat stones arranged in the German style of a camp fire ring, slightly different from what a Peruvian would build. Ash in the center. He holds his bare hand two inches above the ash. Warmth — faint but real, the residual of a fire damped down with snow rather than exhausted. Burned sometime in the last four hours.
- He looks around the hollow: a boot-compressed area where two people slept, with the faint depressions of tent stakes. A German Mauser cartridge case, half-buried in snow — someone reloaded here, or checked the rifle in the cold. Mariano picks it up, turns it over, pockets it.
- He walks around the rock step to where Holroyd and Isobel are waiting. He says, in Spanish: "Riedel was here this morning." He holds up the cartridge case. "Two men."
- Holroyd takes the cartridge case. He reads the headstamp. He says: "Mauser. One day ahead?" Mariano: "If they slept long." He looks at the sky — the cirrus is thickening, the light going faintly diffuse. "We should not sleep long."
- Isobel looks at Holroyd. Holroyd looks at the ridge above them. Neither speaks.

**The bowl entrance sighted:**
- Mariano, before they begin moving again, looks up along the northeast ridge of SM-7. At 18,200 feet — he estimates by the bearing against the summit ice — there is a dark horizontal line in the cliff face. A gap. A shadow. Arce's account in his oral tradition describes this: the fault in the rock through which the bowl is entered, a passage wide enough for one person with a pack removed. He has known it from description since boyhood. He is looking at it.
- He does not point it out to the others. Not because he is withholding — because they will see it from high camp, and the information that matters now is not where the entrance is but how many hours ahead of them Riedel is.
- He says: "High camp. Before dark." He begins climbing.

---

## Outcome / Turn

The gap has collapsed. Nine days to thirty-six hours. The German party slept here this morning, which means they are one good push — six to eight hours — from the bowl. Holroyd's party is approximately eight hours from high camp and two hours from the bowl once they depart high camp. If the weather holds, they will reach the bowl entrance before Riedel — but only if they move without delay. The cirrus from the west says the weather may not hold.

---

## New Question or Pressure Raised

The German party is moving faster than it should be this late in their field time — faster than acclimatized men at altitude typically move. Are they cutting corners, overextending? Or are they in better condition than Holroyd's estimates? And: Mariano can see the bowl entrance from here. Does Riedel know exactly where the entrance is, or is he still searching? Riedel has the right bearing (friar Arce's tree-line feature from the north), but the exact entrance point is not obvious from below.

## Continuity Notes (New Facts Introduced)

- **Cleto and Rufino turn back at 15,000 feet** with the four mules. Cleto takes alternate route instructions from Mariano in case the Pampa Chica pass is closed on the return.
- **Mariano attempted to reach SM-7 alone in March 1933** and was turned back at approximately 17,200 feet by an uncramponed ice face.
- **Mariano purchased crampons in Cusco before the expedition** using his own money.
- **Riedel's camp fire ring is found at 16,500 feet**, still warm — the camp was broken within the last four hours.
- The gap between the parties is now **approximately 36 hours or less**, not nine days.
- **Mariano can identify the bowl entrance** from below — a dark horizontal gap in the cliff face at approximately 18,200 feet, matching the description in his oral tradition and in Arce's account.
- A **German Mauser cartridge case** is recovered from the fire ring site and pocketed by Mariano.
- A high cirrus weather system is advancing from the west — a significant storm is possible within 24–48 hours.
