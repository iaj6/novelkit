# Chapter 5 — The Storm

**One-line summary:** An overnight storm at 17,000 feet batters both camps; at dawn Holroyd's binoculars show Fischer down and Agustín working over him; Riedel sends Fischer and Agustín back and continues upward with only Kern; the storm lifts to reveal a clear view of the bowl entrance two hours above.

**POV:** James Holroyd — third-person limited, past tense.

**Setting / Time:** High camp at approximately 17,000 feet on SM-7's north-northeast approach; the open snowfield; the German camp visible across a ridgeline 400 meters to the south-southwest. Very early September 1938. Night into dawn.

---

## Goal

Survive the weather event with the tent, the equipment, and the people intact; be ready to move at first light when the storm window opens.

## Obstacle

The storm itself — wind above 60 miles per hour at altitude, temperature dropping to minus 22°C by midnight, the improvised tent poles (hardwood branches lashed with alpine rope) not designed for this load. Holroyd's altitude headache has graduated to a dull insistence behind the eyes that aspirin manages but does not resolve. He is making decisions in a state that slightly compromises him, and he is slightly compromised enough that he does not fully know this.

---

## Action Beats

**Making high camp:**
- They reach 17,000 feet in late afternoon with the light going orange on the western ridges and the cirrus overhead now a solid grey-white sheet. The temperature is already minus 12°C and falling. The campsite: a flat snow shelf in the lee of a granite spur, just wide enough for the tent and a two-foot working perimeter. Holroyd pounds the first tent stake and it holds in the hard-packed snow. The second stake goes into rock six inches down and takes three hammer strikes to set.
- Setting up the tent without poles: three people required. Isobel holds the canvas peak while Mariano rigs the alpine rope ridge from the uphill stake to the rock spur. Holroyd feeds the branch braces into their rope loops. His fingers are clumsy on the toggle buckle of the main line — too cold, too fast. He removes his glove for twenty seconds to manage the buckle. The cold is specific: it begins at the nail beds, converts to burning at the second knuckle, and then becomes numbness at the fingertip. He gets the buckle, puts the glove back. The tent is up.
- The primus stove: pumped in the tent vestibule, sheltered from the wind. It catches on the fourth pump. The flame is blue and reluctant. Water takes eleven minutes to reach the temperature where it will hydrate the dried chuño. They eat hardtack and chuño paste. The sardines from the last two cans. Nobody is hungry but the body at altitude must be fed or it stops cooperating.
- Holroyd through binoculars before dark: the German camp, visible across the snowfield 400 meters south-southwest, at slightly lower elevation. A canvas tent — better quality than his, oil-treated, purpose-built for altitude. He can see two figures working on the tent — Riedel and one other. The tent is fully up before dark. He cannot see Fischer or Kern separately.

**The storm arrives:**
- At nine in the evening the wind changes direction — south to northwest — and rises from a steady pull to a hammering within twenty minutes. The canvas flexes in ways that are not the gentle flex of a night breeze; it strikes and releases, strikes and releases. The alpine rope ridge line hums. One of the hardwood branch poles bows noticeably at its joint; Holroyd watches it in the lamplight and decides it will hold. It holds.
- Temperature inside the tent: minus 8°C with three bodies and the residual primus heat. Outside, by the mercury thermometer Holroyd holds out through the vestibule gap for sixty seconds, minus 22°C.
- Watches in shifts: Isobel takes the first (10 PM to 1 AM), then Mariano (1 to 3 AM), then Holroyd (3 to dawn). During Holroyd's watch the storm is at its worst — wind sustained above 60 mph by his estimate, the tent flexing to within a foot of his face on the windward side. The bowing branch pole has deflected further. He puts his right hand flat against the canvas peak and holds a steady counter-pressure. He is doing this in the dark, by feel, at minus 8°C inside the tent, at 17,000 feet, and he is aware of how absurd and necessary it is simultaneously.
- Around 4 AM, the wind drops in a way that is not a lull but a withdrawal — the storm moving east toward the Amazon basin. The tent stops hammering. The sudden quiet is disorienting; the absence of noise wakes both Isobel and Mariano immediately.
- Isobel: "Is it over?" Holroyd, hand still on the canvas: "It's moved east." He removes his hand. The canvas stays up. He says: "Get another hour if you can." Isobel: "I can't."

**Dawn — the German camp:**
- 5:30 AM. The sky is cleared to hard stars, fading to blue at the eastern horizon. The temperature is minus 20°C and still. The snow around the tent is sculpted into wind-carved ridges, sharp-edged, the surface hard enough to walk on without breaking through. The light is coming.
- Holroyd with binoculars on the German camp. The camp has taken more damage than his: the purpose-built tent is partially collapsed — one side down, ballasted under accumulated wind-blown snow. He can see three figures, which tells him Fischer has not descended in the night.
- The three figures: one lying in the snow beside the collapsed tent section, one crouching over him — Agustín, the dark poncho distinctive — the third standing with camp equipment in his arms. Agustín is rubbing the prone man's hands with quick strokes, then stops and tips water from a flask into the man's mouth.
- The prone figure: Fischer. He is alive — Holroyd can see the movement of response when Agustín gives the water. But he does not sit up. He is being managed, not assisted.
- The standing figure: Kern, breaking down the collapsed tent section, moving with the careful economy of a man who is cold but functional. And then Riedel — the fourth figure, which Holroyd missed at first because he is standing still forty meters upslope, face toward the mountain.
- Riedel looks at Fischer. He looks at the mountain. Holroyd watches this sequence through the binoculars and can read nothing from it except that it happens twice, in the same order. Then Riedel walks back down to the camp and crouches beside Fischer and Agustín. There is an exchange Holroyd cannot read. Then Riedel stands and hands Agustín a coil of rope — not the climbing rope, the load rope, the rope for assisting someone who cannot fully manage their own weight on a descent.
- Fischer, with help, gets to his feet. He stands for a moment and Holroyd sees his right hand: it is wrapped in what appears to be spare cloth, not a glove. His fingers are not working correctly. Agustín shoulders Fischer's pack in addition to his own. The two of them begin moving downhill — south, toward the puna, away from the mountain.
- Riedel watches them go for perhaps two minutes. Then he turns to Kern. They begin loading the remaining equipment. They are going up.

**The information delivered:**
- Holroyd lowers the binoculars and turns to the tent where Mariano and Isobel are watching him. He says: "One of Riedel's men is going down. Agustín is taking him. Two are continuing up." Mariano: "Which two?" Holroyd: "Riedel and the German." A pause. "Fischer's lost something. His hand was wrapped." Isobel: "Fingers. Or his lungs." She looks at the sky. "Either way, Agustín takes him down four thousand feet and that's the end of Agustín's expedition." A pause. "Riedel knows that. He sent them anyway."
- Mariano has already turned from the conversation. He is looking at the ridge above. He points: the dark horizontal line in the cliff face, visible in the morning clarity — the bowl entrance. "There." Holroyd looks. "How far?" Mariano: "Two hours in this." He means this snow surface, this cold, this state of their bodies after a night in the storm. "Maybe less." He pauses. "They are behind us." He looks at the downhill movement of Fischer and Agustín. "But not by much."
- Holroyd goes back into the tent and drinks the remaining tea from the primus. It is not warm enough but it is there. He does the arithmetic: two hours to the bowl. Riedel's party, starting from 400 meters lower and slightly less rested, perhaps two and a half to three hours behind. If they leave now and move without stopping, they arrive first.
- He checks his notebook. He pulls the Leica from inside his jacket and ensures the shutter mechanism has not stiffened in the cold — it operates correctly. He loads a fresh roll of film he has been keeping against his body. Thirty-six exposures. He puts it back.
- He says: "Pack. We leave in thirty minutes." No one argues.

---

## Outcome / Turn

The storm has done its damage asymmetrically: Holroyd's party is shaken but intact; Riedel's party is reduced to two. Fischer's incapacitation — the nature and severity of it, seen only through binoculars at 400 meters — is left ambiguous in the prose; he may lose fingers, may lose more. He is gone from the expedition. Riedel is reduced to himself and one man, and he is still going up, which tells Holroyd something about the degree of Riedel's commitment that no prior intelligence could have told him.

The weather window is open. The bowl entrance is visible. The race is in its final stage.

---

## New Question or Pressure Raised

Riedel sent Fischer down knowing it cost him Agustín too — he chose the mountain over the logistical safety net. Is this the choice of a man who is close enough to feel the finish line, or a man who is too far in to calculate correctly? And what does a two-person German party at 17,000 feet represent — a greater or lesser threat to Holroyd's party than five men would have been?
