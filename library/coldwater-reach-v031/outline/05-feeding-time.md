# Chapter 05 — Feeding Time

**One-line summary**: Hannah establishes her daily routine around the POWs; she and Karl have their first real exchange across the fence.

**POV**: Hannah Pfeiffer  
**Tense**: Third-person limited, past  
**Setting**: The Pfeiffer farm: barn, feeding alley, fence near the icehouse  
**Time**: November 1943

---

## Goal
Hannah wants to understand Karl — not romantically, but with the fascination of someone for whom these men represent a piece of her family's silence made audible.

## Obstacle
The language barrier is genuine (Karl has no English; Hannah's German is functional but haltingly spoken). Lonzo Reed is always present, though his attention varies. Hannah's own uncertainty about what this exchange would mean stops her from pursuing it directly.

---

## Action Beats

- Chapter opens at 5 a.m.: Hannah's routine. The darkness before light; the sound of the cows registering her presence as she moves through the barn. The cold of the feed bucket handle; the smell of the barn in November — hay, manure, the specific fug of breath from twenty dairy cows. This is home in the way home means your body knows where everything is without looking.
- The POWs are brought out for farm work at seven. The three older men go to the north pasture fence-repair detail; Karl is assigned to manure hauling near the barn — the worst job, which means it goes to the youngest.
- Hannah observes him through the barn's south gap. He works efficiently, without complaint. His clothes are still Army-issue denim, slightly better fitting after Magda let out the seams — Hannah registered this without her mother saying a word about it.
- One morning she leaves the feed bucket on her side of the gate rather than carrying it through — it's heavy and she's tired and she's watching the ice on the puddle — and Karl, coming past with the wheelbarrow, picks it up and brings it to the fence post. He does not hand it over the fence; he places it where she can reach it, which is the right call. He says something in German — polite, she can tell by the register: something like *there you are* or *here it is*. Nothing she can directly respond to. She says: *Danke.* He looks up.
- He is surprised she spoke German. It costs him something — she can see him recalibrate.
- She says, in German, deliberately: *Mein Vater kommt aus Bayern.* She has been holding this sentence for three weeks, deciding whether to say it.
- He considers. In careful German: *Ich auch.* (Me too.)
- Nothing more. She takes the bucket. He goes back to the wheelbarrow. Lonzo Reed, ten yards away, is adjusting the strap on his boot.
- For the rest of the morning she thinks about *auch*. The word means *too* or *also* — an ordinary conjunction, the humblest way of placing yourself in the same category as someone else. *Ich auch.* He is from Bavaria too. He is the same as her father in this one way that her father won't discuss.
- At noon she eats at the kitchen table. Her father has been up since 4 a.m.; he comes in for the noon meal, eats standing up, goes back out. Her mother stirs something on the stove. No one speaks. Hannah thinks: two languages in this house and still no words.

---

## Outcome / Turn
The first real exchange between Hannah and Karl. Three words each, in German, across a fence. The connection has been made — at its most minimal possible level, which is the right level for now.

## New Question or Pressure Raised
What does Karl know about Bavaria that Hannah's father won't say? And what does Albrecht think about having this young man from near his hometown on the property?

---

## Continuity Notes
- Karl has no English at this point in November 1943. Canon fact #13.
- Hannah is "functionally fluent" in German — she understands almost everything spoken to her but speaks it "haltingly, with the grammar her father used." Canon character note.
- Albrecht Pfeiffer is from Straubing; Karl from Regensburg — about 50 km apart. Canon facts #10, #12.
- Lonzo Reed: 61, bad knee, always in the chair or close to it. Canon fact #28.
- Hannah's German in this chapter: *Danke. Mein Vater kommt aus Bayern.* Spoken haltingly but correctly. This is her first voluntary use of her father's language with a stranger.
- Karl's response: *Ich auch.* Two words. This is his first real verbal contact with Hannah.
- Magda letting out the seams of Karl's uniform: a small act of domestic care that Hannah has noticed and not remarked on. This should be rendered as an observation, not a symbol.
- The word *auch*: in Hannah's narration it may carry the weight of a whole thought. Do not translate or explain it beyond the context.
