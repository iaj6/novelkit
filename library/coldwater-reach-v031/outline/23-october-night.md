# Chapter 23 — October Night

**One-line summary**: Hannah takes Karl to the ridge above Coldwater Reach to see the harbor lights; she brings him back before full dark and goes to bed knowing what she chose.

**POV**: Hannah Pfeiffer  
**Tense**: Third-person limited, past  
**Setting**: The back fields of the Pfeiffer farm; the ridge road; the ridge above Coldwater Reach; the farm road back  
**Time**: October 1944 — the same evening as Chapter 22

---

## Goal
Hannah wants to give Karl what she told him she would give him — the harbor, which he has never seen. This is the most deliberate thing she has done in the novel.

## Obstacle
The obstacle is the act itself: the transgression is obvious, the potential consequences are real, and she is doing it anyway. The chapter's interest is not in whether she will be caught but in what it feels like to choose this clearly.

---

## Action Beats

- Two days before: Karl said to her, in English, complete sentence — "You will show me the harbor?" He had been practicing it; she could tell. She said: *morgen Abend.* Tomorrow evening. He nodded.
- She has thought about it since — not about whether to do it, she decided that when she said the words, but about how. She knows the back path through the upper pasture, the old stone wall at the field's edge, the ridge road that the farm horses use in haying season. From the ridge you can see the harbor lights of Coldwater Reach and the black stretch of water beyond.
- The evening: after the headcount (which she doesn't know about) and after her parents are settled — her father at the radio, her mother in the kitchen. Hannah puts on Rolf's coat. She tells her mother she is going out to check on one of the cows that was off her feed earlier. Her mother nods. This is a plausible thing.
- She takes the back path. At the stone wall she stops and waits. After ten minutes Karl comes through the pasture gate — she has arranged this through a note left at the fence, the back of a word-card: *come through the gate at the top of the upper pasture at nine o'clock.* He had read it and nodded.
- They walk together on the ridge road without talking. October dark, the stars very clear, cold enough that their breath shows.
- At the ridge: Coldwater Reach below them. The harbor light on the breakwater. The lights of the Reach Road buildings. The black that is the sea.
- Karl looks for a long time. She watches him look. He doesn't say anything for several minutes. Then, in German: *Das ist sehr groß.* (That is very large.) She says, in English: "Yes."
- He says, in German: "The light at the end — is that the harbor?" She says: "The breakwater. They found a man there last year. He had drowned." She doesn't know why she says this. She hasn't said it to anyone. Karl says: *Ich habe davon gehört.* (I heard about that.) The POW network, the rumors that travel.
- They stand. The cold comes in off the water. She is aware of everything she has done to be standing here: the word-cards, the fence-line walks, the word *morgen Abend* said without thinking through all of what it meant.
- She says, in German: "We should go back." He says: "Yes." They walk back the way they came. At the stone wall they separate — she on the farm path, he on the perimeter. She crosses the yard without looking back. She checks on the cow (it's fine). She goes inside.
- Her father is asleep in the radio chair. Her mother has left the kitchen lamp on. Hannah goes upstairs, takes off Rolf's coat, hangs it on the hook. She sits on the bed.
- She doesn't feel guilty. She doesn't feel vindicated. She feels the particular solidity of something she chose — the same feeling as the word *mündig*, which she thinks about now: having her own mouth. She spoke with it. She made an arrangement and kept it. She is not sure it was right; she is sure it was hers.
- She lies down. She does not sleep for a while. Outside, October.

---

## Outcome / Turn
Hannah has done it. The choice is made and it is hers. She isn't running from it or performing certainty about it. The chapter closes with the feeling of having decided, not with resolution.

## New Question or Pressure Raised
Can she hold this indefinitely? Karl will eventually leave — repatriation, the war's end. What has she built with him that will outlast his presence? And what does Karl's mention of "hearing about" the body mean?

---

## Continuity Notes
- Chapter 22 and Chapter 23 are the same night. They must be consistent: Karl leaves the icehouse after the 9 p.m. headcount (which Lonzo marks present), meets Hannah at the stone wall around 9:15–9:30, and returns to the icehouse at 10:15.
- The ridge: established in the chapter map as the high ground above Coldwater Reach from which the harbor lights are visible, approximately a mile from the farm. Hannah has been here before; it is not a remote location, just a slightly elevated one.
- The breakwater light: Coldwater Reach has a working harbor light on the breakwater. Eira's office overlooks the anchorage; the light would be visible from the ridge.
- Karl says *Ich habe davon gehört*: the POWs have heard about the body on the breakwater. This travels through the Bangor provost marshal area's POW network as a piece of local news — not espionage talk, just the way news moves. Hannah knows about the body from town talk; Karl knows from the POW network. They have not previously connected these two things.
- Hannah telling Karl about the drowned man: she doesn't plan to say it. It arrives. The Drafter should render this as an unplanned disclosure — not the most significant one in the chapter, just something she said because they were standing at the harbor lights and the breakwater was there.
- Rolf's coat: she wears it for the walk. The coat is her brother, her protection, the thing she borrowed before she knew she was borrowing it permanently.
- "I am not sure it was right; I am sure it was hers": this is the formulation of Hannah's arc at this chapter. She is not claiming moral certainty; she is claiming authorship. This should not be stated explicitly in the narration; it should be the felt conclusion of the chapter's action.
- What Hannah tells no one: she tells no one about this night in the rest of the novel. Not her parents, not Rolf in V-Mail, not Marcus Klein. The secret is held.
