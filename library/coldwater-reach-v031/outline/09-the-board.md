# Chapter 09 — The Board

**One-line summary**: Marcus learns the school board discussed his position at the December meeting; Henry advises him to do nothing and wait for them to act first.

**POV**: Marcus Klein  
**Tense**: Third-person limited, past  
**Setting**: Coldwater Reach High School; Henry and Lillian's house  
**Time**: January 1944

---

## Goal
Marcus needs to understand the threat's shape — what the board said, what it might do — so he can decide how to respond. The goal is situational clarity, not action.

## Obstacle
He doesn't know what was said. The board isn't required to tell him. The person who tells him (Edna Locke) knows only what she observed and is already taking a risk in telling him this much. Marcus cannot ask directly without appearing either anxious or aggressive.

---

## Action Beats

- January faculty meeting: distribution of the spring schedule. Marcus's name is on it as usual — German I, German II, Latin I, Latin III. He files this without visible relief.
- After the meeting, the other teachers file out. Edna Locke — history teacher, been at the school since 1919, a woman who treats him with the steady consideration of someone who has had occasion to think about fairness — stays at the table after the others have gone. She waits until the last footstep has retreated down the corridor.
- She tells him: at the December board meeting there was "some talk." Concern from some parents. After the holidays there may be a formal item on the agenda — a review of his appointment. She doesn't know more than this; she was told by the board secretary, who thought she should know.
- Marcus thanks her. He says he appreciates it. He means both sentences. She nods and goes. The exchange takes under three minutes; she has a class in four.
- He goes back to his classroom and teaches the afternoon Latin section. His voice is as it always is — precise, measured, not performing calm but genuinely deploying the part of himself that finds Latin grammar interesting. *The ablative absolute,* he tells them. *A construction that carries an entire subordinate action in two words. Economy as a Latin virtue.*
- That evening: chess with Henry. Marcus tells him what Edna Locke said. Henry listens without interrupting; he is studying the board. He moves his rook; then: "You want to know what I'd do?"
- Marcus says yes.
- Henry: "Nothing. Do your job. Make them act first. If you make a move before they do — write a letter to someone, complain, ask a question at the board meeting — you've given them a reason to move faster. Let it sit."
- Marcus: "And if they do act?"
- Henry: "Then you have a case. Right now you have a rumor."
- Marcus moves a bishop. He thinks about the logic of this — it is not cowardice; it is the practice of making the other side expose itself. He has seen it applied to worse situations.
- He writes a letter to the Emergency Committee at Cambridge before bed. Not a request for relocation. An informational note: the board has raised questions about his appointment; he does not believe action is imminent; he is continuing to teach. He signs it and puts it in his coat pocket to post in the morning.
- He thinks, before he sleeps, about the word *Duldung* — German, meaning toleration, being put up with. It is not the same as acceptance. He has been the object of *Duldung* in several countries. He knows what it feels like. He knows also that it is not nothing; it is a space you can work in.

---

## Outcome / Turn
Marcus knows the shape of the threat. He is not going to make a move before the board does. He has Henry's counsel as both strategy and companionship. The letter to Cambridge is an insurance filing, not a withdrawal.

## New Question or Pressure Raised
When will the board's formal action come, and what form will it take? Does Marcus have allies — besides Edna Locke — he doesn't know about?

## Continuity Notes
- Edna Locke: history teacher, Coldwater Reach High School, been there since 1919. She is Marcus's one overt ally on the faculty. Her appearance here is her first; she returns at Chapter 24 (the hearing).
- Henry's advice — "make them act first" — is the wisdom of someone who has navigated discrimination with precision for a long time. It should read as counsel from experience, not as a lesson Marcus is receiving. Henry has been through this. He doesn't explain that.
- The Emergency Committee at Cambridge: Marcus notified but not alarmed. The letter is filed in continuity — it becomes relevant if the board hearing in Chapter 24 goes badly.
- The word *Duldung*: Marcus's interior. Not translated; context carries it as "the space of being put up with."
- Marcus's classes: German I, German II, Latin I, Latin III. The spring schedule lists them; the Drafter can reference these as needed.
- The ablative absolute: a real Latin construction. Marcus genuinely finds this interesting; the parallel to his own life (carrying subordinate contexts in compressed forms) should be implicit only, never stated.
- Chess: Thursday evenings. Established here. Henry wins more than Marcus.
