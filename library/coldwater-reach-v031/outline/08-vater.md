# Chapter 08 — Vater

**One-line summary**: At the December dinner table, Albrecht acknowledges — in German, without explanation — that Karl comes from near his own hometown; Hannah is the only one listening.

**POV**: Hannah Pfeiffer  
**Tense**: Third-person limited, past  
**Setting**: Pfeiffer farmhouse kitchen; the barn (briefly); Hannah's room  
**Time**: December 1943 — a weekday evening

---

## Goal
Hannah wants to understand what her father makes of Karl's presence on the farm — whether he recognizes the proximity (Straubing to Regensburg, 50 km) and what it means to him.

## Obstacle
Her father's silence is not a wall but a habit; it lets very little through, and what does come through arrives in a language she understands imperfectly and cannot respond to fully. Her mother's silence is something else — climate, not communication.

---

## Action Beats

- The chapter begins in the barn at evening, the last feeding of the day. The cows are settled. Hannah can hear, through the barn wall, the POWs in the icehouse — the sound of a door, someone speaking low. She cannot make out the words. She finishes her work.
- At the house: her mother has made soup from the root cellar's produce — potato and turnip, very little else. The kitchen smells of this and of the woodstove. Her father is already at the table.
- Dinner: the three of them without conversation. This is the norm. Hannah eats; her mother eats; her father eats and then, without looking up, begins to talk — in German, as he always does now at home — about the north pasture fence, which the boys have been repairing. He says Karl works quickly with the post driver. He says this with what is, for Albrecht, something close to approval.
- Hannah asks, in German — she has to gather herself to do it, because speaking German with her father feels like entering a room she's only supposed to observe: *Hat einer von ihnen etwas gesagt über Straubing?* (Has any of them said anything about Straubing?)
- Her father pauses. He puts down his spoon. He says: *Regensburg ist nicht weit.* (Regensburg is not far.) He says it the way he says things that have been sitting in him for a while: flat, without inflection, as if stating a weather fact.
- Then he picks up his spoon and finishes his soup. He folds his napkin. He goes to the radio chair.
- Hannah's mother has not looked up from her bowl. After a moment she rises and takes the dishes to the sink. She hums something — not a song Hannah recognizes, something old, Bavarian maybe. She stops after a few bars.
- Hannah washes the dishes. The first hard snow fell this morning; she can see it on the barn roof through the window. Blue light on white.
- She thinks about what her father said: *nicht weit.* Not far. Not: *I know where that is.* Not: *he is nearly my neighbor.* Just the statement of proximity — the distance reduced to a geographic fact, which is the only safe form it can take.
- She goes upstairs. Rolf's coat on the hook. She sits on the bed. She thinks: her father knows. He has always known. The knowing is not what he's protecting; the question is what the knowing costs him, and whether Karl's presence makes the cost higher or lower.
- She thinks about the word *Heimweh* — she has heard her father use it, not about Germany specifically but in the abstract, the way you use a word that doesn't need a referent because the feeling is already understood. Homesickness. Except it isn't sickness exactly — *Weh* is pain, ache. Home-ache. Different thing.

---

## Outcome / Turn
Albrecht has, in his minimal way, acknowledged Karl's proximity to his hometown. He won't discuss it, but he has registered it. Something between them — father and daughter, father and the young prisoner he has not yet looked at directly — has shifted.

## New Question or Pressure Raised
What does Karl represent to Albrecht — threat, proof, memory, or something he has not named? And what does Hannah do with the knowing that her father knows?

## Continuity Notes
- Albrecht Pfeiffer: from Straubing; Karl from Regensburg, approximately 50 km away. Canon facts #10, #12.
- Albrecht speaks only German at home at this point. Canon fact (from characters.md). His English is correct but slightly formal when used; it is simply not used at home.
- Magda: her near-silence is a climate, not a subject. She hums something Bavarian — this is not a scene about her; it is a fact registered by Hannah. Do not editorialize.
- The word *Heimweh*: Hannah's interior, not spoken. Not translated in narration; context carries it.
- The word *Vater* in the chapter title is Hannah's German-interior name for her father. In narration she thinks of him as *Vater* when she is operating in the German mode of her consciousness.
- The first snow: period-accurate for downeast Maine, December. The blue light on white through the window is an image, not a metaphor; let it be a thing Hannah sees.
- Rolf's coat: present in Hannah's room throughout the novel as a constant. It is not symbolic; it is her brother's coat that she wears.
- This chapter should be quiet, even spare by Hannah's chapter standards. The density of the interior should be in what she does not say to her father rather than in reflection after the fact.
