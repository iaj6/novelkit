# Chapter 13 — Ada Gile's Window

**One-line summary**: Eira goes to the post office with a purpose and learns from Ada that the postcard addressed to "A. Schell" contained a date and a time — nothing more.

**POV**: Eira Bowman  
**Tense**: Third-person limited, past  
**Setting**: The Coldwater Reach post office (Reach Road); the harbor office  
**Time**: March 1944

---

## Goal
Eira wants to know what Ada Gile knows about the body inquiry — specifically whether anything came through the post that might connect to the man on the breakwater.

## Obstacle
Ada does not gossip. She informs, on her own terms, when the question is asked correctly. Eira has to ask without appearing to investigate, because investigating means explaining that her official report is incomplete.

---

## Action Beats

- A March morning, the harbor quiet, an east wind coming off the water that carries sleet. Eira makes the walk to the post office herself — she usually sends one of the dock hands, but today she goes.
- The post office: the counter, the pigeonhole grid behind it, Ada Gile's reading glasses on her nose, the smell of paper and ink that Eira has associated with this room since childhood. Ada knows the harbor business through the mail it receives; she and Eira have had a professional relationship for eight years.
- Ada hands Eira the harbor office mail bundle: three Bureau of Navigation circulars, a quarterly report from the Lighthouse Service, a V-Mail from the buoy tender's schedule coordinator.
- Eira lingers. She says: "Ada. That business last fall — the body. Did the Coast Guard speak with you?"
- Ada: "They did."
- Eira: "About mail."
- Ada, not a question: "Yes."
- Eira: "Was there something?"
- Ada: "A postcard. Came in the first week of October. General delivery, the name 'A. Schell.' No last name used as a first name or any other way I could place it. Coldwater has no one by that name."
- Eira, carefully: "What did it say?"
- Ada considers this question — considers whether it is within her professional scope to answer it, and decides it is: "A date and a time. Nothing else. I assumed it was a meeting of some kind."
- Eira: "The Coast Guard took it?"
- Ada: "In November. Along with a statement from me about when it arrived."
- Eira: "Thank you."
- She walks back to the harbor office. Sleet has turned to rain by the time she arrives.
- At her desk: she opens her personal log to the Macomber section and writes: *A. Schell — general delivery — postcard — Portland postmark — early October. Contents: date and time. No other text. Coast Guard has it.*
- She sits back. She looks at what she has. A postcard sent from Portland to a man in Coldwater Reach who had no established presence in the town — no boardinghouse, no known address — with a meeting time on it. The *Macomber* coaster, running a Navy district contract out of Portland, in Coldwater Reach the night of October 9. A man at the end of the pier with a canvas bag. The body three days later.
- She does not write the connection down. It is a set of coincidences, not evidence. But it has a shape now, and the shape is specific.
- She makes coffee. She does not drink it.

---

## Outcome / Turn
Eira now has enough to form a theory: August Schell (not yet known by name) received a postcard arranging a meeting in Coldwater Reach in early October. The meeting may have been connected to Macomber's presence. He came, the meeting happened or didn't, and then he was dead. The theory explains the shape without resolving it.

## New Question or Pressure Raised
What was the meeting? Who sent the postcard from Portland? Is there a way to find out without making her own omission visible?

---

## Continuity Notes
- The postcard: addressed to "A. Schell," general delivery, Coldwater Reach. Portland postmark, first week of October 1943. Contents: a date and a time. No other text. This is the same information Ada gives Marcus in Chapter 12, but Eira receives additional detail (it was a postcard, not just "a piece of mail"; she learns the contents).
- This is a Drafter continuity check: Chapter 12 (Marcus, March) and Chapter 13 (Eira, March) happen in roughly the same week. Marcus visited Ada first. Ada gives Eira the same information and a bit more. Ada does not remark on Marcus having asked — it is not her habit to report one inquiry to another asker.
- The theory Eira forms: coherent but not provable. The narration should render it as a professional assessment — the way she'd assess tide height from visible data — not as an emotional revelation.
- "She does not drink the coffee": this is established as a signal in Eira's chapters — when she makes coffee and doesn't drink it, she is preoccupied. Cf. Chapter 07.
- Ada Gile's manner: "not a gossip; an informer." She answers questions asked correctly; she does not offer. She is Coldwater Reach's institutional memory in a way that parallels Eira's relation to the harbor.
- The harbor office's running logs (official vs. personal): the personal log is where Eira does her actual thinking. The official log is the record. The distinction is important throughout.
