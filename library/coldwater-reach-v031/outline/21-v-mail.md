# Chapter 21 — V-Mail

**One-line summary**: Hannah writes to Rolf and omits everything that matters; walking home past the school she sees the light in Marcus's classroom and wonders what he's doing there after hours.

**POV**: Hannah Pfeiffer  
**Tense**: Third-person limited, past  
**Setting**: Pfeiffer farmhouse; Reach Road; the post office; the school (exterior)  
**Time**: September 1944

---

## Goal
Hannah needs to write to Rolf — the V-Mail she owes him is a month overdue. She also needs, in writing it, to decide what she tells him and what she keeps.

## Obstacle
V-Mail is censored. More than that: Rolf is a soldier in a war zone, and she cannot write what is actually happening at home without it seeming either alarming or beside the point. The habit of omission she is developing has reasons beyond censorship.

---

## Action Beats

- September: the farm in early fall. The leaves are just beginning on the ridge. The potatoes came up and have been dug. The cows are still out on the pasture but not for much longer.
- Hannah sits at the kitchen table with the V-Mail form. She has been putting it off for three weeks. Her mother is not in the kitchen; her father is out at the barn with Karl and the others, doing the pre-winter fence work.
- She writes: the potatoes (good yield, better than last year). The cows (the big one, the one Rolf named Martha when they were children, died in July — a mastitis case; she decides not to tell him). The weather (cooler already, the fog every morning). *Mutti sends her love. Vati says the north pasture fence should hold through another winter.*
- She doesn't write: Karl's name. She doesn't write: the word-cards. She doesn't write: the walk along the fence, the conversation about the butcher shop in Regensburg. She doesn't write: *mündig,* and what she's been doing with having her own mouth.
- She thinks, writing: what would it mean to tell him? She would have to start three years back, with Vati's shift to German-only, and Mutti's silence, and the four men who arrived in the Army truck. She would have to explain Karl without making him sound like a story of any particular kind, when he isn't a story — he is a person who is there, on the farm, and she has been talking to him through the fence.
- And the censors. Whatever she writes, a censor in a room somewhere reads it. That is the other thing.
- She writes: *I am well. The weather is changing. I think of you.* She signs her name. She folds the form and walks to the post office.
- Ada Gile takes the form without comment. Hannah buys a two-cent stamp and waits for Ada to frank it. She says, on an impulse: "Do you hear news from the boys?" Ada, looking at the stamp: "Some. Fewer letters than there were."
- Hannah walks home. She goes the long way, past the school, because it is a September afternoon and the road is nicer that way. As she passes the school building she notices the light in Marcus Klein's classroom. After hours — it's past four. She slows. She can see, through the window, Marcus at his desk. He is not grading; he is reading. His posture is the same as it is in class — very straight, very contained, but not quite still. He is doing something private that looks, from the outside, indistinguishable from what he always does.
- She walks on. She thinks: everyone is keeping something in this town. She thinks this without self-pity and without irony — just as an observation, the way she would observe that the leaves are beginning on the ridge.

---

## Outcome / Turn
Hannah has written the letter and omitted everything that matters. She is developing fluency in this — choosing what to tell, not as deception but as care, as the recognition that not everything belongs to everyone. The glimpse of Marcus in the classroom closes the chapter with the observation that she is not alone in keeping.

## New Question or Pressure Raised
Is Hannah's omission — from Rolf, from her parents, from anyone — going to become a problem? She is choosing silence the way her father chose it. Is this the same thing?

---

## Continuity Notes
- V-Mail format: a specific form, photographed and reduced to microfilm. Censored. The convention is that soldiers on both ends know not to say anything that will be crossed out. Hannah knows this.
- Rolf in Italy: alive as of fall 1944. His V-Mail is delayed and irregular. He is not described in detail; he is a presence through Hannah's letters and memories.
- Martha the cow: named by Rolf in childhood, dead of mastitis in July 1944. Hannah decides not to tell him this in the letter. This is a small, specific choice that demonstrates her developing omission practice.
- The potatoes: recurring Rolf/Hannah correspondence touchstone. He asked about them in the April birthday letter (Chapter 14).
- Ada Gile: "fewer letters than there were." She means casualty rates are reducing correspondence volume — she sees this through the mail. She says it neutrally; Hannah hears what she means.
- Marcus in his classroom after hours: he is reading, not grading. What he is reading is not specified. He reads in English; he annotates in German. He is at his desk after four on a weekday — he stays late sometimes, because the school is a place he can be still in without explaining himself.
- "Everyone is keeping something in this town": Hannah's observation, not editorial. It should read as an empirical claim she makes at this age, about these people, not as a thematic statement.
- Karl: not mentioned in the letter, not mentioned at the post office. His absence from the V-Mail is significant. The Drafter should ensure this feels like a choice, not an oversight.
