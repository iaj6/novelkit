# Chapter 04 — The Coast Guard

**One-line summary**: A Coast Guard lieutenant from Portland asks about vessel traffic on the night before the storm; Eira answers with what is in the log and not one word more.

**POV**: Eira Bowman  
**Tense**: Third-person limited, past  
**Setting**: Harbor office; the dock  
**Time**: November 1943

---

## Goal
Eira needs to manage the Coast Guard's return inquiry without exposing her incomplete log entry — and without lying in a way that would be discoverable if the record is examined.

## Obstacle
A lieutenant from the First District (Portland) is more thorough than the petty officer from Machias was. He has a file. He asks about vessels she did not mention in her initial report — specifically whether she saw any vessel she did not log.

---

## Action Beats

- A morning in early November, steel-grey, the inner anchorage choppy under a northeast wind. Eira is at the desk when the motor surfboat ties up and Lieutenant Pruitt comes down the dock. He is young enough that she notices his age before his rank. He has the manner of someone who went to school for this job.
- He sits across from her desk. The file he opens is thin: her incident report, Petty Officer Gervais's field notes, a pathologist's preliminary — she can see the top sheet without reading it clearly. *Adult male, drowning, approximate age 38–48, no identifying documents, no identifying marks.*
- He asks: vessel traffic, October 9, between 2200 and 0400. She gives him the log: lobster boats *Thistle* and *Doris V.* at mooring; coaster *Macomber* (Birch Macomber, Machias), arrived October 8, departed October 11; the Lighthouse Service ice barge *Ploughman* at anchor.
- He asks: did she observe any vessel she did not log? She says no.
- He asks: did she observe anything unusual on the pier or dock during that period? She says she was out at approximately 0130 checking the *Macomber*'s mooring bridle — the first gusts were running and she was concerned about the set of the bridle on the bollard. No unusual activity.
- She does not say: there was a man at the end of the pier with a canvas bag.
- The lieutenant notes what she's said. He tells her the investigation has identified no evidence of foul play; cause of death is drowning. The case is open but not active. They will notify her if anything develops. He thanks her.
- After he leaves, Eira stands at the window. The motor surfboat rounds the breakwater and opens up toward Machias. She watches it until the fog takes it.
- She goes back to the log and reads what she entered for October 11: *Macomber — departure 0430.* The handwriting is hers. She does not remember making the entry. She would not normally be in the office at 0430 — that would have been before she came in, in the dark, in the second day of the blow.
- She looks at this for a long time. Then she closes the log.
- She goes to the bolted drawer in the chart cabinet and checks the vessel documentation. Birch Macomber's *Macomber*: registered Machias, 1937. She knows Macomber by sight — a man in his late fifties, spare, cautious with money. Reliable. She has no reason to think anything about him except: a coaster does not put out on the second day of a nor'easter without reason.

---

## Outcome / Turn
The Coast Guard's official position is drowning, no foul play. The case is not closing so much as ceasing to be pursued. Eira has now actively withheld the thing she saw — not by lying, but by answering the precise question asked and not the one she should have asked herself. The departure log irregularity is now in her attention.

## New Question or Pressure Raised
Who made the 0430 departure entry in her log? If not her, then who was in the harbor office that morning? And why did Birch Macomber put out in the second day of the storm?

## Continuity Notes
- Lieutenant Pruitt, Coast Guard First District (Portland). Not a major character; named for consistency.
- The October 1943 log entries, specifically: lobster boats *Thistle* and *Doris V.* at mooring; coaster *Macomber*, Birch Macomber, Machias; ice barge *Ploughman* at anchor. These are the vessels in harbor during the storm.
- The *Macomber* departure entry: October 11, 0430, in Eira's handwriting, which she does not remember making. This is the key irregularity. Drafter must treat this consistently: it's not that someone forged it — it's that Eira entered it in a state of exhaustion at the end of the storm's second day and genuinely does not remember doing so. The content matters: it's possible the *Macomber* departed before Eira came in that morning and she logged it from a note left by the overnight watch (the harbor has no formal overnight watchman, but fishermen sometimes leave word). She cannot now reconstruct this cleanly.
- Eira's precise answer to "did you observe anything unusual" — she mentions checking the mooring at 0130 but omits the man. This is her true withheld statement. It must be consistent with the full account she writes in Chapter 20.
- The chart cabinet: the pending drawer. Canon character detail (Owen's things are in it). The bolted drawer is for vessel documentation, separate.
