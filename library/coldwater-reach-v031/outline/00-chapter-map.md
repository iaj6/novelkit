# Chapter Map — Coldwater Reach

## Overview

**Total chapters**: 30  
**Target length**: ~80,000 words  
**Word target per chapter**: 2,500–3,000 words (regular); 1,200–1,800 words (exceptional POV chapters)  
**Time span**: October 1943 – late April 1945 (ending approximately two weeks before VE Day, May 8, 1945)  
**POV rotation**: Third-person limited, past tense. Approximately 9 chapters each for Eira Bowman and Marcus Klein; 10 chapters for Hannah Pfeiffer; 3 exceptional short chapters from secondary perspectives (Henry Crow, Lonzo Reed, Karl Reinmar).

## Arc Shape

### SETUP: October – December 1943 (Chapters 1–8)
The three lives are established in their working worlds. The body arrives and is taken. The Coast Guard comes and goes. Each character takes on the weight of the question — Eira through what she didn't file, Hannah through the strangers in her family's icehouse, Marcus through rumor and the town's ambient suspicion. No character has yet moved toward the others. The situation is settling.

### RISING ACTION: January – June 1944 (Chapters 9–15)
Winter deepens all three situations. The school board's suspicion of Marcus moves from ambient to organized. Hannah and Karl develop their exchange through word-cards and fence-line conversations. Eira's informal investigation of the body's origins begins to yield: the name Birch Macomber, the Portland contract, the postcard addressed to "A. Schell." D-Day (Chapter 15) is a hinge in the world but not in the plot; each character takes it in from inside their own life.

### COMPLICATION: July – October 1944 (Chapters 16–23)
The body acquires a probable name — August Schell, German-born Swedish national — through different routes for Eira (naval inspector's visit) and Marcus (library research in Bangor). Karl's off-duty movement begins; Hannah's relationship with him deepens into real conversation. Henry Crow's exceptional chapter (18) shows the school board organizing against Marcus. Lonzo Reed's exceptional chapter (22) shows the supervision of the POWs becoming deliberately porous. Hannah takes Karl to the ridge above the harbor to see the sea: the novel's central small transgression, made knowingly.

### TURN / CRISIS: November 1944 – February 1945 (Chapters 24–27)
Marcus faces the school board hearing and keeps his post, narrowly. Eira faces the selectmen's pressure and decides to hold the harbor office by doing the job so well that displacement requires active malice. Hannah receives word of Karl's father's death and holds the secret. Marcus and Henry's deepest conversation — the chess game in Chapter 27 — closes with Marcus stating that he thinks he has stopped moving: the pivot of his arc.

### CODA: March – Late April 1945 (Chapters 28–30)
Karl's exceptional chapter (28) renders his interior — the uncertainty of returning to a Regensburg that may not exist. Eira's final chapter (29) closes the body's official record and files her private addendum. Hannah's final chapter (30) is the farm in late April, two weeks before VE Day: the war not yet over, the story's resolutions finished.

## Body-on-the-Breakwater: Information Disclosure by Chapter

The identity and circumstances of the body are assembled by the reader across multiple POVs. Disclosure is governed by:
- **Canon fact**: Eira saw a man on the dock the night of October 9; the body came in three days later.
- **Canon fact**: The body's origins are mundane, not espionage.
- **Resolved by end of novel**: The man was probably August Schell, German-born crew member of the Swedish merchant vessel SS *Ångermanälven*, who received a postcard at Coldwater general delivery and was apparently waiting for a meeting that either didn't happen or ended on the pier in the storm. He fell — or was swept — from the dock. No crime. No spy thriller. A man in the wrong sea in the wrong time.

## Key Unresolved Threads (Deliberate, per Brief)

1. **Who sent the Portland postcard to August Schell, and what was the meeting?** Never answered. Birch Macomber is the most probable connection (he left Coldwater on October 11 in conditions suggesting he was running from scrutiny), but this is inference, not fact. Macomber is never confronted.
2. **Karl Reinmar's postwar fate.** He is still at the Pfeiffer farm in April 1945. Repatriation will come but is not shown.
3. **Rolf Pfeiffer.** He is alive in Italy as of late 1944. His return — or failure to return — is not shown.
4. **What Eira's addendum means legally.** The document exists in the permanent record. She has not been deposed. Whether anyone ever reads it is not answered.
