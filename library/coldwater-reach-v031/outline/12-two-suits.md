# Chapter 12 — Two Suits

**One-line summary**: Marcus decides to stay in Coldwater Reach; he answers Cambridge's offer with no; Ada Gile mentions a Coast Guard inquiry about a piece of mail that did not belong to anyone in town.

**POV**: Marcus Klein  
**Tense**: Third-person limited, past  
**Setting**: Henry and Lillian's house; the post office; the street between them  
**Time**: March 1944 — a Saturday

---

## Goal
Marcus needs to answer the Cambridge letter — a Boston university posting has been offered — and in doing so must commit to what he is doing in Coldwater Reach.

## Obstacle
He is not sure yet whether choosing to stay is humility, fear, or wisdom. He has been carrying the letter for three weeks without knowing which.

---

## Action Beats

- Saturday: Marcus puts on the older suit (Thursday suit, but it is Saturday — he uses the schedule loosely on weekends). He has been carrying the Cambridge letter in his coat pocket for three weeks. The letter offers a position at a Boston-area university: visiting instructor in German philology. It would be a step toward his actual professional life. He knows this.
- He walks to the post office for stamps. This is what he tells himself he is going out to do. He is also, he knows, testing whether leaving the house on a particular errand changes his thinking.
- Ada Gile is at the counter. She knows who he is — she knows who everyone is; it is her professional requirement. She is not unfriendly to Marcus; she treats him with the same direct efficiency she applies to everyone, which is its own kind of respect.
- He buys stamps. He lingers slightly — not enough to be obvious, he thinks, though Ada misses nothing. She is sorting a bundle of V-Mail for delivery.
- He asks — carefully, the way he asks most things, framing the question so the answer doesn't have to be personal: "Has there been any further inquiry about the business last fall? The man on the breakwater."
- Ada says: "The Coast Guard came to me in November. There was a piece of mail that came through in the first week of October — addressed to general delivery, no full name. I held it the usual time and then it was collected by the Coast Guard when they asked. They seemed to know what they were looking for."
- He says: "What was the name on it?" She says: "A. Schell." She says it neutrally, as a fact that belongs to the record, not to a conversation. "No one of that name in Coldwater."
- He says thank you. He goes out. He walks home.
- At the kitchen table: Lillian brings coffee without being asked. He takes the Cambridge letter from his coat pocket and puts it on the table. He reads it again. Boston. Philology. Colleagues. A life he can recognize from the inside.
- He thinks about the word *heimisch* — not for the first time. He cannot say he is *heimisch* in Coldwater Reach. He also cannot say he was *heimisch* in Cambridge, or in London, or in Paris, or in the London boarding house he lived in for eleven months in 1939–1940. He does not know that the condition can be recovered.
- He thinks about Henry and Lillian. He thinks about Edna Locke in the corridor after the faculty meeting, choosing to tell him a thing that cost her something. He thinks about Hannah Pfeiffer looking out the window in German class, understanding more than she answers.
- He takes out a sheet of paper and writes to the Emergency Committee. One page. *I am grateful for the offer. I intend to remain in Coldwater Reach for the present year. I will write again in the autumn.* He does not explain further. He seals it and puts it with the stamps.
- He drinks his coffee. He thinks: *A. Schell.*

---

## Outcome / Turn
Marcus has committed to staying — provisionally, for the year. This is the first genuine act of investment in Coldwater Reach. It is not triumphant; it is a decision made by process of elimination and something he cannot yet name.

## New Question or Pressure Raised
Who is "A. Schell"? Marcus has the name now from Ada; the question is open.

---

## Continuity Notes
- The Cambridge letter: a Boston-area university posting, visiting instructor in German philology. This is a real alternative to Coldwater Reach; it should not read as obviously inferior. Marcus's choice to stay is not sentimentality.
- Marcus's answer to Cambridge: staying "for the present year." He writes "I will write again in the autumn" — which means by Chapter 24 (the hearing, November 1944) he will have had to follow up. Whether he did or not is a detail the Drafter can handle; the Emergency Committee does not reappear as an active plot element.
- Ada Gile: the postcard addressed to "A. Schell," general delivery, Coldwater Reach. Postmark Portland, early October 1943. Coast Guard collected it in November 1943. She says "a piece of mail" — she has not said it was a postcard, but Eira will learn that detail directly in Chapter 13. Marcus only knows "A. Schell" and the general delivery address.
- Marcus now has the name "A. Schell" — this connects to what he finds in Bangor in August 1944 (Chapter 19). The Drafter should ensure that Marcus doesn't rush to use this; it sits in him, unresolved, until the Bangor trip.
- The word *heimisch*: Marcus thinks it, does not speak it. This is a repeat from Chapter 03 (where it appears in his narration); the repetition is intentional — it is the word his displacement always returns to.
- The two-suit detail: Saturday, he uses the older suit, which is technically the Tuesday/Thursday suit. He is using the schedule loosely on weekends. The Drafter can note this as a small act of loosening in a man who holds himself carefully.
