# Chapter 19 — August

**One-line summary**: Marcus takes the bus to Bangor, finds August Schell's name in shipping notices at the public library, and rides home holding the knowledge alone.

**POV**: Marcus Klein  
**Tense**: Third-person limited, past  
**Setting**: The bus Machias–Bangor; Bangor public library; the bus home  
**Time**: August 1944 — a weekday during the summer recess

---

## Goal
Marcus wants to find whatever record exists of the shipping inquiry around the October 1943 body — specifically to see if "A. Schell" from the postcard appears in the maritime record.

## Obstacle
Library records are incomplete; he's not sure what he's looking for precisely or which archive to approach. He is also not sure, the whole bus ride up, why he is doing this — what use the knowledge will be to him, what right he has to pursue it.

---

## Action Beats

- Marcus boards the Machias bus at 7:15 a.m., arrives Bangor at noon. He has his lunch in a brown paper bag from Lillian — a routine she established without being asked, for his long-day trips. The bus is half-empty: a few farming families, a soldier on leave, an old man who sleeps the whole way.
- He thinks, on the bus, about what he is doing. Ada Gile gave him the name "A. Schell" in March. He has sat with it for five months. He doesn't know why this autumn he decided to go find it in a library rather than continue sitting with it. He considers several explanations and finds none of them quite adequate.
- The Bangor public library: a good building, well-stocked. He knows how to work a library; he has worked in far better ones. He goes to the periodicals room and asks for the Bangor Daily News on microfilm, October through November 1943.
- He searches the shipping notices — daily columns of maritime arrivals and departures, vessel names, ports of origin. Portland arrivals, October. He finds the SS *Ångermanälven* in the Portland shipping column: arrived October 7, Portland; departed October 12, Portland, bound Halifax. Swedish merchant vessel.
- He searches the November issues for the missing crew report. He finds it in the seamen's bureau notices, mid-November 1943: *SS Ångermanälven reports crew member missing — August Schell, German national, Swedish citizen, age approximately 40, missing from vessel during passage October 10–12, 1943.*
- He sits with this for a moment.
- He takes a note in his Kurrent script — not a summary, just the key facts: name, vessel, dates, "German national, Swedish citizen."
- He returns the microfilm. He goes to the reading room for an hour and reads a journal article about medieval Bavarian dialects that he has been meaning to read since January. It is exactly as interesting as he expected.
- The bus home: he has the Penobscot River at the window for twenty minutes south of Bangor, and the August light on it is the kind of light that makes you think a country is beautiful, which it is, alongside everything else it is.
- He thinks about August Schell. German national, Swedish citizen. Age approximately 40 in 1943 — which means born approximately 1903, which means he would have been thirty-five when the Anschluss happened, thirty-seven when France fell, thirty-nine when Marcus arrived in Coldwater Reach. The arithmetic of a life displaced.
- A German-born man who became a Swedish citizen and sailed neutral merchant vessels through wartime seas. A postcard in his name, sent from Portland to a small Maine fishing town, with a date and a time. He came for the meeting. He drowned in a storm. Nobody knows what happened on the pier.
- Marcus thinks: he was not a spy. He was not an agent. He was a man who got out of Germany before the war made getting out impossible, and made himself useful as a sailor on a neutral ship, and came to a meeting in Coldwater Reach on the night of October 9, 1943, and died.
- Marcus knows this with the certainty of someone who has thought about it very carefully, not with the certainty of someone who has evidence. He does not confuse the two. He tells no one what he found.

---

## Outcome / Turn
Marcus now knows August Schell's full probable identity: German national, Swedish citizen, crew of the SS *Ångermanälven*. He has arrived at this independently from Eira, who received the same information from Commander Whitmore in July. Both characters now hold the probable name; neither knows the other holds it.

## New Question or Pressure Raised
Why does Marcus say nothing? What would it mean to tell someone — Eira? Henry? The Coast Guard? The chapter does not answer this; Marcus himself cannot fully answer it.

---

## Continuity Notes
- Bangor public library: a real institution. Period-accurate; good periodicals collection.
- The *Bangor Daily News* shipping notices: historically accurate format for Maine coastal papers. Arrival and departure listings appeared regularly in maritime columns.
- The SS *Ångermanälven*: Portland, October 7–12. The missing crew report filed mid-November. This is consistent with the naval inspector's account in Chapter 16.
- August Schell: German national, Swedish citizen. Born approximately 1903. This timeline is consistent with Marcus's arithmetic in the chapter.
- Marcus's Kurrent note: he writes in his natural script when not performing for others. The note is taken in shorthand; he does not transcribe it into anything that could be found.
- Marcus's reasoning about Schell — "not a spy, not an agent, a man who got out of Germany" — is not a conclusion from evidence but a reading of the arithmetic of displacement. He is projecting his own experience partially, and he knows this; the narration should acknowledge the projection without endorsing it as fact.
- "He tells no one what he found": this is the key turn. The question is not why he found it (curiosity, the pull of a name, the professional instinct of a philologist to track a word until it has a referent) but why he keeps it. The chapter should resist a clean answer.
- The medieval Bavarian dialects article: not symbolic. Marcus reads it because he wants to and it is good. This is his version of Eira going to check the mooring after hard news.
