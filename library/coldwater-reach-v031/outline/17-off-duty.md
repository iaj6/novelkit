# Chapter 17 — Off Duty

**One-line summary**: Karl earns limited off-duty movement; he and Hannah walk the fence line together and exchange real conversation for the first time.

**POV**: Hannah Pfeiffer  
**Tense**: Third-person limited, past  
**Setting**: Pfeiffer farm: the fence line, the pasture perimeter  
**Time**: July 1944 — a Sunday afternoon

---

## Goal
Hannah wants to understand Karl as something more than a figure at the fence — she wants the conversation that the word-cards have been building toward.

## Obstacle
The fence is still there — literally and in every other sense. Off-duty movement means he can walk a perimeter around the farm; it doesn't mean the ordinary rules have suspended. Lonzo Reed watches from his chair.

---

## Action Beats

- By the summer of 1944, Karl has been assessed for limited off-duty movement under the Geneva Convention's provisions for POW welfare — a bureaucratic process that takes months and produces a form, which Lonzo Reed countersigns with his own name as supervising guard. The practical result: on Sunday afternoons Karl may walk the perimeter of the farm property, within sight of the icehouse or the barn at all times.
- Hannah has known this for two weeks. She has thought about it for two weeks.
- Sunday: the farm is slower. The other POWs play cards inside the icehouse or sit in the July sun on the south side. Karl walks. He goes along the fence line that borders the upper pasture.
- Hannah is hanging laundry on the line near the barn — a real task, she is not manufacturing an occasion. Karl comes by.
- She says: "You want more words?" She holds up an imaginary card. He half-smiles — the most she has seen from him — and says, in careful English: "Maybe." He has a vocabulary now: tools, weather, farm animals, some numbers, a handful of complete sentences. He is not conversational but he is functional.
- They walk, both of them, along the fence line — she on the inside (the farm side), he on the perimeter. The fence between them is agricultural wire and wooden posts; it is about four feet high. The situation is absurd in a way that neither of them names.
- He asks about the sea again — not the way he asked before, but as a continuation of the existing conversation. He says: "You say three miles. You go there?"
- She says: "Yes. Since I was small."
- He says: "What does it look like."
- She thinks about how to describe it in terms that will make sense to someone who has only read about the sea. She says: "Very wide. The color changes. In the summer it is sometimes green, sometimes grey. In the storm it is white at the top of the water." She runs out of simple words. She says, in German: *Es riecht nach Salz.* (It smells of salt.)
- He nods. He says: "In Regensburg, the river smells of mud and of the ships." Then: "Here it is different."
- She says: "Yes." Then: "Your family lives by the river?"
- He says — slowly, choosing the English — "My father. His shop is near the river road. He is a butcher." He pauses. "Was. I think was. I do not know now."
- She understands: the war, the Allied bombing, the uncertainty of letters from a country being dismantled.
- She says: "My brother is in Italy. I don't always know what is true."
- He says: "Yes." *Ich auch.* He has used this phrase with her before; it is still exact.
- Lonzo Reed is visible from the fence line, reading something in his chair. He doesn't look up.
- They walk the rest of the perimeter slowly, without more words. At the far corner, their paths separate naturally — she back to the laundry, he back toward the icehouse. She notices, walking back, that the July light on the pasture has the particular quality of late afternoon, very golden, the kind that makes everything look like it's been placed intentionally. She doesn't think that the light means anything. She thinks that it looks like that.

---

## Outcome / Turn
Karl and Hannah have had a real conversation — incomplete, cross-lingual, conducted along a fence, but real. The connection is no longer mediated entirely by word-cards. Karl's uncertainty about his father, Hannah's about Rolf — they are held in the same hand for a moment.

## New Question or Pressure Raised
Karl has never seen the sea; he said so again. Hannah knows where the harbor is. The question is still not asked and not answered, but it is closer.

---

## Continuity Notes
- Limited off-duty movement: Geneva Convention provisions for POW welfare. Bureaucratically slow; Karl is approved by summer 1944. Lonzo Reed countersigns as supervising guard. This is historically accurate to how the POW program operated.
- Karl's English as of July 1944: functional (tools, weather, animals, numbers, some sentences) but not conversational. Nine months since arrival.
- Karl's father: a butcher in Regensburg, near the river road. This detail recurs in Chapter 28 (Karl's interior chapter). By winter 1944, the father is dead — Allied bombing. The Drafter should not foreshadow this explicitly; it should simply be present as uncertainty: "I do not know now."
- The fence: four-foot agricultural wire and wooden posts. It is always there; the absurdity of conducting friendship through a fence should be present in the narration without being remarked on editorially.
- *Es riecht nach Salz*: Hannah's shift to German when her English runs out is natural to her character. Karl understands.
- *Ich auch*: Karl uses this phrase again. It is his verbal tic in this relationship — the simplest claim of commonality. By this point it carries history.
- Rolf in Italy: still alive as of July 1944. Hannah receives V-Mail irregularly; she speaks about him with honest uncertainty.
- Lonzo not watching: this chapter begins the pattern of Lonzo's deliberate inattention. He is visible; he simply chooses not to see.
