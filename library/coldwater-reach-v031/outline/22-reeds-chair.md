# Chapter 22 — Reed's Chair

**One-line summary**: Lonzo finds Karl absent at the October headcount, marks him present, and waits in his chair in the dark until Karl comes back.

**POV**: Lonzo Reed  
**Tense**: Third-person limited, past  
**Setting**: The Pfeiffer farm icehouse; the yard; Lonzo's chair outside the icehouse  
**Time**: October 1944 — an evening (this is the same night as Chapter 23)

*Exceptional chapter — target 1,200–1,800 words*

---

## Goal
Lonzo is conducting the 9 p.m. headcount before lockup. His goal is to account for four men. He can account for three.

## Obstacle
The fourth bunk is empty. Lonzo knows what filing a genuine count would mean: Karl reported absent, the Bangor provost marshal notified, the farm's arrangement potentially terminated or reviewed, and Karl — and Hannah — in serious trouble. He is not supposed to care about this. He cares about it.

---

## Action Beats

- 9 p.m. Lonzo takes his lantern and does the icehouse round. He has done this every night since November 1943 — eleven months of the same circuit, the same three paces from the door to the first bunk, the same count. Three of the four men are in their bunks. He counts again. Three.
- He stands in the icehouse with the lantern. He can see the shapes clearly: the gray-bearded one asleep already; the big one reading the German-language Bible they were issued; the one with the bad knees on his side with his face to the wall. Karl's bunk: empty, blanket folded back, the butter-box lid with an English word on it on the crate that serves as his shelf.
- Lonzo takes out the headcount book. He writes, in the column for Karl: *present.* He dates and signs it.
- He goes back outside and sits in his chair. October: the nights come fast now; it is already full dark at 9. The farm is quiet except for the cows, which are restless in the cold. His knee is bad tonight.
- He thinks: he's known since July that something was happening between the girl and the youngest one. He has not exactly watched it; he has been present while not watching it, which is its own kind of watching. He has seen the word-cards. He has heard them talk through the fence — German mostly, which he doesn't understand, but the register of it was clear enough.
- He thinks about what it would cost to file the count accurately. The Bangor office would send someone. The arrangement would be reviewed. The Pfeiffers might lose the labor for the winter. Karl would be reassigned — to a camp, probably, with stricter supervision, which is not what Karl needs. He has watched Karl for eleven months; Karl is nineteen and was drafted and wants to fix fences and grow food and go home, same as all the boys who aren't supposed to want what they want.
- He thinks about the flask in his coat pocket. He thinks about the bad knee. He thinks about what useful looks like when you're sixty-one with a bad knee on a farm outside Coldwater Reach with a folding camp chair and a rifle you haven't fired at anything in two years.
- He waits. At 10:15 he hears footsteps coming down the path from the direction of the ridge road. Light footsteps, careful. Karl comes around the barn corner, slips through the icehouse door without looking at Lonzo.
- Lonzo notes the time: 10:15. He sits for another fifteen minutes. Then he goes in, does the late check, and locks up. Three men asleep; Karl in his bunk, eyes shut, breathing even.
- Lonzo goes to his room in the Pfeiffer barn. He lies on the cot. He thinks: what does it cost, what does it cost. He doesn't finish the sentence. He goes to sleep.

---

## Outcome / Turn
Lonzo has covered for Karl. He did it consciously, with full understanding of what he was doing and what it could cost him. The system's permeability is now deliberate, not accidental — this is not a guard who fell asleep; this is a man who decided.

## New Question or Pressure Raised
How long before someone else — from Bangor, from the provost marshal's office, from a neighbor — notices what Lonzo is not noticing? And what does it cost Lonzo to be this kind, this tired, this decent?

## Continuity Notes
- This chapter and Chapter 23 are the same night from different perspectives. October 1944. They must be consistent: Karl absent from headcount at 9 p.m.; returns at 10:15 p.m.; Lonzo marks him present throughout.
- Lonzo's bad knee: canon fact #28 (winch accident, 1937). It is worse in cold weather. October is the beginning of the hard cold.
- Lonzo's flask: established in canon (characters.md): "he has started, by the winter of 1944, bringing a flask." This is the chapter where his drinking is first named. He thinks about it; he doesn't drink from it in this scene.
- The butter-box lid with an English word on it: one of Hannah's word-cards, on Karl's shelf. Lonzo has seen them and knows what they are.
- Lonzo's assessment of Karl: not a fanatic, not a volunteer — a nineteen-year-old who was drafted and wants to go home. This is consistent with canon characterization of Karl.
- The headcount book: Lonzo's responsibility. He signs his name to the falsified count. This is the document that would incriminate him if anyone looked closely.
- "What does it cost, what does it cost": Lonzo's refrain for the chapter. He doesn't finish it because he doesn't know the answer and the answer may be nothing or may be everything. He doesn't find out in this story.
- Voice note: Lonzo's narration is "measured, dry, not given to complaint." He is not performing his tiredness or his kindness; he is simply there, in the chair, being what he is. The chapter should feel like sitting in a chair in October dark — still, a little cold, longer than comfortable.
