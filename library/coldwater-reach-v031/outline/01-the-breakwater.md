# Chapter 01 — The Breakwater

**One-line summary**: The body comes in after a three-day blow; Eira walks the breakwater, files her official report, and leaves one field blank.

**POV**: Eira Bowman  
**Tense**: Third-person limited, past  
**Setting**: The granite breakwater; the harbor office (converted pilot house, pier shed); the anchorage at Coldwater Reach  
**Time**: Dawn, Tuesday, approximately October 13, 1943 (three days after the storm began)

---

## Goal
Eira must document the body, manage the Coast Guard's arrival, and produce an official incident report — all within the scope of her authority as harbormaster.

## Obstacle
The Coast Guard takes the body and most of the control of the situation. What Eira knows privately — what she saw on the dock the night of October 9 — cannot be unknotted from her official role without admitting she withheld it from the log.

---

## Action Beats

- Chapter opens in motion: Eira is already on the breakwater, walking its granite spine toward the far end where two lobstermen stand looking down. First light. The storm has moved offshore; the bar is settling but not settled; there is still sea running under the fog.
- She reaches the far end of the breakwater. She registers what she sees with professional exactness: male, apparent age 35–45, maritime work clothes (oilskin over a wool sweater, rubber boots), no identifying documents visible, no vessel insignia. The body has been in the water for some time. The position suggests he came in on the eastern chop, not swept from the breakwater itself — he came from the anchorage side.
- A Coast Guard motor surfboat is already coming around the point from Machias: someone called ahead. Petty Officer Gervais takes charge of the scene. Eira gives him what she has (her physical observations); he thanks her with the particular efficiency of someone who considers her presence provisional.
- Eira goes back to the harbor office. She opens her incident log to the correct date. She writes what she observed. She leaves the field for *vessel traffic, evening prior* incomplete — marks it with a dash, which in her log means no anomalies noted.
- She knows the dash is not accurate. She remembers, without permitting herself to revisit it at length: October 9, after midnight, checking the mooring bridle on the coaster *Macomber* after the first gusts of the blow came through. A man at the end of the pier she did not recognize — canvas bag, standing still, looking at the water. She had meant to ask where he was berthed. Something on the mooring wanted her attention first. When she looked back he wasn't there. She assumed he had gone into the pier shed for shelter.
- She makes coffee in the office's tin percolator. She drinks it standing at the window overlooking the anchorage.
- The Coast Guard motor surfboat departs with the body. Gervais tells her she'll hear from the First District when they have anything.
- She rinses the cup, hangs it on the hook beside the one that has been there since 1935.

---

## Outcome / Turn
The body is gone. Eira's report is on file, incomplete. The question is already inside the harbor office, sitting in the dash she put where a record should be.

## New Question or Pressure Raised
What did she see on October 9, and what does the fact that she filed nothing mean — now that there is a body?

---

## Continuity Notes
- The body washes in approximately October 13, 1943 (three days after the storm's start October 10). Canon fact #5.
- Coast Guard Petty Officer Gervais, from the Machias station. (Named here for the Drafter; he is not a major character.)
- The coaster *Macomber*, skippered by Birch Macomber, was in harbor the night of October 9. Eira was checking its mooring bridle in the gusts.
- Eira saw a man — the man whose body came in — standing at the end of the pier on the night of October 9, canvas bag, looking at the water. She did not speak to him. She did not enter him in the vessel-traffic log.
- The second coffee cup on the hook: Owen's. Eira has not removed it. Canon character detail.
- The harbor office faces the anchorage; the chart cabinet is to the left of her desk.
- The dash in the vessel-traffic field: the narrative must treat this small falsification consistently across all subsequent Eira chapters. She did not write anything false — she left a blank. This is the fulcrum of her arc.
