# Chapter 29 — April

**One-line summary**: The case is officially closed on August Schell; Eira goes to the breakwater, then files her addendum in the permanent record.

**POV**: Eira Bowman  
**Tense**: Third-person limited, past  
**Setting**: Harbor office; the breakwater; harbor office again  
**Time**: April 1945 — a morning in the second or third week of April

---

## Goal
Eira's goal, after Whitmore's call, is to close the record properly — to move the addendum from Owen's pending drawer to where it belongs.

## Obstacle
There is no obstacle. This is arrival — quiet, not triumphant, the way most arrivals are when they have been coming for eighteen months.

---

## Action Beats

- A clear April morning. The ice has been out of the inner anchorage for three weeks; the lobster boats are running again. The buoy tender will put in by the end of the week on its first full spring run. Eira is at the desk with the weekly report when the telephone rings.
- Commander Whitmore, naval district: a brief call. The First District has processed the case of the October 1943 unidentified decedent, Coldwater Reach. Cross-referenced with the SS *Ångermanälven* missing crew report, August Schell, German national, Swedish citizen. The district is satisfied that the two records refer to the same individual. The case will be listed as: *August Schell, lost at sea, North Atlantic coastal waters, October 1943. Accidental drowning. Case closed.* She'll receive written notice in a week.
- Eira thanks him. She writes the name and the determination in the official harbor log, beside the incident number from October 1943. *August Schell. Lost at sea. Accidental drowning. Case closed, April 1945.*
- She puts the pen down.
- She goes to the chart cabinet. Owen's pending drawer. She takes out the folded addendum — the sheet she wrote in September 1944. She unfolds it and reads it once. She folds it again.
- She takes out the *Macomber* entry from her personal log — the pages she has filled across eighteen months, from November 1943 to the July 1944 inspector's visit. She reads them through.
- She walks to the breakwater. April morning, the air still carrying winter, the sea very blue in the April light. She walks to the far end of the breakwater — the place where the body came in, where she stood that October morning watching the Coast Guard take him away.
- She stands there for a few minutes. She is not thinking about what she should have done, or what she could have done. She is noting: this is where she was, this is where he was, this is what happened, and none of it is changeable, and she is still here.
- She walks back.
- At the harbor office she goes to the permanent files cabinet — the bottom drawer, where closed cases go. She files the addendum behind the tab marked *Schell, August — Incident October 1943.* She files her personal log pages with it. She closes the drawer.
- She makes coffee. She drinks it.
- The buoy tender comes in sight around the point, the lighthouse service flag clear in the April sun. She picks up the vessel arrival log and goes to the dock to meet it.

---

## Outcome / Turn
Eira has closed the record — officially and privately. The addendum is in the permanent file, honest and complete, where it belongs. She has not been deposed; she has not confessed; she has not been vindicated. She has done what she could do with what she had. She goes to the dock.

## New Question or Pressure Raised
None explicitly raised. One unresolved thread remains: what happened on the pier between August Schell and whatever circumstances led to his being in the water — whether an accident, a fall, a fight — is not in the record and will not be. Orcutt's pressure on the harbor post is not resolved; Eira will face it again. The story does not show this — it ends here, in April, with the buoy tender coming around the point.

---

## Continuity Notes
- Commander Whitmore (naval district, First District Portland) — re-appears from Chapter 16. He calls; the determination is administrative, not dramatic.
- The case determination: *August Schell, lost at sea, North Atlantic coastal waters, October 1943. Accidental drowning.* This is the official record. It is not a lie; it is the best the evidence supports. Whether there was another person involved (Macomber? someone else?) is not addressed by the case determination and remains unknown.
- The addendum: written September 1944, filed in Owen's pending drawer. Moved to permanent record April 1945. Filed under *Schell, August — Incident October 1943.* The addendum is now part of the official record — but "official" means accessible if sought, not broadcast. No one is looking for it; it may never be read by anyone other than Eira.
- The breakwater: Eira goes to the far end — the specific place where the body came in. The chapter should not sentimentalize this; she goes there because it is where the thing happened and she wants to stand where it happened. She doesn't throw anything in. She doesn't say anything. She stands and then leaves.
- "She is still here": not stated in narration — rendered through her action. She notes the geography of what happened, not as absolution but as fact. Then she goes back.
- The coffee: she drinks it. This is consistent with Chapter 20's coffee detail (she drinks it when something has settled).
- The buoy tender arriving: the chapter closes on the next task. Eira goes to meet the tender with the vessel arrival log. The work continues. This is the chapter's final image.
- Owen's pending drawer: now emptied of the addendum and personal log pages, except for his two original files (*Lighthouse Service correspondence, 1934* and *Tidal survey, pending, 1935*). Those stay.
- Howie Beal: he has come home (December 1944) and the pressure from Orcutt continues, but this is not in the chapter. Eira's holding of the post is not resolved in the narrative — the story ends before that is settled. This is one of the deliberately unresolved threads.
