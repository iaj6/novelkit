# Chapter 26 — The Last Winter

**One-line summary**: Karl learns his father died in Regensburg; Hannah holds the news alone; her mother begins to speak again in small sentences.

**POV**: Hannah Pfeiffer  
**Tense**: Third-person limited, past  
**Setting**: Pfeiffer farm: the fence, the barn, the farmhouse kitchen  
**Time**: January 1945

---

## Goal
Hannah wants to give Karl somewhere to put his grief — not a solution, but a witness. She does not know this is what she wants; she finds out by doing it.

## Obstacle
The grief belongs to Karl and she has no right to claim it; she can only receive it. And receiving it means holding another secret, one heavier than the October night.

---

## Action Beats

- January: the farm in deep cold. The inner anchorage is frozen (she knows this from town talk); the cows haven't been out in two weeks; the pasture is iron-hard. The icehouse is cold even with the sawdust insulation — she can see the POWs' breath when the door opens.
- Karl has not spoken to her at the fence in four days. Not avoidance; he comes to the fence when the work brings him there, but he doesn't linger and he doesn't look at her fully. She notices the difference without naming it.
- A morning in the second week of January: she is bringing the feed bucket to the fence. Karl comes to take it. He says nothing for a moment. Then, in German, formally — not the abbreviated German of their fence-line exchanges but the careful German of serious speech: *Mein Vater ist gestorben.* My father has died.
- She looks at him. He holds the bucket.
- She says: *Das tut mir leid.* I'm sorry. It is the German for condolence — literally, "that does me sorrow" — and she has said it before, in small circumstances. Now she means the whole weight of it.
- He nods. He doesn't say more about it. He takes the bucket to the feed trough. She goes back to the barn.
- She doesn't tell anyone. Not at dinner, where her father eats and goes to the radio and her mother has begun, in the past week, to say small things: about the cellar's root supply, about the weather, about whether the pipes in the barn will freeze tonight. Small sentences, offered without inflection, like a person testing whether the habit of speech still works. Hannah answers them: *The carrots will last to March. It will be colder tomorrow. The barn pipes should be fine if you leave the tap running.*
- She doesn't tell her father about Karl's father. The two men spoke in November — her father walked out to where Karl was splitting wood, and they talked in German for twenty minutes while Hannah watched from the kitchen window, unable to hear anything. She doesn't know what they said. She knows only that her father came back inside without his usual expression of containment and poured himself a cup of water at the sink and stood there for a while.
- At night: she thinks about what Karl has now. The letter from the Red Cross POW mail system said his father died December 28, 1944. Probably the Allied bombing of Regensburg — the city has been hit several times. She knows this from the radio. She doesn't know if Karl knows how his father died; she doesn't ask.
- She thinks: she has Rolf. He is in Italy, still alive as of the last letter in November, though the letters are irregular now. She has her father, who won't speak English but speaks constantly in German about the farm. She has her mother, who has started saying small things again, which is not nothing. She has something Karl does not have, and she is holding his grief not because she was asked to but because it landed on the fence between them and someone had to take it.
- She thinks about the word *Bürde* — burden, in German. Her father's word, which she has only heard him use once, about the war. *Die Bürde trägt sich nicht allein.* The burden does not carry itself alone.

---

## Outcome / Turn
Karl has told Hannah his father is dead, and Hannah is holding it. Her mother's returning voice is a separate but parallel movement — something that was locked has begun to open. The chapter ends with Hannah in the long winter dark, holding what was given to her.

## New Question or Pressure Raised
What does Magda's returning speech mean for the household? When will the war end, and what will that mean for Karl, for the farm, for what Hannah has chosen?

## Continuity Notes
- Friedrich Reinmar (Karl's father): died December 28, 1944, in Regensburg. Cause unstated in the notification but probably the Allied bombing of Regensburg (the city was bombed multiple times in 1944–1945). Canon continuity note: Karl's father is dead. This recurs in Chapter 28 (Karl's interior chapter).
- The Red Cross POW mail system: the means by which Karl receives the notification. This is historically accurate — the ICRC coordinated mail for POWs.
- Magda Pfeiffer: "has begun to speak again" in January 1945. Canon continuity note from Chapter 00 map: her silence has eased by this point. She says small sentences about weather, the cellar, the pipes. She does not explain why she is speaking again; the Drafter should not explain it either. Something has loosened.
- Albrecht and Karl's November conversation: mentioned briefly. Their twenty-minute conversation was in German, private, not witnessed by Hannah. What they said is not disclosed. The fact that it happened is important: Albrecht has connected with Karl directly in some way, man to man. Hannah was excluded. This exclusion is part of the chapter.
- *Die Bürde trägt sich nicht allein*: Hannah's German interior. The burden does not carry itself alone — a phrase she has heard her father use. It arrives in her thinking here because it is the structure she is enacting: taking Karl's burden and carrying part of it.
- Rolf: last V-Mail November 1944. He is in Italy, alive, but letters are irregular. Hannah does not know his current status precisely. He will survive the war (the story does not resolve his return, but his survival is implied).
- The Allied bombing of Regensburg: historically accurate. Regensburg was bombed by the USAAF in 1943 and 1944, targeting the Messerschmitt factory. By late 1944, general Allied bombing of German cities was ongoing.
