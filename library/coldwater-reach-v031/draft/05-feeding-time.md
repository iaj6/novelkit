# Feeding Time

The cows knew her by her boots. The sound she made crossing the barn floor in the dark — the flat drag of rubber soles on frozen board — distinguished her from her father, who moved in wooden-soled clogs that knocked against the concrete lip of the gutter; distinguished her from the barn itself, settling in the cold. She didn't need a lamp for the five o'clock feeding. Her hands knew the latch, the hook, the weight of the pail before her eyes adjusted.

The smell in November was particular. In summer the barn aired through the big east doors; in November it concentrated: hay, the animal heat of twenty dairy cows packed close, the sharp undertone of manure from the previous evening's cleanup. Not unpleasant — she had grown up with it the way you grew up with the smell of your own house, not smelling it anymore except when she tried to smell it deliberately. Her mother still had a faint bewilderment on her face when someone from town wrinkled their nose at the dooryard. After twenty years you would think she would have stopped being surprised.

Five o'clock. She worked the alley from south to north. Else was always restless in the cold and needed the bucket first; she was liable to start in on the next cow's feed if left to wait, and the next cow was Hilde, who would not stand for it. Then down the line to Berta, who was patient the way some animals were patient, who turned her head to look at nothing in particular when Hannah came with the pail. She put her hand flat on Berta's flank as she went by. The cow breathed — a sound like a slow bellows, warm and even.

She was thinking: three weeks, almost four.

Three weeks since the truck came in the afternoon with the four men in the back. The mornings had a new shape to them now — not in the tasks themselves, which were the same tasks they had been since she was tall enough to carry a pail, but in the fact of something she was tracking at the edge of her attention. The sound of the icehouse door some mornings before light. The Army truck at seven when the sky had color but no light yet. She had not been sleeping as solidly as she usually slept, and she put that down to November.

She had been watching.

---

The truck came at seven, when the frost on the gatepost caught what light there was. The three older men went north to the pasture fence; she could see them go from the south side of the barn, three figures past the equipment shed with the guard walking wide of the group. They had been at the fence for three weeks now. The work was good, her father had said, once, at dinner — the only favorable thing she had heard him say about any of it.

Karl got the yard.

Manure hauling: the wheelbarrow, the pile at the far end of the working yard, back and forth. She had known from the first week that this was how it worked. The worst job went to the youngest. On farms and presumably everywhere else.

Between the third and fourth boards of the south barn wall there was a gap that she had found in those first days, narrow enough to keep out most of the cold and wide enough to see the working yard clearly. It was lower than the east wall gap, shoulder height when she stood, and it looked out onto the full south run of the yard: the gate in the fence that divided the barnyard from the working field, the icehouse at the near edge of her sight, Lonzo Reed in his folding chair. She had not told herself she was looking for a way to watch him. She had just found the gap and found herself using it.

He moved without complaint. This was the thing she had settled on early, as a fact she could rely on: he did not complain, or at least he gave nothing to the yard that looked like complaint. He navigated the frozen rut near the barn door at the same angle each time — she had counted it, five mornings and six and eight — the angle that kept the wheel from catching in the ice that had formed over the rut's lowest point. He had worked it out in the first two days and kept to it without varying. The approach, tested, arrived at, maintained.

What she noticed in the second week was that the seams of his jacket had been let out. The cloth ran smooth across his shoulders now — before it had pulled and bunched when he lifted anything — and the sleeves came to his wrists instead of riding up. She had not seen her mother take the jacket; she had not been present for any of it. But the stitch was Magda's. The same straight-line stitch on every garment in this house that had needed mending in the last twenty years: the trousers of Rolf's that she had worn through at the knee two winters ago, her father's work shirts, the curtain at the kitchen window that had torn at the rod pocket in the spring. Magda's stitch: regular, close, functional, no backstitch at the ends because she said backstitch wasted thread and made a ridge. Her mother had done the work without being asked and said nothing about it after. Hannah did not say anything about it either. These were the things her mother did when she had run out of other ways to do something.

Lonzo Reed sat in his folding chair beside the icehouse door. His bad knee kept him from walking circuits the way a younger man might, and so he sat and the yard was in his sight and most mornings he had something to read — a newspaper folded to a quarter of its size, or one of the small pamphlets the Coastal Patrol issued to its people. Some mornings he just sat. He did not seem like a man who minded sitting.

---

The gate in the south fence was for equipment: wide enough for the tractor when the tractor ran, which was less often since the gas ration. She used it every morning to carry the grain bucket to the calf pen on the other side. The bucket was metal, with a metal bail, and the bail was cold through her work gloves in November — the particular cold that came through metal in this weather, which felt almost deliberate, as if the temperature had a specific grievance with her palms.

On this morning it was cold enough that her breath sat in front of her face for a second before dispersing. The puddle beside the gate post had a serious quarter-inch of ice on it: not the overnight skim but a sheet that had been accumulating for several days, gray and bubbled in the center where the water had been deep, clear-edged where it had been shallow. She stood at the gate with the bucket in her right hand and looked at the ice.

She was tired from the milking. The bucket was heavy. The calf pen was thirty feet away, on the other side, and she stood at the gate and did not open it and looked at the ice on the puddle instead.

She set the bucket down on her side of the gate post.

Karl came around the corner of the barn with the wheelbarrow.

She had not planned this. He was just there, coming around the corner in his let-out jacket with his arms braced against the handles of the loaded barrow, navigating the frozen rut at the angle he had settled on in the second day. He saw her standing at the gate with the bucket at her feet and he stopped.

He set the wheelbarrow handles down. He came to the fence — to the post on his side — and reached over and picked up the bucket by the bail. He carried it to the gate post and set it at the top of the rail, where she could reach it from her side. He did not push it through the gate or hand it to her over the fence. He placed it where she could take it, at the post, and stepped back.

The right way to do it. She noticed that it was the right way to do it, and she noticed that she had noticed, and she did not know what to do with someone who understood that.

He said: *Da, bitte.*

She said: *Danke.*

The word came out before she had decided to say it, the way a word came out when you had owned it since before you were old enough to make decisions about words. She heard herself say it and then there was a silence in which Karl was looking at her with an expression she had not seen on him yet — something going still in his face, settling. The German coming from this girl with the pails. He had not known she had it.

She reached over and took the bucket handle.

She said — because she had been carrying this sentence for three weeks, since the night she stood at Rolf's east window looking at the dark icehouse, since the night she lay in Rolf's bed with the geography book open to the map of Bavaria, her finger traveling the distance between two dots on a river —

*Mein Vater kommt aus Bayern.*

She said it haltingly. With the grammar her father used, which was the only grammar she had for German — the grammar of a language that had reached her secondhand, through someone else's mouth. She was not certain about the verb even as she said it. She heard the faults in it the way you heard the faults in something that had seemed correct in rehearsal.

Karl stood on his side of the fence with his hands back on the wheelbarrow handles. He looked at her.

He said: *Ich auch.*

Then he picked up the barrow and went back across the yard.

---

Lonzo Reed was ten yards away. He was bent over his left boot, working at the lacing with one finger, trying to get the tongue settled right beneath the top eyelet. He straightened and resettled the rifle across his knees and looked at the icehouse. He had not been watching.

Hannah carried the bucket to the calf pen and fed the calves and came back and did what came next, in order, thoroughly. She did not drop anything.

She was thinking about *auch*.

It was the most ordinary word — it appeared in sentences the way punctuation appeared, so commonly you stopped registering it separately. *Das auch*, this too. *Noch einmal, und auch das*, once more, and that as well. Her father used it constantly in the German he spoke at home, the auditing word, the word for checking things off a running total. But Karl had said it alone. *Ich auch.* Two words. A complete sentence. The most compressed possible way of saying: I am in that category also. Without naming the category. Without saying what it held.

She had named the category: *father from Bavaria*.

Her father was from Straubing. She had always known this the way you knew facts from before your own life — as a thing that had happened somewhere else, to someone you had not yet met. She had looked it up years ago in the geography book: Straubing on the Danube, a small dot in the curve of the river. She had run her finger west to Regensburg and back again. Not very far. Two cities on the same river, or near the same river — she was not precise about the river's exact route, only that her father had once said the Danube went past his door, or close enough to past his door, and she had filed this as a fact about a place she had never seen.

Karl was from the same river.

Her father had said it in the second week of the POWs' arrival, at dinner when the lights were low and he was finishing his beer: *Regensburg ist nicht weit.* Regensburg is not far. Said to the air, or to the beer, or to no one at all — not as a statement directed at anyone at the table, just a fact he appeared to be noting to himself. Hannah had heard it. Her mother had probably heard it. Neither of them had said anything. Her father had drunk the rest of the beer and gone to check the cows.

*Ich auch.* He had placed himself beside her father with two words, the gentlest way to do it — not *I am from Bavaria* with the full declaration, just *also me*, that one word doing everything.

She thought about her father's voice when he spoke German at home and the German was from somewhere specific, not the everyday German of instructions but the German that came from further back. There was a register to it she had been listening for since she was small — a loosening of something, a running-together of syllables that didn't run together in the everyday version. She couldn't have said what words. Only that the language her father used for the farm and the house was not entirely the same language he used when he was speaking from a place she hadn't been. Karl was fifty kilometers from that place. He had his own version of it, whatever it was.

She thought: and neither of them will tell me anything about it.

She thought: *auch*. Ten words across a fence this morning. Which was ten more than the house had managed in a month.

---

At noon her father came in from the north pasture. The heavy tread on the floorboard, the cold off his coat as he passed. He was up since four; she knew this not because she had seen him but because the creak of the bedroom floor at four was part of the morning the same way Else's impatience was part of the morning, the same way the cold bucket bail was.

He had mud on his knees and his face was the face of a man who had been outside for six hours in cold weather: closed down, used up in the specific way of outdoor work that left the body functional and quiet. He served himself from the pot without sitting, ate at the counter standing up, which he had been doing since Rolf left and which Hannah understood now had nothing to do with hurry, and rinsed the bowl in the washbasin, and went back out.

He did not say anything.

Her mother was at the stove. The kitchen smelled the way it had smelled every day since October: long-simmered broth with root vegetables, parsnip and carrot and something beneath it, the last ghost of the ham bone that had been gone for weeks. A patient smell. A smell that asked nothing.

Her mother stirred.

No one said anything.

Hannah sat at the table with her bowl and her cup of tea and the warmth coming back slowly into her hands from the morning's cold. She looked at her mother's back, the motion of her arm, the wooden spoon making its circles. She looked at the empty place at the counter where her father had been standing.

She thought: *Deutsch* and English both, in this house, twenty years of both, her father's Bavarian and her mother's Bavarian and the English they had built together word by word from neighbors and the radio and the town. All of that, all those words in two languages, and at noon the kitchen was as quiet as a room that had no one in it.

She thought: *auch.*

She washed the bowl and went back out to the barn. The ice on the puddle beside the gate post was still there. The sun was low and it had not reached the north side of the post, where the puddle sat in the shadow. It would be there at five o'clock too. It would be there tomorrow morning.

She went inside the barn. The cows knew she was back by her boots.
