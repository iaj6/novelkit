# Mud Season

The mud pulled at her boots with each step — a slow, reluctant suction that was nothing like the good solid footing of November or the brittle crust of February, but the April kind, the deep-thaw kind, the earth still cold below and moving above, each footfall leaving a pool behind it that collected the gray pre-dawn light. She had misjudged the milk pail on the first crossing and had to come back for the handle with both hands. By the time she reached the barn the knee-height of her barn pants was dark and the skin of cold water had already found the sock.

The cows knew. They were restless in a way they hadn't been in weeks — Hilde pressing her nose against the partition between her stall and Else's, the others shifting and lowing in the complaint that was less about hunger than about the smell coming through the boards. They wanted grass. They knew April was here even if the ground hadn't sorted itself out yet. Vater had gone to the north pasture two days ago and come back without speaking, which meant the pasture had given somewhere it shouldn't, which meant the cows were staying in, which meant the barn smell was winter that had gone on too long and didn't know it yet.

She worked the milking in the usual order. Hilde, then Else, then the three others along the west wall. Her hands knew the work and her mind went where it went, which in the early morning before full light was usually nowhere in particular. The milk came in a good stream and the warmth of it traveled up through the pail into her forearms when she braced them, and that was the one warm thing about April before sunrise.

She finished and carried the pails to the separator and stood in the barn door watching the east ridge. The light was starting somewhere above it — still gray, still mostly dark, but different from a week ago. Some quality of almost.

Today she was eighteen.

---

Her mother had set the plate before anyone came down.

It was a cake in the way you could call it that — flat, pale, the color of the inside of a winter potato, which was to say it had no icing because there was no sugar for icing, and the texture when she cut it was somewhere between dense bread and the thing it was trying to be. There was a hint of sweetness in the smell. Whatever Mutti had found to sweeten it — the last of the powdered milk, something put by — had worked partway.

Her father looked at the cake. Then he looked at her.

*Herzlichen Glückwunsch, Hannchen.*

Hannchen. She had been too old for Hannchen since she was twelve and she knew it the way she knew which boards in the upstairs hall would creak if you stepped on the left side rather than the right — perfectly, without minding. He had been calling her Hannchen since before she could remember him calling her anything else.

*Danke, Vater.*

She ate the cake. It was not bad. It was dry and only faintly sweet and the texture was wrong, but it was cake in the sense that counted: her mother had made it and set it on the plate and it was on the table in the morning like proof of a thing that didn't require saying aloud. Her mother sat with her coffee and said nothing, which was usual. What was different was the angle of her — a slight orientation toward Hannah rather than away, the quality of the silence altered by a degree or two from its ordinary direction.

Hannah finished the cake and washed the plate and put her coat on.

---

The road to school was the worst part. Three miles each way, and today the mud had reached the center of the road, the frozen crown giving up all at once, and there was no safe line along the verge because the verge had gone soft and the shoulder was worse. She walked the edge of the wheel-ruts and placed her feet with care and it still added twenty minutes to the walk, and she arrived at school with the hem of her skirt three inches darker than when she left.

She hung her coat and went in to second row left.

Marcus Klein came in with his usual precision — the good suit, which meant it was a Friday, a German-class day — set his notebook on the lectern, and turned to the board. He wrote without preamble. Today the unit was legal and civic vocabulary: *Bürger*, *Staatsbürgerschaft*, *Wahlrecht*. They had been in it since Monday. Hannah could parse these words; she couldn't quite feel them. They were German shapes for American ideas, formal and correct, vocabulary she would need for something she hadn't done yet.

Marcus wrote one word on the board:

*mündig*

He turned and looked at the class with the look he had when something genuinely interested him — not performed, not the brisk satisfaction of a lesson proceeding as planned, but the look of a man who had been thinking about a word for some time and was glad to have occasion to say why.

"This word," he said. "It is the condition of being of age. Of having majority. The legal and civic state of adulthood." He paused the way he paused when he wanted the next thing to have room to arrive. "But look at its root."

He wrote beside it:

*Mund*

"Mouth," he said. "To be *mündig* is literally to have your own mouth. To speak for yourself in the record — without a guardian entering your name on your behalf, without another voice claiming to represent you. The person who is not *mündig* cannot speak in their own name. They require an intermediary, an appointed voice. To become *mündig* is to become the one who speaks for yourself."

He moved on to the next term on the lesson sheet. Curtis Aiken in the front row wrote quickly, systematically, the way he approached everything. The two girls from Harlow Street compared their notebooks. Hannah wrote in the margin of hers, beneath the row of civic vocabulary she'd copied from the board:

*mündig — having your own mouth*

She looked at what she'd written.

The word-cards she left on the fence post were six inches by two, cedar planks from the butter boxes, English on the left and German on the right, a ruled pencil line down the center. Barn and Scheune. Gate and Tor. Harbor and Hafen. They were about naming things: the right German word for the world of the farm, the right English word for the world Karl was learning. But *mündig* was not about naming things. *Mündig* was about whose voice the naming came from.

She was eighteen today. *Hannchen* was too old for *Hannchen* and Vater still called her that, and Marcus Klein had written the German word for the thing she was becoming on the board, and the root of it was *Mund*.

She underlined what she had written and turned the page.

---

She heard the post-driving before she reached the fence — the dull percussion of a sledge on post-stock, the half-second carry between blows, a rhythm she could have set her watch by if she'd had one. She had not planned to stop at the fence. She stopped.

Karl had shed his jacket and was working the line of posts along the near edge of the north field, not his usual south yard assignment but spring work, the fence-repair work that came out when the ground softened enough to take posts. He worked the twelve-pound sledge with both hands, raising it square and bringing it down on the post-cap with the accuracy of someone who has learned to let the weight do the labor rather than fighting it. The post took it and went in another inch. He raised the sledge again.

She said, in English: "Today I am eighteen."

He set the sledge down — handle against the post, the motion unhurried — and looked at her. She could see the English working through him: *today, I, am, eighteen.*

"I see," he said.

Then, in German: *Glückwunsch.*

Not *Herzlichen Glückwunsch* — not the full ceremony. Just *Glückwunsch.* Said without decoration, in the tone he had for things he meant: not dressed up, just accurate. The same tone he'd used for *Ich auch* in November.

She nodded. She walked home.

---

The V-Mail was on the table.

She knew it from the door — the small folded form, the particular paper, the slightly blurred quality of the reproduced microfilm that all V-Mail had, which always made the handwriting look like itself at a small remove, slightly different from the actual hand and yet precisely recognizable. Ada must have brought it while Hannah was at school. Ada delivered things like this herself when she could.

Hannah washed her hands at the kitchen basin and sat down and opened it.

*Dear Hannah —*

*We are in Italy now, which is difficult to describe. The weather here has been cold and wet, but it is improving. I am well. I am sorry to miss your birthday. You are eighteen — I cannot picture it.*

*The potatoes — have they come up yet? By the time this reaches you it should be the right time. Tell Vati the north corner is slow. He'll know why.*

*Give my love to Mutti and Vati. Tell them I think about the farm.*

*Alles Gute, dein Rolf*

He had written her name at the top of the letter in the hand she recognized from every letter he'd sent — the letters slightly larger than they needed to be, the r in Hannah rounded like he was drawing it, the H at the crossbar a little uncertain. He put potatoes in his letters home. He thought about the north corner. He thought about the farm.

She read it again.

She put it back on the table.

Her mother came in from the hall. She saw the letter on the table and stopped. She came to the table and picked it up with both hands — carefully, the way you pick up something breakable — and sat in her chair and read. Her face did not change. Mutti's face when she read Rolf's letters was the same face she kept for the rest of the house: gathered inward, careful, closed to the outside. But she read slowly, more slowly than the letter required, and when she reached the end she did not put it down. She held it in her lap. Her hands flat on either side of the paper, not pressing, resting. The letter and her hands and her eyes down.

After a long moment she folded it, once, and placed it back on the table.

Her father came in from outside. He looked at the letter, then at Hannah.

"From Rolf," she said. "He's well."

Vater took the letter. He stood at the table and held it with both hands, didn't sit, didn't move to sit. He held it the way he held anything that required holding. He did not unfold it. He stood there long enough that Hannah thought he wasn't going to read it at all, and then he sat down and held it in his lap and looked at the table.

That was how they ate supper.

---

After she washed the dishes she took Rolf's pen and the V-Mail form from the desk drawer and sat down at the table.

*Dear Rolf —*

*Today is the fourteenth, which you know. I am eighteen.*

*The potatoes are not up yet. Maybe next week if it warms. The mud this year is something — every path from the barn a problem, and Vati says the north pasture can't hold the cows yet, so the barn smell is something, too. Mutti made a cake for the birthday. No icing, the sugar wasn't there for it, but she found something sweet enough to count, and it was a cake.*

*The farm is the same. Mutti and Vati are well.*

*Alles Gute —*

*Hannah*

She didn't write: Marcus Klein said *mündig* today and I wrote it in the margin of my notebook and thought about it for most of the afternoon. She didn't write: Karl said *Glückwunsch* at the fence and I went home.

She folded the form and left it on the counter to post in the morning.

She took Rolf's coat from the hook by the door. It still smelled of him somewhere under the months — hay and cold air and the wool of the barn — or maybe that was the coat's own smell by now, her smell, after all the mornings she'd worn it. The sleeves came down past her wrists. She put it on and went outside.

---

The yard was wet and April-dark, the ridge above the farm a black line against a sky gone from full dark to the deep blue that came before light without being light yet. The ground was cold through her boots but not frozen. The air moved. It had a smell that was not quite mud and not quite anything green, but the seam between them — the smell of the thing that was about to begin.

The ridge was dark. Rolf was in Italy. Her parents were inside with the folded letter.

She stood in the yard until the cold found the gap between the coat collar and her neck, and then she went in.
