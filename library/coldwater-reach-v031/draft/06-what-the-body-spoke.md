# What the Body Spoke

The message had been slipped under his classroom door, on school letterhead, in Mrs. Farwell's handwriting: *Mr. Fenn would appreciate five minutes at half-past eight, when convenient.* Marcus arrived at twenty-five past. It was a Tuesday, and he was wearing the older suit.

Fenn's office occupied the northeast corner of the building. Two windows looked north toward the ash trees, bare by now, and between their branches a band of sky the color of old pewter. Fenn stood when Marcus entered, which was his habit, and sat again once Marcus had taken the chair across the desk.

"Dr. Klein. I appreciate your time."

"Of course."

Fenn had a manila folder open in front of him, though his eyes were not on it. He had the manner of a man who had prepared this conversation in advance and was now delivering his prepared opening accurately, which Marcus respected. "You're aware of the body found on the breakwater last month."

Marcus said he was.

"There's been a development of a minor kind. The Coast Guard made a general inquiry — asked whether anyone in town had heard the man speak before the storm. A fisherman came forward. Ed Larrabee. He was patching a trap buoy at the commercial float that afternoon, the ninth, and he heard the man say something." Fenn placed a brief pause before his next words, as if to indicate that what followed was not his characterization but someone else's. "Something European, he said. Possibly German."

The word *European* was doing considerable work. Marcus allowed it to do that work in silence.

"The Coast Guard forwarded the report to the harbormaster's office, which mentioned it to the selectmen, which mentioned it to me." Fenn glanced once at the folder — some record of this chain, Marcus assumed. "I wonder whether you'd be willing to speak with the harbormaster. In case she has any materials — a phrase, something in writing — that might benefit from identification. Not as an investigator, in any sense. I would characterize it as a civic resource."

He had chosen the phrase *civic resource* with some care; Marcus felt the care in it. He was being used and also being asked, and Fenn was trying to accomplish both without the use becoming visible. This was decent of Fenn, whose decency on most matters was genuine if occasionally misaimed.

"I'd be glad to help," Marcus said. He meant this and was also aware that saying otherwise would have read as avoidance, and that avoidance in a particular direction was itself a kind of statement. The body had been on the breakwater, and someone had heard what the man said, and what the man said may have been in a language that was Marcus's by birth and training.

"Thank you." Fenn's relief was brief but visible, which told Marcus something about how Fenn understood his position in Coldwater Reach — that some real possibility of refusal had seemed available.

"I'll stop at the harbor office after my last class."

"Excellent." Fenn stood. Marcus stood, buttoned the older suit's coat, and went out.

---

He had Latin in the afternoon — the third declension, eight students, a standard Tuesday. He moved through it with adequate attention, and at half-past three collected his coat and hat from the faculty coatroom and went out the building's north door into the November afternoon.

The light was going. Not dark yet but committed to it: the sky a flat ash gray, the sun already behind the inland ridge, what remained of the day a kind of residual illumination without source or direction. He went south on Main Street. Burleigh's was closed, as it was on Tuesdays. The post office showed Ada Gile's handwritten notice about V-Mail hours. The general store had ration-book advisories taped to the inside of the glass. He passed all of it without entering any of it — the town familiar in the way a foreign country became familiar after sustained residence, which was to say: known without being owned.

He had been to the harbor office once before. He remembered the occasion exactly, as he tended to remember occasions in Coldwater Reach exactly, each one still distinct enough to locate precisely. He had gone in the spring of 1942, preparing a classroom exercise on maritime regulation — the Lighthouse Service's jurisdiction, the harbor log as a legal document, the intersection of admiralty law and local authority. An exercise that had ultimately not been used. He had not been sure, when it came to it, whether a visit to the harbormaster's office would be received as a legitimate pedagogical inquiry or as something less clearly categorized. He had decided the uncertainty was sufficient reason to use a different exercise.

The pier lane smelled of salt and diesel even from the road. He heard the harbor before he saw it: the draw of water against pier pilings, the creak of mooring lines taking the tide's shift, a bell buoy at the anchorage's outer edge. The pier shed stood at the lane's end with a light burning over its door. At the near end of the shed the harbor office, where the structure changed from newer plank to older converted pilot house.

---

The office was twelve feet by fourteen, or close enough. A chart cabinet stood to the left of the desk, its drawers flush and latched. Above the south window: a VHF radio set on its shelf, dial unlit. On the east wall, at a height that would have suited a taller person, an aneroid barometer of the older enclosed type. Below the barometer: two hooks bent from a length of copper pipe, a coffee cup on each — one with a chip at the rim, one without. The room smelled of brine and the heating element and the particular cold that came in at the sill.

On the peg beside the door: an oilskin coat, too large for her frame, hanging with the particular weight of something used past the point of newness.

Eira Bowman was at her desk. She looked up when he came in. She did not stand. Her hands came to rest flat on either side of the open logbook — not a defensive posture but the posture of someone for whom this room was the full extent of the relevant territory, and who had not issued any invitation to renegotiate its dimensions.

"Dr. Klein."

"Mrs. Bowman." He stood inside the door. "I apologize for coming without notice. Principal Fenn asked me to stop by — regarding the inquiry about the man on the breakwater."

She waited.

"A fisherman — Ed Larrabee, I understand — reported hearing the man say something that sounded European. Possibly German. The report went from the Coast Guard to the selectmen to Mr. Fenn, who asked whether I might be of use as —" Marcus applied Fenn's phrase with intention "— as a civic resource. In case there were any materials. A phrase, a written word. Something that might benefit from identification."

She considered him for a moment with the attention of someone gathering information rather than forming judgment. Then she said: "The Coast Guard has what I know."

The sentence was precise in the way of a sentence chosen for its precision: it said the Coast Guard possessed what she presently knew, which was true, and it said nothing about the conditions under which what she knew might later change, or expand, or become applicable in a different context.

"Of course," he said. "I won't take up your time."

He had his hand on the door latch when he stopped. He could not have said, after the fact, whether this was calculation or something less deliberate; he had turned back before he'd resolved the question.

"If it would be useful," he said, and heard his own formality and made an effort against it, and the effort produced the same formality at a slightly different angle. "Not to the Coast Guard, necessarily. To you. I know something about identification documents from that period — the late thirties, the early forties. Varieties of German. Regional and national variation in written forms. I'm at the school."

Eira Bowman looked at him. She was not deciding whether to trust him — that would have implied a current use, which she did not have — but the look had the quality of something being assessed for later applicability, the way one assessed a piece of equipment for a season not yet arrived. Two or three seconds. Then: "I'll note it."

Marcus thanked her, in a minor key, and went out.

---

The lane was darker than when he had come down it. The lamp above the pier shed door held the near ground but did not reach the road. He went north. Behind him the harbor worked against the pilings in its ordinary manner — water's commerce with structure, mechanical, without reference to what any particular tide had brought in.

He thought about the English phrase: *a man without papers*.

In English the phrase carried the inconvenience of a filing problem. A man who had not submitted the correct documentation, whose identity could not be properly processed because the supporting evidence was absent. Unfortunate. Correctable, in principle. Somewhere the proof existed; it had simply not been submitted.

In German the phrase was *ein Mensch ohne Papiere*. The word *Mensch* was not *Mann* — not man in the gendered or particular sense but person, human, the categorical term that the German philosophical tradition had spent considerable effort defining and that German legal tradition, in the decade just past, had spent considerable effort qualifying and subdividing and finally setting conditions on. To be *ein Mensch ohne Papiere* in Germany in 1936, or 1938, or 1941, was not to have neglected paperwork. It was to be without the documentation that confirmed your standing as a legal person in a system that had chosen to make personhood conditional on documentation. The papers were not evidence of identity; they were identity, in the only form the system would recognize. Remove the papers: remove the person. Not from the physical world — the body continued, as anyone looking at it could see — but from the world that the century had increasingly decided was the real one, the legal and administrative one, the world of records and proofs and the right to be processed.

He had arrived from somewhere and come to rest at the foot of an iron ladder at the end of a granite breakwater. The question of who he was could not be answered from the body because the body's authorship had been removed — by the water, or by whoever had placed him in it, or by himself, if the removal had been intentional.

Had he known, Marcus wondered. Had the man understood what he was without — not just the practical inconvenience of it but the full weight of what the last decade had loaded into that absence. Had he understood himself to be *staatenlos*: without a state to claim him, without a floor of legal existence to stand on. The man who could not be proved.

It was possible the man had been an ordinary sailor with inadequate records, a document lost to water or carelessness. Marcus did not exclude this. He was also aware that he was carrying his own experience into the question, which was not sound methodology. But he carried it regardless, because this kind of knowledge did not wait for an invitation.

He had learned the weight of papers in a particular room, in a particular year, when a man in a uniform had looked at his documents and then looked at him and made a decision about what they proved. He was not sure he could do anything useful with it here. He had made the offer anyway.

The ridge crested ahead. Below: the town's lights spread across the valley floor, the Congregational steeple white against the dark sky, chimney smoke running south on the harbor wind.

---

Lillian had left the kitchen lamp on. The low oven held supper — onion and potato, something preserved and darkened in the cooking, smelling of vinegar and thyme. He hung his coat and sat at the table. Henry came in from the back hallway a few minutes later, in his custodian's canvas, the key ring clipped at his belt. He washed his hands at the sink.

"You're late," Henry said. A notation, not a complaint.

"I stopped at the harbor office." Lillian set bowls on the table and went to slice the bread. "At Fenn's request. The fisherman who heard the man on the breakwater — what he heard, apparently, sounded German."

Henry sat. He took up his spoon and did not immediately use it. "How'd the harbormaster take it?"

"With appropriate reserve. She said the Coast Guard has what she knows."

Henry nodded once.

"I told her I could be of use with identification documents. Varieties of German. I said I was at the school, if she needed it."

"What'd she say to that?"

"She said she'd note it."

Henry ate. After a moment: "She'll use you when she needs you. Not before."

He found it correct. Henry's readings of these kinds of transactions were, as a general rule, more reliable than his own, which tended toward the philological and away from the practical.

"Yes," he said. "That is what I understood."

They ate. Lillian moved in the adjacent room, the sound of her work steady and unremarkable. Outside the kitchen window, the wind off the harbor worked the bare branches of the elm in the yard.

After supper he washed his bowl at the sink and dried it and went upstairs. He sat at the desk with the annotating pen in his hand. On the desk: the Latin papers, the open dictionary, the grade book in its brown cover. He did not open the grade book. He sat for a while with the pen, and then he capped it and put it down.
