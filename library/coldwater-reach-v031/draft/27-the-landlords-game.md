# The Landlord's Game

The coffee Lillian had left on the back of the stove was still hot. She had banked it before going up, not asked whether they wanted it, not made the gesture into a gesture. The pot was there; the cups were there; the rest was between them. Marcus had been in this house three years and had not stopped finding the quality of her attention remarkable.

The board was between them on the kitchen table. Move fifteen. Henry had both knights developed and his queen-side bishop free. Marcus had castled early and built a solid defensive line that he had been tending, with minor adjustments, for the last four moves. The kitchen was warm. Outside the window the February cold had settled into a particular stillness — no wind, temperature still dropping, the night quiet in the way that meant it was consolidating rather than spending itself. Marcus recognized this kind of February quiet; he had learned the sounds of Maine winters.

The chess set was Henry's cousin's — a wedding gift sent from Baltimore in 1919, hand-carved in dark and light fruitwood, the pawns perhaps a touch wide for the regulation square. One bishop had been broken before the set ever came to Coldwater Reach and replaced, and the repair was so old it had become part of the set's character: the replacement was a quarter-inch shorter than the original, the difference perceptible only when both bishops were on the board at the same time. Marcus handled the shorter one with a slight additional care, without having decided to.

Henry played the way he did most things: deliberate, forward-looking, not showy. He would consider a move, set the piece in one square, look at it, take it back, move it elsewhere. He had, as far as Marcus had observed across two years of Thursday evenings, never made an impulsive move. He played with the patience of a person willing to spend several moves preparing a position from which, at the right moment, he would do something decisive. Marcus played the opposite way. The whole board was always held in his mind; it was the committing to what he saw in it that he could not quite manage.

On move twenty-two, Henry lifted his light-squared bishop — the original, the taller one — and placed it on the square Marcus had been watching it approach since move sixteen, while telling himself there was still time to prevent it. The bishop took Marcus's remaining rook. Marcus looked at the board, looked at what remained, and tipped his king.

"I never learned to play aggressively," he said.

Henry began resetting the pieces to their home squares, starting with the pawns. "You see the whole board," he said. "You still play safe."

Marcus considered this. It was accurate. "Where I grew up, aggression in the wrong moment cost more than the game."

Henry set a bishop on its square and looked up at him. The look was not intrusive — it was simply accurate. He said: "Norfolk was the same way."

He let it stand there for a moment. Then he sat back in his chair.

Marcus refilled his cup from the pot on the stove. When he came back, Henry was still looking at the pieces, half-arranged in their starting positions, and then he began to talk.

His cousin had written from Bangor in the fall of 1923. There was work at the shipyard; a man could live on what they paid. So in the spring of 1924 Henry and Lillian had come north: a trunk, a set of kitchen things, two weeks' wages in reserve. The work had lasted two years. He did not elaborate on how it had ended.

After the shipyard, someone at the Bangor labor office had known about a school in a town down the coast that needed a custodian. A man named Felling — the principal before Fenn — had offered the position and a reference to a house on Gull Street where the rent was seven dollars a month for two rooms. He mentioned Lillian's first winter the way a navigator mentions a reef: briefly, precisely, with the implication that everything that came after had been calibrated by the knowledge that it was there.

"First year or so," Henry said, "some men in town figured out what I was." He set a rook on its home square. "And what I wasn't." He paused. "And they arranged themselves accordingly."

Marcus said: "You've been here twenty-one years."

"Yes."

"Does it feel like staying?"

Henry did not answer immediately. He considered the board — the pieces in various states of organization, some at their home squares, some not, the whole thing somewhere between two games. He seated his queen-side rook. Then he moved a pawn on the queen side, and Marcus looked at it and assumed it was a standard opening and then looked again and understood that it was not. The pawn was not the beginning of the approach; it was the third step in something that had started two moves earlier, while Marcus was attending to the king side.

Henry said: "It feels like what you do after you stop moving."

Marcus looked at the pawn.

He thought about March 1944 — the Emergency Committee's letter, two weeks in his coat pocket, a deferral he had allowed to extend past the point where it could honestly be called accident. He had gone home one morning and written one page, three paragraphs, which said he would remain in Coldwater Reach for the foreseeable academic year. He had sealed it, posted it, and called it a deferral in his own thinking.

He thought about a November corridor. The third overhead fixture near the library door, the one that had been flickering all autumn with a loose contact — steady that night; he had noticed it because it was usually otherwise. Henry mopping from the far end, the dry scrape of the mop head on the boards. Henry saying, without pausing, without looking up: *Five to two.* Marcus saying *yes.*

He thought about Hannah Pfeiffer at the second desk from the window in his German class — not looking at him but at the gray February rectangle beyond the glass, at some middle distance she had been occupying since she turned eighteen.

He thought about a summer afternoon in Bangor. The periodicals room at the library on Harlow Street, the maritime bureau notices in the *Daily News*, and the comprehension that came slowly and then completely, like a photograph developing in a tray: August Schell, German-born Swedish national, crewman of the SS *Ångermanälven*. He had ridden the bus home and written a note that evening — in Kurrent, his old Vienna hand, the script he used when the thought was not for anyone but himself — and folded it twice and put it in the drawer of his bedside table. He had not taken it out since. He had not told anyone the name.

He said: "I think I have stopped moving."

Henry looked at him. He said: "Then you should reorganize your defense."

Marcus looked at the board. He had been watching the king side and Henry had been building on the queen side in the time he'd spent watching the wrong place. He saw it now: the whole architecture of it, patient and forward-looking.

He moved his knight to the center.

---

They played for another forty minutes. Henry won the first game; the second was closer. Marcus made better moves in it — not aggressive, exactly, but more willing to commit, to place a piece in a square from which it could not easily be retrieved. He did not win.

At eleven o'clock Henry cleaned up the board. He put the pieces in their box in a particular order — pawns, then rooks, then knights, then queens and kings, then the bishops last: the mismatched pair, the replacement placed precisely where the original went. He said good night and went upstairs. His tread on the staircase was even and unhurried. The house absorbed it.

Marcus stayed at the kitchen table.

The pot had perhaps a cup left in it. He poured it and sat with it. The kitchen was very quiet in the particular way it was quiet when Henry and Lillian were asleep: the tick of the pipe behind the wall near the stove, the occasional small sound of the house contracting against the cold.

The German word he was thinking about was *Ansässigkeit*.

He had not applied this word to himself since 1937. The word itself was not one he had forgotten — it was a legal and bureaucratic term more than a literary one, the kind that appeared on official forms: sedentariness; fixed residence; the condition of being established in a place. *Ansässig sein*: to be domiciled. The construction was transparent in the German manner: *sitzen*, to sit; the prefix *an-*, at or in proximity to; *ansässig*, seated at a place; *Ansässigkeit*, the fact of one's sitting having become part of the legal and social record of a particular location. He had been *ansässig in Wien* from birth without the word ever requiring thought. Then the documents had changed — the categories available to certain people had changed — and the word had acquired a retroactive edge, the way all the simple words did when they became instruments rather than descriptions. After 1937 he had not applied it to himself anywhere. Not in Paris, not in London, not in the nine months at Camp N in Sherbrooke, which was the least *ansässig* place he had ever lived, designed to prevent the condition. Not in Cambridge, where the Emergency Committee had treated him, with every kindness, as a man in transit. Not here.

But here, now, in February 1945, in a kitchen in Coldwater Reach, Maine, the word had arrived. Not as ambition. As recognition.

He thought about what stopping meant in practice — what one built, in a place one had committed to, that was different from what one had built on provisional tenure.

Outside the kitchen window the February night was still and very cold. Two streets down was the school; one street below the school, the harbor — which was frozen in its inner anchorage this month, he had heard this from somewhere. He passed the harbor twice a day.

He turned the cup in his hands.

He washed it and set it on the draining board beside Lillian's, and he went upstairs.
