# The Word for Harbor

She was at the chopping block with the butter-box planks when she thought about making the new card.

The cedar dried fast behind the kitchen stove — three or four days, and what had been green-edged and faintly resinous came out pale and dry, holding a pencil mark the way good paper held it. Her father split the boards into kindling-lengths with his hatchet on the cold side of the woodpile, three strokes per board, Saturday mornings when the week's work let up enough for that kind of patience. She had been taking lengths before they reached the woodbox since January — three weeks now — slipping two or three boards out of the stack when her father was in the barn, then cutting them down on the cold side of the building with her pocket knife: six inches by two, roughly the size of a playing card, each face sanded clean with the fine side of the whetstone.

She had ten cards done. *Barn / Scheune. Gate / Tor. Bucket / Eimer. Rope / Seil.* And the more recent ones: *fence / Zaun. stone / Stein. field / Feld. morning / Morgen.* And two newer cards she'd put out in the second week of February and hadn't gotten back yet. The completed cards — given and returned — lived in the deep pocket of her apron alongside the pencil and whatever else a day accumulated. She turned them over without thinking sometimes, the way you turned a coin: English side, German side, English side. The English sides wore fastest. She didn't know why. Something about the letters, or the way they were handled.

The system: a card flat on the fence post nearest the icehouse, held down with the gray stone she'd moved from the field edge in October. By the following afternoon: gone. Two days later, sometimes one: back under the stone. He had been memorizing. She knew this from the wearing of the cards, and from the way he sometimes said an English word when he came to the fence — not working at it but using it, placing it where it belonged in a sentence.

Today she had the harbor card to bring out.

---

She'd made it the previous afternoon at the kitchen table, her mother out with the chickens, the house quiet around her. *Harbor / Hafen.* She drew the dividing line down the center with the ruler edge, pressed the letters on both faces, and held the card for a moment before tucking it into her apron.

The word had been in the edge of her thinking since October — not that she had details. The talk had reached her the way talk reached everyone in a town this size: in pieces, through the gaps. At school in the corridor, at the general store, after Sunday meeting in the road. A man. Unidentified. The breakwater. She didn't know more than that. The pieces hadn't arrived in any better form. But the word *harbor* had acquired something from those months of partial information — a quality of holding-in, a particular weight on both sides of the word. The harbor as the place that took you in from the sea. She turned the card over and thought about that, and then put it in her pocket.

She laid it under the gray stone before her morning work, face-down, and went about the day.

---

The three older POWs left for the north pasture with Lonzo at eight, which was when they always went. Karl stayed in the south yard. His standing assignment was the working-yard maintenance — manure hauling, the secondary clean, the daily accumulation that kept the south yard manageable. She could hear him before she saw him on most mornings: the barrow wheel on frozen ground, its particular stop-and-go rhythm telling her where he was in the task.

By two o'clock Lonzo was back from the pasture, settled in his chair beside the icehouse door with his coat collar turned up. He had his rifle across his knees and a small flask tucked in his coat's left breast pocket — she had begun noticing the flask in January, the way it had become part of the daily arrangement of him, as habitual as the chair. He was looking at the middle distance in the way that meant present, not watching. The expression of a man whose job was to be there rather than to attend.

Hannah came to the south gate with a bundle of baling wire she'd been meaning to move for a week. She set it against the fence post, let her eyes drop to the post's top.

The harbor card was gone.

She straightened without looking at the icehouse.

Karl came across the south yard with the slop bucket, moving at his ordinary pace. His coat was buttoned to the throat — the Army-issue denim her mother had let out at the seams without being asked, all buttons present, which she noticed the way she always noticed without meaning to. He set the bucket on the fence rail.

Then he reached into his coat pocket and took out the card.

He'd folded it once across its length to keep it flat in the pocket. He unfolded it carefully, with both hands, and held it with the English side facing him. She could see the letters from where she stood: the H of *harbor* worn nearly through, the rest still legible but going. He'd been working at it hard.

He said, in English: "Har-bor."

The word settling in his mouth. Not a question, not a statement — something between the two.

She said: yes.

He looked at the card, then at her. He said, in German: *Was ist das, ein Hafen, hier?* What is that, a harbor, here?

His German had the Bavarian roundness she recognized from her father, but set differently in the mouth — the vowels opened further forward, a younger voice in the same regional key. He asked questions as genuine questions. She had noticed this early and kept noticing: he was not asking in order to seem engaged. He wanted to know what a harbor was, here, in relation to where he was standing.

She said, in German: *Ja. Drei Meilen.* Three miles. And pointed south — past the barn, past the far field boundary, roughly toward the ridge line you couldn't see from the south yard.

He turned to look in the direction she was pointing. The bare birches at the field's edge, and beyond them the February sky, gray and flat and closed. He studied it the way you studied a map when the detail you needed wasn't printed — calculating from what was visible toward what wasn't.

He said, still facing south: *Ich habe das Meer nie gesehen.*

I have never seen the sea.

He said it with the particular tone he kept for things he meant completely — not louder or softer than his ordinary voice. She had heard it twice before: in October when he'd said *da, bitte* setting her grain bucket on the rail; and in December when he'd noted, almost under his breath, that the east barn wall was separating from the foundation beam.

She looked at him.

Regensburg was ninety kilometers up the Danube from Munich, inland and river-anchored. She'd found it on the school atlas in October, placed her thumb beside Straubing to measure the distance — same river, same Bavarian valley. You could live your whole life in that valley with the Danube as your water and the sea as a mark on a map. And Tunisia: she'd traced the North African coast when the news came through about the surrender. A coast, yes. But Karl had come to that coast as a prisoner, at the end of something — she thought that was different from standing at the shore and looking at it, the way you looked at something that had been waiting for you and was now simply there.

She picked up the baling wire.

*Ich könnte es dir zeigen* — I could show you — is what she didn't say. The sentence arrived in both languages at once, the way certain thoughts did when she'd been thinking in German all morning, and she held it there while she gathered the wire, and did not say it. Because she didn't know yet whether she meant it. And she had learned — not from anywhere in particular, from the general running of a house — that you didn't say a thing until you knew what it would cost.

She took the bucket from the rail as his hands released it, the handoff wordless the way most things were, and went toward the barn.

Lonzo had not moved from his chair. He was watching the ice that had built along the near edge of the water trough at the icehouse corner, or watching nothing in particular; it was hard to say from across the yard.

---

She worked until four. Baling wire sorted and coiled on the barn's north hook. Second-stall trough drained and refilled. Hilde and Else brought in from the half-gate and settled in the milking alley. The tasks had their order and she followed it, and the thought moved with her through the order the way a thought did when it had gotten into the day's grain.

She had been nine years old when she walked to the harbor by herself. A clear October morning, the road dry, her shoes dry when she started. She had not asked permission because it had not occurred to her that permission was the relevant category — you walked south for three miles and the harbor was there, the same harbor it always was, and the sea beyond the harbor mouth was large and gray-green and smelled of brine and old rope and diesel from the lobster boats, and larger than she'd known any surface could be when you were standing at its edge. The tide had come in while she stood on the breakwater and soaked her shoes through to the wool socks. She had walked home in wet socks and told no one. There was nothing particular to say. She had gone and the sea had been there and she had come back.

She had never thought of it as something you could be denied. She had never thought of it the way she thought of meat ration or coffee — as something allocated, doled out, possible to be refused. The harbor was three miles south and it was simply there, whether or not you went to it. You could walk to it on any clear day without asking anyone's permission. That was the entire situation.

Rolf had grown up here, three miles from the coast his whole life. She couldn't say whether he'd thought much about it growing up. Boys went outward toward the fields; she had gone toward the water; they had never discussed it because there had never been a reason to discuss it. And then the war had taken him to sea, and the sea was now something he crossed.

Karl Reinmar had lived nineteen years and never stood at the shore and looked at it.

She brought Hilde and Else in and settled them and cleaned the alley and went in for dinner.

---

Potato soup and bread. Her mother had the oil lamp on the table again — her eyes, in the February evenings. The lamp made a smaller circle than the ceiling fixture, and the three of them sat inside it with the kitchen in shadow around them. Outside the window the dark was already full, February dark that settled early and stayed.

Albrecht ate without speaking. Magda's spoon moved in its quiet way.

Hannah ate her soup with one hand and turned the harbor card over in her apron pocket with the other. English side, German side. The English side going smooth under her thumb, the letters almost gone by feel.

Then she said, in German: *Hast du das Meer gesehen, bevor du nach Amerika kamst?*

Had you seen the sea before you came to America?

Her father looked up from his bowl.

The question sat between them. She heard it arrive with more weight than she'd intended — not just a question about geography but about the years before she was born, about the man who had stood at a ship's rail in Hamburg in 1922 and watched the city go small behind him. She had not intended all of that.

Her father set his spoon down. He looked at her with the weighing expression — not warm, not cold, something in between deciding and knowing. At the far end of the table, Magda had gone still.

He said: *Erst in Hamburg.*

Not until Hamburg. When he'd boarded the ship, going out. The sea had been the threshold, and he had crossed it once, going, and here he was on the other side of it.

Hannah nodded.

She did not ask what it was like to stand at the rail and look at it for the first time, the water opening before you in every direction. She did not ask whether he had understood, in that moment, what the crossing meant.

Her father picked up his spoon. He finished his soup, folded his napkin with his two folds and the tucked corner, and went to the radio chair. He did not turn the radio on.

Magda rose and took the bowls to the sink. Hannah followed.

Through the window above the sink: the February dark, complete and flat. Three miles of it between here and the harbor, and the harbor there behind the dark, and the sea beyond the harbor mouth, and all of it there whether anyone looked at it or not.

Her mother handed her a bowl to dry.

She dried it and put it away.

The card was still in her apron pocket. She could feel it when she pressed her hand flat against the apron cloth: English side worn almost smooth, *Hafen* on the other side still legible, the letters still holding their shape.

Her mother handed her the last bowl.

She dried it and put it on the shelf.
