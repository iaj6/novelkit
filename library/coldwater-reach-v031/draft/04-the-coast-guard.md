# The Coast Guard

The northeast had been building since before light, and by seven the inner anchorage was throwing a chop that put white on the mooring lines. Eira was at the desk with the mooring register, working through the fall assignments against what she could see from the south window — the two lobster smacks at their positions, the anchorage otherwise empty now that Birch Macomber had taken the *Macomber* out. The register confirmed what the window showed. She noted the wind direction in the margin and did not write a number yet; the weather station at Machias would have a proper reading by eight, and she did not guess at what she could wait for.

She had made coffee at six, four measures of grounds in the tin percolator, and it was gone by the time she heard the engine.

Not the Machias pattern — bigger, and the rhythm was different, steadier, the sound of a boat running to a schedule rather than to fish. She went to the window. It came around the point past the breakwater — First District, the white hull at an unhurried pace, no running signal. Whatever its errand, it was not urgent.

She went back to the desk, straightened the mooring register, and did not put it away. She took the log from the drawer. She found October 9 and left the log open on the desk.

The surfboat tied up at the commercial float below the pier ramp. She watched through the south window as one man came up. Young — she placed his age before his rank, mid-twenties at the most, and then the rank after: a lieutenant's bar on the collar of the peacoat. He carried a folder under his arm and walked the way people walked when they had been taught to walk toward a purpose — not hurried, not looking around.

She turned from the window and sat down.

He came down the pier and knocked with one knuckle at the door, which was not quite a knock but the announcement of arrival.

"Come in," she said.

---

He was perhaps twenty-six. A straight fair face, deliberate in his manner. He looked at the harbor office the way new people looked at it — quickly, ceiling to floor, taking inventory — then looked at her.

"Mrs. Bowman. Lieutenant Pruitt, First District." He produced the folder from under his arm and sat down across the desk before she indicated the chair, not rudely. "You spoke with Petty Officer Gervais on October thirteenth."

"I did."

He opened the folder on his knee. She could see the top sheet at an angle, upside down, typed at the top and handwritten below: her incident report on the left half, and on the right what appeared to be Gervais's field notes in the compact shorthand petty officers learned. Below these, further sheets she could not read clearly — but the top line of the third page resolved, at this angle, into a typewritten summary: *Adult male, drowning, approximate age 38–48, no identifying documents, no identifying marks.*

She had known the age. She had known the absence of documents; she had checked the pockets herself. The last phrase was new: no identifying marks. Nothing on the skin. Nothing that would carry back to a record, a service file, a tattoo registry. She made a note of this, mentally, and said nothing.

"I want to review the vessel traffic for the night of October ninth," Pruitt said. He had a small notebook out, pen uncapped. "Between twenty-two hundred and oh-four hundred. Your log covers that period."

"It does." She turned the log toward him slightly — enough that he could see the entries, not enough that he could read the whole page easily. "Lobster smacks *Thistle* and *Doris V.* at mooring. Those are Coldwater boats, Roy Blaine and Delmar Stannard. Coaster *Macomber*, Birch Macomber master, Machias — arrived October eighth, departure logged October eleventh. Lighthouse Service ice barge *Ploughman* at anchor, inner anchorage, during that period."

He wrote. His print was precise, the kind kept legible as a matter of policy.

"The *Macomber*." He looked up. "A coaster — what was her cargo?"

"Bagged lime. Machias to Lubec, calling here on the way."

"She was in harbor through the storm."

"She came in on the eighth with weather moving. Macomber is a careful man. He waited on it."

Pruitt wrote.

"Any vessel you observed and did not log?"

She had known this question was coming. Traffic on the water, and then the water itself.

"No," she said.

He looked up from the notebook.

"Nothing at anchor or underway you didn't account for."

"No."

He wrote.

"During that period — the evening of the ninth through the morning of the tenth — did you observe anything unusual on the pier or dock?"

She looked at the open log on the desk. She did not need to look at it; she could have recited what was there from any room in the building.

"I was out at approximately oh-one-thirty," she said, "checking the *Macomber*'s mooring bridle. I'd noted a chafe point at the thimble two days before, and the wind was building. I wanted to verify the set of the bridle on the bollard before the blow came on full."

"And at that time?"

"Nothing unusual. The bridle was holding."

Pruitt wrote for several seconds. She waited.

"That's the full extent of your activity during that period."

"I was in and out during the storm, checking moorings. But nothing significant to report."

He wrote. She watched his pen complete the line, pause, and then move to underline something. Whatever it was, he did not share it.

He closed the folder on his knee. "I should tell you where we are with it," he said, which was a courtesy — he did not have to tell her anything. "Cause of death is drowning. The medical findings don't support foul play. No evidence of injury prior to submersion." He paused. "We haven't identified him."

"No."

"The case is open, but it's not — actively worked, at present." He stood, tucking the folder back under his arm. "If we develop anything further, you'll hear from us. If anything comes to your attention on your end, I'd ask you to contact the Machias station."

"I'll have the log available," she said.

He nodded and went out.

---

She heard the surfboat's engine turn over.

Then she went to the window. The boat had backed off the float and swung its bow toward the harbor mouth. She watched it pass the end of the pier — Pruitt visible at the stern, not looking back at the dock — and round the breakwater. The fog sat low on the water two miles out to the southeast; she could see the horizon where the fog began, the grey wall of it, and the boat going into it. She watched until the shape of the hull was gone and then until the engine was gone.

Then she turned back to the log.

---

She had opened it to October 9 when she first heard the surfboat. She turned forward now, one page, to October 11.

The entry was in her hand. *Macomber — departure 0430.*

She knew her own handwriting in this log the way she knew the angle of the tide gauge in February, or the weight of the padlock on the pier shed door, or the particular resistance of the chart cabinet's second drawer. It was hers. The *M* of *Macomber* with its particular lean, the zero in *0430* closed at the bottom in the way she had always closed zeros — she had tried once to change this habit and not succeeded. No question of whose hand had made the entry.

She had no memory of making it.

October 11 was the second day of the blow. She had been in and out of the harbor office across the two days of the storm — checking moorings every two hours when the weather allowed, logging conditions when she came in, running on whatever the body would give her. On the storm's first night she had been out at midnight and again in the dark hours; in the morning of the eleventh she had come in from the second night soaked through Owen's oilskin, the cold in her hands clear to the wrist. She would have sat at this desk and made entries. She could account for that.

She could not account for 0430 specifically. 0430 on October 11 was before dawn, before she had come in from the second night, a time when she was in the weather and not at the desk.

The harbor had no formal overnight watchman. It had not had one since 1941, when the last man the selectmen employed for the position enlisted. But the pier shed was unlocked at night, and it was not unheard of for fishermen to leave a note — Blaine did it when his boat came in odd hours; Stannard occasionally. Word left on the hook beside the door: *Doris V. in at 0320, secured.* She would find it in the morning and enter it in the log at whatever hour she came in, logging the event with the fisherman's time rather than her own. This was common practice; the log did not require her to have witnessed every entry she made, only to have verified it.

She could have found a note at 0430. She could have come in before the dawn she remembered coming in, made the entry from a note, and gone back out. She had been on six hours in and two hours out for two days; there were portions of those days she could not now reconstruct cleanly.

She looked at the entry. The blow had run through October 11; a careful man did not put a coaster out on the second day of a nor'easter without reason.

---

She took Owen's oilskin from the wall peg and put it on. The oilskin had settled to her shoulders over eight years of use, the collar the right height, the sleeves the right length now that she had rolled them inside to take up what Owen's longer arms had required. She did not think about any of this; it was the coat she put on to go outside, and she put it on and went outside.

The wind on the pier was twenty knots, maybe a little over, out of the northeast and carrying spray off the anchorage. *Thistle* and *Doris V.* lay at their moorings in the positions she had noted in the register — pulling against their bridles with the indifference of things properly secured, the lines taut and the boats riding the chop without strain. The anchorage beyond was empty. She stood at the pier head for a moment and looked at the breakwater and then at the water south of the breakwater and then at the fog on the horizon.

There was nothing there that needed looking at.

She went back into the harbor office, rinsed the percolator in the handbasin, and refilled it with fresh grounds — four measures, the amount the pot took. She set it on the burner and sat back down at the desk, opened the mooring register to the page she had left it on, and took up the fall assignments where she had stopped.

Outside, the northeast held steady. She noted the time, noted the wind, and went on working.
