# The Breakwater

Halfway down the breakwater she could see them: two yellow slickers at the far end, standing still, looking down. The fog was in close. The storm had moved offshore the day before, but the sea was still working off three days' worth of accumulation — she could hear it in the low sound running beneath the fog, a drag and suck at the base of the granite that wasn't the storm anymore but wasn't settled water either. Underfoot, the breakwater was wet and in two places glazed from a freeze that had let go by four in the morning, leaving the stone the color of wet slate.

She had walked this granite in every condition the Maine coast produced. She walked it now at the same pace she always walked, reading each course of stone by the memory of her feet.

She placed them as she got close. Roy Blaine's height and the way his hands were in his pockets. His nephew Eddie Dunst, Roy's sister's boy, standing with his weight on his right leg. They had not touched the body. They stood back from it six feet or so, and they said nothing when she came up.

"Morning," she said.

"Morning." Roy kept his hands in his pockets. "We come out to check my moorings. Found him before we got there."

"When?"

"Half past five. Before first light. Before we called."

She looked past him.

The man lay in the angle between the seaward face of the breakwater and the iron ladder bolted into the last course of granite at the far end. Facedown. Left arm outflung, right arm tucked under his body, feet pointing south. He was wearing oilskin over a wool sweater — dark wool, brown or black, the color indeterminate under fog and wet — and knee-length rubber boots in the standard pattern. Nothing on the oilskin indicated a vessel or a service. No name. No insignia. No painted mark.

She crouched at the edge and looked without touching. The iron ladder's lowest rung was eight inches above the waterline at this tide, the tide four hours past the ebb and still dropping. She traced the geometry of how he lay. The position was wrong for a fall from the structure: a man coming off the breakwater would have landed differently, closer in, at a different angle. He had come from the anchorage side. The eastern chop, wave action working off the storm, carrying him up against the granite when the tide had been higher and the water had been running higher along the ladder iron. He had not fallen. He had been deposited.

She checked his pockets. One hand in each side pocket of the oilskin, methodical, feeling without looking. Left pocket was empty. Right pocket had water in it and something that had been cloth before three days in the sound had taken whatever shape it had. She could not retrieve it without tearing it apart. She withdrew her hands.

His left hand — the one she could see, the outflung arm — was a working hand. Weather-roughened. The nails were close-cut and one was cracked at the quick.

She stood up.

"Go back to the shed," she told Roy. "The Coast Guard will need statements from both of you."

Eddie Dunst opened his mouth. Roy's hand came up and Eddie went quiet and they went.

---

She heard the surfboat before she saw it: a diesel note coming around the south point, careful in the fog, finding its way without rushing. Petty Officer Gervais stepped off the bow before the line was properly fast, which told her something about the way he operated. He was young — thirty, perhaps a year either side — and he kept his face easy. He came up the ladder and stopped when he saw her.

"Petty Officer Gervais, Machias station." He did not extend his hand. "Are you the harbormaster?"

"Eira Bowman. Yes."

"Did you disturb the body?"

"I checked the pockets. Nothing retrievable. I didn't move him."

He went past her to look, and she stepped back to give him the space for it. His crew came up the ladder with a canvas litter and a camera. Gervais crouched where she had crouched and looked at the same things she had looked at, and then looked at the ladder iron, and then stood.

"Your observations," he said.

She gave them to him in order of relevance: body position, angle inconsistent with a fall from the structure, placement consistent with arrival from the anchorage side on eastern chop. Clothing: oilskin, wool sweater, rubber boots, no insignia, no vessel markings. Apparent age thirty-five to forty-five. Working hands. Pockets empty or waterlogged, nothing retrievable.

He listened without writing anything down.

"Any unusual vessel traffic in the past seventy-two hours?" he said.

"Nothing cleared in or out during the blow. Standard, in a three-day nor'easter."

"Anything prior to the storm?"

She looked at the anchorage. The fog was thinning. She could see the *Macomber* at her mooring — Birch Macomber's coaster, in harbor since before the storm, waiting on weather — and the two lobster smacks on the south side, and the empty water between.

"Nothing unusual," she said.

He asked several more questions in the same efficient order, and she answered them the same way — precisely, within the scope of what she had noted in the official record. He thanked her. The crew moved to the body with the litter, and Eira stepped back farther and let them work. When they were done and the canvas was lashed, Gervais turned and looked at the anchorage the way she had looked at it.

"She'll hear from the First District," he said, not quite to her. "When there's anything."

She walked back down the breakwater to the office.

---

The harbor office was twelve feet by fourteen, the converted pilot house at the near end of the pier shed. Chart cabinet to the left of her desk. VHF set on the shelf above the south window. Owen's barometer on the east wall at the height where Owen had hung it, which was not quite the height she would have chosen but was the height she had left it in, eight years ago, and now it was simply where the barometer was.

She had not reorganized the chart cabinet. She had not moved the barometer. She had added to the office over eight years — the new tide tables folder, the civil defense binder, the second mooring-line coil she kept under the desk in winter — and changed what the work required her to change, and left the rest where it was, and by now what had been Owen's arrangement and what was hers had grown together until she could no longer have said with certainty where one ended and the other began.

She opened the incident log to Wednesday, 13 October 1943.

She wrote the date and time: 0612. She wrote the entry in order. Male victim, apparent age 35–45, maritime dress — oilskin, wool sweater, rubber boots — no identifying documents or vessel markings recovered. Body position consistent with arrival from the anchorage side via eastern chop and inconsistent with a fall from the breakwater structure. Reported to the harbormaster at approximately 0545 by two fishermen who came out to check their moorings. Coast Guard Petty Officer L. Gervais, Machias station, attended the scene and took custody of the body at 0631.

She turned back two pages to Friday, 8 October, and read the last full entries before the blow: the coaster *Macomber*, in harbor since the third. The buoy tender *Samoset*, in and out on the fourth. Two lobster smacks, day trips, both back before dark.

She turned forward to Saturday, 9 October.

Her entry at 1400 on the following day — written Sunday afternoon, filling in from memory, the storm's second day — read: *Vessels in harbor: Macomber (coaster), moorings checked and secured, 0015. Weather deteriorating, bar seas building N to NE. Blow expected 1800–2000.*

Below the line: the column for vessel traffic, evening through midnight, which she had not completed when she wrote the entry and had not returned to.

She put her pen to the column now.

She thought about what it needed to say.

She made a dash.

---

It had been just after midnight when she went out. The mooring bridle on the *Macomber* had a chafe point she had noted two days before — wear at the thimble, the kind that would worsen fast in heavy weather — and with the barometer already well down from the afternoon reading and the first gusts beginning to make the pier lines snatch, she had gone out to check it before the main weather came. The walk from the office to the *Macomber*'s mooring pennant was three minutes in good conditions; in the first gusts from the north it was five.

She had been at the bow cleat, working the chafe point with a hand torch, when she looked up and saw him.

He was at the shore end of the pier. Standing at the edge of the overhead lamp's reach — in the light but only barely, which gave her the shape of a man and no more. Canvas bag on the ground beside his feet. He was looking at the water.

She had meant to call over to him. Ask where he was berthed. Ask what vessel. But the bridle was the immediate problem — one of the seizing wires was pulling and needed resetting — and she had attended to it, and when she straightened up and turned around, the end of the pier was empty.

She had assumed he had gone into the pier shed for shelter. Men did this: transient crew, waiting on weather, finding the shed door unlatched. She would find him in the morning and sort out the paperwork.

She had not found him in the morning. The blow came in at 0130, earlier than she had expected, and by 0200 there was no morning in any ordinary sense — only the harbor in storm, the *Macomber* pulling hard at her mooring, the smacks sheering, and the work of keeping it all from coming apart.

She looked at the dash in the column.

A dash in her log meant no anomalies noted. She had not written that; she had made a mark. The column had been blank before, and it was filled now. A man standing at the end of the pier, before a three-day blow, canvas bag at his feet, facing the water: this was not vessel traffic.

She had not entered him because she had not known where he was berthed, and without a vessel she could not make the entry complete, and then the storm had come. She could not enter him now.

The dash was not inaccurate to what the column was for. It was not accurate to what she had seen.

She closed the log and went to fill the kettle.

---

She made coffee in the tin percolator — four measures of grounds, which was right for the size of the pot — and stood at the south window while it came up. Outside, the fog was gone. The anchorage was clear in the October morning, the *Macomber* on her mooring with the settled motion of a vessel that has been through weather and is done with it. To the south, Roy Blaine's smack was heading out across the bar, the engine carrying that uneven idle she had been meaning to mention to him for three weeks.

The surfboat was still alongside the breakwater, finishing. She watched Gervais's crew, small at the far end, making their preparations. When they were done the surfboat's engine started and the boat swung away from the granite and came down the anchorage, giving wide berth to the *Macomber*, throttle opening as it cleared the pier end. Its wake spread across the flat water and reached the *Macomber* and lifted her briefly, then reached the near pilings and touched them and flattened out.

The harbor was the harbor again: its sounds, its smells, the diesel and the brine and the October cold that was coming off the water now that the fog was gone.

She drank her coffee.

---

She rinsed her cup at the utility sink and hung it on the hook below the barometer. She had put both hooks in herself, the week she moved into the office: a length of copper pipe from the bilge of a derelict smack, cut and bent and mounted below Owen's barometer on the east wall. She had put the second hook in the month after Owen died, when the office was hers and she had been deciding where things would go. His cup had been on its hook since 1935. She washed them both each morning and hung them back — hers on the left, Owen's on the right — and had done so for eight years.

She took Owen's oilskin from the wall peg. It was the right weight for the work.

Outside, the breakwater lay in the October morning — granite and ordinary, the sea beyond it running a low swell, nothing that asked anything of anyone. She began the inspection at the pier shed door and worked seaward, the way she always did.

On the desk behind her: the incident report, signed. Beside it, the log. The dash where it was.
