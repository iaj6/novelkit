# Monday, Wednesday, Friday

The harbor reached him before the harbor was visible.

He had learned this about the walk from his first week in Coldwater Reach: the road from the Crow house to the school ran along the inland side of the ridge, and then at a point roughly eight minutes from the house it crested, and for perhaps half a minute the smell came — brine and diesel exhaust and the cold particular to open water in October — before the road turned back down toward the north end of town and the harbor fell away, replaced by the back of the Congregational church and the bare maples behind it. He had been making this walk for nineteen months. He knew it as one knows a repeated thing: without novelty, and without quite losing the fact of it.

He had left the house at twenty past seven, in the good suit. Monday, Wednesday, and Friday were the good suit; Tuesday and Thursday were the other one. This was not a rule he had stated; it had arrived as a consequence of his schedule and remained. The good suit was a medium gray worsted, bought in London in the autumn of 1938 — Cheapside, a tailor who measured him in silence and made no remark about his accent — and it was still good, with qualification. The elbows were going. He had been intending to address this for three months.

The Congregational church sat at the corner where the ridge road met the main street. It was never unlocked at the hour he passed it. He had been inside it twice in nineteen months, both times for community gatherings that his position on the faculty made appropriate to attend; on every other occasion he knew it only by its bell, which rang the quarter-hours and which had entered his internal chronometry without his permission. On the stretch of road between the ridge and the school he knew what time it was by the bell rather than the watch in his coat pocket, and this had been true for some time.

The high school occupied what had been a wool processing mill at the north end of the village — three stories of timber-frame and brick, the brick the dark orange-red of nineteenth-century New England industry, the smell inside a compound of chalk dust and old wood and the heat from the basement plant that Henry Crow had been managing for nineteen years. Henry's connection to the building and Marcus's were of entirely different kinds, and they arrived at different hours and by different doors; but they lived in the same house, and the school was the shared fact of their days in the way that the harbor and the farm were shared facts for others in the town.

He arrived before his students and wrote the paradigm on the board in chalk. *Ich habe. Du hast. Er hat.* He had trained himself to print for the blackboard, because his natural hand was the old Viennese Kurrent — letters joined in a form that a stranger would read as encryption — and there was no pedagogical advantage in writing what the students could not read. In his grade book, in the margins of examinations, he still wrote Kurrent: faster, more exact, and his, in the way that certain things remain one's own even when one has given most of the rest away.

---

The German class met on Mondays, Wednesdays, and Fridays. There were six students. He had expected this number; in a coastal town in wartime Maine, German was not a subject that recommended itself to practical parents. The six who were there were there for their own reasons, and he had learned to stop inquiring too directly into what those reasons were.

He had formed his opinion of each in the first weeks. There was Curtis Aiken, who had decided that German grammar was a solvable problem and was solving it with the single-mindedness of a man winning an argument with a machine. There were two girls in the front row, attending with consistent thoroughness, whose note-taking was immaculate; Marcus suspected that what interested them was the act of taking notes rather than the subject of the notes, but he did not consider this a defect. There was a boy who came irregularly and translated everything phonetically, with a sincerity that was touching and a method that was wrong. There was a girl in the back row whose last name Marcus had mispronounced twice — she had corrected him the second time with a precision he found professionally admirable — and had not mispronounced since.

And there was Hannah Pfeiffer, who sat in the second row on the left and already knew more German than any of them.

He had established this in the first week. She was not concealing it from him — she was deciding, with each exercise, how much to show, and that was her right. She knew the declensions. She knew the conjugations. When he asked for a translation and the room went quiet with effort, her pencil moved and stopped, and after the pause that indicated she had made a calculation, she gave the answer. Correctly, each time. He noted this in the margin of his grade book in Kurrent — *Sie weiß mehr, als sie zeigt*: she knows more than she shows — and said nothing to her about it directly. She would give him more when she decided to. Students usually did.

On a Friday in the third week of October he picked up the chalk to write the assigned passage from Goethe and wrote instead, without quite intending to:

*Wer, wenn ich schriee, hörte mich denn aus der Engel Ordnungen?*

The opening line of Rilke's first elegy — not on the curriculum, not what he had planned; it had come out of the chalk before the intention had caught up with the hand. He stood back and looked at it. Hannah Pfeiffer looked at the board for a longer time than the others did. He erased it before they had finished copying it and wrote the Goethe.

---

In the teacher's lounge at noon, the body came up.

The lounge was a small room adjacent to the principal's office. Aldous Fenn, the principal — fifty-five years old, twenty-two years in this particular post, a man of brisk administrative fluency who had reduced his job to something manageable without reducing it further — was refilling his coffee cup when he said it: "That body on the breakwater. Coast Guard still hasn't put a name to him." He replaced the pot and turned to the table. "No papers. No identification. Some kind of drifter, most likely."

There were four of them in the room: Fenn, Marcus, Thibodeau the mathematics teacher, and Calloway the home economics teacher, who had been at the school a year longer than Fenn and who Marcus understood to be its institutional memory. The body had been found two weeks before. Marcus had heard of it already, in the manner one heard of things in a town of nine hundred.

Thibodeau said: "Coast Guard say anything further?"

Fenn said: "Just that they were looking into it."

There was a pause. During the pause, Thibodeau and Calloway each looked at Marcus for a fraction of a second, and then looked at the table.

Marcus drank his coffee.

The pause lasted four seconds. Then Fenn said something about the coal delivery and the conversation reorganized itself around that.

He had been calculating this particular reflex — the half-second redirection of gaze — since at least 1936. He had been calculating it long enough that he no longer experienced it as injury. What it was, precisely, was a category error: when a German-seeming thing appeared in proximity to a German-seeming subject, the category operated. That he was Austrian rather than German, and that his relation to Germany's current behavior was rather different from what the category implied, was not information the reflex could accommodate. Reflexes were by definition prior to information.

He noted it, as he noted all such things, with accuracy rather than feeling. He was not offended. He had expended a certain portion of himself on being offended, between 1936 and 1940, and found the expenditure disproportionate to the result.

Walking back to his classroom, he thought: the reflex was a fact. Like the harbor smell at the ridge. One could know where the water was without seeing it.

---

The evenings at the Crow house ran on Lillian's schedule, which was a reasonable schedule. Dinner at six. The radio in the front room after, or reading, or chess with Henry on Thursday evenings if neither of them was needed elsewhere. He had adopted these rhythms by attrition — the steady pressure of nineteen months in the same house — and had stopped noticing that he was following one. Lillian had said once that he was the easiest person she had ever fed. He was not certain whether this was a compliment; it was, at minimum, accurate.

That week — the last of October, the body still recent enough to surface in conversation — he told Henry about the teacher's lounge.

Lillian had made salt fish with potatoes and there was bread, and they ate with the attention that a good plain meal rewards. After a while Henry said, without particular weight: "They find out who he was?" He meant the body.

"Not yet," Marcus said. "The Coast Guard is handling it."

Henry considered the bread. "They will or they won't," he said. "Doesn't change anything for us."

Marcus thought about this. It was a sentence that could have been resignation and was not; what it was instead was more precise — a declarative that came from a position Marcus recognized: the position of a man who has lived long enough inside systems that do not recalibrate for his presence to be surprised any longer by that fact. *They will or they won't.* An accurate forecast of probability. *Doesn't change anything for us* — and by *us*, the specificity was deliberate: this table, these two men, the house around them. Not offered with emphasis. Offered as a fact.

He thought about the word *Duldung*. Toleration: being-permitted-to-remain. One was *geduldet* by a town, by a board, by a room that looked at one for half a second. The grammar was passive; the condition was not chosen. In most of Coldwater Reach he was *geduldet*. In this kitchen he was not any particular category. He was at the table.

"No," he said. "It doesn't."

---

The civil defense warden meeting was on a Thursday evening in the first week of November, in the school basement. The basement had been the mill's boiler pit; Henry had made it habitable over nineteen years of custodial attention without overcoming its essential character as an underground room. The ceiling was low. The walls were old stone. Voices arrived already absorbed, as if the room had made its own prior arrangements with sound.

Two long tables had been pushed together at the center. A ring of men sat around them.

The civil defense warden was a man named Haverstock. He called the meeting to order at half-past seven. Around the table: Haverstock; two selectmen Marcus knew by sight; the fire chief, a quiet man named Gould, who attended these meetings with the patience of someone for whom the agenda held no surprises; two Coast Guardsmen in uniform; and six or seven others — the practical men of a small town who accumulate roles the way any surface accumulates what settles on it, simply by being present long enough.

He was there as the faculty representative for the building. It was his Tuesday fire watch rotation that would be confirmed tonight; he already knew this.

Across the table from him: Eira Bowman.

He knew who she was. He had seen her at the harbor — the figure in the oilskin coat at the pier shed door, the person who walked the breakwater at low water. He had known her name since his second month in Coldwater Reach and had encountered it since in the context of harbor business. She was the only woman at the table. She was not performing this fact; she had a folder of papers in front of her and she was listening to Haverstock with the quality of attention that assesses what is being said against what is already known, registering the difference without announcing it.

The meeting was procedural. Blackout compliance — the winter schedule, adjusted for the earlier nightfall. Fire watch rotation for the coming month. His Tuesday assignment was confirmed; he wrote it in his notebook. Forty-five minutes of the careful management of theoretical emergencies, conducted by men who understood that the theoretical and the practical were not entirely separated by geography. Then Haverstock said that was all, and the men pushed back from the table.

---

He and Eira Bowman reached the corridor door at the same moment.

He held it.

She stepped through without pausing and he followed and they were in the school hallway — chalk dust, old wood, residual warmth from the day's classes. She was moving toward the main entrance at the same pace he was, the pace of a person returning to something, direct, without social adjustment. He did not attempt conversation.

At the main door she stopped and looked at the sky. Clear, and cold; there would be frost before morning. He stopped beside her.

"You're the German teacher," she said. Not a question.

"Austrian, in fact," he said.

She turned slightly and looked at him — not with surprise, with the expression of a person indexing a correction — and then looked at the road. "Same road home," she said. Meaning the direction.

He fell in beside her. They walked without speaking. The silence had the quality of two people who had established, without discussion, that talk requires a reason. Fifty yards along, her road diverged from his and she turned left without a word — not abruptly, not with any social announcement; simply turned, because the road went that way, and she went with it.

He walked on alone.

---

At the desk under the north window of his room, he opened the grade book.

He kept things in order — the papers stacked on the right side of the desk, the pen in its place, the books on the shelf Henry had brought up from the basement storeroom. A man who has moved eight times in five years learns to keep things in order, because the order will need to survive the next move, and the one after.

He opened to the German class. His annotations in the margins were in Kurrent — *GU* for *gut*, *S.* for *Schwäche*, weakness, and occasional longer notes where the nature of the weakness rewarded recording. Beside Hannah Pfeiffer's name, in the margin of her last examination, he had written: *Sie weiß mehr, als sie zeigt.*

He had meant it as an observation of her method, which was a teacher's legitimate concern. Looking at it now in the script that no one else in Coldwater Reach could read, he thought that it might also be said of several people in this town — the harbormaster who had turned left without farewell; the men who had looked at him for a fraction of a second and looked at the table; the town itself, which had permitted him to be present for nineteen months without quite closing around him.

He thought about the word *unheimlich*.

It had no precise English equivalent — he had looked, professionally, and found only approximations. *Uncanny* came closest but carried a note of the supernatural that the German did not require. The literal structure of the word was plainer: *un-heimlich*, the negation of *heimlich*, which was itself double. *Heimlich* meant familiar, intimate, belonging to the house — and also, by extension, hidden, secret, kept from outside view. To be *heimlich* in a place was to be absorbed by it, to have grown so native to it that the place no longer showed you from the outside. *Unheimlich* was the reversal: the sensation of being in a place where you were exposed precisely because you had not grown familiar enough to disappear into it.

He had not been *heimisch* anywhere since 1937. He was not, in noting this, doing anything he would have called self-pity; he was being accurate, which was a different exercise, and one he had considerably more practice with.

Outside the north window, the night was clearing toward frost. The harbor smell came over the ridge in the wind — faint, cold, exact.

He put the cap on his pen and left the grade book open on the desk.
