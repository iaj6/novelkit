# Ada Gile's Window

The east wind had come in overnight, and by the time Eira reached the far end of the pier shed the sleet was running sideways — small and sharp, the kind that found the gap between collar and neck without trying. She pulled the collar up and worked the inspection from the pier shed door outward, same as any other morning.

The bollards were holding. The float gangway had come through the winter without requiring attention to the hinge, which she had expected to replace before the new year; she added it to the inspection sheet anyway — *examine before spring season* — and moved on. Roy Blaine's smack was at its mooring, the fenders riding right, the mooring lines showing the correct tension for a northeast wind. The Doris V. was in; Stannard must have come back before the weather moved, which the tide chart had suggested was possible. The coaster berth on the outside face of the pier was empty. It had been empty since the last vessel cleared in February. The inner anchorage was open again — the ice had broken up in the mild spell ten days prior, and the water was back to its March color, gray-green under overcast, the channel clear to the bar.

She checked the splice on one of the Doris V.'s mooring lines — showing chafe at the thimble, not urgent but worth noting — and came back into the pier shed out of the wind.

The harbor office mail had been accumulating at the post office for the better part of a week. She had sent one of the dock hands for it the two Thursdays previous, and the week before that, and this morning she pulled her oilskin off the peg and went herself. The dock hands could manage the pier for an hour. The walk to Reach Road was twenty minutes each way in good weather and somewhat longer in sleet, and she had been meaning to go for a week, and she was done meaning to.

---

The road up from the harbor curved north past the trap storage lot and then east to Reach Road. The east wind was head-on for most of the second leg. She kept her hands in her pockets and walked at the pace the sleet allowed — the pier pace, the one she used on the breakwater in any weather that didn't preclude walking.

The town was quiet at this hour on a Wednesday morning. The hardware was open, a man she didn't look at coming out with a length of pipe under one arm. The pharmacist's light was on behind the glass. The diner on the corner — Burleigh's — was dark at this hour, though it opened Wednesdays; whatever time the proprietor considered reasonable for a Wednesday opening was not this one. A woman she recognized as one of the Harlow girls crossed the road ahead of her and went into the general store without looking up.

The post office stood between the hardware and the pharmacist's, where it had stood since before she was born, in the same shingled building with the same black lettering above the door. She had been coming here since she was small enough to see over the counter only if she stretched. The smell of the place — paper and ink and the particular dry warmth of old wood in an enclosed room — had been the same in every decade she could account for. She had been collecting the harbor office's mail from this counter since 1935, when Owen drowned and the work became hers, and before that she had sometimes accompanied Owen on his post office errands and waited while Ada sorted the official correspondence. The harbor's business passed through here. Lighthouse Service notifications, Bureau of Navigation circulars, V-Mail from vessel crews whose families were in town, letters from the district maritime office in Portland. Ada had been the harbor's institutional memory in that particular way for eight years: she did not volunteer what came through the counter, but what came through the counter she did not forget.

Ada Gile was at the counter with her reading glasses on, sorting V-Mail forms into the pigeonhole grid behind her. The grid held mail for most of the families in town; she worked it without apparent effort, the address on each envelope registered with a glance and the envelope placed in the correct pigeonhole without pause or correction. The glasses chain lay against the collar of her dress. She looked up when the door opened.

"Mrs. Bowman."

"Ada."

Ada set down the envelope she was holding and turned to the shelf behind her and came back with the harbor office's bundle: envelopes bound with a length of brown twine. She set it on the counter and untied the twine.

"Three Bureau of Navigation circulars — district quarterly distribution. The Lighthouse Service quarterly report, came in Tuesday. A V-Mail from the buoy tender schedule coordinator."

Eira checked the return addresses on the circulars. Standard district distribution, all three; she would read them at the desk. The V-Mail from the Samoset's schedule coordinator out of Jonesport was the March buoy-work calendar, or possibly an amendment to the February one, which had had a correction note attached. She tucked the bundle inside her coat and set the V-Mail on top of the counter where she could see it.

She stayed at the counter.

Ada had returned her attention to the pigeonhole grid. She placed three envelopes in three boxes without looking at the boxes — the motion of someone who knows a geography by its contents rather than its coordinates. She did not press Eira with a look or a question.

"Ada." Eira set the V-Mail aside. "That business last fall. The body."

Ada's hands stopped on the grid. Not the stop of a woman surprised, but the stop of a woman shifting registers with the same attention. She looked up.

"Yes."

"Did the Coast Guard speak with you."

"They did."

"About mail."

Ada set down the envelope she held. She faced Eira directly with the expression she had applied, in Eira's observation, to every postal matter from a certified letter to a change-of-address form: no warmth, no coldness, the accuracy of a woman who dealt in facts and gave them to the people entitled to receive them. "Yes," she said. "In November. A man came and asked, and I gave him a statement about what I had."

"Was there something," Eira said. "Before they came."

Ada considered. She answered questions asked correctly and she did not answer questions asked otherwise.

"A postcard," she said. "Came in the first week of October. Addressed to general delivery, Coldwater Reach. The name on it was A. Schell." She paused in the way of someone giving a listener time to be accurate. "No last name used as a first name or any other way I could place it. Coldwater Reach has no one by that name."

Eira said: "What did it say."

Another calibration. A shorter one.

"A date and a time," Ada said. "Nothing else. I took it as a meeting arrangement of some kind."

"The Coast Guard took it."

"In November, yes. Along with a statement from me about when it came in and what the inquiry process was." She reached for the envelope she had put down. "They knew what they were looking for when they came."

Eira said: "Thank you."

Ada nodded once — the acknowledgment that completed a transaction conducted correctly — and returned to the pigeonhole grid. Eira took the V-Mail from the counter and tucked it inside her coat with the rest of the bundle and went out.

---

The sleet had gone over to rain while she was inside. Not heavy — the same gray cold air but with the sharpness dissolved, the drops barely registering on her coat as individual impacts, more mist than rain. She walked back down Reach Road into the wind, which had softened along with the sleet. The mud at the road's verges had turned darker with the wet.

She turned onto the harbor road and began the descent. The harbor came back into view below — the moorings in their positions, the flat gray water, the pier shed running out from the near shore. The bar was visible at the harbor's mouth at this tide height, the granite just above the water, gray in the gray light. She went down and in.

---

The coal stove in the northeast corner was cold. She cleared the ash plate, put in kindling from the box beside it, added coal, and stood with her hands open over the first heat until her fingers had come back. The harbor office smelled of paper and the mineral cold of a closed room in March — different from the sleet outside, which was at least moving. She waited for the stove to take hold before sitting down.

She took the plain brown notebook from the second left-hand drawer. The Macomber entries ran across several pages. She turned to them: December — the departure entry at 0430, the question about source, the first *Inquiry?*. February — the conversation on the dock with Hartley, the contract, the papers, the district office in Portland. She had added to these pages when information came to her and not before; she had not gone looking, she had logged what arrived.

She turned to the blank space below the February entry and uncapped her pen.

*A. Schell — general delivery — postcard — Portland postmark — early October. Contents: date and time. No other text. Coast Guard has it.*

She read the line back. She set the pen down and sat back.

She looked at what she had.

A postcard, sent from Portland — the postmark was Portland, which she had not known before this morning — addressed to a name that had no established presence in Coldwater Reach. No boardinghouse registration, no rental on the town books, no family she had heard of. The name not in any directory she could consult. The Coast Guard had known the name before they arrived to collect the postcard.

The postcard had contained a date and a time.

The Macomber's Navy district contract was out of Portland; this she had from Estelle Hartley, logged in February. The postcard's postmark was Portland. Birch Macomber's harbor fee receipt, dated October 8, was in the bolted drawer below her left hand. He had come in from Calais, lime and general cargo. The postcard had arrived the first week of October — before Macomber docked.

On the night of October ninth, past midnight, she had gone out to the pier to check the Macomber's mooring bridle. At the shore end of the pier, at the outer edge of the overhead lamp's reach, a man had been standing with a canvas bag at his feet, looking at the water. She had not seen his face. She had not spoken to him. She had not written him in the log.

The blow came in on the tenth. The body came in from the anchorage side on the thirteenth, three days later, on an eastern chop.

What she had was a set of coincidences, and coincidences were what the record looked like when it was incomplete. A postcard was not a coaster, and a coaster was not a man on a pier, and a man on a pier was not a drowned man on the breakwater. The four points had positions. They formed a shape. The shape was specific. It was not evidence.

She held the theory the way she held a theory about the set of a mooring or the behavior of a tide at the inlet. The theory explained the shape.

What she could not answer: what was the meeting? Who had sent the postcard from Portland?

What she could not ask without answering the first question: how to approach either inquiry without clarifying why she was making it, which would require clarifying what the existing record was missing.

She had not committed a falsehood. The vessel-traffic column for the night of October ninth was marked with a dash, her convention for no anomalies noted, and there had been no anomaly recorded — what there had been was an observation she had not recorded, which was a distinction that remained technically accurate. She had been holding this distinction for five months.

She capped the pen. She closed the notebook and put it back in the second left-hand drawer.

She got up and went to the percolator. She measured four scoops of grounds into the tin and filled it from the water jug and set it on the small burner beside the stove. While it heated she stood at the south window and looked at the harbor.

The rain had thinned to almost nothing. The anchorage was flat and dark, the moorings standing in their positions — Blaine's smack, the Doris V. — as they had stood since October, more or less, the water between them still and gray-green under the overcast. The bar at the harbor's mouth was covered again on the rising tide. The channel ran clear.

The percolator began to sound.

She took her enamel mug from the left hook below Owen's barometer and set it on the shelf beside the stove.

She did not pour the coffee.
