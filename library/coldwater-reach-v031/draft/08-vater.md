# Vater

The cows had been in their stalls since four, and by the time Hannah came for the evening feeding the barn had gone warm in that particular way it did in winter — the heat that had nothing to do with any stove but with eight large bodies breathing in close quarters, their warmth filling the space until the cold outside became a different world. She moved down the alley with the grain bucket, starting with Else as always, going to Hilde, going down the line. Their breath came off in small clouds that dissolved before they reached the rafters. The sound of grain falling into the wooden feed-box was the same sound every evening and she did not hear it anymore.

Through the east wall she could hear, faintly, the icehouse. A door — the heavy sound of it, wood on frozen wood. Then a voice, low, and then nothing. She could not make out the words. She had listened for them in the first weeks with a particular attention and then stopped.

She set the last bucket, checked the water in the back trough — still open, she would have to watch it when the temperature dropped further tonight — and went back down the alley, unlatching the barn door and pulling it shut behind her.

The cold came in at her face. The snow from this morning had been the first real one, not a dusting but several inches, enough to change the shape of the yard. The ruts were smoothed. The corner posts of the fence were blurred at their bases. The blue of the last light was on everything: the barn roof, the field south of the gate, the top board of the fence line running toward the road. She looked at it for a moment — the snow and the particular color the sky put on it at this hour, the color that would last maybe fifteen minutes before the dark took it. Then she went toward the house.

---

The soup was on the table already, potato and turnip, a smell that said root cellar and woodstove and not much else. Her father was at his place, both hands flat on the table beside his bowl, waiting the way he always waited — not with impatience, not with anything she could name, just the particular stillness of a man who has arrived where he intended to be and will wait for the rest to follow.

Her mother set the bread. Did not speak. Sat down.

Hannah took off her barn coat and hung it on the peg inside the door and sat in her place, which was across from Vater and to her mother's left.

They ate. The soup was thin but warm, and there was enough bread, and the woodstove had the kitchen at a good heat. This was the norm: the three of them at the table, the sound of the spoons and the settling of the stove and the draft in the flue on a cold evening, nothing else. She had grown up in this silence. It did not feel like silence anymore, exactly — it felt like the way the room sounded.

Her father finished most of his bowl and set the spoon at the bowl's edge. He said, in German: the north fence was coming along. The boys had made good progress this week, with the frost holding the ground hard enough to work.

She looked up. He was looking at his bowl.

He said: the young one was quick with the post driver. Knew how to set the weight before the swing, not after. Good technique.

He said *der Junge* — the young one. Not a name. But there was only one young one.

She watched him pick up his spoon and take another mouthful.

She had been thinking about the question since the afternoon. She had been rehearsing the German of it in the back of her thinking while she was doing the feeding, working out the verb — *gesagt*, past participle. Whether she had the grammar right. Whether she wanted the grammar to be wrong, as an excuse not to ask.

She put her spoon down.

She said, in German: *Hat einer von ihnen etwas gesagt über Straubing?*

Her mother did not move. The spoon in her mother's hand continued on its path, bowl to mouth, and returned to the bowl.

Her father set his spoon down. This was different from the way he usually set it — not the end of a mouthful but a deliberate pause, a setting-aside. He looked at the bowl. He did not look at her.

He said: *Regensburg ist nicht weit.*

Regensburg is not far. From Straubing. From where he came from. From the river they shared.

He picked up his spoon. He finished the soup.

He folded his napkin, which was his custom, with the two folds he always made and the corner tucked under. He pushed back from the table and went to the chair by the radio. He did not turn the radio on. He sat in it the way he sat in it every evening, and after a moment Hannah heard the familiar sound of him opening the back section of the newspaper — the farm prices and the agricultural calendar, which was what he always read last.

---

Her mother rose from the table without speaking. She gathered the bowls — Hannah's first, then her father's, then her own — with the efficient economy that was her way with everything domestic: no wasted motion, nothing asked about. She carried them to the sink.

She stood at the sink for a moment with her back to the room. Then she began to hum.

It was not a song Hannah recognized. Something slow, not a hymn, something older than hymns — the sound of it had the quality of music that had been handed down rather than written down, the kind of thing that might have come from a grandmother or from a grandmother's grandmother, and had no name because it had never needed one. A few bars only. Then she stopped, and there was only the sound of water running into the first bowl.

Hannah cleared the bread board and brought it to the counter. Her mother moved to make room without looking up.

They washed in the order they always washed: bowls first, then the bread knife, then whatever the cooking had used. The window above the sink looked out onto the south yard and the barn roof, and the barn roof had the snow on it still, and the last of the blue light was on the snow — almost gone now but not quite, the color still blue rather than gray. She looked at it while her hands went through the motions of the washing, the hot water cooling fast in the cold air near the window.

*Regensburg ist nicht weit.*

Not far. Fifty kilometers, she thought — she had looked at it in the school atlas two months ago, in the room at the back of the library where the old reference books were kept, too large to check out. The map of Bavaria, the Danube running through it like a seam. Straubing at one point on the river; Regensburg at another. The distance between them the width of three fingers on that scale, which was nothing.

He had not said: *I know where that is.* He had not said: *that is near where I grew up.*

She thought: What he did with the facts beyond stating them was somewhere she did not have access to.

Her mother went to bed early, as she always did in winter. Hannah dried the bowls and set them in the cupboard. Her father turned a page in the newspaper. The stove clicked and settled.

She turned off the kitchen lamp and went upstairs.

---

Rolf's coat was on the hook by the door, where it had always been. She had not moved it when she moved into the room — had moved everything else of her own things into the drawers and onto the shelves, but not the coat. It was dark wool with a lining gone thin at the elbows, the kind of coat you kept for work when your good one had to last. She had been wearing it for barn work and for the walk to school since he left. The shoulders were still a little wide on her, though not as much as they had been.

She sat on the bed in the dark without lighting the lamp. The east window was black — the last light gone while she was at the sink. She could hear, distantly, the sound of the newspaper being folded downstairs, a small precise sound, her father always folded papers the same way, and then the creak of the radio chair when he shifted his weight.

He knew. He had always known.

She had understood this since the evening in the second week when she had been carrying dishes from the table to the counter and heard him say it — *Regensburg ist nicht weit* — to no one in particular, or to her mother's back, or to the air above the table. She had heard it and understood what it meant and had not known what to do with it, and had been carrying it since then the way you carry something in a pocket that you keep reaching for without taking out.

Tonight was different. Tonight she had asked, and he had responded to her question with the same fact he had been sitting with, and in responding he had pointed it at her rather than at the air. Small, maybe. But she had felt it.

She lay back on the bed with her hands on her ribs and looked up at the ceiling she could not see.

The question was not whether he knew. The question was what the knowing cost him. There was a young man from fifty kilometers from the town he had left in 1922 — left without, she understood, a simple or comfortable reason — sleeping in the icehouse thirty yards from his kitchen window. There was that young man doing his work, learning the barn, eating the provisions. Speaking a dialect her father spoke, probably, with the particular sounds of that part of the river. And every evening her father went to the radio chair and opened the newspaper and did not turn the radio on.

She thought about the word *Heimweh*.

She had heard him use it, not often, and not about Germany specifically — not about Straubing or the river or any particular place he had left. He used it in the abstract. Home-ache.

He had come here because he chose to come, or because circumstances had made choosing necessary, and he had built this farm and this life and he had not gone back. But she thought the young man in the icehouse might be, for him, a particular kind of proof. Not proof of anything good or bad. Proof that the place was still real. That Regensburg existed and was still producing twenty-year-old men who knew how to set a post driver correctly.

She did not know if her father had looked at Karl directly yet — she had not seen it happen. She thought: I will not ask him again. Not because she was afraid to ask but because she understood now that asking was not the way in. What existed was the thing he had given her: the same fact, delivered twice now, the second time at close range: *nicht weit.* Not far. That would have to be enough.

She reached over without getting up and touched the sleeve of Rolf's coat. The wool was rough under her fingers and faintly warm from the room's heat. Outside the east window the dark was complete, but the snow on the ridge above the farm held whatever light was left — not much, only the faint paleness of it visible at the window's edge if she looked.

She looked.

Then she closed her eyes and lay still and listened to the house settle around her in the cold.
