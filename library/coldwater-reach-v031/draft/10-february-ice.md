# February Ice

The north end of the inner anchorage had closed by the first week of January. By February it was hard out forty yards from the granite shore — ice opaque from the top and solid all the way down, nothing moving beneath it. At the seaward edge it thinned to a ragged fringe where the channel current kept the water turning, and this fringe advanced and retreated a few yards each day depending on what the overnight brought. The overnight had brought twelve degrees and no wind shift. The fringe had advanced.

Eira saw it before she reached the pier shed, in the thin light of a February morning that hadn't fully committed to itself yet: a coaster sitting wrong in the mooring field, its bow canted a few degrees to port, its riding light still burning. Not in distress. No whistle, no flare, no light signals. But sitting in a way no vessel sat by choice.

She unlocked the pier shed door, went through, and came out on the dock.

The coaster was about sixty feet, wooden, working trim, a Rockland registration from the wheelhouse color scheme — light blue, a color Eira didn't see often this far downeast. It had come in on the late tide, she thought, or past midnight, when the ice fringe would have been set back a few yards further and the mooring field would have looked clear. Then the overnight had done the rest. The tide had dropped away and left the keel on the ledge that formed at the mooring field's north edge, where the shallow water froze solid to the bottom and built upward. The vessel was sitting on this ledge, canted to port, the keel found.

Someone was standing in the bow. Not probing with the boat hook they held, just standing there in the way of a person who has already assessed the situation and is waiting for circumstances to change.

---

Eira went down the dock ladder to the float. The coaster's name was painted in dark blue on the bow: *Wren.*

She called up: who's master.

A woman in a wool watch cap came to the rail. She said: I am. Hartley. Estelle Hartley. Her expression was the expression Eira recognized — not from a mirror exactly, but from having lived inside a version of it for eight years: the look of a competent person confronting a situation that is inconvenient and non-catastrophic and requires patience rather than action. There were three or four women skippers working the Maine coast, the war having opened certain posts that would not otherwise have opened, and Hartley's manner made clear she had been sailing this water well before any of those openings.

She said: when did you come in.

Hartley said: half past eleven last night. Ice was back another five yards. She paused. It built overnight.

Eira said: it does that here in February. What's your draft.

Eight foot six.

She looked at the ledge. The tide mark on the nearest piling told her she had four hours to the next high, and even at high water she doubted the *Wren* would float free. The ledge ran the full width of the mooring field's north edge and had been building since early January; it was not going to release a keel on its own.

She said: you can try rocking her off with the engine. Your odds improve when the tide comes up, in about four hours.

Hartley said: and if the tide doesn't do it.

Eira said: I'll call the buoy tender. It can take you under tow from seaward and haul you over the ledge's edge. The ledge depth at the inner face runs about nine feet at this tide stage — your keel clears by half a foot.

She said: what's the tender's ETA.

Eira said: the Samoset's on its coastal run out of Jonesport. I'd have it here by half past nine.

Hartley looked at the ice-ledge and then at the harbor's mouth, where the outer reach was open water. She looked at the sky, which was February-white and had nothing helpful to say about the next four hours. She said: call it. I have a consignment in Lubec tonight.

---

Eira went back to the harbor office and used the wireless. The Samoset's station answered in seven minutes and said it could make Coldwater Reach by half past nine, which was forty minutes from now. She called for a tow — non-distress, routine assistance, ice grounding — and gave the *Wren*'s particulars and the state of the mooring field. The station acknowledged. She signed off and wrote the time in the harbor incident log.

Then she made coffee. The percolator was on the coal stove's back plate. She emptied the morning's grounds and measured four into the basket, filled the pot at the sink, set it back on the plate. While it came up she opened the incident log and wrote in the *Wren* — arrival estimated 2330 the prior evening, position in the mooring field, keel grounded on ice-ledge at the field's north edge, no structural damage reported. She pulled the tide chart off its nail and confirmed the high at 0953. The Samoset would arrive before it, which was the right order.

She poured the finished coffee into her thermos and went back to the dock.

---

Hartley had sent her crew below. They were young men, both of them, and there was no work to be done on the deck while the vessel sat still. Hartley herself was at the rail when Eira came up from the float, and when she saw the thermos she climbed down to the dock.

They stood side by side at the float's edge. Eira poured into her enamel mug and gave Hartley the thermos lid, which held enough.

The cold was particular. Eira had stood on enough winter docks to know the difference between the cold that came off open water — weight and moisture to it, a cold that pressed — and the cold that came off ice. The ice-cold was drier. Stiffer. It radiated from the flat surface rather than rolling in off moving water, and the effect was not like being in cold air so much as being in cold that had already settled and had nowhere further to go. It met you at the wrists and cheekbones and stayed.

Hartley wrapped both hands around the thermos lid. She said: this is good.

Eira said: four measures.

They were quiet for a while. The harbor outside the mooring field was open water, gray-green and working, the chop from an easterly still running cross to the channel current and making the anchorage's surface irregular in the way that looked colder than it was. Above the harbor mouth the sky was February-white and flat.

Hartley knew the coast the way you knew it after the years on it — not the broad strokes but the specifics, the behavior of particular passages in particular conditions, which anchorages froze first and which didn't, where the ledges were that didn't appear on the charts until you were on top of them. She said she had been sailing it since 1927.

They went through the tow plan. The Samoset would bring a line to the *Wren*'s bow cleat, take a strain, haul from seaward; Hartley would put the engine ahead slow on the pull to help. Fenders on the port side, though the ledge was smooth and the risk of hull damage was low if they came off cleanly. Four minutes to agree on this, another four to stop talking about it.

Then they were back to standing.

The cold off the ice had a smell to it as well — something dry and faintly mineral, different from the salt and diesel of the open harbor. The ice-cold smelled like chalk.

After a while she said: you work far down this way often?

Hartley said: Rockland to Eastport, the full circuit. Lath and shingles and lime and general cargo, depending on what was moving. She named the stops: Machias, Lubec, Jonesport sometimes for the Lighthouse Service freight, occasionally further east. She had come into Coldwater Reach twice before, she said, but not often; the inner anchorage was tight.

Eira poured more coffee into her mug. She said: you see Birch Macomber down your way? He's Machias-registered, works the downeast trade.

Hartley considered. She said: Portland, mostly, these days. She paused. He's been running something regular down there since the fall.

Eira said: October-ish?

Hartley said: could be. Maybe September. He turned down a run from me in September — I had pine lath for Bath, decent money, the kind of thing Birch takes in any ordinary year without blinking. He said he had a contract. That it paid better than I was offering.

Eira said: what kind of contract.

Hartley gave the brief, total-shoulder shrug that meant the question was reasonable and the answer was absent. She said: he didn't say, exactly. He said he had papers — said it the way you say something when it explains the situation and you don't intend to say more. Something out of Portland, Navy district maybe. She paused. You know Birch. He doesn't explain what he doesn't see the need to explain.

Eira said: hm.

Hartley said: is there something with Birch?

Eira said: he was through here last fall. I've been reconciling his circuit for the annual fee schedule — trying to account for the stops.

Hartley accepted this. She poured what remained in the thermos lid and looked at the ice-ledge, which was doing what ice-ledges did in February, which was nothing.

Twenty-two minutes past nine, the Samoset came around the harbor's outer point. Its gray hull was the color of the February sky and the color of the harbor water — visible by its shape and movement, not by any contrast with the weather around it.

---

The tow took forty minutes, unhurried. The Samoset's mate brought a line across to the *Wren*'s bow cleat; the tender came to a slow strain; Hartley put the engine ahead slow on the pull. The *Wren* moved off the ice-ledge with one low, dense sound — a brief groan of wood finding its way back to buoyancy — and then the keel was free and the vessel was in open water, moving seaward behind the tow line at walking pace.

Eira watched the waterline as it cleared the ledge. It was clean. The *Wren*'s trim was unchanged from its moored position; nothing had shifted.

Hartley slipped the tow line in the fairway and held station while her crew brought the fenders back inboard. She leaned over the rail and ran her eye along the hull's waterline on both sides, looking for any change in the vessel's sit that might speak to the keel below.

She said: looks right.

Eira said: have her hauled when you're next in port.

Hartley said: I'll do that. She looked down at Eira on the dock and said: I appreciate the tow call.

Eira said: safe weather.

The *Wren*'s engine came up to running speed and it moved south through the harbor's mouth. The Samoset turned east, toward Jonesport and its regular circuit. The inner anchorage, without the *Wren* in it, was the mooring field and the ice and nothing else — the *Thistle* and the *Doris V.* at their moorings well clear of the ledge, both boats already back from the morning's haul, gear stowed.

Eira walked back to the harbor office.

---

She spent the rest of the working day on the quarterly maintenance report and a response to the district about the spring ice barge schedule. The percolator ran dry by midday. She refilled it, drank another cup standing at the south window while the harbor settled into its afternoon quiet, and went through the incident log entry once more to be sure it was complete. At four o'clock the light had gone flat and she banked the coal stove and locked the pier shed and went home.

She ate at the kitchen table. Owen's spare oilskin coat on the right hook in the entry hall, where it had been since 1935. She drank tea from her mother's cup and looked at the table surface, which had a water ring in the wood near the left edge that she had been meaning to deal with for several winters.

At half past five she went back to the harbor office.

She put a few coals back in the stove and stood at the south window. The riding lights of the *Thistle* and the *Doris V.* were on now, small and steady in the dark, their reflections working on the anchorage's chop. The ice was invisible in the dark but it was there, forty yards of it.

Birch Macomber. Papers. Navy district. Portland, since at least September — since before October, which meant since before he came through Coldwater Reach with lime out of Calais, waiting on weather at the outside face of the main pier, from the eighth through the eleventh. The departure she'd logged at 0430 on the storm's second day, in her own hand, source uncertain, harbor closed outbound since 0200 October 10.

Papers meant a record. The district office in Portland held vessel contracts by master and registration — she had processed the inquiry paperwork on two vessels out of Machias in the previous winter when the district had needed documentation for their records. If she wrote to the district asking about a vessel's fee status, routine administrative correspondence, they would reference whatever contract documentation existed.

She went to her desk. Second left-hand drawer. She opened the plain brown notebook past the December entry and found the next blank line.

She wrote: *Macomber — Portland, Navy district contract — fall 1943. Per Hartley (Wren / Rockland). District office Portland holds vessel contracts by master and registration — file would exist. Inquiry: what was the contract? What dates?*

She looked at the entry. She wrote below it: *Papers = record somewhere.*

She put the notebook back and closed the drawer.

The riding lights moved on the anchorage's surface. The ice out at the north end of the mooring field was the same ice it had been this morning — unchanged, unhelpful, doing what February ice did. She looked at it for a moment. Then she put on Owen's coat and walked home through the cold, which was the open-water kind again now that she was away from the dock, and had weight in it.
