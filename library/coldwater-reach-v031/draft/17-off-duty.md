# Off Duty

The last of Vater's work shirts was still damp through the back when she pinned it. She smoothed it flat against the line anyway, the way Mutti did, and stepped back to look at the row: two sheets at the far end already beginning to stiffen in the heat, then the flannels, then the three shirts, and the pillowcases still in the basket at her feet. The air moved just enough to keep the sheets from hanging dead straight. It was the particular July heat that sat in the yard as if it had been there for days and had not the slightest intention of being moved by a little wind.

Mutti was inside. She lay down most Sunday afternoons now, had since spring — not ill, just tired in a way that had its own schedule, its own hours that she kept to without explanation. Vater had gone out after lunch to look at the east meadow fence and hadn't come back yet. The farm on Sunday had a different sound from the farm on other days: not less work, exactly, but a different quality of quiet, as if the week's ordinary sounds had been set deliberately to one side for a few hours.

From behind the icehouse came the murmur of the other three. She had seen them go around to the south side when she came out with the basket: two sitting in the sun, one crouching over something, and then the low sound of voices she couldn't make out from this distance. Once, a short burst of laughter, the tired and patient kind, the laugh of men who had told all their best stories to each other months ago and had moved on to lesser ones.

Karl was not with them. She had noticed this before she told herself she had noticed it.

She had known for two weeks that the off-duty clearance had come through. The paperwork had arrived from the provost marshal's office in Bangor and she had overheard Lonzo explaining it to Vater at the kitchen door one morning — the words *Geneva provision* and *countersignature* and *in sight at all times*. Vater had taken the form, read the front of it without sitting down, and handed it back. He had not asked a question. On Sunday afternoons Karl could walk the farm perimeter, the fence lines that ran the property's edges, as long as the barn or the icehouse was in sight. The other three stayed in. Lonzo had to be present.

She had not planned anything for either Sunday since then. The first one, she had been in the kitchen garden when she heard him on the fence line from across the yard; she had stayed where she was, weeding, working her way down the row, until he was past the far corner and gone. The second she had been at the chicken gate with the latch and had watched his shape moving along the east fence without going over, and then had gone inside.

Today there was laundry, which was a real task and had been on Mutti's list since Thursday.

The pillowcases were still in the basket when she heard Karl coming along the north fence.

---

She pinned the near corner of the first one before she looked up.

He was coming along at the pace he had on Sundays, which was not the work-week pace — that one had a task at the far end of it and moved as if it knew. This was slower, the pace of someone with a direction and nothing particular waiting there. He had left his jacket off. The heat had been in the upper eighties since Thursday; she had watched him make the calculation about the jacket before, when to put it on and when to leave it.

His English by now was not what it had been in November, or in February. He had words: the tools, the animals, the weather, numbers enough for time and measurement, a handful of complete sentences that came out careful and a little thick, like something still being broken in. She thought of the cedar cards she had been making since January — forty-some of them, she had stopped counting in March — English on one side and German on the other, exchanged under the gray field stone by the fence post. They had gotten him from *Da, bitte* in November to where he was now. She had not made a card for *sea.* She had been thinking that the sea was something you had to see first before you had a word for it.

She held her hand up when he was close enough, fingers pressed together the way she held the cards, though there was nothing in them.

"You want more words?"

He looked at her hand, then at her face. The corner of his mouth moved — worked itself slowly into something she had not seen from him before. In nine months she had learned his expressions the way you learned the expressions of someone you observed daily: the working face, the listening face, the closed one he had when something was said among the others that she couldn't hear. It made him look nineteen instead of older.

"Maybe," he said.

She picked up the next pillowcase and kept moving along her side of the wire. He kept moving on his.

The north fence ran between them: agricultural wire on treated posts, the same wire she had helped Vater restring when she was twelve, carrying the spool while Vater worked the stretcher. She knew the sag at the near corner and the way the third post from the end had always leaned slightly east. Four feet of it — the height a cow would not bother trying to cross. She was on the farm side. He was on the outside, the perimeter, walking the edge of the property. They were about two feet apart with the fence between them.

The upper pasture lay on her side: long grass, thick and warm in the July afternoon, heavy with the smell that midsummer grass had when it had been growing for weeks without anyone cutting it — sweet and almost too much, the kind of smell that filled the back of the throat. The cows had drifted to the far corner under the elm where the shade was. Above them the light was still high and white and without opinion. Flies moved along the top wire of the fence.

He said, after a while: "You say three miles. You go there?"

She had to place it: the word-cards, the cold, the card for *Hafen* with the number written in the margin. He had been keeping the number. She had not known he was keeping it.

"Yes," she said. "Since I was small."

They walked a few steps. A crow came and went in the elm at the far end of the pasture, loud and brief.

"What does it look like," he said.

She thought about what she would say to someone who had only a picture of the sea in their head.

"Very wide," she said. "The color changes. In the summer it is sometimes green, sometimes grey." Another step. "In the storm it is white at the top of the water." She held her hand flat and made a small rolling motion. She ran out of the English for it there.

*Es riecht nach Salz,* she said.

He nodded. "In Regensburg," he said, "the river smells of mud." A pause. "And of the ships." Then, after another step: "Here it is different."

She thought of Vater's river — not the Regen there but the Danube at Straubing, which Vater had described when she was small in a voice lower and more certain than his everyday voice, the voice of something he still had access to even after twenty years away. The color of it in winter, he had said. Grey-green. The sound of the stone pilings at the weir above town. She did not know if the river Regensburg sat on was anything like what Vater had described, but they came from the same water somewhere upstream or downstream. She had the reflex of wanting to say *Vater kommt aus Straubing* but she did not.

"Your family," she said instead. "They live by the river?"

He took a moment. She had learned to wait for his moments.

"My father," he said. "His shop is near the river road. He is a butcher." A short pause. "Was. I think was. I do not know now."

The bombing — the BC-1206 reports, the language of strategic targets, the rail yards and the bridges and the industrial works, the cities being taken apart from the air piece by piece. She had heard reports that named Regensburg. She did not know which streets were near the river road, or whether the word *was* was right because the building was gone or because the man in it was gone or because both.

"My brother is in Italy," she said. "I don't always know what is true."

Rolf, who signed his letters *Alles Gute, dein Rolf.* She had three V-Mails and each one said he was fine, and she believed them, and she also kept a small percentage back from believing them, held in reserve against something she did not allow herself to imagine.

He said: "Yes." Then: *Ich auch.*

The same phrase he had said in November at the south gate — the first real thing, before they had any words between them, with the cold coming in from the north and the feed bucket on the gate rail. She had turned it over in her thinking for the rest of that morning, turned it over many mornings after that without knowing she was still doing it. *Ich auch.*

She looked at the fence wire. He looked at the fence wire.

---

Lonzo was back at the icehouse corner in his folding chair, the paperback held flat across his knee. She glanced back once. He was looking at the page — she could see the angle of his head, the deliberate tilt of someone reading. He did not look up.

---

They walked the rest of the perimeter without more words. North corner, where the ground dipped and the wire had been raised on a longer post to keep the grade. The east fence with the old bramble pushing through the wire at the far corner post, the same bramble that came back every year regardless of what Vater did to it. The south corner where the lane came in and the gate was. The light was moving the whole time, tilting — she could feel it in the angle of the shadows, the fence posts throwing shapes east that had not been there an hour before.

At the south corner the fence turned and their paths separated naturally: hers back across the yard to the laundry, his along the last stretch toward the icehouse.

She walked back through the barnyard. The basket and the remaining pillowcases were where she had left them, beginning to warm through in the afternoon heat. She went to the line and picked up where she had stopped — third pillowcase, then the fourth. She did them left to right, near corner first, two pins each, the way Mutti had shown her when she was eight and just barely tall enough to reach the wire.

From the kitchen came the sound of Mutti moving — the pump handle, then something set down on the counter, and after a moment the low pop of the burner taking. She was up from lying down.

The July light had moved across the upper pasture while they were walking. It was past its high point now, going toward gold, the kind of late afternoon light that lay across the long grass in a particular even way — as if it had been placed there with some care, as if every fence post and blade of grass and distant cow on the far hill was sitting exactly where it was meant to sit. The fence threw long shadows east. The elm in the corner of the upper pasture was very green.

She had seen this light on this pasture every July she could remember. She thought it looked like that.

The last pillowcase went on the line. She picked up the empty basket and went in.
