# Addendum

The fog had burned off by six but left the inner anchorage still and grey in the way September fog left it — the water flat, no texture, the lobster boats at mooring sitting in their own reflections. By seven o'clock the light was coming in at a low angle from the east and the water had taken some color back. Eira was at her desk.

The vessel traffic log was open to the previous day's entries. Three lobster boats in and out between five in the morning and two in the afternoon; the barge *Calvert* at anchor since Monday on its way to Lubec; the buoy tender due Friday. She had written the buoy tender into the incoming schedule in red pencil — the harbor's shorthand for expected, not arrived.

She closed the log.

The desk held the usual accumulation: correspondence from the Lighthouse Service regarding the fairway buoy on the south channel, which had been dragging its mooring since the August blow and was now approximately twelve feet out of position. A request from the selectmen for the harbor fee schedule, to be updated and resubmitted before the November town meeting. A carbon of the fuel oil delivery record for the month, which she had checked and initialed and which needed to be filed.

She moved these to the right side of the desk.

Then she opened the center drawer and took out a sheet of harbor office stationery.

The stationery had the harbor office heading printed at the top — HARBOR OFFICE, COLDWATER REACH, MAINE — in a dark blue-green that had been close to black when the stock was new and had come out slightly paler in the last order, the ink thin. She placed the sheet square on the blotter in front of her. She took up her pen.

She wrote the date: *September 14, 1944.*

Below it she wrote: *Addendum to Incident Report, October 13, 1943 (Unidentified Decedent, Breakwater).*

She looked at the heading. Then she wrote.

---

*On the night of October 9, 1943, at approximately 0130 hours, the undersigned Harbor Master departed the harbor office and proceeded to the main pier to inspect the mooring bridle of the motor vessel Macomber, which was berthed at slip 4 on the south side of the pier. The mooring had been rigged the previous afternoon and the undersigned had noted in the vessel traffic log that conditions over the following sixteen hours would include gusting to 20–25 knots from the northwest, with an incoming swell from the south expected after midnight. In the undersigned's judgment the bridle was at risk of chafing against the starboard bollard as the wind shifted. The inspection was conducted at 0130 to allow time for adjustment before the first gusts of the incoming blow.*

The handwriting was the same as it was in the harbor log: even, slightly compressed, legible at a working distance.

*On proceeding to the pier, the undersigned observed a male individual at the far end of the public pier — the fixed structure south of the private vessel berths. The individual was standing still, facing the anchorage. He was carrying a canvas bag, single strap, worn over the right shoulder. He appeared to be in good physical condition. His clothing was consistent with maritime working dress. The undersigned did not recognize him as a resident of Coldwater Reach or as a known user of the harbor.*

She set down the pen. She read the last sentence. She picked up the pen.

*The undersigned did not approach the individual. The undersigned heard at that moment a sound from the direction of the Macomber's mooring — believed to be the bridle under strain — and turned to attend to it.*

*The inspection of the mooring required approximately five minutes. The undersigned checked the bridle at the bollard, found light chafe consistent with the angle of the wind against the current, adjusted the bridle by a half-turn of the starboard chock, and confirmed the adjustment satisfactory. On completing the inspection, the undersigned looked back toward the far end of the public pier. The individual was not visible. The undersigned assumed that he had taken shelter in the pier shed, which was standing open.*

*The undersigned returned to the harbor office at approximately 0140 hours. No entry was made in the vessel traffic log.*

She stopped.

She had been carrying the fact of that sentence for eleven months. She looked at it now.

She wrote the next sentence.

*No further investigation was conducted.*

Then:

*On October 13, 1943, the body of an unidentified male individual was recovered from the far end of the breakwater by the harbor patrol and subsequently transferred to the custody of the United States Coast Guard, First District, per standing protocol. See Incident Report, October 13, 1943, filed with the District office. The undersigned has reason to believe that the individual observed on the public pier on the night of October 9, 1943, was the same individual whose body was recovered on October 13, 1943.*

*This addendum is submitted as a correction to the record of the above-cited incident. No explanation is offered for the delay in its submission. The facts stated above are accurate to the undersigned's knowledge and recollection.*

*E.M. Bowman, Harbor Master*
*Coldwater Reach, Maine*
*September 14, 1944*

---

She read it back.

It took two minutes. The handwriting was even throughout — no place where she had pressed the pen harder or released it.

She read the third paragraph again.

*The undersigned did not approach the individual.*

Five words.

She looked at this for a while.

Through the east window the harbor was fully lit now. One of the lobster boats had started its engine — the *Gertrude B.*, she knew the engine by sound. The diesel carried cleanly across the flat water the way sounds carried in September, when the air had thinned enough to let things through without distortion. In six weeks those boats would be pulling the last of the gear. By the first week of November the inner anchorage would be down to whatever Mayhew still chose to take out and the trap skiffs at the south mooring that nobody used after October.

She was looking at the addendum.

She folded it in three.

She folded it the way she folded documents for the pending drawer: two crisp parallel folds, no overlap, the corners squared. Owen had shown her the fold the first winter of their marriage, when she had started helping him with the harbor correspondence. She ran her thumb along the crease to set it.

She went to the chart cabinet.

The pending drawer was the bottom one on the right side — the drawer Owen had designated for pending items when he reorganized the filing system in 1929, and which she had been using in its same configuration ever since. She pulled it open. At the front her own working files: the Lighthouse Service notice, the selectmen's request, a pending fuel inspection she had not yet scheduled. She moved these to the side.

At the back of the drawer were two manila folders in Owen's handwriting. She had not moved them and had not thrown them away. His handwriting was still readable after nine years — still his. The particular way he formed the capital letters; the G in *Correspondence* slightly enlarged, as if the letter required the room; the way his words sat down squarely on the ruled line as if he had been taught not to leave them uncertain.

*Lighthouse Service correspondence, 1934.*

*Tidal survey, pending, 1935.*

He had been working on the tidal survey the autumn he drowned.

She placed the folded addendum in front of his two folders.

She closed the drawer.

---

She went outside.

The morning had established itself fully by now. The fog was entirely gone, the ridge above town showing the first small turnings in the maples at the upper edge — not orange yet, not red, but the leaves beginning to consider it, a lightening in the green that was visible from the dock if you were looking for it. The air had the September quality: thin and clear but with the warmth not yet fully out of it, so that standing in direct sun on the dock felt almost like August, while the pier shed's shadow fell cool enough that she could feel the difference of five degrees when she walked through it.

She walked to the end of the dock.

The *Gertrude B.* had her engine running but was still at mooring, the old man doing his pre-departure check — she could see him moving on the afterdeck. Mayhew's skiff alongside at the north mooring, no name readable on the transom. The *Calvert* at anchor further out, her deck cargo covered in canvas, her anchor light still burning from the night, a pale smudge in the morning brightness. Beyond the barge the open water, and beyond that the breakwater: granite, running northeast, three hundred and forty feet of it in two sections with the gap for the passage channel, the far end just visible as a darker line against the lighter water outside.

She had walked the breakwater two hundred and some times since Owen drowned. She had walked it in November ice when the footing was wrong and in August fog when she could not see the far end from the near end.

The far end was where the body had come in.

She stood at the end of the dock and looked at the end of the breakwater, which was empty, as it was on most mornings, as the far end of the public pier was empty now behind her, the boards bare in the September morning.

She looked at the distance.

Then she went back inside.

---

The coffeepot was on the shelf above the duty log, where she kept it. She filled it from the water can, measured out the grounds from the tin she ordered monthly from the general store, and set it on the portable burner. While it heated she went back to her desk and read the Lighthouse Service letter regarding the fairway buoy, which was substantively unchanged from the previous letter and which required a reply she had been putting off. She drafted the reply on a notepad in abbreviated form — the buoy's position, the hazard assessment, the request for a correction tender in the next available service window — and set it aside to copy to stationery before the noon mail.

The coffee was done. She poured a cup.

She sat back down at her desk and drank it while it was hot.

The vessel traffic log was still open. She picked up her pen and wrote the date at the top of the new line: *September 14, 1944.* She noted the time: *0718.* Weather: *clear, light wind west-northwest, glass 30.14 and steady.* Anchorage: two lobster boats at mooring, one barge at anchor. Buoy tender expected Friday. Fairway buoy south channel continues approximately 12 feet out of position — see Lighthouse Service correspondence, pending.

The *Gertrude B.* cleared the north mooring at 0723. She noted that too.

In Owen's pending drawer, behind her working files: a document on harbor office stationery, folded in three, with his two unfinished files behind it. She did not note this in the vessel traffic log.

She picked up the Lighthouse Service letter and began to draft her reply in full.
