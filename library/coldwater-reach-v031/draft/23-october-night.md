# October Night

The stone wall was three courses high at the east corner, lower in the middle where frost had worked two winters in a row. She stood against the east corner in the October dark and waited.

Two days ago he had said: *You will show me the harbor?* In English, complete sentence, each word placed deliberately — the way you placed a glass on a surface you weren't sure was level. The sentence had the careful flatness of something rehearsed, which was not the same as the words sitting comfortably. In German she might have known better whether the tone was question or request. In English she could only hear that he had decided to ask it.

She had said: *morgen Abend.*

She stood for a moment after saying it, hearing what she'd said, and then he had nodded and gone back to carrying the water.

She had thought about it since. Not about whether — she had decided that the moment the words left her mouth, the moment she heard herself using the German for *tomorrow* and the German for *evening*. She had thought about the *how*: the back path through the upper pasture; the gate at the top of the field; the stone wall at the field's edge where the farm road became the ridge road; the ridge road itself, which the horses used in haying season, which was wide enough for two people side by side.

She had also thought about the note. The back of a word-card, blank cedar, the ones she'd been writing for nearly a year now. She had written in English: *come through the gate at the top of the upper pasture at nine o'clock.* His English was better than her German now — that was their arrangement, his English improving, her German not improving but becoming, through use, less careful. She had put the card under the stone at the fence post in the afternoon, face down — that was how they did it, face down for messages that weren't word-cards, face up for a word — and had not gone back to check whether he had taken it. He would either take it or he wouldn't.

He had taken it.

---

She had put on Rolf's coat at half past eight. Her mother was in the kitchen; she could hear the sound of her mother's chair on the kitchen floor, a particular scrape it made on a spot near the stove. Her father was in the front room with the radio on — a man's voice reading something official about the Pacific, she hadn't listened, the radio was not there to be listened to but to be in the room the way the furniture was in the room.

She went to the kitchen doorway. Her mother looked up.

*Ich geh kurz raus,* Hannah said. *Eine von den Kühen hat heute Nachmittag nicht gefressen.*

This was true. The second milker from the end — the one they hadn't named, the one whose left ear had a notch from a fence wire when she was a calf — had left her grain and stood with her head low. Hannah had checked her at five and she hadn't improved but she hadn't worsened either. The alibi and the fact were the same fact.

Her mother nodded. Her mother's hands were folded on the table and not doing anything, which was unusual. Her mother's hands were almost always doing something. She went out.

---

The back path went through the upper pasture at an angle, skirting the cows' nighttime field where the grass had been cropped to the root. The ground underfoot was firm — not frozen yet, but the wet had been worked out by two dry weeks, and her boots found the path without thinking. No moon. The October dark had a clarity that summer dark didn't have: the sky was the particular deep of a cold night without cloud, and the stars above the ridge were very clear, each one in its place. In February she knew them by name; in October she knew them by direction. She was walking south and slightly west.

The gate at the top of the upper pasture was pine, weather-checked, the latch a piece of bent iron that she could do by feel. She unlatched it and stood on the far side and latched it again. The stone wall began twenty feet beyond, catching what little light there was and holding it in the pale of its granite face. She walked to the east corner and put her back against it and waited.

The ridge road beyond the wall was darker than the pasture — the maples had dropped most of their leaves but the crowns still cut the sky. She could see the lighter dark of the road between them. She stood and breathed. Her breath showed.

After ten minutes she heard the gate latch lift on the far side.

---

He came through without speaking and latched the gate behind him. He was wearing the jacket she always saw him wear — dark wool, too thin for October, the only coat he had. She had thought, once, about giving him Rolf's, and then had decided against it in the same thought. The coat was on her back. That was where it was.

They stood a moment. He looked at her. She looked at the road.

*Gut,* she said, and they started walking.

---

The ridge road ran south along the top of the field and then curved west where the hill began. A mile from the farm to the high ground above Coldwater Reach — she had walked it in summer since she was small, had walked it once last February in the wrong boots for the ice. Tonight the road was dry and they went quickly and without talking, their breath visible in the cold air, the sound of their boots on packed gravel the only steady sound. The farm fell away behind them. The ridge rose on either side as the road narrowed into the tree line, the maples close enough that their stripped branches occasionally clicked together above the road.

She had thought, in the last two days, about what she might say on this walk. She had thought of things, and let them go, and arrived at nothing in particular by the time they were actually walking. There was nothing that needed to be said. She knew where she was going, and he was following her, and the October night was cold and clear and very quiet.

At the crest of the hill the trees fell back from the road and the ground opened westward, and there was Coldwater Reach.

---

The harbor below: the dock lights, the shapes of the boats at their moorings just visible as darker forms on dark water, the trim of a mast line here and there. The lights of the buildings on Reach Road — the diner closed at this hour, the post office dark, the general store with a faint square of light still in the window. Beyond Reach Road the rooftops of the town, and beyond the town the sea. From this height the sea was a black that was different from the sky's black — heavier, textured by its own movement in a way that the sky wasn't, and so wide that it made the harbor and the town and the ridge itself seem like things at the edge of something very large that went on without them.

And at the end of the breakwater, the harbor light.

Karl stopped walking.

She stood beside him and let him look. He looked for a long time — his face in the starlight had the fixed quality of a person attending to something that was doing more than they expected it to do. She watched him and not the harbor. She had not seen anyone see it for the first time.

*Das ist sehr groß,* he said finally.

"Yes," she said.

He was quiet again. He looked at the breakwater light, which was steady at this distance — white, pulsing one flash per four seconds, though from here you couldn't separate the periods. It was just there, and then there.

*Das Licht am Ende,* he said. *Ist das der Hafen?*

"The breakwater," she said. "They found a man there last year. He had drowned."

The harbor was below her and the breakwater was the shape at its edge and the light was on the breakwater and somehow the man had arrived into her sentence before she had decided to put him there. She had known about the body since October of last year — everyone in town had known, it had circulated at the post office and at Burleigh's and she had heard Lonzo speak of it once in a sentence that was mostly English and included a word she hadn't caught. She had not said it to anyone since.

He was quiet for a moment.

*Ich habe davon gehört,* he said.

She looked at him. He was still looking at the breakwater light.

She had not considered that he would have heard. She had thought of it as local news, the kind that lived in the town and stayed there — contained by where the knowing could go, the way a family's trouble stayed within the family. But he was not contained by the town's limits. She had seen it in small ways: the way information moved between the four men in the icehouse, the way Karl sometimes knew a thing she had thought only the farm knew. The Bangor arrangements, the satellite details, the other camps. News traveled that way too.

*Was hast du gehört?*

*Dass ein Mann kam vom Wasser,* he said. *Ohne Papiere.* She thought about that phrase — *ohne Papiere* — the way it named what the man lacked rather than what he was. She did not know who he had been. She did not know if Karl knew more than this or less than this. She did not ask.

---

The cold off the water came in a slow way — not gusting but building, adding itself to the night air in a steady accumulation you didn't notice until you had been standing still long enough. Her hands were in Rolf's coat pockets. The coat was two sizes too large still, the sleeves past her wrist cuffs, the shoulders wider than hers by the width of a spread hand on each side. She had been wearing it for three years. She had stopped thinking of it as borrowed without quite deciding to.

Karl turned up the collar of his wool jacket. His ears were red. He put his hands in his pockets and she saw, from the way he held himself, that he was cold and was not going to say so.

She thought about the word *mündig.* Marcus Klein had written it on the board in the spring — *having your own mouth, the state of speaking for oneself.* She had turned eighteen then. She had been returning to the word since, the way you returned to a stone in your pocket, when your hand went in and found it there.

She had said *morgen Abend* two days ago and meant it; she had put the note under the stone; she had walked through the gate in the October dark and up the ridge road and now she was here, and the harbor was below her, and Karl was beside her, and she had said the thing about the drowned man without planning to, and even that felt like it had come from her rather than through her. She had been here for it. She had not stopped it.

*Wir sollten zurückgehen,* she said.

*Ja,* he said.

---

They walked back the way they'd come. Faster on the return — the cold, the hour. She did not calculate the hour exactly but she knew it was past when she had said she would be back, and she didn't linger on the road. Karl matched her pace without comment. They walked back through the trees, back along the ridge road's southward turn, back to where the wall and the gate were visible against the lighter sky of the open pasture.

At the stone wall she stopped. He stopped beside her.

*Also,* she said.

*Also,* he said.

They stood a moment in the dark. She unlatched the gate on the farm side. He turned back toward the ridge road and the long way around to the icehouse. She went through and latched the gate behind her without looking back.

---

The second milker from the end was standing with her head low, the same position as five o'clock. Hannah stood at the stall door and looked at her. The cow breathed. Her sides moved. She was warm with animal heat and the smell of her was hay and body-warmth and the particular bovine unconcern that Hannah had grown up inside of, which was not comfort exactly but was the nearest thing to neutral she knew. She put her hand flat on the cow's flank. The cow didn't react. Hannah kept her hand there for a few breaths, feeling the rise and fall.

The cow would be fine by morning.

---

Inside: the front room first. Her father had fallen asleep in the radio chair, his head tipped sideways against the wing, the radio still on — a different voice now, reading the weather. *Fog expected along the coast, clearing by morning.* She turned the volume down to silence, not off. She did not turn it off because he had turned it on and she did not know why he kept it on at night except that he did, had for as long as she could remember, and she respected the reason even not knowing it.

The kitchen: the lamp on. Her mother not there. The cup on the table, tea gone cold.

---

Upstairs. She took off Rolf's coat and hung it on the hook on the back of the door, the hook that had always had something on it — her father's good hat in the years when he wore a good hat to things, her grandmother's apron after that, and now this. She hung it straight. She stood for a moment looking at it in the dim from the hall — just the shape of it, broad in the shoulders, hanging the way a coat hangs that knows where it belongs.

She sat on the bed.

There was no guilt-shaped space in her that the evening had not filled — the shape of the evening was the shape of the space, and they matched. She also didn't feel righteous, or brave, or certain that she'd done the right thing. She was not going to work through whether it was the right thing tonight, here, in this room, alone.

What she felt instead had no single word, or she didn't have the word yet. *Fest.* The particular solidity of something she had decided and then done and then had it be a thing that had happened — moved from the column of what she might do into the column of what she had done, permanent and specific and hers. She had said *morgen Abend.* She had put the card under the stone. She had walked the ridge road. She had stood at the top of the hill above Coldwater Reach and looked at the harbor with Karl Reinmar. Karl had never seen the sea. She had shown him the sea.

*Mündig.* She was not sure it was right. She was sure it was hers.

She lay down without taking off her shoes, and then, thinking better of it, sat back up and took them off and set them by the chair. She lay back down. The ceiling was the ceiling she had looked at all her life. The house was quiet in the way of a house at night where everyone inside it is in their room and accounted for.

She did not sleep for a while.

Outside, October.
