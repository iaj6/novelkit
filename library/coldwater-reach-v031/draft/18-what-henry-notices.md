# What Henry Notices

The piano lid in the music room was up again.

Henry set his bucket down and brought the lid closed, careful not to let it drop. Talmadge taught summer students on Wednesdays and Fridays — children whose parents wanted something to occupy the weeks before September — and she never remembered the lid. He always did. The instrument hadn't held its pitch reliably since the pipe-sweat situation above the ceiling in 1941, but that was no argument for leaving it open to the August humidity. He straightened the bench against the keys.

He started at the top of the building and worked down. Third floor to first: the mop bucket was easier on the stairs in that direction, and the third floor stayed cleaner than the floors below because people used those rooms and left. The building at six in the evening was not quiet — it was never quite quiet; it lowered itself into a register of pipes and settling joists and the particular creak the third-floor crossbeam made when the temperature dropped toward dark — but it was as empty as it got. Three classrooms and a storage room for the band chairs and stands, plus the room labeled EXAMINATIONS since 1931 and still labeled that, though what was examined in it had changed twice since Henry arrived. He collected two music stands from the hall and racked them. He checked the east window latch, which had been intermittently failing since the winter of 1937. The requisition for a new mechanism had gone through three times without result. He was prepared to submit it a fourth.

Down one flight.

The second floor was the floor he knew most specifically. He'd been cleaning Edna Locke's history room for nineteen years, which meant he knew it the way she knew it and in different ways: where the chalk tray collected deepest, which desk wobbled on its back leg, and which corner of the canvas map on the east wall had begun to lift from its mount. The map had been there before Henry arrived and would be there after; Edna had been there longer still. The red pin in Normandy had gone in on the sixth of June; he had been mopping this same floor two days after when she drove it in. The pin in Florence was newer — the paper head still bright red against the faded coastline — put in last week when the news came over the radio. He moved the wastebasket from under the radiator, where it always drifted, back to its place below the chalk rail.

He went through the other second-floor rooms in order: the science room with its smell of sulfur that never entirely left, Marcus's room with the conjugation paradigms still written in neat columns on the right-hand half of the blackboard, the small room where Thibodeau taught mathematics to whoever still needed it at that hour.

The principal's office at the end of the hall: dark, door locked. Through the glass panel above the handle he could see the desk, and on the desk a letter on school committee stationery — the blue-grey kind with the raised seal in the upper corner. He could read the name in the address block; he recognized it. He could read the first sentence of the first paragraph, which said *the matter of Dr. Klein's continued appointment*, and he didn't read further.

He mopped the second-floor hallway and the north bathroom and went down.

---

The teacher's lounge was off the main hall — originally a loading dock, still with the old iron rings along one wall and a floor that sloped slightly toward the former freight door. Three wastebaskets. A long table, six chairs. The coffee urn on the sideboard, there when Henry started and going to outlast him, the same as it had outlasted the urn before it. He knew this room's particular habits: the two cups always put in the basket under the sideboard rather than the sink, the chalk-dust footprints that gathered near the door in winter, the way the third basket — near the table, near the spot where people stood and talked in the mornings before the classrooms filled — collected things of a different kind than the others. Papers reconsidered. Notes started and set aside.

He went through them in order. Near the door: corrected papers, a wax paper bag, a pencil ground to nothing. Under the sideboard: two coffee cups in the basket instead of the sink. The third basket held one piece of paper that had been crumpled and then worked nearly flat again — the creases still in it, the corners not quite square.

He didn't take it out.

From where he stood he could read the header in block capitals: *Petition to the Coldwater Reach School Committee*. Three names below it in a column. He recognized all three. Two he knew as parents: a woman whose daughter sat in the junior class, a man whose son had played cornet at last May's spring concert. The third was Seavey, who sat on the school committee. Henry had been doing evening work in the building when three board meetings ran past six o'clock; he'd seen Seavey in the corridor on two of those occasions, coming out.

He smoothed the paper enough to read the first sentence.

*We, the undersigned, respectfully request a formal review of the employment of Dr. Marcus Klein on the grounds of his foreign national status and the uncertain loyalties attendant thereto.*

He put the paper back. The same angle against the basket's side. Crumpled the same way it had been.

He emptied the other two baskets, swapped the liners, and mopped the floor from the windows back toward the door. He finished the building at half past seven, locked the front door, and walked home.

The August evening had some cool in it from the sea, not much. The street was quiet — Burleigh's dark, the post office dark, a light or two in the upstairs rooms of houses along the main road, and in one of them a radio playing something he couldn't make out from the street. The elms on the last block before the turn had gone darker than the sky behind them. He walked steadily.

---

Lillian had made corned beef hash with the last of the week's red points. Marcus was at the kitchen table with his grade book open beside his plate, working through it in the tight Kurrent hand he used for his own notes — the letters pressed close and vertical, different from what went on the blackboard. Henry sat down across from him. Lillian brought the food.

They ate. Lillian asked Marcus whether the window in his room was still sticking; he said he'd used the block plane Henry had left; she said good. Nobody said more for a while. Outside, the late August light was still in the elm beyond the kitchen window.

When the plates were clear, Lillian took her tea to the front room. The kitchen settled.

Henry moved a piece of bread along the edge of his plate. He said: "You have at least one friend on the board. Edna Locke."

Marcus looked up from the grade book.

"And you have organized opposition. Not many people. But they have a draft petition."

A moment passed. Marcus said: "How organized?"

"Three names. One of them is on the committee."

Marcus was quiet. He set his pen inside the grade book and closed it. After a moment: "Do you want to tell me how you know?"

"Not particularly," Henry said.

Marcus nodded once. He said: "Thank you, Henry."

Henry moved the piece of bread. "You already knew something was coming. Now you know what it looks like."

"Yes," Marcus said.

"That's all I've got."

Marcus's hand rested on the cover of the grade book. He looked at the window. The elm outside still held a little of the last light.

Henry got up and carried his plate to the sink. Marcus brought his. They washed the dishes together — Henry washing, Marcus drying — the water running hot enough to raise a little steam in the cooling kitchen. Neither of them said anything. After a while the light in the elm was gone, and then it was just the sound of the water and the clatter of clean plates going into the rack, and after that the silence when the tap was off and the work was done.
