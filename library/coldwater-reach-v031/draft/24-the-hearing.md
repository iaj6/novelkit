# The Hearing

The good suit had been pressed since Sunday. He had done it himself, in the kitchen, on the board Lillian kept behind the pantry door, working the iron in careful strokes along the trouser legs and then the jacket's sleeves and then the jacket's front, doing it correctly rather than quickly, the way he did anything that could not be undone if done poorly. He had hung the suit on the back of his bedroom door and left it there through Monday and into Tuesday. All day Tuesday — through the morning's Latin class, through lunch eaten at his desk, through the afternoon's German recitation — he had known it was hanging there. He had changed into it at half past five and walked to school through the November cold, the leather portfolio under his arm, arriving at the building's main door at twenty minutes before six.

The library windows were lit from the inside. He could see them from the street while he was still thirty yards from the door: the yellow warm rectangle of them, the shadow movement of people taking their seats at the long table. He stopped on the walk for a moment, not from hesitation, but from the minor curiosity of seeing a room he knew from a different angle. In daylight the library windows were unremarkable — they faced south and admitted the flat November light in two rectangles that fell across the card catalog and the periodical shelves without distinction. From the street at dusk, with the room lit from within, they were specific: he could see the top of the tall case of encyclopedias, the back of one board member's head, the ceiling's dropped plaster where the water had come in three winters ago and had been repaired but not replastered to match.

He went inside.

---

The library had been arranged. The long table from its usual center-wall position had been drawn toward the windows and seven chairs placed on the far side of it, leaving the near side open. A single chair was positioned on the near side, across from the board. At the table's far end, slightly separated, a school chair from the corridor — the kind with a paddle arm — had been placed against the wall. Edna Locke was sitting in it.

She was not a board member. Her presence had been listed in the formal notice as *faculty representative*, which meant that someone on the board had thought her presence was appropriate, or useful, or expedient — Marcus had not determined which, and she had not volunteered it. She had not told him she would be there; either she had not known until the last moment, or she had decided not to tell him. He was not sure which she would have thought was kinder.

At the library's side wall, turned at an angle to face neither the board nor Marcus but some space between them, Principal Fenn sat in an observer's chair. He held his hands folded in his lap with the composure of a man who had practiced being present at decisions without being responsible for them.

The seven board members were ranged along the table's far side. Marcus did not know all their names; he recognized four faces. Aldrich, the chair, was at the center, a man of perhaps sixty with an administrator's careful face, the face of someone who had learned, over years of keeping proceedings orderly, that his own opinions were a disruption to the orderliness of proceedings. To Aldrich's left, three faces; to his right, three more. Among the seven: two whose families' names Marcus recognized from the petition Henry had described — he did not look at them directly.

He sat in the chair they had placed for him. He set the leather portfolio on the table before him, unopened. He put his hands on it.

Aldrich said that the hearing was called to order at six o'clock on the evening of November fourteenth, nineteen forty-four. He read the names of those present for the record. He read the petition's concerns. He read them in a measured voice — not hostile, not warm — the voice of a man reading aloud material he had not authored and for which he was taking no personal responsibility. Foreign national status. German origins — the word *German* stated twice in consecutive sentences, without the modification that would have made the distinction Marcus intended to make. The appropriateness of a German national in a wartime classroom. The question of undisclosed organizational membership.

Aldrich finished and looked up. "Dr. Klein. You've received the written petition."

"I have."

"Would you care to respond to the concerns as stated?"

"I would."

He had decided, between Sunday and today, that he would respond in the order the petition had presented the concerns, not in the order he might have preferred. Responding in the board's order was a form of cooperation he could extend without sacrifice; it also meant beginning with the error he was most able to correct.

"I am Austrian," he said. "I was born in Vienna, in 1896. I have not been a German citizen at any point in my life." He paused. "The distinction the petition has not made — I make it here, because accuracy is the appropriate standard for a proceeding of this kind."

He told them about the Anschluss. Not at length; the essential structure was simple and he presented its structure, not its texture. Austria absorbed in March 1938 into Germany, without the consent of the elected government, and in the view of most observers — he chose that phrase precisely, not wanting to claim more authority than the historical record provided — against the will of most of the population. He had left Vienna in March 1938, the same month. He had gone to Paris. After the fall of France in 1940 he had gone to London. In the autumn of 1940 he had arrived in Cambridge, Massachusetts, on the rolls of the Emergency Committee in Aid of Displaced Foreign Scholars.

He heard his own voice managing the room's acoustics. The library was smaller than a lecture hall; the sound returned to him more quickly, and the board members were closer than students, and there was no lectern. He was sitting, which was different. He spoke at the same pace he would have used for a lecture — measured, allowing time for the note-takers to keep up, neither rushing toward the difficult material nor lingering before it.

"I was interned at Sherbrooke, Quebec," he said. "Camp N, for civilian internees — enemy aliens, in the administrative language of the period. The internment lasted approximately nine months, in 1941. I was released and cleared by both the Canadian and American authorities before taking the position in Coldwater Reach in March of 1942."

He stopped. He did not elaborate on the experience — the specific fact of it was relevant to the board's inquiry; the experience itself was not; and he had learned, over several years of being questioned by various administrative bodies in three countries, that elaboration was rarely requested in good faith, and that the absence of elaboration was not, in any case that mattered, mistaken for evasion.

"As to organizational membership," he continued, "I hold membership in the Modern Language Association and in the Linguistic Society of America. I am not a member of any political organization. My field of study is philology. I am a philologist."

The room was quiet in the way rooms went quiet when people were deciding what they were permitted to say next.

---

A board member to Aldrich's right — a woman named Ansel, whose family ran the feed store on the south end of Reach Road, and whose son Marcus had not taught because the boy had graduated in the spring of 1942 and gone directly to the Navy — said: "You mentioned Paris, Dr. Klein. What was your situation in France?"

He described the period: the Emergency Committee's Paris office, the academic network, the manner in which the fall of France had accelerated the Committee's effort to move its scholars to England and then America. He described it in the same flat register he had used for the rest, because the period in Paris had contained things that were not appropriate for this room, and because the things that were appropriate could be stated accurately in that register, and because the register was, in any case, his.

She nodded and wrote something.

Another board member — second from the left, a man with a gray mustache who had not spoken yet — said: "Do you have family in Austria, Dr. Klein?"

Marcus looked at him.

The question was, in structure, the simplest question that had been asked. A single subordinate clause. An answer that was, structurally, a single syllable or a brief phrase. He had been in this vicinity before. He knew the quality of the ground.

"I did," he said.

The board member looked at him. Marcus watched something change in the man's face — not sympathy, or not only that, but a kind of recognition that the footing had shifted. The man's pen was in his hand. He did not write.

He did not follow up.

Aldrich looked at the table. The room held itself for a moment longer than usual before someone's chair shifted slightly and the proceeding continued.

---

Near the end — after two board members had asked about his formal qualifications and he had answered them, after Aldrich had asked whether the Emergency Committee had documentation of his cleared status and Marcus had produced the provost marshal's release from the portfolio and placed it on the table — a man near the head of the table on Aldrich's left said: "I'll put it plainly. Do you consider yourself loyal to the United States of America?"

Marcus considered the word *loyal*.

He considered it for the length of time it took to consider a word seriously: two or three seconds, which was not long by a clock but was long enough in a formal proceeding that several board members looked up from their papers to see if he would answer.

*Loyal* came through French from the Latin *legalis* — lawful, of the law — from *lex*, the binding rule. In its feudal application it had named the bond between a person and the authority to whom that person was subject; one was loyal *to* a lord, a sovereign, an institution, by virtue of belonging under that authority's dispensation. The question it answered was not *what do you do* but *to whom do you belong.*

"I consider myself here," Marcus said. "Teaching your children German and Latin. And I expect to be here next term as well."

The man who had asked the question looked at him for a moment. He wrote something on his paper. He looked at Aldrich.

Aldrich looked at the board and said: "I think we've addressed the substance of the petition. Is there other business before we move to discussion?"

There was not. He thanked Marcus for appearing and asked him to wait in the corridor while the board deliberated.

---

The corridor was lit by three overhead fixtures. The third one, near the library door, had a loose contact; it had been flickering for two weeks, during which Marcus had noted it flickering on his way to the Latin class that met in the room opposite. It was not flickering tonight. He stood under it and looked down the corridor toward the main entrance and waited.

They deliberated for eleven minutes. He counted. At eleven minutes Aldrich opened the library door and asked him to come back in.

Five in favor of maintaining his employment. Two opposed. The result was formal and final; Aldrich read it into the record. He said the review was concluded. He thanked Marcus again, using the word *forthright*, which Marcus received without comment.

He collected his documents from the table — the portfolio open now, the papers returned in their order — and put on his coat. He said good evening to the room. Several board members said good evening in return; one did not look up; Edna Locke, from her school chair at the far end, held his look for a moment and then looked at her folded hands.

He went out.

---

Henry was in the corridor.

He was working from the far end, his mop in long even strokes, the wet head spreading ahead of him across the linoleum, the bucket on its wheeled base behind him at the corridor's far end. He had not looked up when the library door opened.

Marcus walked the length of the corridor. His footsteps were audible on the linoleum. The flickering overhead fixture was steady. He reached the main door.

"Five to two," Henry said. He did not pause the mop.

"Yes," Marcus said.

"Better than I expected."

"Than I expected as well."

Henry drew the mop back and sent it forward in the same even stroke, the head fanning the width of the corridor, spreading the clean water in a pale arc.

Marcus pushed open the main door and went out into the cold.

---

The November air had the density it acquired before snow — not the clean cold of a clear night but the held, flattened cold of a sky that had come down and stayed. The clouds were not visible as clouds; they were visible as the absence of stars, the low ceiling that absorbed the town's light without giving any of it back. There would be snow by the weekend. He had been in New England long enough to know the quality of this particular dark.

He turned up Reach Road. The school was behind him. Ahead: the general store lit, everything else dark.

He thought about *loyal*.

He had given the board a substitution: *I am here* for *I belong to you.* It was not evasion — evasion would have been to give them the word they wanted attached to a sentiment he did not feel. He had chosen to be here; he had declined to leave when leaving had been available to him; he had sat in a school board review at six o'clock on a Tuesday evening in November and answered questions in the board's order, with documents, and would continue to teach German and Latin to the children of Coldwater Reach in the spring.

That was not what the man at the table had meant by loyal. The man at the table had meant something closer to the feudal sense: to whom do you belong, where does your allegiance finally rest, who are you ultimately. Marcus had been asked versions of that question in Vienna, in Paris, in London, in an administrative building in Sherbrooke Quebec, and he had answered it differently each time, and none of the answers had been wrong but none of them had been complete, because the question assumed a kind of settled relationship to sovereignty and to belonging that he had not had since 1937 and perhaps had not fully had even then — had not had since he had understood, watching the early political agitation in Vienna, what the question was eventually going to be used for.

He was not sure this was the same as *heimisch* — the condition of being at home in a place in the way a person was at home who had never needed to decide to be there. He did not think it was. He thought it was the thing before *heimisch*: the act of placing yourself in the vicinity of something that might, over time, become the condition. It was a practice, not a state. He was practicing it.

The Crow house on Birch Street had its kitchen light on, as it always did at this hour. He went up the porch steps and knocked once — his habit, though neither Henry nor Lillian had ever suggested it was necessary, and Lillian had said explicitly on two occasions that it was not — and opened the door.

The kitchen was warm. It smelled of something that had been on the stove in the afternoon and been put aside and not quite cleared, the smell of a household that cooked in quantity and ate the surplus with satisfaction. Lillian was at the kitchen table with her mending basket, a cotton nightgown half-done in her lap. She looked up at him with the expression she had that contained patience and information at once — she knew; Edna Locke had told her, or Henry had sent word, or she had simply sat in the kitchen long enough for the news to find its way through the walls of the house.

He set the portfolio on the chair inside the door. He hung his coat on the hook beside it.

"Five to two," he said.

She nodded once. The needle continued through the cloth. She said: "Sit down and I'll get you something."

He sat down at the table. She put the mending aside and went to the stove and he heard the lid of a pot come off and the sound of the ladle, and she set a bowl in front of him without ceremony — soup, dense with potato and whatever the week's ration had allowed — and went back to her chair and picked up the mending and did not look at him in a way that was not unconcern but rather the opposite.

He ate. The kitchen was warm. Outside, the November sky was settling toward its decision — the clouds low and patient, the cold accumulating, the weekend's snow organizing itself in the dark above the town. The two votes that had gone against him were still two votes, and would remain two votes; the men who had cast them would still be on the school board when the veterans came back, and what happened at the next review, if there was a next review, was not a question for tonight. Tonight the soup was hot and Lillian's needle moved in and out of the cotton and the kitchen light was on and the house was warm and he was in it.

He finished the soup. He said: "Thank you, Lillian."

She said: "There's more," and he shook his head and she said: "All right, then," and she went on with the mending, and outside the cold continued to assemble itself toward snow.
