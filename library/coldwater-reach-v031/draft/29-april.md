# April

She was copying arrival figures to the weekly report — seven vessels in the first two weeks of April, noted by type and date in the arrival log, to be totaled in the report — when she heard the first lobster boat of the morning come in from the near grounds and throttle back for the harbor mouth. She glanced at the clock above the duty log. Nearly half past eight. The boats had been going out before six this past week, which was the first full week of real spring work since the ice broke out of the inner anchorage in late March.

Through the east window the anchorage was the particular color it was in April — not the winter green-black and not the summer blue, but something between, a clarity the cold did its work to produce and left behind when it went. She had noted the breakout date in the harbor log on March 24, a Saturday, the ice going out in a northwest blow that sent the pans through the harbor mouth overnight. Three weeks now. Two lobster boats had been working the near grounds since the first of April; a third was laid up at its winter mooring with a stern cleat that needed replacing, which she had logged and which Mayhew had not yet addressed. The trap-haulers at the south mooring were active again, the gear stacked on their afterdecks in the mornings when they went out.

The buoy tender was due Thursday. She had its arrival in the incoming schedule in red pencil — first full spring run, coming down from Rockland and working the buoys east. She had the Lighthouse Service notice on the desk behind the report, listing the corrected positions for three buoys in the approach channel that had dragged their moorings through the winter.

She was finishing the arrival total when the telephone rang.

She set down her pen.

"Harbor office," she said.

"Miss Bowman." A man's voice, careful with its consonants, clear on the spring line. "Commander Whitmore. First District, Portland. I hope this is a convenient time."

"Yes," she said.

"I'm calling with the finalization on the October 1943 case. The unidentified decedent, Coldwater Reach."

She turned to the clear edge of the blotter and took up her pencil.

"I'm listening," she said.

"The cross-reference is complete. We've matched the October 1943 decedent against a missing crew report from the SS *Ångermanälven* — Swedish-flagged freighter, operating in North Atlantic coastal waters in the fall of that year. The manifest lists one August Schell, German national, Swedish citizen, as missing on or about the seventh of October 1943 in North Atlantic coastal waters." He paused, a pause of review rather than hesitation. "The district has examined the physical record, the timeline, and the geographic data. They are consistent with the October 1943 decedent from your breakwater."

She wrote the name. *August Schell.*

"The district's determination: August Schell, lost at sea, North Atlantic coastal waters, October 1943. Accidental drowning. Case closed." Another pause, this one transitional — the move from finding to administration. "You'll have the written notice and the formal determination for your permanent file within the week."

"Thank you, Commander," she said.

"The notice will include the First District case number and a copy of the cross-reference summary for your records. If you have questions when it arrives, the district office is the right contact."

"Understood."

The line had the quality of a clear spring day — steadier than the winter connections, the static quieter. She could hear, faintly through the receiver, the sound of papers on a desk.

"Thank you for your patience with the inquiry, Miss Bowman," he said. "These cross-referencing procedures take longer than anyone would prefer."

"Yes," she said. "Thank you, Commander."

He said good day. She said good day. The line was clear.

---

She hung the handset in its cradle.

She took the harbor log down from the shelf — the current volume, running from January 1943 — and turned it back to October. The incident from the thirteenth occupied the bottom third of the page: date, hour, nature of the event, response, disposition. The disposition column read *Transferred to First District USCG custody per standing protocol. Case referred to district for identification.* The vessel traffic column for the night of October 9 through 13 still carried the dash.

She set the volume flat and found the margin below the October 13 entry. She wrote:

*Cross-reference completed, April 1945. Decedent identified as August Schell, German national, Swedish citizen, crew of SS* Ångermanälven, *reported missing North Atlantic coastal waters on or about October 7, 1943. First District determination: lost at sea, accidental drowning. Case closed. — E.M. Bowman, HM*

She set the pen down. She read the entry back once. It said what it said.

She closed the harbor log and returned it to the shelf.

---

She crossed to the chart cabinet and opened the pending drawer — bottom, right side.

Her current working files were at the front: the Lighthouse Service notice on the spring buoy positions, the fuel oil delivery receipt for March she had not yet filed, the selectmen's fee schedule revision request. She moved these to the side.

Owen's two folders were at the back where they had always been, in his hand on the tabs: *Lighthouse Service correspondence, 1934.* *Tidal survey, pending, 1935.* She did not touch either of them.

In front of his folders, where she had placed it the fourteenth of September last year: the addendum. A folded document, three panels, the harbor office heading visible at the top edge of the upper fold.

She took it out and unfolded it on the desk.

*Addendum to Incident Report, October 13, 1943 (Unidentified Decedent, Breakwater).*
*September 14, 1944.*

She read through it. The handwriting was her September handwriting — even, compressed, slightly smaller than her correspondence hand because that was how she wrote in official documents. Six pages on harbor office stationery, the ink still dark. The bridle inspection that had put her on the pier at 0130. The individual at the far end of the public pier: canvas bag over the right shoulder, maritime working dress, good physical condition, not a known user of the harbor. The sound from the Macomber's mooring. The five minutes at the starboard chock. The bridle adjusted and confirmed. Looking back toward the far end and finding the pier empty. *The undersigned assumed that he had taken shelter in the pier shed, which was standing open.*

She had known, writing that sentence, that it was not an assumption she had actually made. *No entry was made in the vessel traffic log. No further investigation was conducted.*

She read those two sentences and moved on. Her signature at the bottom of the last page, and the date.

She folded the addendum back in three — the two parallel creases, corners squared — and set it on the desk.

She reached to the working shelf for the personal log: the small cloth-bound notebook, black cover, that she had started in November 1943 and finished the evening after Whitmore's July visit. Fourteen pages in her hand. She opened it to the first.

*November 6, 1943. The CG has the body. The dash in the October 13 log stands. Inquiry?*

She read through to the last entry: *Cross-reference to Ångermanälven pending. Schell, August, German national, Swedish citizen. Note: does not change what is in the pending drawer. That stays until the case closes one way.*

She closed the notebook.

---

She put on her coat and went out.

The April sun was full on the pier, the boards dry, the float at the ladder riding at mid-tide on its proper line. She walked past the pier shed without stopping and started down the breakwater.

Three hundred and forty feet, two sections, the gap between them showing the southeast swell running low and regular on the outside face. She walked it. The cap was dry except in two places in the middle section she already had on the maintenance list; the hand cable was sound on both sides; the capstones at the far end had not shifted. She reached the end and stopped.

The April sea. The small swell lifted against the breakwater's foot and let back with the soft sound of calm water on granite — a sound she had heard ten thousand times, this sound in particular, the particular contact of this weight of water with this angle of stone. Below the surface the weed moved in long slow shifts. On the outer face of the seaward section, the wrack line from the winter's high water ran brown and horizontal, eighteen inches above the current waterline, the storms' record.

She could see the approach channel from here — the two gear buoys strung out toward the mouth, the fairway buoy on the south side that the service tender had repositioned in March. The northeast point where the harbor's outer granite head turned the swell was visible clearly in the April light. Inland, the town ridge, the upper maples showing the first new green.

The tidal set from a position north of the harbor mouth over three days, with the swell running southeast: the body would have come in against the north face of the seaward section and settled into the angle between sections at this end. She had worked it through in November of 1943 and had not needed to work it again. The body had come in at this end and she had been the first person from the harbor to find it.

She stood.

She had been at this end of the dock in the early hours of October 10 looking toward the far end of the public pier, and she had walked back across the pier to the harbor office, and three days later there had been a body at the angle of the granite below where she was now standing, and she had filed what she had seen and what she had not done in a document that was now in her coat pocket.

The swell lifted and let back. Above the harbor the ridge was fully in the morning sun.

She turned and walked back.

---

Inside, she crossed to the permanent files cabinet against the north wall — the gray steel four-drawer that the Lighthouse Service had standardized across the Maine district stations, delivered to the Coldwater Reach harbor office in 1938. Three drawers held the district correspondence in order by year. She pulled the bottom drawer.

The tabs ran forward from the back: vessel inspection reports from 1937, the ice damage incident from February 1943, the *Calvert* grounding from that August. At the front of the drawer, a tab she had made from card stock the previous September: *Schell, August — Incident October 1943.* Behind the tab was the single Lighthouse Service cross-reference form she had completed in August at the district's request.

She filed the addendum behind the tab.

She filed the notebook behind the addendum. She settled both until they lay flat.

She closed the drawer.

---

She made coffee.

The coffeepot had been on the shelf since morning. She refilled it from the water can, measured the grounds from the tin — the general store order, which she placed monthly — and set it on the portable burner. While it heated she went back to the desk and the weekly report. The arrival log total was done. She moved to the mooring-field count.

The mooring ledger had the figures: fourteen active moorings, down from sixteen at the end of last season, the two vacancies still standing from the fall. She copied the count into the report.

The coffee came up. She poured a cup and set it beside the report.

The approach buoy notation was one line. She wrote it. She wrote the harbor master's signature at the bottom of the form and the date. The weekly report was done.

She drank the coffee.

The anchorage was quiet at mid-morning, the two boats in from the near grounds now at their moorings, the third still laid up with its unresolved stern cleat. The water had taken on the slight midday chop of an April southerly — nothing, a small agitation, the kind of day the harbor logged as calm. One of the trap-haulers was leaving the south mooring on a late start, its exhaust carrying across the flat water.

She set the empty cup on the draining board and went to the desk for the Lighthouse Service notice on the buoy positions, which she needed to review against the tender's work schedule before Thursday.

She was reading it when she heard the buoy tender.

The engine note: a slower, heavier frequency than the lobster boats, a frequency that belonged to a vessel of that tonnage running at service speed in a flat harbor approach. She was at the east window.

The tender came around the northeast point — the granite head that was the harbor's outer protection on the northeast side — at twenty past ten. She was running her Lighthouse Service ensign and the service pennant, both clear in the April air, the flag staff at the bow picking up the morning light. Her hull was the standard service gray. The working deck forward of the wheelhouse was staged for the run: the davit rigged, locker hatches open, the foredeck crew in their working positions for a harbor approach. First full spring run.

She found the vessel arrival log on the desk and picked it up.

She put on her coat.

The buoy tender was through the harbor mouth and making for the pier when she came out of the harbor office and started down the dock to meet it.
