# Before the Day

The barn smell was different now — warmer, animal, and underneath the animal smell something she could only call spring: not sweet, more like something tamped down all winter finally releasing. She hung the lantern on the hook by the near stall and went down the row.

The cows had come in from the pasture on their own at milking hour, which they had been doing for four days since they went out. The first morning, she had watched from the yard: the gate opened and they stood in it for a moment — Hilde first, looking at nothing in particular — and then Hilde went through and the others followed without hurrying, spreading out across the wet grass as if they had been told to take their time. The pasture was very green. Magda had stood at the kitchen window in her housedress watching them until the last one cleared the near fence. She had not said anything. Hannah had gone inside for coffee.

Now the cows were outside by day and in by night and would be until October. She went down the row with the hay and set up for milking.

It was quieter than winter. The thick cold was gone. Through the boards she could see gray-pink light beginning over the east ridge, and she could hear — or thought she could — the crows starting, earlier every morning now. She did the milking in the usual order: Bess first, then Maren, then the others. The pails filled. The barn had its own warmth now, the stored warmth of animals overnight, different from the kerosene warmth of winter mornings when you brought in a heater. She noticed this — the warmth that required nothing from her, just the bodies of fifteen cows in a closed space through the night.

By the time she finished the sky through the gaps in the boards was gray. She carried the pails to the cooler, hung the lantern back on its hook, and went out.

---

The kitchen garden was already occupied.

Her mother was kneeling at the near bed, working something with her hands. The garden fork was propped against the fence post at the corner. Hannah set the milk pails on the stone step by the cellar door and crossed the yard.

Magda said — to the soil, not to her: *Die Erde ist noch kalt.*

"Give it a week," Hannah said.

Her mother pressed her palm flat against the earth and held it there. Then she lifted it and looked at the bed she had been preparing for the better part of a week: smoothed and loosened and turned, the clods broken down, the surface raked. It was ready. The earth was still cold.

Magda nodded, not quite at anything, and went back to her hands.

Hannah went inside to put on her coat.

---

The lilac by the front gate had buds — tight and dark, purple-green, barely suggesting what they were going to be. Another week, maybe ten days. She went through the gate and down the road.

She heard them before she saw them: voices in German, direction and acknowledgment, the metal clank of the post driver. They were at the south fence line, all four, doing the spring maintenance. The big one — the schoolteacher from Hannover — was on the driver. Two others held the post. Karl was carrying a wire spool across the field toward them.

She went fifty yards down the road before she slowed. He had seen her; she knew by the small adjustment of his walk, nothing performed about it, just the noticing. He set the spool down and came to the fence line, the others not looking up from the driver. The old pasture wire between them was low — lower than the yard fence — and she could see him clearly. The denim jacket the Army had issued him was worn at the shoulder seams. He had been on this farm eighteen months.

He said: "Your German is better."

She said: "Your English is better."

He said: "Yes."

They stood. On her side the road bank, still damp at the root line. On his side the cows had already been across the grass that morning and marked it. She looked at the ridge. The trees there were at the edge of something — a haze across the branches, not leaves yet, just the willingness. From here you could not see the harbor. The ridge was in the way. She knew where the harbor was. She had stood at the top of that ridge in October with Karl three feet to her right on the outside of the fence and Lonzo's chair empty back by the icehouse, and they had looked down through the trees at the breakwater light and the gray water past it, very large in the dark.

She looked at the ridge. Karl looked at the ridge.

Neither of them said anything about October.

After a moment she said: "The wire at the north gate corner is going."

He said: "I know. We'll get to it this afternoon."

She nodded. He went back to the spool. She walked on to school.

---

She was wearing Rolf's coat.

It had been two sizes too large when she took it from his closet in November of 1943 — the week after the POWs arrived, and Rolf already at Camp Edwards, so there was no one to ask. She had worn it that way through a year and more: shoulders past her shoulders, sleeves folded up. By the second fall the sleeves fit. Now the seams sat where seams were supposed to sit. She had grown into it. She had not replaced it and she was not going to.

The three miles to school were the same road she had walked her whole life: the south fence, the Belanger woodlot, the fork where you always went right, the ridge road and down it into Reach Road proper and north along the main street to the old mill building. This time of year the road was dry and her footsteps made a sound. The crabapple trees near the ridge were not in blossom yet, but they were thinking about it, the buds swollen and reddish in the morning light. She passed the Congregational church, the post office, Burleigh's diner with its Tuesday-closed sign still in the window even though it was Wednesday. She kept walking.

---

German was second-to-last period.

There were six of them left in the class. There had been nine in September; Donna Parkhurst and one other had graduated mid-year, and the girl whose father got the Bangor paper mill position had gone with him. The six remaining sat in the front four rows at Klein's request. The room smelled of chalk and beneath it the building smell — old mill wood and cold damp, the particular odor of a structure that had been something else for too long to entirely stop being it.

Klein was reviewing the dative case. He had the declension chart on the board and was going through it with the methodical care he brought to everything, as if the procedure itself were worth attending to. She had been watching him do this for three years and he had not changed in any way she could point to.

*Dem Mann, der Frau, dem Kind.*

She had known this for years before she knew she knew it. Her father declined nouns at the dinner table when he was talking to himself or to Magda without thinking of it as teaching — *mit dem Brot, unter dem Tisch, nach der Schule* — just the way the words went. She had stored it without meaning to. This was how most of what she knew had come to her: through repeated presence, until a thing was part of her without the ceremony of learning.

She looked out the window.

The trees on the ridge were the same ones she had passed that morning.

She thought: *mündig.*

Klein had written the word on this board a year ago, last April, and she had barely been paying attention — Karl had said *Glückwunsch* at the fence that afternoon, without ceremony, just because it was her birthday, and her mind had already been partly there at the fence, partly at the afternoon. But the word had stayed. It was the kind that stays.

The state of having your own mouth. The age of speaking for yourself, legally, in full responsibility for what you said.

She thought about what she had said and not said in the year since.

The word-cards. Cedar rectangles she had cut with her father's knife and inked with his good pen, English on one side and German on the other, slid under the fence post stone through two winters. They had stopped making new ones in the fall — not because anything ended but because the words had moved off the paper into conversation, and conversation had moved into something she still did not have a name for: the understanding that builds out of enough accumulated mornings, the way a house fills with the sounds of the people in it until the sounds are just the house. She knew where the cards were. In the wooden box under Karl's bunk, with his papers, the photograph of his mother, and the Red Cross letter he had read twice and not taken out again.

She knew his father's name. *Friedrich.* Friedrich Reinmar. She had not said it to anyone — not in a letter to Rolf, not to her parents at the dinner table, not to Lonzo Reed, not to anyone in this room. She was holding the name the way she held a few other things: not pressing, not requiring anything, just present and hers. She had learned it in January, at the north fence, when Karl told her matter-of-factly — *mein Vater ist gestorben* — and she had said *das tut mir leid* and meant it, and walked back to the house and held the name through supper and through her mother's small careful sentences about the pipes and the cellar.

She had written Rolf in February. She had said: potatoes held well, weather has been hard, Mutti and Vati are fine. She had not said: Mutti is talking again, small sentences, the carrots in the cellar and what to do with the soft ones. She had not said anything about Karl's father, or the October ridge, or the harbor lights at night and the way the water had looked from up there, very large and going on.

These were not accidents. She had read back what she had written and known what was missing and folded the letter and sent it anyway.

Klein called on her. She came back to the room.

*Den Häusern,* she said. The dative plural.

He nodded and moved on.

---

Three weeks until graduation.

She had been thinking since January about nursing school in Bangor. Eastern Maine General took girls right out of high school — the pamphlet at the post office had been clear about that — and the program ran two years and you came out knowing how to do specific things under pressure and with precision. She was good at that. She had been practicing it her whole life without calling it by a name.

She had also been thinking about staying. Not because she couldn't imagine leaving. The farm was not a trap; she had never felt it as one. But she could imagine both equally and neither one pressed, and the farm needed what it needed: Albrecht's back had been bad since October, the cows were fifteen and not twelve, and Rolf would come home when the war ended and the farm would be four people again instead of three.

And then there was the question of what happened to Karl when repatriation came.

It would come. The radio made this clear enough for anyone paying attention: the Allies were across the Rhine, the German positions were losing the shape of positions, and when it ended — when it did — the eight thousand German POWs scattered across Maine on satellite details would need to go home, eventually, after the Army worked through the paperwork. It might be months. It might be longer. But Karl would go back to Regensburg, or to whatever Regensburg was by then. She did not know how much of it was standing. The radio said things about German cities but often not which ones specifically, or to what degree. His father's shop might be there or it might not. The tile floor, the east window. She had heard about these from Karl in October, at the ridge, in the way things come out when you are standing somewhere high and looking at something large and your language and his language are both almost sufficient to what you mean to say.

She did not know if she would write to him after.

She had thought about it enough to know she could not know in advance. There might not be an address, or not a reliable one. There might be reasons a letter went nowhere useful. She had decided not to try to work this out before it arrived. The future had its own business and she had hers. When repatriation came she would find out what it meant and decide then.

She picked up her books when the bell rang.

---

Her mother was still in the garden.

The spare trowel was in the bucket by the farm gate, the same bucket that held the stakes and string and the rest of the small tools that lived outside from April through October. Hannah picked it up on her way through, not deciding to, just picking it up. She went across the yard to the bed where her mother was kneeling.

Magda had the seed tray across her knees — the old one with the broken corner, the same one Hannah could remember using as a mudpie base when she was small, before she understood what it was for. In it: onion sets, small and papery, the beginnings of green at their tips. Her mother held the tray steady, then held it out.

*Hier,* she said. Here.

Hannah took the sets. She turned to the bed — dark and worked and cold, the cold that went but went slowly this time of year — and pressed the first one in with her thumb. Blunt end down. An inch and a half. The soil came under her fingernails. She moved along the row.

Her mother worked beside her. The light was going soft, the April light at this hour flattening as the sun dropped toward the ridge. The cows were somewhere out past the near fence in the green pasture. The lilac by the front gate had buds that in a week or ten days would open and smell like themselves, which was a thing she could never hold in her mind between years, only recognize when it came back.

The war had not ended yet. The radio gave you conclusions to draw, not dates. What was hers was this: the earth under her hands, still cold and giving slightly to her weight, and the onion sets going in one by one, and her mother's breathing beside her, and the whole summer ahead of them in which those sets would come up.

She pressed another set in and moved down the row.
