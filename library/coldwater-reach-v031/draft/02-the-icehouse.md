# The Icehouse

She had her hands on Berta when she heard it.

Not a farm truck — the note was wrong, too low and too heavy, the kind of engine that didn't need to hurry because it weighed enough not to. The road from the county line came past the woodlot before the farm, and in October with the leaves thinned she could sometimes hear vehicles before they turned into the drive. She heard this one for a full half minute before it turned.

Berta shifted. Hannah kept her hands where they were.

The barn smelled the way it always smelled in the morning and in the late afternoon both: hide and manure and the particular vegetable sweetness of the hay in the upper mow, and underneath all of it the cold, which was October now, coming up through the concrete in the floor and pressing through the board-and-batten of the south wall whenever the wind had anything in it. By December the cold would be something else — something that worked on you instead of just sitting there — but for now it was only present, only a reminder of what was coming. She had left her coat on the hook by the stall partition. Her hands were warmed from the work.

The truck stopped somewhere past the barn. Then the engine cut.

She gave Berta another thirty seconds and then moved to the gap in the boards.

She had known about the gap since she was nine years old. It was between the fourth and fifth boards on the east wall, knee-high when she was nine and now at her waist, and it gave her a narrow rectangle of the yard and the lane and the icehouse front.

The truck was Army. Olive drab, a canvas-covered bed, two soldiers in the cab. Another one standing in the truck bed with his back against the cab wall, rifle at sling arms. He was young — younger than she'd expected, though she hadn't known what she'd expected until she saw him and understood he wasn't it. The four men were already stepping down from the bed.

They did it without being told to. That was the first thing she noticed: no order, no gesture from the soldier, just the four of them stepping down in sequence with the practiced ease of men who had done this exact thing before, in other truck beds, in other farm lanes. They wore Army-issue denim — work clothes, the same cut on all four, none of them quite fitting. The denim was a pale blue-gray that in the October afternoon light looked almost colorless.

She cataloged them.

Three older men — not old but older, in the way that forty-something looked when the work had been physical for long enough. The first was heavyset, gray-bearded, with the settled quality of a man whose size had been a fact of his life long enough that he'd forgotten it. The second was very large, the kind of large that made him conspicuous in the denim as though the clothes had been sized for the idea of a man rather than this particular one. The third moved carefully when he stepped down from the truck, favoring his right leg, setting his weight with the deliberateness of someone who had learned to be deliberate because the alternative was pain.

The fourth was young.

She knew how old she was, which was seventeen, and she knew the arithmetic: he was maybe two years older than she was. Maybe three. He stepped down last, after the careful man, and stood in the lane with his hands at his sides and looked at the icehouse. Not at the barn. Not at the farmhouse. At the icehouse, which was what he would be living in, and which was a long low building of board-and-batten she had grown up next to and taken entirely for granted until three days ago when a man from the Army had come to talk to her father.

She had seen that look before. She had seen it on Rolf's face the morning he left for Bangor on the bus, standing in the yard with his bag, looking at the farmhouse. He had been nineteen.

---

Lonzo Reed came down from the passenger cab last. He took the time to find his footing on the running board, his left hand on the door frame, his right holding the rifle by the action so the barrel cleared the truck frame as he stepped down. He put his weight on the right leg first. He stood still for a moment with one hand still on the door, letting whatever was happening in his knee finish happening, and then he straightened and walked around the front of the truck to where the four men were standing.

He was not a tall man. He was not, as far as Hannah could see, a frightening one. He had the rifle across the crook of his arm now, the way you carry a rifle when you're not pointing it at anyone but you haven't put it down. He looked at the four men and then he looked at the icehouse and then he turned and said something to the younger soldier, who reached into the truck bed and produced a folding canvas camp chair and carried it to the side of the icehouse door and opened it.

Lonzo sat down in it. He put the rifle across his knees.

That was all. That was apparently what he did.

---

Her father was in the farmhouse doorway.

She had not seen him come out, but he was there: in the door frame with one hand on the jamb, not coming down the porch steps, not crossing the yard. He watched the truck the way he watched most things that came onto the farm from outside — with the kind of attention that was absolute and gave nothing back. The younger soldier came up the porch steps with a clipboard. Her father looked at it. He took the pen the soldier held out and signed where the soldier indicated and handed the pen back and then he was looking at the yard again, at the four men in their denim, at Lonzo in his chair, and he had not said one word.

The soldier went back to the truck.

Hannah moved her hands back to Berta without meaning to. Berta made a sound that was impatience.

Gut, she thought, gut, ich komme schon.

The older soldier gestured toward the icehouse door. The four men went. The careful man went first, the young one last. Before he went through the door the young one turned and looked at the farmhouse for a moment — not at anything specific, just at the building itself, the way he had looked at the icehouse — and then he went inside.

The door closed.

The two soldiers got back in the truck. The engine turned over, the olive-drab bulk swung around in the lane, and the truck went back down the road through the woodlot and the sound of it faded away.

Lonzo Reed sat in his chair. He did not appear to be doing anything in particular. He had his hands on the rifle across his knees and he was looking at the icehouse door, and his expression was the expression of a man who had made his arrangements for a long afternoon.

Her father went back inside the farmhouse.

The yard was the yard. The icehouse was the icehouse. The October light was coming down the way it did in the late afternoon, going amber in the lower quadrant, and Lonzo Reed sat in his chair in it and the icehouse door was closed.

Hannah went back to the milking.

---

The light was different when she carried the pails out to the cooler.

Lonzo Reed was still in his chair. He looked up when the barn door opened and then looked back at the icehouse when he saw it was only her. She did not look at him. She carried the pails across the yard with her eyes on the cooler building and went in and set the pails and came back out and the yard was quiet in the October afternoon, just the wind in the woodlot and the sound of the cows settling inside and Lonzo Reed in his chair with the rifle across his knees.

She went inside.

---

Her mother was at the stove when Hannah came into the kitchen. She had been at the stove for an hour, from the smell of it — root vegetables and the end of a ham bone, the smell of a soup that had been going long enough to have opinions. She did not look up when Hannah came in. She moved from the stove to the table and back with the precision of a person who had made the same circuit ten thousand times and required nothing to be in any position other than where it always was, and did not acknowledge the difference between a kitchen with another person in it and a kitchen without.

Hannah washed her hands at the sink and stood and listened to the sound of the soup.

Her father came in from the front of the house and sat down at the table. He had been somewhere — upstairs, the front room — but he brought nothing back with him, no book, no newspaper, nothing in his hands. He sat and put his hands flat on the table and looked at them. His hands were large and rough and he had worn them down over twenty years of farm work into something that no longer looked like the hands of a man who had come from a town to do this — they looked like they had always been farm hands, except she had seen a photograph once of him before, younger than she could imagine, with the hands of someone else.

*Das Essen wird fertig sein*, her mother said, without turning from the stove. The food will be ready.

Her father said nothing. He looked at his hands on the table.

They ate. Her mother had made the soup and also bread, dark bread from the rye flour, and there was butter for it because they made their own and the rationing only bit so deep into what you produced yourself. Her father ate with the systematic attention he brought to eating, which was the same attention he brought to most things: complete, and turned inward.

After a while he said: *Die Arbeit, die sie machen sollen, ist gut.*
The work they're supposed to do. It's good work.

He was not talking to either of them. He was stating a fact in the way he stated facts that he had been working out on his own and arrived at a conclusion about. He said: *Der Zaun auf der Nordweide muss repariert werden. Sie werden damit anfangen.* The fence on the north pasture needs to be repaired. They will start with that.

Her mother put down her spoon and picked it up again. That was all.

Hannah ate her soup. She had not told either of them what she had seen through the boards — the four men stepping down, the young one looking at the icehouse, the particular way Lonzo Reed had lowered himself out of the cab. Her father knew more than she did; he had signed the papers. Her mother knew whatever she knew, which she kept the way she kept everything.

*Wie viele Kilometer ist es von Straubing nach Regensburg?* she thought, which surprised her — she had not meant to think that, had not known she was going to, but there it was: how many kilometers from Straubing to Regensburg? She had looked at a map of Bavaria in her geography book once, years ago. The cities were close together. They were both on rivers that were the same river or near enough. She could not remember the number.

She thought about the young one's face when he had turned to look at the farmhouse before going in.

She could not have said what his expression was. Not frightened, she thought. Not angry. Something else — something that didn't yet have a name.

*Schau nicht so*, her mother said — don't look like that — without looking at her. Hannah looked down at her soup. She had not known she was making a face.

Her father said: *Morgen früh.* Tomorrow morning.

---

Rolf's room was hers now, which meant it was the room she slept in and the room that had Rolf's coat on the hook by the door. She had moved nothing when they gave it to her — his room was bigger, and it had the east window that caught the first light, and when her parents suggested the move she had agreed because it was the larger room and not because she wanted his things, and then she had moved in and not moved his things and now they were hers in the way everything becomes yours if you live with it long enough, which was to say they were still Rolf's.

The coat was on the hook. She changed into her nightclothes and stood at the east window in the dark. The yard below: the barn, the icehouse, the woodlot beyond. There was no light in the icehouse. There was no light in the yard except what came through the farmhouse windows. Lonzo Reed's chair was gone from beside the icehouse door — someone must have brought it inside, or taken it away at shift's end. She hadn't heard another truck. She didn't know how it worked. She didn't know any of the particulars of how this was going to work.

She thought: fremd.

It was one of her father's words, one he used when he meant something that was foreign or strange or not-from-here — a word that had three meanings that were almost the same meaning, and she had grown up hearing it applied to things that arrived from outside: a new variety of seed, a piece of machinery, a way of doing something they'd heard about from another farm. Das ist fremd. That is strange. That is not from here.

She did not apply it to anyone in particular.

The icehouse was dark. The woodlot moved in the wind.

She got into bed in Rolf's room, in the dark, and the word sat there for a while, and then she was asleep.
