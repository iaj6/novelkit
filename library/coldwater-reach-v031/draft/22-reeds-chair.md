# Reed's Chair

The first bunk: the gray-bearded one, flat on his back, arms at his sides, breathing like a man with opinions about sleep who has gone to the trouble of holding them. The second bunk: the big one, the one with the Bible — a German-language Bible they'd been issued by the Army, who knew where it thought they were going. He was reading it by lantern, which was more than Lonzo did with his Bible, which he hadn't opened since his mother's funeral in 1929. The third bunk: the one with the bad knees, on his side, his face to the wall.

The fourth bunk: empty. Blanket folded back, folded the way it was always folded, the way the youngest one made his bed every morning with the particular care of a person who had been taught to do it and had decided to keep doing it even here. On the crate that served as a shelf, a cedar piece with a word on it — HARBOR — turned English-side up in the lamplight.

He held the lantern up a little longer. As if looking again would change the arithmetic.

One. Two. Three.

He counted again. One. Two. Three.

He stood in the middle of the icehouse floor for what felt like a reasonable interval. The cold that the sawdust walls couldn't entirely stop came off the floor in the way it always did, working up through the soles of his boots. The gray-bearded one kept breathing. The big one turned a page.

---

Outside. The headcount book was in his coat pocket, same place it always was. He opened it against the icehouse door, found the October line, found the column. He kept his pencil sharpened — habit from twenty years of logging trap hauls, where a dull pencil in wet weather was nobody's friend. He looked at the column for the youngest one's name. He looked at it for what it would cost to put an accurate mark there.

He wrote: *Present.*

He wrote the date. He wrote *L. Reed, CP.*

He put the book back in his coat pocket and went to sit in his chair.

---

The chair was a folding camp model, canvas back and seat, that he'd been using since the arrangement started in November of 1943. Eleven months in this chair, give or take. He had a thorough knowledge of the chair by now — the left rear leg's tendency to stick in soft ground, the particular angle you got when you sat all the way back against the canvas versus when you stayed upright. He stayed upright tonight. His knee did not favor the other option.

October. Full dark at nine, which he still hadn't made his peace with, though he supposed he wasn't going to. The farm was quiet except for the cows in the barn, which were restless the way they always were in the first cold — shifting weight, knocking against the stall boards, that sound of large animals disagreeing with a change in weather. He didn't know if they felt the temperature or the smell of it or something he didn't have a name for. He'd asked the Pfeiffer man about it once, in the spring, in the brief period when they spoke English to each other. Pfeiffer hadn't known either, or hadn't known in a language they shared at that particular moment.

The knee told him the temperature. The winch accident had been 1937, on Murchie's boat, and the knee had been in the forecasting business ever since. Tonight it said October and meant it.

---

He had known since July. Not known the way you'd be required to put it in a report — known the way you knew a thing when you'd been sitting in the same chair watching the same yard for eleven months. You learned the yard when nothing was happening. You learned the particular shape of each man's way of moving through it and what each man looked like when the movement had a reason and when it was looking for one. The youngest one had started going to the north fence line more than the work required. The girl had started appearing on the far side of the fence at times that corresponded.

He had watched this by not watching it. There was a way to sit in a chair with your eyes open and your attention distributed across the whole yard that involved not directing your attention at the thing you weren't supposed to be seeing. He had not learned this method on this farm. He had learned it earlier, in other situations — on the boats, mostly, where you understood early that three men in a small space for fourteen hours needed a particular kind of privacy that wasn't available in any material sense and had to be manufactured from attention.

The word-cards had been obvious from the start. Cedar pieces, small enough to pass under a stone at the fence post — he'd seen the exchange more than once, English on one side, German on the other, a side going out and a different side coming back. He didn't read German. He understood what the cards were for.

He had been watching the youngest one for eleven months. What he had seen:

A nineteen-year-old who had been drafted and would have preferred not to have been. From the sound of the German the Pfeiffer man used with him — *der Metzger*, once, the word for a butcher, and the boy nodding the particular nod of someone hearing the name of the thing he was — a butcher's apprentice, from the province the Pfeiffer family was from, which was not a coincidence in any ordinary sense of the word coincidence. Not a fanatic. Not a volunteer. A boy who fixed fences when told and did it right and asked the right questions about the right way to do it — the same way he'd ask about the right way to break down a cut, efficient and attentive. He wanted to go home.

Lonzo took the flask out of his coat pocket and held it. He turned it in his hand and put it back.

What filing the count accurately would cost: the Bangor provost marshal's office would send someone within two days. The man from Bangor would review the arrangement — the farm, the icehouse, the work contract, Lonzo's logs going back to November 1943. Reviews of arrangements, in Lonzo's experience, tended to conclude that the arrangement was the problem. The Pfeiffers would lose the labor. The four men would go somewhere with more supervision per man — not Houlton, somewhere stricter, somewhere appropriate for men whose guard had taken his eye off. The youngest one would go there with the others. Lonzo could not say what happened after that, because what happened after that was not his to say.

He thought about what useful looked like, at sixty-one, with a bad knee, on a farm outside Coldwater Reach, with a folding camp chair and a rifle he hadn't fired at anything in two years. He thought about it the way you thought about whether a particular piece of line had another season in it. You made a judgment. The judgment was either that the line would hold or it wouldn't, and you made your call and lived with what the call cost you.

He sat.

---

At ten-fifteen he heard footsteps on the path from the direction of the ridge road. Light, careful on the gravel — the step of someone who knew how to move quietly. He heard the footsteps come around the barn corner and then the creak of the icehouse door opening and closing, and then nothing.

He did not turn around.

He noted the time by the watch on his wrist. He sat for another quarter hour. The temperature had dropped another degree; the knee confirmed this. The cows had settled. The yard was dark and empty and quiet.

At ten-thirty he took the lantern and went inside.

---

The gray-bearded one: asleep. The big one: Bible closed, face to the wall now. The one with the bad knees: asleep, same position as before.

The youngest one was in his bunk. Eyes shut. Breathing even.

Lonzo looked at him for the length of time it took to be certain of what he was looking at. He did the rest of the lockup and went out.

---

His room was in the Pfeiffer barn — one stall partitioned off with board lumber, a cot from the Bangor provost's supply, a chair and a table that Pfeiffer had provided without discussion when Lonzo arrived in November of 1943. There was a small iron stove that took a piece of kindling and a split of hardwood, which was sufficient for the room's size. He had not lit the stove tonight. He had not yet decided whether the night required it.

He lay down on the cot.

The headcount book was still in his coat pocket. He did not take it out.

*What does it cost*, he thought. *What does it cost.*

He lay there with the question. He did not finish it. It was a question with the shape of something that wanted a number at the end — something you could put in a column and settle. Maybe it cost nothing. Maybe it cost something he didn't have a word for yet. He would find out or he wouldn't, and it would not be tonight.

Somewhere in the barn, one of the cows shifted in her stall with the sound of a large animal redistributing its weight. He closed his eyes.

He went to sleep.
