# The Log

The coal stove in the northeast corner had been going since Eira came in at six, and the office held a working heat — not the kind that invited lingering, but enough to keep the logbook pages from sticking together in the cold. Through the south window the anchorage showed as a darkness differentiated by the riding lights of the two lobster smacks still at mooring, and at the inner anchorage's north end the ice margin had reached six inches past the wood float, close enough that she could hear it working against the float's outer face: a slow, patient knock, the harbor testing its own edges. Now and then a mooring line took the tidal draw and complained against a piling — a low groan, more felt than heard, the sound of the harbor settling into its winter register.

She had come out on a Thursday evening, after supper, and told no one she was going. There was nothing remarkable about the harbormaster working evenings. The harbor was not a nine-to-five enterprise and never had been, and she had worked most of the town's Christmas programs in some capacity or another for eight years. But tonight there was no scheduled work and she had come anyway.

The Congregational church stood a quarter mile up Reach Road, and its steeple lantern was burning for the program. She had seen it on the way down the pier lane — the white steeple light against the December dark. Owen had gone every year they were married. He had a good voice and he liked the hymns, and he would tell her about the program afterward with the slight exaggeration of a man who had genuinely enjoyed himself. She had not gone in eleven years and she was not thinking about it now. The town was occupied. The harbor road was empty. The lamp above the pier shed door held the near ground and nothing else.

---

The October logbooks were in the rolling cabinet beneath the east window: vessel traffic in the left-hand volume, harbor incidents in the right. She pulled October's vessel-traffic book and set it on the desk.

The office had been a pilot house before the town purchased it in 1921 — the same year Owen took the post, before Eira had any relation to it except as a building she passed daily. The conversion had kept the pilot house footprint: twelve feet by fourteen, a space designed for two or three people to work in proximity, not for meetings or long storage. The desk occupied the center. To its left, the chart cabinet with its four drawers latched by the brass tongue-and-groove fittings Owen had fitted after the original latches stripped, and at the bottom left the bolted drawer with its own lock. The tide chart hung pinned above the cabinet with its four brass tacks — the current one December's data, in the Lighthouse Service's standard column format.

Owen's barometer hung on the east wall at the height where Owen had hung it: four inches above what was comfortable for Eira to read without tilting her head. She had tilted her head at it for eight years of monthly calibrations and had not once considered moving it. Below the barometer, the two hooks she had bent from a length of copper pipe salvaged from the *Lettie Morse* the January after he died: her coffee cup on the left hook, the chip at its rim facing the wall, and Owen's cup on the right, washed that morning at the office sink as every morning and set back. The cups were there when she came in and would be there when she locked up and would be there in the morning.

The coal stove settled in the corner with a sound like a long exhale when a piece of coal shifted. She had not opened the pending drawer since September, when she had put Owen's tide tables in it to get them off the working surface.

She had been through the October vessel-traffic log three times before tonight. On the morning of the thirteenth, before Gervais arrived, to prepare the harbor record for his file. On the fourteenth, for her own incident report. In early November, when Pruitt had sat across the desk and asked her to walk him through the vessel traffic for the night of October ninth. Each time she had read the pages with the attention of someone confirming what she already knew.

She turned to the week of October seventh. Went in order.

Lobster smack *Thistle*: Roy Blaine, Coldwater Reach registration, at mooring from October fifth. No movement noted through the period of the blow. Mooring bridle inspected October seventh — her own notation, good condition.

Lobster smack *Doris V.*: Delmar Stannard, Coldwater Reach. At mooring from October third. Same period, no movement.

Lighthouse Service ice barge *Ploughman*: arrived October eighth from Jonesport, anchor in the inner anchorage, single crewman aboard, standard tender call. Departed October fourteenth, after the bar had smoothed. She had logged that departure herself from the pier.

Coaster *Macomber*: Birch Macomber, master; Machias registry; arrived October eighth out of Calais with lime and general goods; berth at the outside face of the main pier. Harbor fee paid October eighth, Macomber's signature on the receipt. She had the receipt in the bolted drawer.

And the departure entry.

She read it again, though she had not forgotten it: *Macomber — departure 0430 — Oct. 11.* Her handwriting. The same hand that had made every other entry in the logbook, the same pen, the same deliberate spacing she had used for harbor records since she learned the format from the Lighthouse Service field manual in 1935.

She did not remember writing it.

---

October eleventh had been the storm's second full day. She had the harbor incident log for that morning: *Weather. NE 40. Arrive 0700. Harbor closed outbound. Inner anchorage holding.* She had made that notation on arrival, before she had done anything else — same as Owen had done, same as she had done every storm watch since she took the post. NE forty knots from the northeast. She had closed the harbor to outbound traffic at 0200 on the tenth, when the wind came on full and the bar began breaking. A coaster drawing eight feet under load could not make the entrance channel in those conditions without real risk.

If the *Macomber* had put out at 0430 on the eleventh, it had gone two and a half hours before she arrived that morning. She did not remember stopping any departure or clearing one through on that date. Stopping a vessel, or passing one out in storm conditions, was logged as a separate event — not folded into the general storm notation. There was no such event log for October eleventh. Either she had been there at 0430 and cleared the *Macomber* out, and the memory had not survived the exhaustion and what came after. Or the *Macomber* had gone without her knowledge, and the departure entry had come from somewhere else.

She had worked through these possibilities in November. She did them again now.

The first: she had been there at 0430. She had come out in the dark on the storm's second night, checked the harbor in the way she did when she couldn't sleep and the wind was too loud to ignore, and had found the *Macomber* making ready to depart, and had cleared it through, and had logged the departure before going home to sleep. This was possible. She had not slept more than three hours combined on the nights of the tenth and the eleventh — the tenth because she had been out checking moorings, the eleventh because the wind had not settled enough to allow more. Exhaustion did things to memory. She knew this from the long storm watches before the war took the harbor's staff: the actions taken in the small hours of a blow sometimes went sideways in the recollection afterward, compressed or simply gone. She had been out on the tenth at 0015 to check the *Macomber*'s bridle — she knew this — and again at 0130 when the blow came on. What happened between 0130 and 0700 she could not account for, hour by hour.

The second possibility was a note. The harbor had no formal overnight watchman since Harlow left in forty-one, and the informal arrangement was the hook beside the pier shed door, where fishermen left slips when they came in or went out at odd hours. Blaine did this regularly. Stannard less often. She had transferred departures and arrivals from these notes to the official record before, more times than she could count; the harbor's reduced wartime traffic made the informal system workable. The slips she did not keep once they were logged. If Blaine had come out before first light and seen the *Macomber* standing out through the entrance channel, and had left a note with the time he reckoned it had gone, and she had transferred the time to the log the next day without thinking — it was possible.

The third possibility gave her the most trouble. Sometime on the fourteenth, going back through the week's logs in preparation for the incident report, she might have noticed that the *Macomber* departure was unconfirmed. She might have written *0430* as a placeholder — a time she estimated from the pattern of Macomber's habits, intending to verify it when the storm business was finished, intending to ask Blaine or Stannard or Macomber himself when the next supply run brought him back to Coldwater. She had not made that inquiry. The body came in on the morning of the thirteenth and everything that followed it was large enough to displace the placeholder from her attention, and the unverified time had stayed in the record as if she had confirmed it.

She did not believe she made unverified entries in the official log. It was her rule and Owen's before her and the Lighthouse Service district's before Owen. She did not put in what she had not witnessed or could not source to a witness. But she had been holding this belief about herself, and tonight she was less certain it excluded the possibility that she had once, in the middle of a storm and a body, done what she said she did not do.

She sat with all three and did not decide between them.

The coal stove settled. The ice at the north anchorage knocked against the float. The mooring lines held their cold tension against the pilings.

What she could not set aside was Birch Macomber himself. She had spoken to him twice in four years — once about the set of his mooring, once about the harbor fee schedule — and in both conversations she had come away with the same impression: a spare man, careful with money, careful with his hull, not given to improvisation. Running lime and general goods along the downeast coast was not a trade that rewarded careless weather decisions; a coaster that put out in conditions the prudent men waited on came home shortened, or didn't come home. A man who had worked this coast for twenty years knew where the bar broke and how the entrance channel set in a nor'easter. He knew all of it and he had gone out anyway, at 0430, with the wind still running hard off the northeast.

She pulled the second left-hand drawer and took out the plain brown notebook — the personal log, not the official record, not submitted and not audited. She had kept it since the second year of the post, when she understood that there were observations the official record was not the correct place for: questions not yet resolved into reportable facts, leads she was not ready to formalize. She uncapped the pen and wrote:

*Macomber — departure Oct. 11, 0430. Machias reg. Reason for departure in storm conditions: unknown. Harbor closed outbound since 0200 Oct. 10. Log entry in my hand, source uncertain. Vessel clear of harbor before 0700. Did not observe departure or clear it through by record. Inquiry?*

She capped the pen. She read the entry once. Then she closed the notebook and put it back in the drawer.

---

The pier lane was cold outside the lamp's reach. She pulled the office door shut behind her and tried the latch once, which was habit even when she knew it had caught, and went up toward Reach Road.

The main street was quiet. The Congregational church at the far end showed its lit windows, and she could hear, at this distance, something that might have been singing — bass notes carrying further than the rest in the cold air. She went the other way.

Her house sat a quarter mile south on Reach Road, second back from the road, with the white porch railing Owen had painted in the last spring of his life. She repainted it every spring whether it needed it or not. Inside: the kitchen table set for one, the back bedroom's door closed, the hooks in the entry hall holding what she put there.

She took off her boots at the door. Owen's oilskin coat — the spare, the one she'd brought home rather than leaving at the office — she hung on the right hook, which was where it had hung for eight years, and went to the kitchen.

The water took its time coming to heat. She stood at the counter and listened to the harbor wind work in the eaves, the particular sound it made around the southeast corner of the house where Owen had never gotten around to repointing the mortar. Then she made the tea in the good china cup — the one that had been her mother's, not the enamel mug — and sat down at the kitchen table with both hands around it.

She did not drink it. She sat with her hands on the cup and felt the heat going out by degrees, and thought about Birch Macomber at the wheel of a loaded coaster in forty-knot weather, pushing out through the entrance channel in the dark before dawn on the second day of a blow, and she thought about what could make a cautious man do that.

When the tea was cold she took the cup to the sink and poured it out and went to bed.
