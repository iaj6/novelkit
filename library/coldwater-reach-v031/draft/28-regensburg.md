# Regensburg

The radio in the provost marshal's field office came through the wall at night when the wind was east, which it was tonight, and Karl lay on his bunk and listened. The announcer's voice was thin through the board-and-batten — place names, troop movements, the particular confidence of American radio voices that was unlike any broadcasting he had heard growing up. Frankfurt. He knew Frankfurt; his father had gone there once in 1939 for a meatpackers' cooperative, had come home with his good hat and a jar of apricot preserves and told Karl that Frankfurt was a city like any city, larger, and that the preserves were finer than anything from the market in Regensburg. *Eine sehr große Stadt.* Cologne. He knew Cologne from the school atlas, the cathedral unmistakable even in a small map — the two towers. He did not know what it looked like now.

Not Regensburg yet. He could draw the line in his head from where the war was to where his city was, and the line was not long.

The wall kept the worst of the cold out. In January the sawdust between the boards had been dense and settled, the cold a fact you stopped noticing because it would not change. In March it was different in a way he could feel but not name exactly — some quality of the air on the other side of the boards, a loosening, as though the season had decided to become the next one before the temperature had agreed. He had rolled his blanket into a cylinder against the wall.

He thought about Regensburg.

The cathedral spire was visible from three miles outside the city. He had not thought about this in some time, and now he did: the moment each time coming back from his grandfather's, when the road crested the rise and before the city itself there was the spire, which meant you were nearly home. The last few kilometers were just the last few kilometers.

The Danube did not run straight at Regensburg. It bent. He had lived his whole life along that bend without knowing what a straight river looked like — had not known there was something to know.

There was a Roman wall along the edge of the old city, gray stone, older than anything else in the city by a very large amount. He had walked past it every morning on the way to his father's shop, running his hand along it sometimes without thinking about it, because it was simply there the way the river was there, the way the spire was there — things that did not require thought because they had always been present and would go on being present.

The shop had a tile floor. Cream-colored tiles with a dark blue border that ran around the edge of the room. They were original to the building; his father had said this not to take pride in it but because it was a fact about the floor.

The shop smelled of blood and sawdust. There was no other smell for a butcher's shop — those smells were in the walls, in the tiles, and when the morning light came through the east window they were what the light moved through.

His father's hands were large. They were certain in the way that came from knowing the work exactly — not hurrying, not performing, just doing it as it needed to be done. He had shown Karl the same cut many times, each time as though he expected Karl to get it right this time, which Karl usually had.

In January the Red Cross notification had come. He had read it twice. Then he had put it in the wooden box under his bunk — the box where he kept his discharge papers, his identification documents, the photograph of his mother from before she died, the first word-cards the girl had given him — and he had not taken it out again. He knew what it said.

*Friedrich Reinmar, gestorben 28. Dezember 1944.*

He looked up. There was a knot in the board directly above his bunk, about the size of a five-pfennig coin, with a pale grain ring around it. He had been looking at that knot since October of 1943.

He did not know what was left of Regensburg. He had known since the previous year that the city had been bombed — from the way the other prisoners went quiet when the radio came through the wall clear, from the particular tone the guards used when Germany came up in conversation. He thought about the cathedral spire. He tried to see it on the road from his grandfather's, the way it rose above the crest of the rise before the city itself came into view, and then he understood that the image he had was from three years ago, and that what stood there now he could not know. The spire might be standing. It might not. He had been setting this thought aside and picking it up again since summer and it was the same thought each time.

He thought about the icehouse. The smell of it — pine pitch, straw, damp wool — was as familiar to him now as the shop had been. He had been here seventeen months, longer. He knew which boards creaked. He knew the cows' schedule the way he had known the schedule of his father's workday: the five o'clock sounds, the particular change in the afternoon air that meant the day had turned. He had walked the fence line here hundreds of times. He knew where the ground went soft in the thaw. He knew which boards on the fence had been replaced and which had not, and that the wire at the corner by the north gate was rusting and would need to be changed before the cows went out to pasture.

The word-cards the girl had made from the cedar butter-box — he still had them in the wooden box with his papers. He did not look at them. The words had gone from the cards into wherever words go when you no longer need to look them up.

In October she had taken him to the ridge above the harbor. He had stood on the high ground and seen the dock lights below and the sea beyond the lights, dark and very large, going on past where his eyes could follow it. He had not stood at the sea before that night. He thought about it still. The cold salt smell. The sound of it in the dark.

The German word *bleiben* meant to stay, to remain. But in German the word did not always mean choosing. *Er ist geblieben*: he remained, he stayed — which might mean he had decided to stay, or might mean only that he had not yet gone. He turned it over. He did not know if staying in America after the war was a thing a person decided or a thing that happened, or whether the distinction would matter. He did not know if Regensburg was standing well enough to go back to, and if it was, he did not know what he would do there: no father, no shop, a Roman wall he had walked past every morning without thinking about it.

The girl had brought him to see the harbor. She had made a plan and kept it and brought him, and it was the first time anyone in this country had done something for him that was not the minimum the rules required. He had not asked her why she did it and she had not explained. He had been trying since October to find a word for this kind of exchange in either of his languages and the word had not come. There was *Freundschaft*, friendship, but that was a category. There was *Freundlichkeit*, kindness, but that was also a category, and also not exactly right. He did not have a word for that.

The big one — the Hannover schoolteacher, the one who read the German Bible every Sunday morning on his bunk — had started snoring. The sound was familiar, regular as any machinery, the same each night. Karl turned onto his side. The bunk creaked.

Outside, March. The ice on the puddles in the yard would be gone in two weeks. The cows would go out to pasture in a month when the ground was firm enough. The north gate wire needed replacing.
