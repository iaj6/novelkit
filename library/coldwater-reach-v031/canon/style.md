# Style Guide

## Core Mechanics

**POV**: Third-person limited, past tense. The narrative stays inside the chapter's POV character with strict fidelity — no access to another character's thoughts, no omniscient pullback. The narrator's language reflects the POV character's register, vocabulary, and habitual attention.

**Tense**: Simple past as baseline. Past perfect only when flashback is unavoidable; avoid extended flashback sequences — deliver backstory in passing, through present action.

**Sentence rhythm**: Varies by POV (see below), but across all three: declarative sentences do the heavy lifting. Questions are physical, not rhetorical. Avoid sentences that begin with abstract nouns or emotional states — begin with an agent, an object, a location, an action.

**Dialogue density**: Moderate. Dialogue is functional, not social. Characters say what they need to say and stop. Long speeches are a sign that something has gone wrong. Silence is often the response.

**Chapter length**: Target 2,500–3,000 words per chapter. The exceptional short chapters (Henry Crow or Lonzo Reed POV) may run 1,200–1,800 words.

**Chapter openings**: Begin in the middle of something — a task, a movement, a sound, a piece of weather. Do not begin with a character waking up, looking in a mirror, or thinking about what has happened. Begin with what is happening now.

**Chapter endings**: End on an image, an action, or a piece of dialogue — not on a summarizing reflection. The last sentence should close the scene without resolving the emotional question. Do not explain what the ending means.

## Per-POV Voice Register

### Eira's Chapters

The controlling metaphor of Eira's narration is harbor work — not as metaphor but as the actual substance of how she perceives. She notices the angle of a vessel's trim before she notices the expression on the face of the person aboard it. Her sentences are short to medium length, declarative, and move from observation to inference without ornament.

**What Eira's narration does**:
- Names things precisely (the specific type of knot, the specific weather system, the specific regulation citation).
- Renders emotion as physical sensation or behavioral change: she does not *feel grief*; she *reaches for Owen's second coffee cup and finds it's not there*.
- Uses the diction of harbor management, maritime law, and practical seamanship. Period-accurate terminology used correctly.
- Clipped dialogue: questions without preambles, answers without elaboration.

**What Eira's narration avoids**:
- Extended interior monologue.
- Simile. She does not compare the sea to things; the sea is the sea.
- Editorializing about her own feelings or the feelings of others.
- Flashback. Her past surfaces in objects, habits, and reflexes — not in scenes.

**Example register** (draft-level approximation):
> The buoy tender put in at half-past seven, which was twenty minutes later than the manifest said. Eira noted it and said nothing. Sprague, the first mate, had a face like he'd been on rough water, which he hadn't — the bar was glass from Machias down. She signed the arrival log and let it stand.

### Hannah's Chapters

Hannah's narration is the most interior of the three. The narrative stays close to her sensory world — smell, cold, the sounds of the farm before light — but also follows her thinking where it leads, including dead ends and revisions. She is seventeen, which means she has strong opinions and uncertain foundations.

**What Hannah's narration does**:
- Uses German words and phrases where her father's voice has shaped her interior language. These are not italicized if they have been fully absorbed into her thinking (e.g., *Vater*, *still*, *ach*). Translation is not provided; context must carry it, or it does not need to be carried.
- Notices the farm in granular physical detail: which cows, which smells, which sounds at which hour.
- Thinks associatively — a fact about Karl leads to a memory of her father leads to a word in German leads back to the present.
- Permits more free indirect speech than the other two POVs.

**What Hannah's narration avoids**:
- Self-consciousness about her own narration (she is not an intellectual).
- Heavy irony. Hannah has a dry eye for absurdity but she doesn't stand back and perform it.
- Adult retrospective wisdom. She is seventeen; she does not yet know how this ends.
- German phrases as decoration. They appear because they arrive in her thinking, not to signal her background.

**Example register** (draft-level approximation):
> The smallest of them, Karl, had a cold. She could hear it in the way he breathed when he came past the fence line with the feed bucket, that particular wet labor of it. Vater had the same kind of cold in February every year. She looked away before he could see her looking.

### Marcus's Chapters

Marcus's narration is the most formally constructed. His sentences are precise and sometimes long, with subordinate clauses that perform the work of qualification. His narration occasionally carries the slight strangeness of a man who has translated his own thinking: a word held at arm's length, a sentence constructed differently than an American would construct it.

**What Marcus's narration does**:
- Approaches the world through language: he notices words — their etymology, their range, the gap between what someone says and what they mean.
- Uses formal vocabulary without being precious about it. He is not showing off; he is being exact.
- Shows the displaced intellectual's double attention: the scene in front of him and the scene behind it, the present country and the absent one.
- Weighs sentences. A fact is rarely stated without some acknowledgment of how it is being framed.

**What Marcus's narration avoids**:
- Exposition dumps about history, Nazism, or what happened to the Jews. He knows; the reader does not need him to explain it.
- Sentimentality. His distance from feeling is not coldness — it is the shell that grief and displacement grew around him.
- Unearned warmth. He thaws slowly; the narration should follow.

**Example register** (draft-level approximation):
> The word *heimisch* had no precise English equivalent — he had looked, professionally, for years, and found only approximations. *Homely* was too quaint. *Familiar* too neutral. *Belonging* came closest but was a gerund, not an adjective, and carried an implication of agency that *heimisch* did not require. One did not *belong to* a place; one was *heimisch* in it, or one was not. He had not been *heimisch* anywhere in nine years. He did not know whether the condition was one that could be recovered or merely — he chose the word carefully — abandoned.

## Period Accuracy in Prose

- Use period-accurate terms for military service, matériel, and administration. Do not use anachronistic idioms.
- Render rationing, V-Mail, casualty telegrams, and wartime regulations as texture, not set dressing — they constrain and shape action, not merely describe it.
- No character should appear to understand the war's outcome before it happens. Write from inside the uncertainty.

## What All Three Voices Share

- Trust the reader. Do not explain the emotional significance of what just happened.
- Observation before reflection. See first; feel afterward, and feel quietly.
- Action carries weight. A character who picks up a letter and puts it down without opening it has communicated more than two paragraphs of interior monologue about the letter.
- The sea, the farm, the classroom: each POV's setting is a working environment, not a mood device.
