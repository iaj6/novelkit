# World

## Location

**Coldwater Reach** is a fictional coastal fishing town on the Maine coast, situated at the mouth of a tidal river. Population roughly 900 in 1943, down from 1,100 before the war took the young men. The harbor is small but working: a granite breakwater, a wooden pier shed that smells of brine and diesel, three lobster smacks still operating, the trap-hauling boats of older men who couldn't enlist, and a buoy tender run by the Lighthouse Service that puts in twice monthly. The harbor office is a converted pilot house attached to the pier shed; Eira Bowman's desk overlooks the anchorage. The chart cabinet beside it has not been reorganized since Owen Bowman's death.

The town sits on a ridge above the harbor: a main street (Reach Road) with a post office, a general store that doubles as a ration-book distribution point, a Congregational church, and a diner called Burleigh's that has closed on Tuesdays since 1942 for lack of staff. The high school occupies a converted mill building at the north end of town; its basement serves as the civil defense warden's office.

Three miles inland, the road forks to farms — the Pfeiffer dairy among them. The icehouse on the Pfeiffer property, a long low building of board-and-batten construction insulated with sawdust packed between the walls, was cleared in late 1943 to house the POW detail. The bunkhouse smell: pine pitch, straw, damp wool, the particular cold that sawdust insulation can't entirely stop.

Real Maine places — Portland, Bangor, Houlton, Machias, Rockland — are referenced correctly and in their proper geographic relation to Coldwater Reach (positioned roughly between Machias and Lubec, downeast). Sherbrooke, Quebec; Cambridge, Massachusetts; Vienna; and Munich appear in backstory and correspondence.

## Period: Autumn 1943 – Spring 1945

By autumn 1943 the acute panic of 1942 — U-boat scares, coastal blackouts, beach patrols — has subsided but not vanished. The mood is endurance, not emergency. Most of the town's able-bodied men aged eighteen to thirty-five are gone. The men who remain are old, or exempt, or waiting: Lonzo Reed, the Coastal Patrol private assigned to the Pfeiffer POW detail, is sixty-one with a bad knee from a lobster-trap winch accident in 1937.

The POW labor program in Maine is real. Camp Houlton (established 1944) is the main camp; satellite work details — small groups of prisoners contracted to farms for agricultural labor — operate throughout Aroostook and Washington counties. The Pfeiffer detail predates the formal Houlton establishment, operating under a local arrangement with the provost marshal's office in Bangor. Four prisoners, one guard, one farm.

## Material Texture

**Rationing**: Ration Book Three in effect by autumn 1943. Red points for meat, butter, cheese. Blue points for canned goods. Gasoline at four gallons per week for non-essential drivers. Shoes rationed. Sugar a constant scarcity. Burleigh's diner serves what it can get.

**Communication**: V-Mail (censored microfilm letters) for family correspondence to soldiers overseas. BC-1206 and similar wartime radio receivers in homes and public spaces. The OWI (Office of War Information) is a presence in the language of radio announcements. Casualty telegrams delivered by Western Union to the front door; the postmistress, **Ada Gile**, knows things before most of the town does because she watches who receives telegrams.

**War presence**: A civil defense warden does rounds. Coastal blackout rules remain on the books even when not enforced. Scrap drives, war bond drives, victory gardens. The town's young men are distributed across the Pacific and European theaters; some are in North Africa, Italy, later France and Germany. Letters arrive delayed and censored.

**Transportation**: Most people walk or use horses for short distances. Gasoline rationing limits civilian driving. The bus to Machias runs three times a week. Marcus Klein does not own a car.

## Mood and Atmosphere

Autumn arrives early and hard on the Maine coast: fog in September, the first killing frost by mid-October, nor'easters by November. The harbor freezes in the inner anchorage by January and is open water again by late March. Winters are long and quiet in a way that amplifies the silence of missing men. The town's dominant emotional register by late 1943 is a practiced, stoic, slightly brittle endurance — the kind that can't afford to ask whether it's holding.

The sea is not romantic. It is the working surface of an industrial process: tides, drafts, mooring lines, trap hauls, diesel exhaust. The fog is not atmospheric; it is a navigation problem. The cold is not picturesque; it damages gear and makes everything take longer. This is Eira's relationship to the coast. The others' relationships differ.
