# Editor — Pacing Pass
## Macro arc assessment

*Read from: brief.md, canon/threads.md, canon/themes.md, outline/00-chapter-map.md, logs/story-arc.md. No individual chapter drafts consulted.*

---

### Overall shape

The arc is sound at its poles. The setup (ch01–08) establishes all three protagonists cleanly, the body arrives and is taken, and the central question settles into each life at a different angle. The final phase (ch24–30) has the emotional weight of a novel that has earned its resolutions. The main structural stress is in the middle third — specifically the ten-chapter band running from ch10 through ch19 — and in the elongated resolution cascade from ch24 onward.

---

### Midbook sag: real, localized

Chapters 14 and 15 are the sag point. Ch14 (*Mud Season*) advances T4 and T5 through accumulation: Hannah turns 18, the word *mündig* is planted, Rolf's V-Mail arrives and is absorbed in silence. Ch15 (*The Sixth of June*) advances T2 through interiority: D-Day, Marcus in his classroom, the Emergency Committee letter replaced in the pocket. Both chapters are thematically necessary and individually well-conceived. The problem is their sequencing. Two consecutive chapters that derive their pressure almost entirely from internal register — feeling rather than event — land at the dead center of the book. Neither changes the situation; both describe a character sitting with something they cannot act on. Coming right before the first real naming of the body (ch16, *The Inspector*), they function as a long intake of breath that is one beat longer than the arc can afford. Ch16 restores momentum immediately, but the reader may feel the drag without being able to name it.

---

### Marcus's long off-page gap (ch19–24)

Marcus disappears from the POV rotation for five full chapters — roughly 15,000 words — during the period when his school board hearing is visibly approaching. He is last in POV in ch19 (August, the Bangor library trip), then absent through ch20, 21, 22, 23, before reappearing at the hearing in ch24. Henry's exceptional chapter (ch18) establishes the petition's existence; Lonzo's chapter (ch22) and Hannah's two chapters (ch21, 23) fill the gap. This is not wrong structurally — the hearing may land harder for arriving without interiority — but it creates a real risk: five chapters of Hannah and Eira in the foreground while Marcus's highest-stakes external event assembles offscreen. If ch24 opens cold on the hearing without adequate build in the chapter itself, the reader will have lost the felt pressure of T3 accumulating. This gap should be watched in per-chapter review. The question is whether ch24 does enough front-loading to re-establish Marcus's interiority before the board convenes, or whether his absence makes the crisis feel reported rather than inhabited.

---

### Adjacent withholding beats (ch20–21)

Ch20 (*Addendum*): Eira writes the true account and files it privately — deliberate withholding of truth from the official record. Ch21 (*V-Mail*): Hannah writes to Rolf, omitting everything real, and registers that "everyone in town is keeping something." These are consecutive chapters performing the same emotional action: a character choosing to withhold truth, rendered as a character act. Both are individually necessary and correctly placed by thread logic. But placed back-to-back, they risk landing as the same note twice. If ch20 establishes the omission-as-character-choice at full volume, ch21's version — even though it is Hannah's first conscious version of the same act — may register as an echo rather than a development. Worth examining whether the sequencing can be protected by the distinct register of the two chapters (Eira's spare procedural voice vs. Hannah's more interior one), or whether one needs to be moved or the resonance needs to be made more explicit within the text.

---

### Resolution cascade: five chapters of descent (ch24–30)

The crisis lands in ch24 (the hearing) and ch25 (the selectmen). From ch26 onward, the book is in resolution mode across five consecutive chapters: ch26 (Karl's father dead, Magda speaks), ch27 (Marcus says he has stopped moving), ch28 (Karl's exceptional chapter, interior), ch29 (Eira files the addendum), ch30 (Hannah and Magda at the kitchen garden). All five are emotionally necessary. The concern is cumulative weight: each resolution is small and earned, but five descending chapters in a row may feel like the book is settling too gradually — not overstaying in any single beat, but extending the landing further than the reader's momentum can sustain.

The specific risk is ch26-ch28. Ch26 delivers two events simultaneously (Karl's father, Magda's returning voice) and serves as pivot. Ch27 is Marcus's quiet climax. Ch28 is Karl's exceptional interior. These three consecutive chapters are all held-breath, quiet, turned inward — fine individually, but stacked, they form a second sag, this one at the end rather than the middle. Ch29 and ch30 then provide the final two resolutions. The book ends correctly and at the right register; the question is whether the path through ch26-28 generates enough forward pull to prevent the reader from feeling the coda as a long diminuendo.

---

### The exceptional chapters (Henry, Lonzo, Karl)

Placement of ch18 (Henry) and ch28 (Karl) is structurally correct. Henry's chapter comes at the pivot of T3 with enough novel left for the hearing to build; Karl's comes in the coda where his interiority finally becomes available without Hannah mediating it.

Ch22 (Lonzo) gives pause. It falls between ch21 (Hannah's V-Mail, where she's becoming conscious of choosing) and ch23 (October Night, the culmination of T4). That's two preparation chapters before the novel's central small transgression. Lonzo's chapter is essential — it shows the system's permeability becoming deliberate — but from a pacing perspective, ch21 plus ch22 means two chapters of setup before a chapter the reader has been waiting for since ch11. If Lonzo's chapter reads as a beat of its own with genuine pressure, it earns its position. If it reads primarily as authorization for October Night, it delays rather than builds.

---

### Summary of concerns (priority order)

1. **Ch14–15 midbook sag**: two consecutive internal/accumulation chapters at dead center. Low energy for the arc's hinge moment. (Documented; does not require rewriting — monitor in per-chapter pass whether one can supply more external event.)
2. **Marcus off-page gap (ch19–24)**: five-chapter absence during T3's highest-pressure phase. Per-chapter pass on ch24 should verify the opening does enough work to re-inhabit his interiority.
3. **Ch20–21 adjacent withholding beats**: same emotional action in consecutive chapters, different POVs. Whether the register difference is sufficient protection is a per-chapter question.
4. **Resolution cascade (ch26–28)**: three quiet, inward chapters back-to-back before the final two resolutions. Risk of second sag at the end. Examine individually for forward pull.
5. **Ch22 (Lonzo) as double-setup before ch23**: warranted structurally, but if its own chapter pressure is weak, it softens the arrival of October Night rather than preparing it.

### Central beat deflated by retrospective disclosure (fourth section)

The chapter's earned beat is the dinner table exchange: Hannah asks the question she has been rehearsing through the whole feeding, Albrecht responds with *Regensburg ist nicht weit*, picks up his spoon, and the moment passes. The chapter has been building to that exchange as a **first naming** — both for Hannah and for the reader. The story-arc entry confirms this framing: "Albrecht names Karl's proximity for the first time in direct response to Hannah's question."

In the fourth section (Hannah upstairs, sitting on the bed in the dark), the following paragraph retroactively collapses that beat:

> *She had understood this since the evening in the second week when she had been carrying dishes from the table to the counter and heard him say it — Regensburg ist nicht weit — to no one in particular, or to her mother's back, or to the air above the table. She had heard it and understood what it meant and had not known what to do with it, and had been carrying it since then the way you carry something in a pocket that you keep reaching for without taking out.*

This reveals that Hannah has already heard *Regensburg ist nicht weit* — weeks earlier, at close range, at the same table. The dinner scene the reader just witnessed was not a first naming but a second. "Tonight was different" (the paragraph immediately following) is structurally dependent on this disclosure and cannot stand without it, but with it, the chapter's central beat arrives as a confirmation of something Hannah (and, prospectively, the reader via ch05) already held. The tension of the dinner scene — her rehearsing the German grammar, setting down her spoon, the formal deliberateness of the question — is built as if this answer will be new. The upstairs paragraph undoes that build retroactively.

**Connection to ch05 continuity finding:** This is the downstream pacing consequence of the issue already documented in the ch05 note (editor-pacing.md, *"One pacing issue — ch08 beat pre-empted"*). The source error is in ch05 (*auch* meditation, "Her father had said it in the second week of the POWs' arrival..."). The ch05 note correctly identifies the fix: remove the prospective reference there so that *Regensburg ist nicht weit* arrives in ch08 as new information to both Hannah and the reader.

**What changes in ch08 once ch05 is corrected:** If ch05's "second week" reference is cut or revised, the upstairs paragraph in ch08 must also be revised — it becomes internally inconsistent (there was no prior "evening in the second week" for Hannah to have overheard). The likely revision is to cut this paragraph entirely and reattach the logic of "Tonight was different" directly to the dinner table exchange just completed: the difference is not between hearing it aimed at the air versus aimed at her, but between knowing abstractly that her father knows and being told by him directly in response to her question. That reframe can be accomplished in one sentence without the backstory paragraph. The following paragraph ("She lay back on the bed...") is then unaffected.

**Specific location in ch08:** Fourth section (beginning *"She sat on the bed in the dark without lighting the lamp"*), third paragraph, beginning *"She had understood this since the evening in the second week..."* through *"...the way you carry something in a pocket that you keep reaching for without taking out."* This paragraph is the one to cut or revise after the ch05 correction.

---

### Larger issue: the kitchen scene delays the chapter's earned close

The chapter's strongest material is its window meditation (the Rue du Temple, the Marais, the two truths held simultaneously) and its bedside close (the Emergency Committee letter, "the future tense of October 1940, when the future had been a room with no visible walls"). These are the chapter's two most pressure-bearing moments and they belong together — the meditation establishes what D-Day means in the full weight of Marcus's specific knowledge; the letter establishes what staying in Coldwater Reach cost and continues to cost.

Between them sits the kitchen scene (~300 words): Marcus comes home, Henry is at the table, Lillian is in the garden, they listen to the afternoon radio update, exchange five words ("Long way to go still" / "Yes"), Lillian comes in from the garden, Henry says the beach fighting was heavy early and better inland, she nods, supper smell strengthens. The section closes: "The three of them were in the kitchen at the end of the day that the day had been."

The kitchen scene is not wrong — Henry and Lillian are what Marcus's choice to stay looks like in practice, and showing him arriving home to a household is appropriate. But the scene does not advance T2. His choice was made in ch12. The window meditation has already enacted what inhabiting that choice costs on a day like this. The kitchen scene adds ambient community (home, warmth, two people who are his people) without adding felt pressure or new information. And the closing sentence — "The three of them were in the kitchen at the end of the day that the day had been" — names what the prose should leave implicit, summarizing the scene's emotional quality rather than trusting it.

**Effect on pacing:** The chapter runs school → assembly → German II → free period/meditation → kitchen → bedside. The kitchen is a descent in pressure between two ascents. In the context of the macro-identified midbook sag (ch14–15 both accumulation/interiority chapters with no external event changing the situation), an extra ~300-word scene that settles rather than compresses works against the chapter's close landing with its full weight.

**Suggested action:** Consider cutting the kitchen scene and moving directly from the school bell (end of the free-period section) to the bedside scene. Alternatively, compress to one paragraph — Marcus arrives, Henry is at the table, the radio is on, Marcus sits down — and cut the exchange and Lillian's return, ending on a single quiet image of the household before cutting to the bedroom. Either version allows the Emergency Committee letter to arrive as the chapter's closing beat without a 300-word delay.

**This is an author decision**, not a cut the pacing pass should make unilaterally. The scene is not broken; it is inert relative to what surrounds it, and in a chapter that is already the second half of a two-chapter sag, inert is a cost.
