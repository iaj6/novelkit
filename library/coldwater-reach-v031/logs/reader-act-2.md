# Reader — Act 2 Assessment (Chapters 11–20)

## Act Span
February 1944 (ch11, word-cards) through September 1944 (ch20, the addendum). Covers three POVs across ten chapters, including one exceptional POV (ch18, Henry). The act's architecture: T1 names its subject (A. Schell → August Schell → filed record); T2/T3 advance toward the board hearing; T4 and T5 accumulate at the fence; the act ends with Eira's private accounting in the pending drawer.

---

## What Is Working

**Ch15 ("The Sixth of June") is the act's best chapter and possibly the manuscript's.** Marcus holds D-Day with the doubled attention the brief requires: glad, in the full weight of the word, but unable to be *entirely* glad because what was done in the Marais in the summer of 1942 does not yield to the mathematics of what is now being corrected. He names neither the scholars he shared rooms with on the Rue du Temple nor the category of what happened to them — carrying them in exactly the way the brief's style guide demands, as a precision used once and then held closed. The Edna Locke moment (the red pin pressed into the Normandy coastline, observed through glass) delivers more than any direct scene between them would. The chapter ends with Marcus lying down in the dark, the elm full-leafed outside the window, and nothing resolved — which is the only correct ending for this day.

**Ch18 ("What Henry Notices") fully earns its exceptional POV.** Henry's consciousness is rendered through accumulation of custodial knowledge: the piano lid up again, the map pin in Normandy, the petition in the third wastebasket. "He hadn't developed the habit of reading what sat on principals' desks" is exact. The information transfer at the kitchen table — "Three names. One of them is on the committee." / "That's all I've got." — compressed to the point where everything unsaid between two men who know what it is to be tolerated rather than welcomed arrives in the space left by what they don't say. The dishwashing ending is the act's finest image.

**Ch19 ("August") earns its detective structure through the philologist's lens.** The three explanations Marcus rehearses for being on the bus to Bangor — the reflex, the jurisdictional objection, the one he doesn't name — are fully in his voice. The long meditation on August Schell's biography (the arithmetic of displacement, the specific category of man who makes himself "difficult to file" and what Marcus recognizes in that) is one of the act's most sustained pieces of prose and never overclaims. The decision not to say anything is argued, held open to revision, and left there.

**Ch20 ("Addendum") is formally inventive and true to Eira.** The addendum-within-the-chapter is in official harbor-record voice throughout — arrival logs translated into official incident form, emotion rendered as notation. "The undersigned did not approach the individual" sits in the same paragraph as Eira's eight-month accounting without editorializing. The placement of the folded document in front of Owen's two unfinished 1935 files — the tidal survey — is the act's subtlest image: the unfinished record placed alongside another unfinished record, both pending, Owen's still waiting. The chapter closes with Eira writing the vessel traffic log in the same hand, the *Gertrude B.* clearing the north mooring at 0723. The harbor goes on.

**Ch17 ("Off Duty")** handles the perimeter walk with the right restraint. The fence's known geography (the sag at the near corner, the east-leaning post) grounds the conversation in physical space. The *Ich auch* at the chapter's end, carrying the weight of nine months of cedar cards and short sentences, lands exactly as it should. Lonzo not looking up — "the deliberate tilt of someone reading" — does the chapter's most important work without announcing it.

**Ch11 ("The Word for Harbor")** establishes the word-card system as a physical and structural invention, and the harbor card in particular earns its weight through the chapter-long preparation: the worn English side, the dinner table question to Albrecht (*Erst in Hamburg* — not until Hamburg), and the final image of Hannah pressing her hand against the apron pocket. This is the brief's Hannah-register at its best: associative, sensing, never summarizing.

## What Is Not Working

**Ch13 ("Ada Gile's Window") and ch16 ("The Inspector") share the act's central weakness: Eira's interior becomes explicitly analytical in a way that breaks her established register.** The style guide is unambiguous: Eira's narration "avoids extended interior monologue" and moves "from observation to inference without ornament." In ch13, after Eira leaves Ada Gile's counter, the harbor office notebook scene tips into articulation of what the reader can already assemble. The paragraph beginning "What she had was a set of coincidences, and coincidences were what the record looked like when it was incomplete" is the style guide's prohibition stated in Eira's voice: she has become the narrator explaining the investigation rather than the harbormaster making notation. Then ch16 magnifies the same problem. After Whitmore departs, Eira conducts a four-paragraph internal review of everything she knows and doesn't know, culminating in: "These were the boundaries of what she knew." The contrast with ch20 — where the same information is rendered through the addendum's official form, with Eira's thinking visible only through what she writes and then stares at — shows precisely what these earlier chapters should have done instead. The over-articulation is not a single incident; it recurs across both Eira chapters in the act's middle and constitutes a pattern.

**Ch12 ("Two Suits") is the act's most over-extended chapter.** The Cambridge-letter decision is the chapter's spine and it earns its resolution cleanly. But the *heimisch* meditation runs to seven-plus appearances of the word within thirty pages, and by the fifth occurrence the meditation has become recursive — Marcus thinking about what the word means, then why it applies, then why it doesn't apply, then why the distinction matters — without significantly advancing the investigation. The meditation's length is partly justified by Marcus's register, but at this density the word starts doing explanatory work that should be left implicit. The elm-bud image at the chapter's close is correct; getting there takes too long.

**The "Portland postmark" transfer gap in ch13.** Eira's notebook entry (written at the harbor office after visiting Ada) states: *A. Schell — general delivery — postcard — Portland postmark — early October.* But in Ada's dialogue in the same chapter, no postmark is specified. Ada tells Eira the postcard arrived the first week of October, gives the name, and says it contained a date and time — but never says Portland. Eira then treats the Portland postmark as a confirmed fact in both ch13 and ch16. This is either an elided information delivery (plausible — Ada might mention it between sentences the narrative doesn't render) or a genuine continuity gap in how the fact was transferred. Either way, the gap is noticeable to a careful reader, and the postmark matters for Eira's theory (Macomber's Portland contract + Portland postcard = shape).

## Voice Slips

**Ch14 ("Mud Season"), Hannah**: The rhetorical structure following Marcus's *mündig* lesson becomes slightly too sculpted for Hannah's register: "But *mündig* was not about naming things. *Mündig* was about whose voice the naming came from. Who had the right to put the name in." This is not what the style guide means by Hannah's associative thinking; it's Hannah performing a thematic summary in the rhythm of a lecture. The observation is correct — the assembly is too deliberate for a seventeen-year-old thinking alone at her school desk. A lighter hand here would leave the word to do its own work.

**Ch16 ("The Inspector")**, a minor instance: "He had taken a berth on a merchant ship running the Atlantic in wartime, which was not a safe thing to do." This is a narrator's observation, not Eira's. It states an implication any reader already holds. Eira's mode is to note facts, not comment on what they imply.

## Thread Development

- **T1**: Clean arc within the act. Ch13 gives Eira a theory; ch16 gives her the name; ch20 gives her the private accounting. No gaps.
- **T2/T3**: Ch12 (Marcus commits to staying) → ch15 (D-Day, the cost of investment) → ch18 (Henry warns, the petition has a shape) → ch19 (Marcus researches Schell independently). Both threads building correctly toward the hearing. No gaps.
- **T4/T5**: Ch11 → ch14 → ch17. The word-card system, the birthday beats, the perimeter walk. Correctly incremental. T5 is appropriately quiet — Magda's silence held back for the pivot in Act 3.

---

## In-Act Pacing

The act's best sequential run is ch18 → ch19 → ch20: Henry's warning, Marcus's Bangor trip, and Eira's addendum are the strongest consecutive chapters in the book so far, and placing them at the act's end gives the second act real weight. The opening (ch11, ch12) is slower than it needs to be — ch12 especially. The middle (ch16 and ch17) places two long, quiet chapters back-to-back (the inspection, the perimeter walk) without enough tonal counterweight between them; the chapter before the Normandy announcement might have served as that counterweight, but ch15 itself is the largest and most interior chapter. The act does not have a sag so much as a slight heaviness in the ch12–ch16 band before the back end delivers. Nothing irretrievable, but the ch12 and ch16 revisions noted above would ease the center's pace considerably.

---

## Summary

Act 2's floor is high. The exceptional chapters (15, 18, 19, 20) set a standard the rest of the manuscript will need to meet. The act's primary revision priorities are: (1) bring ch13 and ch16 into compliance with Eira's established interior register by cutting or displacing the analytical paragraphs that break it; (2) compress the *heimisch* meditation in ch12; (3) establish the Portland postmark as explicit dialogue in ch13 or account for how Eira received it. The voice slips in Hannah (ch14) and the register break in ch16 are smaller targets that revision should catch in pass.
