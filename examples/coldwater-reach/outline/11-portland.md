# Chapter 11 — Portland

**One-line summary:** Eira drives to Portland on a pretext and asks a customs clerk about refugees crossing from Canada; she learns almost nothing and understands more; on the drive back she sees a man on the roadside and for a moment believes it is the same man.

**POV:** Eira Bowman  
**Tense:** Third-person limited past  
**Arc phase:** Rising Action

---

## Setting / Time

Coldwater Reach and the road to Portland (roughly 80 miles south, two hours each way on winter roads). The Portland customs office. February 1944. Cold, clear. The road is mostly clear of ice but the fields on either side are deep in snow.

---

## Goal

Eira's stated goal: deliver a coastal registry renewal form to the Portland district office that she could have mailed but chose not to. Her actual goal: find out, without asking directly, whether the Naval Intelligence theory — the man crossed from Canada — is supported by a larger pattern.

---

## Obstacle

The customs office is staffed by a clerk who has no reason to share information with a harbormaster from a small Maine town. Eira has no leverage, no official standing to request intelligence information, and no pretext beyond the registry renewal that would survive scrutiny. She cannot ask the question she came to ask. She will have to ask around it.

---

## Action Beats

**The drive:**
- Eira drives her 1937 Ford half-ton on the coast road south. The harbor is in Ames's hands for the day — he is competent for routine. She signed the log over at 7 a.m.
- She thinks, on the drive, about what she knows and doesn't know. The dead man. European clothing. A crossing from Canada. Mr. Hale's careful questions. The October 11 figure on the north wharf. She makes a list in her mind, the way she makes lists when she is uncertain: what is established, what is inferred, what is unverifiable.
- She passes through Thomaston. Then Rockland. Then the road south. Fields and sea. She does not think about the beauty of any of it.

**The Portland customs office:**
- A federal building on Commercial Street, two blocks from the harbor. She finds it without difficulty; she has been here before for registry matters.
- The clerk is a man named Colby, who knows her by name from the registry work. He processes the renewal quickly. She does not leave immediately.
- She says: "I had a question. Peripheral. The body that came in on the Coldwater breakwater in October — the Coast Guard told me there's a theory the man may have crossed from the Canadian Maritime Provinces. Is that a common route? For transients?"
- Colby looks at her. He says: "Harbormaster matters don't usually touch that category."
- She says: "No. I'm asking because I want to understand what I might see. For future reference. Preventative."
- Colby says: "The Maritime routes are a Customs matter, not ours. I can't really speak to that." A pause. He adds: "The thing you'd want to know is that anything coming from the Maritimes tends to come in at night. Small craft. Not commercial routes." He pauses again. "For future reference."
- She thanks him. She leaves.

**On the drive back — the man on the road:**
- Outside of Rockland, on the coast road, she sees a man walking on the road's shoulder. He is wearing a dark coat — not an oilskin, a town coat, heavier than most. He is walking away from her, south.
- She drives past him. In the mirror, before the curve takes him from view, she sees his figure: his gait, his height, the way he carries his shoulders under the coat.
- She knows it is not the man from the north wharf. The figure on the wharf was seen in the dark, at distance, for thirty seconds. She has no confident recollection of his gait or the way he carried his shoulders.
- And yet. She drives for half a mile after the curve before she stops looking at the mirror.

**Return to Coldwater:**
- She signs back into the harbor log at 4:15. Ames has had a routine day. She reviews his entries. They are correct.
- She sits at her desk. The BC-1206 is on — weather channel. Southwest wind, twelve knots, holding.
- Colby's phrase: *anything coming from the Maritimes tends to come in at night. Small craft.* She thinks: a dory would be a small craft. October. Night. 2 a.m.
- She thinks: he did not make it across from Canada to die on the breakwater accidentally. He made it across from Canada. Then something happened. What happened between landing and the breakwater?
- She does not know. She opens the log and makes the day's entry.

---

## Outcome / Turn

Eira has confirmed, obliquely, what the Naval Intelligence theory suggested: small craft, night crossings, the Maritime route. The chapter turns on the figure she sees on the road — not the body, not a ghost, just a man in a dark coat — and the fact that she checks the mirror for half a mile. This is the first time the body has reached into her involuntary nervous system. She is not frightened; she is simply more aware of being changed by this than she has admitted.

---

## New Question or Pressure Raised

- If the man crossed from Canada by small craft at night — how did he get the small craft? Where did he embark? Was he alone?
- What happened between his landing (the north wharf, 2 a.m.) and his death on the breakwater?
- Does Eira now carry this differently? Her private catalog is growing into a private case. Is she investigating, or is she just holding?
- What does the road figure mean to her — is she starting to see him in strangers?

---

## Continuity Notes

- Establishes: Colby at the Portland customs office — he processes the registry renewal and gives Eira the indirect confirmation: small craft, Maritime routes, night crossings.
- Establishes: The Portland drive as Eira's first active step toward her own investigation. She chose to go; she chose to ask. This is different from receiving Farris's or Hale's visits.
- The figure on the road: not the dead man, not a ghost — a man in a dark coat walking south on the coast road. Eira's involuntary response (checking the mirror) is the chapter's turn.
- The drive to Portland: approximately 80 miles south. Two hours each way on winter roads. She leaves Ames in charge of the harbor for the day.

## Notes for Drafter

- The drive should feel like working time — she is thinking on the road. Lists, not reverie. The landscape is there (coast road, Thomaston, Rockland) without being scenic.
- The Colby exchange is the chapter's most delicate scene. He gives her just enough to confirm what she already suspected, wrapped in careful neutrality. Neither of them names what they're talking about. The exchange should be brief, accurate, and done.
- The road figure: do not dramatize this. She sees a man, she drives past, she checks the mirror. The half-mile of mirror-checking is the whole event. Do not make him mysterious — he is simply a man on a road.
- End on the log entry. She opens the log and makes the day's entry. What the day's entry says we don't see — but the act of writing it is the chapter's last beat. Eira is back in the harbor. The harbor is recorded.
