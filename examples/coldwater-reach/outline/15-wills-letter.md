# Chapter 15 — Will's Letter

**One-line summary:** The first letter from Will in months arrives; Albrecht cries silently at the table; Hannah goes out to the fields to work; at dusk Karl is at the fence.

**POV:** Hannah Pfeiffer  
**Tense:** Third-person limited past  
**Arc phase:** Rising Action

---

## Setting / Time

Pfeiffer farm. April 1944. The first spring field work has begun — plowing, the prisoners back to longer hours. The mud is deep. The air smells of turned earth. The days are getting long enough to work until seven.

---

## Goal

Hannah has no particular goal in this chapter beyond the day's work. The letter arrives at the post office and she brings it home — it is a Thursday, she stops at the post office on her way back from the consolidated school.

---

## Obstacle

The letter from Will is not a letter of resolution — it is a letter that shows Will is alive but changed. It is short and strange. The obstacle of the chapter is what to do with the relief that he is alive when the relief and the strangeness cannot be separated. The chapter also carries the obstacle of Karl at the fence at dusk — she is not looking for him, and she doesn't go to him, and he is there.

---

## Action Beats

**After school — the post office:**
- Hannah stops at the post office on her walk home (she walks the three miles; the bus route doesn't reach the farm). Mrs. Gaines hands her a V-Mail envelope with a familiar address in Will's hand.
- She does not open it on the road. She walks fast.

**Reading the letter — the kitchen:**
- She sits at the kitchen table and opens it. It is V-Mail proportions: small, slightly blurred. Will's handwriting, cramped to fit.
- The letter says: he is all right. He has been in a place he cannot name. There was some action. He has a man in his unit named Briggs who showed him a card trick last week and he has been trying to figure it out since. He hopes the farm is well. He says: *Tell Pop I'm sorry I haven't written. Tell him it doesn't mean anything except that things have been going.* Then: *I'm all right, Hannah. I am.*
- She reads it twice. She is holding it when Albrecht comes in from the field.

**Albrecht at the table:**
- She gives him the letter. He reads it. He is standing at the sink, coat still on.
- He finishes reading. He puts the letter on the table. He sits down in his chair.
- He cries. Not loudly — barely any sound. His shoulders move. He puts one hand over his face.
- Hannah does not go to him. She stands at the counter and looks out the window. This is not coldness; this is the specific knowledge that going to him would interrupt something he needs to finish alone.
- After perhaps two minutes, Albrecht takes his hand from his face. He says — in German, to himself as much as to her — *Er lebt. Das ist genug.* (He's alive. That's enough.)
- Hannah says: *Ja.* (Yes.)
- Magda has come to the kitchen doorway. She looks at Albrecht. She comes in and puts her hand on his shoulder. He covers her hand with his. That's all.

**Into the field — afternoon work:**
- Hannah goes out to the south field to do the afternoon work. The prisoners are plowing the north field; she can hear the horses. She works the south side with a hoe, breaking up the frost-heave clods that the plow left.
- She works until she can't see clearly. She is not thinking about Will — or she is thinking about him in the way you think about something while your hands are doing something else: obliquely, in pieces.
- She thinks about the card trick. Briggs showing Will a card trick. The specificity of it. Will watching Briggs's hands. The war in that: that a card trick is worth mentioning.

**Karl at the fence — dusk:**
- She stops when the light goes. She straightens, her back aching, and looks toward the icehouse fence.
- Karl is standing at the fence. Not working. Just standing. He has seen her in the field — he cannot not have seen her; she's been there for two hours.
- He doesn't wave. She doesn't wave. She picks up her hoe and goes toward the barn.
- As she passes the fence — not close, fifteen yards away — she says, without stopping, in German: *Er lebt.* (He's alive.)
- She does not know if Karl hears her. She does not look over. She goes inside.

---

## Outcome / Turn

The chapter turns at the fence at dusk — Hannah's declaration of *Er lebt* to a figure at a fence she doesn't look at. She is not speaking to Karl for the connection; she is saying something that needs to be said in German because the German holds it better than the English. It is her father's construction, her father's logic, her father's language for what is enough. The chapter ends on the permission Albrecht gave himself: *Er lebt. Das ist genug.* That's enough.

---

## New Question or Pressure Raised

- Will is alive, but the letter is strange — short, specific about a card trick, apologetic about the silence. What has he been through?
- What does Albrecht's weeping mean for the rest of the household? He is not hidden from Magda; she comes and puts her hand on his shoulder. Is something thawing?
- Did Karl hear *Er lebt*? What does he make of it?
- The phrase *Er lebt. Das ist genug.* — is it true? Is it enough? Hannah says *ja.* Does she believe it?

---

## Continuity Notes

- Establishes: Will's V-Mail in April 1944. Content: he is all right, some action, a man named Briggs, a card trick. He apologizes for the silence.
- Establishes: Briggs as a name in Will's unit (a minor detail that can appear if Will comes home in Ch 29).
- Establishes: Albrecht's weeping — the only time in the novel he breaks. It is private and then briefly shared with Magda. This is a turn in the Pfeiffer household.
- Magda touching Albrecht's shoulder: first physical connection between them in the narrative. Small. Real.
- Hannah says *Er lebt* to Karl at dusk. She does not look at him. He may or may not have heard.
- The south field hoe work: Hannah's labor as her way of processing. Consistent with her character.

---

## Notes for Drafter

- The letter reading: V-Mail format — small, slightly blurred, Will's handwriting cramped. The prose should make the letter's smallness physical. Do not summarize the letter; render it or near-render it.
- Albrecht's tears: two minutes, his hand over his face. Hannah does not go to him. Render this without judgment — she knows him well enough to know that going to him would be wrong. Then Magda comes. Then it's done.
- The field work: Hannah working until dark. The specific physical detail — frost-heave clods, the hoe, the sound of the plowing in the north field, her back. This is how she thinks. Let the thinking be oblique — she thinks about the card trick.
- Karl at the fence: he is there. She doesn't go to him. She passes at fifteen yards and says *Er lebt.* She does not stop. She does not look. End there, or very close to there.
