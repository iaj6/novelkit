# Chapter 17 — The Log Entry

**One-line summary:** A French-Canadian sailor's manner of speaking triggers something in Eira; that evening she goes home and reads what she wrote in the harbor log for October 11, 1943.

**POV:** Eira Bowman  
**Tense:** Third-person limited past  
**Arc phase:** Rising Action

---

## Setting / Time

The harbor and Eira's house (two blocks from the harbor, the house she and Owen bought). May 1944. The harbor is busy — the spring season is in full swing. Warmer, though the mornings are still cold. The harbor smells of diesel and fish and the first algae bloom of the season.

---

## Goal

Eira's goal: manage the harbor's spring increase in activity. A coastal freighter has stopped in — a small one, carrying lumber south from the Maritimes — and she is handling the transit paperwork.

---

## Obstacle

The French-Canadian sailor from the freighter is not an obstacle to harbor management. He is an obstacle to the peace Eira has maintained about October. He sounds — his accent, his French mixed with English, the particular way he holds himself — like a category of person she associated with the dead man before she fully understood the category. She doesn't know why this is. She cannot stop knowing it.

---

## Action Beats

**The freighter — morning:**
- The *Sainte-Marguerite* — a small coastal lumber freighter out of Moncton, New Brunswick — ties up at the main wharf for a half-day transit stop. Eira processes the paperwork. The captain is a squat, practical man she has dealt with before. He is not the one who triggers the association.
- One of the crew — a young man, maybe twenty-five, who speaks English with a strong Acadian French accent and a slightly formal grammar, as though he learned it at school — is helping with the lines at the dock. He speaks to her about the fender arrangement on the bow. He is correct about the fenders.
- His accent is not the same as the dead man's would have been — the dead man was presumably Central European, not Maritime French. But something in the formal grammar, the careful English of a man thinking in another language, reaches Eira in a way she can't assign.
- She does the rest of the morning's work.

**Afternoon — the harbor:**
- The *Sainte-Marguerite* departs by noon. Eira has a full afternoon: a dragger registering an equipment change, two transient pleasure boats (improbably, in May, in Maine), the mooring fee ledger to post.
- She does the work. She is efficient. She is thinking about October.
- Not about the body — she has done this before. About the log entry. Specifically: about what she wrote and what she did not write. She has not read the October 11 entry since November. She knows what it says. She is thinking about reading it again.

**Going home — the house:**
- She walks home at five-thirty. The house is the house she and Owen bought in 1929 — a cape on Merchant Street, two bedrooms, the front room with Owen's reading chair still in its corner, the kitchen where she eats most of her meals alone.
- She makes dinner. She eats. She washes the dishes. She is stalling.
- She gets the harbor log. She brought it home — this is unusual; she almost never takes work home. She brought it because she wants to read the October entries somewhere other than the office.

**Reading the log entry:**
- She opens to October 11. She reads: *Mooring lines north wharf — all secure. Wind NW 12 knots.*
- She reads it. She reads the next day's entry, and the next, and the entry for October 14 when the body came in.
- She is looking for what she put in and what she left out.
- What she put in: weather, line status, two routine fuel sales, the body's discovery and removal.
- What she left out: the figure on the north wharf at 2 a.m.
- She sits with the log open. Owen's chair is visible in the corner of the front room from where she is sitting. She does not look at it directly.
- She thinks about Marcus Klein's visit to the harbor — his careful hypothesis, his final line at the door: *what happened to him was not anyone's fault. Or not anyone here.* She has been carrying this for ten days.
- She closes the log. She thinks: the log is the record. The log is what I made it. The log does not contain the man.
- She makes tea. She drinks it. She goes to bed.

**Before sleep — the figure:**
- She thinks, in the dark, about the figure on the north wharf. About what she did not do. She ran through it before — Farris, Hale, the Portland drive. She is not running through it again for new information; she is running through it because the French-Canadian sailor's English unlocked something she hadn't named.
- What the sailor's English unlocked: a person thinking in another language. A person whose first words are elsewhere. She has been around enough fishermen and dockhands to know what native English sounds like and what careful learned English sounds like. The man she saw on the north wharf in the dark — she realizes she never processed his speech, because he didn't speak. He was simply there.
- He didn't speak. She didn't hail him. They were thirty feet apart in the dark and he didn't say a word.
- She understands, lying in the dark, that he may not have spoken to her because he couldn't — because his English wasn't good enough, or because he didn't know if she was someone who would help or someone who would hand him to a government.
- She thinks: *he was afraid of me.*
- She does not say this to anyone. She thinks it once and it settles.

---

## Outcome / Turn

The chapter turns on the final realization: the man didn't speak because he was afraid. Not of Eira specifically — of what she represented: authority, harbor management, the apparatus of official detection. He saw a harbormaster checking lines and he didn't know what she would do. So he was silent. And she was uncertain. And he is dead. The chapter resolves into this small, terrible human fact. Eira does not alter anything she has decided. But she understands it differently now.

---

## New Question or Pressure Raised

- If he was afraid of her, was he also afraid of whoever came next — or whoever he was supposed to meet?
- Did someone fail to meet him? Was he supposed to be picked up and wasn't?
- Does Marcus know any of the specific names in the sponsored-passage network — anyone who might have been supposed to receive this man?
- What does Eira do with the knowledge that his silence was fear?

## Continuity Notes

- Establishes: The *Sainte-Marguerite* — a coastal lumber freighter out of Moncton, New Brunswick. A recurring Maritime trade vessel in the harbor; it can appear in later harbor descriptions.
- Establishes: The Acadian French sailor as the trigger for Eira's realization. He is not important beyond this function.
- Establishes: Eira's reading of the October log at home — the specific log entry text (*Mooring lines north wharf — all secure. Wind NW 12 knots.*) repeated. This is the second time the log entry appears verbatim (first in Ch 1).
- Establishes: Eira's final interpretive piece — the man was afraid of her. He didn't speak because he didn't know if she was safe.
- Owen's chair: mentioned. It is in the corner of the front room. She does not look at it directly. This should be a recurring presence in her home scenes.

## Notes for Drafter

- Open on the harbor in May — busy, alive, full of the spring season's energy. This should feel like the harbor working, which is what Eira loves.
- The French-Canadian sailor: describe him specifically and practically. He knows about fenders. He is correct about the fenders. What is notable is not his appearance but the quality of his English — formal, learned, the English of a man who thinks in French.
- The log reading at home: she is stalling and she knows it. The stalling should feel true — she washes dishes, she makes tea. Then she opens the log.
- The log entry, read again: *Mooring lines north wharf — all secure. Wind NW 12 knots.* The reader has seen this before (Ch 1). Let it land differently the second time, in this different context, without explaining why it lands differently.
- The final realization (*he was afraid of me*) should arrive in a single, declarative sentence of interior thought. Do not build to it. Let it fall.
