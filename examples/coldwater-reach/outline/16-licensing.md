# Chapter 16 — Licensing

**One-line summary:** Marcus and Eira meet properly for the first time over a rowboat storage permit; neither names what both are thinking; both leave the harbor knowing something new about the other.

**POV:** Dr. Marcus Klein  
**Tense:** Third-person limited past  
**Arc phase:** Rising Action

---

## Setting / Time

The harbormaster's office, Coldwater Reach harbor. Late April, moving into May 1944. Spring properly arrived. The harbor smells of diesel and low tide. The BC-1206 is on in the background.

---

## Goal

Marcus's stated goal: obtain a storage permit for the school's two rowboats (used in the spring rowing program, stored on the school wharf in winter, requiring annual re-registration at the harbor). His private goal, barely articulated to himself: he has wanted to speak with Eira Bowman since October, and he has not had a sufficient reason, and now he has one.

---

## Obstacle

The permit process is straightforward — this is not an obstacle. The obstacle is the conversational register: both Marcus and Eira are people who do not say more than they intend to. A conversation between them about anything except the permit will require one of them to open something, and neither will do it directly. What passes between them is what passes between two careful people who have each independently arrived at the same private hypothesis and will not say so.

---

## Action Beats

**The walk to the harbor:**
- Marcus walks from the school to the harbor — fifteen minutes on foot along Main Street, then down to the wharf. He is carrying the school board's permit paperwork in a folder. He is wearing his good jacket. He notices that he is wearing his good jacket.

**The harbormaster's office:**
- He enters. The office is what it is: two rooms, wood stove, diesel and rope smell, the BC-1206 on a shelf above the window catching Portland weather. Eira is at her desk with the mooring registry open.
- She looks up. She says: "Dr. Klein."
- He says: "Harbormaster." He puts the folder on the desk.
- She processes the permit application. She asks the standard questions: how many boats, where stored, what size, who has authorization to use them. He answers correctly. She notes the answers.
- He sits across from her. This is standard — the process takes ten minutes.
- He notices: the harbor log, the neat hand, the organized surface of her desk. He notices the BC-1206. He says: "Do you use the shortwave for the weather, mostly?"
- She says: "Weather and harbor traffic. Occasionally Portland." She does not elaborate.
- He says: "I have a Zenith portable at the Crow house. I use it for the news."
- She says: "The news is different from the weather."
- He says: "In what sense?"
- She says: "Weather is predictable if you know how to read it. The news mostly isn't." She signs the permit. She slides it across the desk.

**The turn:**
- He takes the permit. He does not immediately stand.
- He says: "May I ask you something unrelated to the permit?"
- A pause. She says: "You can ask."
- He says: "The body in October. I've been thinking about it — as someone who spent time in transit across Europe and Canada. I have a hypothesis about how he came to be here. I recognize I may be wrong."
- She looks at him. She says: "What's your hypothesis?"
- He says: "That he came across the water from the Maritime Provinces. That he was moving without official documentation. That whatever organization moved him lost track of him after the crossing." He pauses. "I think it's possible I know of the networks that arrange such crossings. I was not moved by them myself. But I know of them."
- She says: "Why are you telling me this?"
- He says: "Because I think you may already know it, and because I don't want to be the only one carrying it."
- A long pause. The BC-1206 catches something — Portland, a weather channel, a voice through static. She reaches up and turns the volume down.
- She says: "I don't know anything officially. The Coast Guard has the case."
- He says: "Yes."
- She says: "I appreciate you coming in for the permit."
- He says: "Thank you for your time." He picks up the folder and stands.
- At the door he stops. He says: "For what it's worth — I think what happened to him was not anyone's fault. Or not anyone here." He leaves.

**Walking back:**
- He walks back to the school along Main Street. He thinks about what she said and what she didn't say. He thinks about her reaching up and turning down the radio — an act of privacy, a closing of a door he didn't know he'd opened.
- He thinks: she knows more than I do. She knew before I said anything. She has been carrying this since October.
- He thinks: *Die Last ist real, aber sie ist nicht neu.* (The weight is real, but it is not new.) He is not translating his own thought — German simply comes first, here. He doesn't know why.

**After school — the Crow house:**
- He tells Henry he went to the harbor. He says nothing about the conversation with Eira. Henry says: "Did you get the boats permitted?" Marcus says yes. They eat dinner.

---

## Outcome / Turn

Both Marcus and Eira now know that the other has arrived at the same hypothesis about the dead man. Neither has confirmed it; neither has named it as confirmed. But the conversation has happened, and it cannot be un-happened. The chapter turns on Eira turning down the radio — a small physical act that is both a closing and an acknowledgment. And on Marcus's final line at the door: *what happened to him was not anyone's fault. Or not anyone here.* This is the chapter's gift, the thing Marcus came to give.

---

## New Question or Pressure Raised

- Does Eira's decision to turn down the radio and say nothing confirm Marcus's hypothesis, or just refuse to deny it?
- What does Marcus's "networks" knowledge mean — does he know specific people who ran the sponsored-passage program?
- Will they speak again about this? (They will, indirectly, in Ch 25 and Ch 28's aftermath.)
- Marcus's final line is a form of absolution — is he right? Was it nobody's fault here?

---

## Continuity Notes

- Establishes: Marcus and Eira's first substantive private conversation.
- Establishes: Marcus has disclosed his hypothesis about the body and the sponsored-passage networks to Eira. She has not confirmed or denied.
- The radio-turning-down is a physical gesture that stands for Eira's acknowledgment. This should be clear to the reader without being labeled.
- Marcus's German thought: *Die Last ist real, aber sie ist nicht neu.* — he thinks in German when the English isn't first. This is consistent with his character.
- The permit itself: two school rowboats, annual spring registration. A minor bureaucratic fact that is the chapter's pretext and should be processed quickly and correctly.
- Continuity with Ch 3: Marcus's hypothesis was first formed in October. He has been carrying it alone for six months. Now he has said it to Eira.

---

## Notes for Drafter

- The permit process should take approximately two paragraphs — it is real, it is efficient, it is not the point.
- The weather/news exchange is the warmup — two people finding a way to have a conversation that isn't the conversation they need to have. The BC-1206 as mediating object.
- Marcus's disclosure: he says it precisely. He is not confessing; he is sharing information. The precision of his phrasing is the character — *I think it's possible I know of the networks.*
- Eira's response — she reaches up and turns down the radio. She says: "I don't know anything officially." Do not explain this. Let it land.
- Marcus's line at the door should be said quietly, not dramatically. It is a gift. He gives it and leaves.
- Marcus does not tell Henry about the conversation. End on the dinner, not on a reflection.
