# Chapter 21 — The Stranger at the Dock

**One-line summary:** A civilian in a European-cut coat comes to the harbor asking about the October body and says he is looking for a relative; Eira sends him to Portland; the picture assembles further.

**POV:** Eira Bowman  
**Tense:** Third-person limited past  
**Arc phase:** Turn

---

## Setting / Time

Harbormaster's office and the harbor, Coldwater Reach. July 1944. Midsummer — the harbor's busiest season of the war years. Hot, bright, diesel and salt and the sound of engines. The breakwater is dry and the kelp is baking in the sun.

---

## Goal

Eira's goal: manage the summer harbor efficiently. The stranger is an interruption she was not looking for and cannot fully process in public.

---

## Obstacle

The stranger is not from any official agency she can identify — not Naval Intelligence, not the Coast Guard, not the Army. He is civilian. He says he is looking for a relative. His coat is European in cut. Eira has seen enough European-cut coats, in memory and in life, to register this without appearing to.

---

## Action Beats

**Midsummer harbor:**
- The chapter opens on the harbor in July — full, active, the summer catch moving through. Transient boats from up the coast, a pair of draggers back from three days out, the mooring float they've been meaning to replace bobbing low in the water. Eira is doing paperwork and physical checks in roughly equal measure.
- She is at the office at mid-morning when a man she doesn't know knocks at the open door.

**The stranger:**
- He is perhaps fifty, dressed in civilian clothes — a dark jacket that is not an American cut; the shoulders are wrong, slightly narrower, the collar button placed differently. He carries a hat in one hand. He speaks English with an accent she registers but cannot place exactly: Central European, but smoothed. German underneath, or something near it.
- He introduces himself: Werner Kahl. He says he has been in Portland on business (he does not say what business) and heard — through the Portland harbormaster's office, he says — about an unidentified body found on the Coldwater breakwater in October 1943.
- He says he is looking for a relative who may have come to Maine through Canada in the autumn of 1943. He has not heard from this relative since September. He shows her a photograph.
- The photograph is of a man — perhaps 45, dark-haired, in a suit. The photo is European in style and quality — the background, the way the subject is posed. It is not clear it is the same man as the body on the breakwater. Eira does not say this.

**Eira's management of the conversation:**
- She looks at the photograph. She says: "The body was taken by the Coast Guard in October. The case is in their files. Have you spoken with the Rockland station?"
- He says: "Yes. They were not able to give me any information."
- She says: "Then I would suggest you go to the Portland district office. The case was referred there." This is true; she is giving him the correct official information.
- He says: "Did you see the body yourself?"
- She says: "I'm the harbormaster. The body was discovered on my breakwater. I was present when the Coast Guard arrived."
- He says: "Did it look like—" He stops. He looks at the photograph again. He says: "Was there any identifying characteristic you remember?"
- She says: "I wasn't able to examine the body. The Coast Guard was on-site quickly."
- This is not technically a lie. She examined the body before the Coast Guard arrived. But she was not able to examine it thoroughly. The distinction is fine enough to carry.
- He thanks her. He puts the photograph in his breast pocket. He leaves.

**After he leaves:**
- Eira goes to the door of her office and watches him walk back down the wharf. He walks with a slight limp she hadn't noticed inside. He is wearing shoes, not boots.
- She goes back to her desk. She thinks.
- Werner Kahl. Looking for a relative. European accent, European coat, European photograph. He came to Coldwater because the Portland harbormaster's office sent him here — which means the case has moved further through the official system than she knew.
- She thinks about the photograph. The man in the suit. She does not know if it was the same man as the body. She did not look long enough in October to be certain. His face had been in the water.
- She thinks: *Kahl*. She thinks: if he found the Portland office, he has resources. He is organized. He knows the Maritime route. He came here because someone told him to come here.
- Who told him?

**The afternoon — working it out:**
- She processes the afternoon's harbor traffic. She keeps working on the question: if Kahl's relative used the sponsored-passage program (which Marcus suggested, which the Ellenbogen letter would support), and if the program had organizers, then Kahl may know who ran the network in this region.
- She considers: should she contact Marcus? They have spoken once, properly, at the permit. She knows where he is. She does not contact him today.
- She does not contact the Coast Guard about Kahl's visit, either.

**End:**
- Evening. The harbor quieting. She makes the log entry for the day — routine harbor traffic, the mooring float note, no mention of Kahl. She closes the log.
- She thinks: someone's brother, or son, or friend, crossed from Canada in a dory and came to this harbor and died on this breakwater. And someone else has come looking for him. The picture is almost complete. What is missing is the middle — the three days between October 11 and October 14.

---

## Outcome / Turn

Kahl's visit assembles the picture further: there is a person who cares about the dead man, who knows the route, who has been tracking it through official channels. The body is not unclaimed — it has someone looking for it, even now. The chapter turns on the question Eira does not ask Kahl directly: *Was that him?* She doesn't ask because she is not certain, and because she has spent ten months arranging her uncertainty as protection. Now she is not sure the protection is serving the right person.

---

## New Question or Pressure Raised

- Was the man in Kahl's photograph the same man as the body?
- Who is Werner Kahl, and how is he related to the dead man?
- Should Eira contact Marcus — who has knowledge of the sponsored-passage network — and tell him Kahl came?
- What happened in the three days between October 11 (the dock sighting) and October 14 (the body)? Where did the man shelter?

---

## Continuity Notes

- Establishes: Werner Kahl — European accent, European-cut coat, looking for a relative, came from Portland. Shows Eira a photograph of the dead man (or someone who may be).
- Establishes: The Portland harbormaster's office has the case details and is directing inquiries to Coldwater.
- The photograph: it shows a man, perhaps 45, dark-haired, in a suit, European-quality photo. Eira cannot confirm it is the body (the body's face had been in the water). This ambiguity is permanent — it is the novel's central unresolved fact.
- Eira does not write Kahl's visit in the log. Second time she has kept something out of the log (first was October 11).
- The question she ends on: what happened in the three days?

---

## Notes for Drafter

- The stranger's entrance should be ordinary — a knock at the open door. He is not menacing; he is organized and sad.
- Eira's management of the conversation: she gives him correct official information. Every statement she makes is technically accurate. The chapter should render this without italicizing the moral tension — let the reader feel it.
- The photograph: describe it precisely. Do not say it matches the body. Eira does not know; neither does the reader.
- The limp: noticed only after he leaves, from behind. A small physical fact. He does not dramatize it.
- The log: she does not write Kahl's visit in it. End the chapter with her closing the log.
