# Chapter 4 — What the Ledger Holds

**One-line summary:** The Coast Guard returns with confirmation that the dead man was not American; Eira answers their questions, keeps her secret, and goes through the harbor log looking for anything she missed.

**POV:** Eira Bowman  
**Tense:** Third-person limited past  
**Arc phase:** Setup

---

## Setting / Time

Harbormaster's office and the harbor wharf. Early November 1943. The blow is long past; the harbor is settling into its winter rhythm. Cold. A northeast wind. The shortwave is pulling in Portland station clearly.

---

## Goal

Eira's official goal: cooperate with the investigation, satisfy the Coast Guard, maintain harbor normality. Her private goal: find out whether there is anything in the log that would either explain or contradict what she didn't report.

---

## Obstacle

The Coast Guard officer — a Lieutenant Farris from Rockland — is asking careful questions. He has some information she doesn't have yet (the clothing analysis), and she has information he doesn't have (October 11). The dynamic requires her to be credible and incomplete at once. The obstacle is the log itself: when she goes through it, she is looking for something that might let her off the hook. It doesn't.

---

## Action Beats

**Lt. Farris arrives — morning:**
- Farris comes with one seaman. He is not hostile but he is thorough. He sits across from Eira at her office desk. The BC-1206 is on low behind her.
- He tells her what they've determined: the man's clothing was not American manufacture. The style and cut are Continental European — probably French or German, the quartermaster's analyst said. Not recent; the coat was at least five years old. No papers. No identifying marks except a small scar on the left hand.
- He asks Eira: had she noticed any unusual vessel activity in the days before the blow? Any boats she couldn't account for? Any individuals on the dock who didn't belong to a registered vessel?
- Eira says: no unusual vessels in the registry. No unregistered activity. She gives this answer with the care of a woman who is not technically lying.
- Farris asks about the north wharf specifically. She says: routine check after the blow. Nothing out of order.
- Farris writes in his notebook. He says they believe the man may have been in the water for closer to one day than three — which means he did not fall from a vessel during the blow. He died after the blow, or during its tail end.
- This is new information. Eira notes it. Her face does not change.
- Farris says they're treating it as an accidental death, likely drowning, pending further inquiry. He thanks her and leaves.

**After Farris leaves — the log:**
- Eira takes the harbor log from the shelf and reads back through the first two weeks of October.
- She reads her own entries. Methodical, tidal, practical. The entry for October 11 — *Mooring lines north wharf — all secure. Wind NW 12 knots.* — is there, as she left it.
- She reads it three times. The man she saw was doing what the log says she found already done: the mooring lines were secure. He was standing near them, or appearing to check them. That's all she can say for certain.
- She goes through the week's entries looking for: unusual mooring requests, fuel purchases for unknown vessels, transient boat registrations. There are none she can't account for.
- She puts the log away.

**Physical work — afternoon:**
- She goes out to the dock. She works through the afternoon: adjusting a mooring float that's been dragging, helping Dakin with his winter lay-up, noting the condition of the granite on the north breakwater. She doesn't go to the north side of the wharf.
- Or rather: she does go to the north side of the wharf, checks the bollard where the figure stood, and looks at the granite. Nothing. The water has been here and left nothing.

**Evening — inventory:**
- Back in the office. She makes a catalog in her mind (not in the log) of what she noticed about the body: European coat, not-laborer hands, dark matter under two fingernails, no papers in outer pockets (she hadn't been able to check the inner lining before the Coast Guard arrived). Age 40–55. Face had been in the water.
- New information from Farris: died after the blow, not during. European clothing. No identification.
- Combined with what she saw on October 11: a man, not from here, checking or appearing to check the mooring lines at 2 a.m.
- She sits with this. She does not write it down. She turns up the shortwave and listens to Portland for the weather.

---

## Outcome / Turn

Farris's revelation — the man died after the blow, not during — changes Eira's picture. He wasn't swept off a vessel in the storm. He came from somewhere and he was on the dock before he died. The chapter turns on this: what she didn't report has become more significant, not less. And she has still not reported it.

---

## New Question or Pressure Raised

- If he died after the blow, where did he shelter during it? (The dory answer, which comes in Ch 14, is not available yet.)
- What was under his fingernails?
- Why was he checking mooring lines, or appearing to?
- Is Farris going to come back? Is the case moving toward her?

---

## Continuity Notes

- Establishes: Lt. Farris of the Rockland Coast Guard station as the investigating officer.
- Establishes: The Coast Guard's initial finding — European clothing, probably French or German manufacture, at least 5 years old. Scar on left hand. No papers.
- Establishes: Time-of-death estimate — man died after the blow, not during. This is a significant revision of the assumed timeline.
- The BC-1206 shortwave: confirm it is on low during the Farris interview, pulled up for weather at the end. Consistent with Ch 1.
- Eira does not write her private catalog in the log — this is important. The log contains only official, recordable fact.
- Dakin appears briefly in the afternoon scene (he will give Eira the dory information in Ch 14).

---

## Notes for Drafter

- Farris should be rendered without villainy or excessive sympathy — he's a professional doing his job. His questions are competent. Eira's answers are technically accurate. The moral weight is Eira's, not his.
- The log-reading scene is the chapter's heart. Eira reads her own words and they hold what she chose to put in them. The prose should feel like a person examining an object they made.
- The afternoon physical work is important — after the interview she goes back to the dock. Eira processes through action. The moment when she goes to the north bollard and finds nothing should be rendered without comment.
- Keep the shortwave as a recurring texture in Eira's office scenes — weather, Portland, harbor traffic. It is her connection to the larger world, tightly controlled.
