# Chapter 18 — The Columbine

**One-line summary:** Hannah turns eighteen; Karl places a columbine wildflower on the fence post where she'll pass; she takes it and is angry with herself for taking it; she presses it into her father's German dictionary.

**POV:** Hannah Pfeiffer  
**Tense:** Third-person limited past  
**Arc phase:** Rising Action

---

## Setting / Time

Pfeiffer farm. Late May, early June 1944. Spring in full work — the farm at its most active. The fence between the icehouse yard and the farm's main yard is lined with grass that has come in thick. Columbine grows at the base of the eastern fence post, the one Karl repaired in the frost.

---

## Goal

Hannah's goal on the day of her birthday: get through the day's work. The birthday is not announced at the farm — her mother doesn't mention it, her father gives her one sentence in German in the morning (*Alles Gute, mein Kind* — All the best, my child) and goes out to the field. She does not expect anything more.

---

## Obstacle

She has been careful. Eight months of conversations through the fence, and she has kept them on the side of the fence that is defensible: practical, informational, two people with a shared language exchanging observations about farm work and weather and family. The columbine changes the grammar. She knows it when she sees it.

---

## Action Beats

**Morning — birthday:**
- She wakes on June 2 (a Saturday in 1944). She is eighteen. Her father says *Alles Gute, mein Kind* at breakfast. Her mother makes no indication she has noticed the date. Hannah does not correct either of them.
- The prisoners are delivered at seven-thirty. Lonzo's truck. She sees them unload from the kitchen window.
- Karl does not look toward the house.

**The day's work:**
- Spring farm work. Full day. Fencing, feeding, the cows' afternoon milking run early because Albrecht needs help in the north field. The prisoners are plowing on the far side; Hannah and her father work the near side with the horses.
- She has not had a conversation with Karl in five days — the spring's busyness has pushed the fence encounters to fewer and shorter.

**Walking to the mailbox — early afternoon:**
- She goes to the mailbox at the road's end in the early afternoon. There is no V-Mail from Will — the April letter was the last; there will be more, but not this week. There is a county ledger form she needs to return.
- On the way back she passes the east fence, the repaired post.
- There is a columbine flower on the post. One stem, with the small red-and-yellow flower. It has been placed in the gap between the post and the wire — it won't fall; it was put there intentionally.
- She stops. She looks at it. She looks around the yard. Lonzo's truck is on the far side of the barn. The prisoners are in the north field, visible as small figures.
- She takes the flower.
- She carries it the rest of the way to the house. She is not happy about taking it. She cannot put it back.

**The anger:**
- In the kitchen she is angry with herself. The anger is specific: she knows what the flower means — not romantically, exactly, but as a mark across the fence. As Karl saying: *I know the date. I chose to mark it.* She has kept the conversations at the fence from becoming this. She has been careful. And she took the flower because she couldn't not.
- She does not throw it away.

**The dictionary:**
- She goes to her father's shelf — the German dictionary, a large *Duden* from 1928, thick and worn. She opens it. She places the columbine flower flat between two pages. She closes the dictionary and puts it back.
- She does not tell anyone. She does not write it in anything.

**Evening:**
- Dinner. The day's work is done. Her father is tired but has the look of a man who has worked correctly. He says: *Heute war ein guter Tag.* (Today was a good day.) He means the field. She says: *Ja.*
- She thinks about the flower in the dictionary. She thinks: tomorrow she will go to the fence at the usual time. She will not say anything about the flower. She will not thank him. She will treat it as though it didn't happen.
- She does not know if this is the right thing to do or the wrong thing. She knows it is what she will do.

---

## Outcome / Turn

The chapter turns on the taking of the flower. Not on the placing of it — that is Karl's act. Her act is the taking, the keeping, the pressing between pages of her father's dictionary. This is the first thing she has done in the Karl situation that she cannot narrate as purely practical. The anger at herself is honest; she is eighteen, she knows what she has done, and she doesn't undo it.

---

## New Question or Pressure Raised

- Will Karl know she took it? (He will, because it's gone.)
- Will anything be said? (No. But something has been said without words.)
- What is the columbine pressed in the dictionary doing to the sentences around it?
- Is this the beginning of something that will need to be ended, or is it the ending of something that was never beginning?

---

## Continuity Notes

- Establishes: Hannah's 18th birthday — June 2, 1944 (four days before D-Day).
- Establishes: The columbine in the *Duden* dictionary. This object recurs in Ch 29 when Hannah looks for it after Will comes home.
- Establishes: The *Duden* (1928 German dictionary) on Albrecht's shelf — a specific, weighted object.
- Karl places the flower intentionally — he knows her birthday from a prior conversation (she mentioned it while discussing spring planting schedules; the date came up). This connection should be available to a sharp reader but not stated in this chapter.
- Hannah's decision — to say nothing about the flower at the fence tomorrow — is a small choice that carries forward.

---

## Notes for Drafter

- The birthday should feel unremarkable at first — this is a wartime farm; Hannah is an adult now; birthdays are not major events. Albrecht's single sentence and Magda's silence are the household's register.
- The columbine on the fence post: let the reader arrive at its meaning the same way Hannah does — she sees it, she recognizes it, she takes it. The recognition should not be overdone. She understands what it is. She takes it.
- The anger in the kitchen is the chapter's moral texture. She is not pleased with herself. She does not dramatize her displeasure. She simply knows she has crossed something.
- The dictionary: the physical act of pressing the flower is precise. She opens to a page — what page? Let the drafter choose something appropriate. She presses it flat. She closes the book.
- End on her decision about tomorrow. She will go to the fence. She will say nothing about the flower. She will treat it as though it didn't happen. This is the last line's register: not tragic, not triumphant. Just: what she will do.
