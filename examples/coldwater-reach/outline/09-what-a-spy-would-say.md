# Chapter 9 — What a Spy Would Say

**One-line summary:** A student asks Marcus in class whether he is a spy; Marcus answers with precision and without defensiveness; afterward he tells Henry, and Henry's response is the chapter's turn.

**POV:** Dr. Marcus Klein  
**Tense:** Third-person limited past  
**Arc phase:** Setup

---

## Setting / Time

Coldwater Reach Consolidated School and the Crow house. Mid-January 1944. Deep winter. The furnace is reliable now. Snow on the ground. The new semester has started two weeks prior.

---

## Goal

Marcus's goal in class: teach the German unit on subordinate clauses. His private goal, which he does not name as a goal, is to survive being observed in a way that does not require him to perform survival.

---

## Obstacle

The question — "are you a spy?" — comes from a student named Rolfe, who is not malicious. He is sixteen, and someone (a parent, presumably) has said something within earshot that reached him. The obstacle is not Rolfe. The obstacle is the long-established reality of Marcus's life: the question has been asked in various forms, in various languages, by various authorities, and he has answered it every time, and the answering does not end the question.

---

## Action Beats

**Opening — January, the new semester:**
- Marcus's classroom in January. He has added a small shelf of Latin texts next to the German ones. The window has a film of ice at the corner. The students settle into the new semester's rhythm.
- He notes: Hannah is in the front row this term. She has moved herself — she was in the third row last semester. He does not remark on this.

**The German class:**
- He is teaching subordinate clauses — *weil, obwohl, damit,* the constructions that require verb-final order. He is writing on the board in German (the English-only instruction does not, as he has arranged it, prohibit writing German on the board, only speaking it in instruction). He writes the examples; he explains them in English.
- A student named Rolfe — the one who made the joke about German prisoners in Ch 3 — raises his hand. Marcus calls on him.
- Rolfe says: "My father says that a man who speaks German and isn't German is either a spy or he's pretending not to be German. Which one are you?"
- The class goes quiet. Hannah, in the front row, goes still.
- Marcus puts down the chalk. He turns to face the class. He speaks carefully: "Neither. I am Austrian, which is a distinction your father and I might have had a longer conversation about before 1938. I speak German because I was educated in it. I am here because this school needed someone who could teach it. If I were a spy, I would, I think, have chosen a more effective posting than a classroom in coastal Maine."
- Two students laugh. Rolfe looks uncertain. Marcus picks up the chalk.
- He says: "Subordinate clauses. *Weil* takes the verb to the end. Let's continue."

**After class — alone:**
- The class empties. Hannah is the last to leave. At the door she pauses and says, without turning: "He's wrong, you know. About the German." She leaves.
- Marcus erases the board. He thinks about what she said and about what she meant: that a person can speak German without being German, and her father's household is the evidence. He thinks also about Rolfe, and about Rolfe's father, and about how the question — *which one are you?* — has been asked by professional interrogators with the same grammar and the same uncertainty about the answer.

**Evening — Henry:**
- Thursday chess. Marcus tells Henry what happened. Henry listens.
- Henry says: "What did you say?"
- Marcus tells him.
- Henry is quiet for a moment. He moves a bishop. He says: "A more effective posting than coastal Maine. That's a good line."
- Marcus: "I thought so."
- Henry: "Did it work?"
- Marcus: "For today."
- Henry: "What about tomorrow?"
- Marcus: "Tomorrow I'll teach subordinate clauses and we'll see."
- Henry takes Marcus's queen. Marcus stares at the board.
- Henry: "The boy's father isn't going to stop thinking what he thinks."
- Marcus: "No."
- Henry: "That's the thing about a line. It works once."
- Marcus looks at him. Henry is not looking at him; he is looking at the board. Marcus says: "What do you do, then?"
- Henry: "You don't do a line. You just keep showing up. That's all it ever is."
- They play. Marcus loses.

---

## Outcome / Turn

The chapter turns on Henry's answer: *You don't do a line. You just keep showing up.* This is not consoling advice; it is a description of the strategy Henry has been using for twenty years in a Maine town that didn't fully want him. Marcus receives it not as wisdom dispensed but as information shared between two people in comparable situations. He loses the chess game. The loss is the turn — he was playing to win and instead heard something true.

---

## New Question or Pressure Raised

- Is Rolfe's father going to do something beyond the classroom? (No — but Marcus doesn't know this.)
- What does Hannah mean by "he's wrong about the German"? What does she know about the difference between speaking a language and being its people?
- Is Henry's strategy — keep showing up — available to Marcus? He has kept showing up. How much longer?
- Elias's name comes up: Henry mentions his son is in the Pacific. Marcus says nothing but registers it — another person in the Pacific.

---

## Continuity Notes

- Establishes: Rolfe as the student who asks the spy question — his previous appearance in Ch 3 (the prisoner joke) makes this consistent character behavior.
- Establishes: Hannah's move to the front row in January — a small choice she makes without explanation.
- Henry mentions Elias Crow (his son in the Pacific) in passing. This is the first time in Hannah's and Marcus's chapters that the Pacific is a shared reference point — Will Pfeiffer is also in the Pacific. Marcus doesn't know this yet.
- The chapter closes the setup arc for Marcus: he has now been questioned publicly, defended himself adequately, and received the clearest statement of his situation from Henry. The next Marcus chapter begins the rising action.

---

## Notes for Drafter

- Rolfe's question should be delivered without cruelty — he is not a villain; he is a kid repeating something he heard. Marcus's answer should be equally precise: not angry, not wounded, not performing composure. Simply accurate.
- Hannah's line at the door (*He's wrong about the German*) should be fast — she says it without turning, and leaves. Marcus does not call after her. He erases the board.
- The chess scene is the chapter's load. Henry's line — *You don't do a line. You just keep showing up* — is the most direct statement of Henry's wisdom in the book. It should land cleanly, not rhetorically. He says it while looking at the board, not at Marcus.
- Marcus loses the chess game. He doesn't usually lose (he plays the Sicilian, he is good). Tonight he is not paying attention to the board. Henry wins.
- Elias: Henry mentions him in passing, not as a subject — something like "Elias was in the Pacific by then" or "Elias wrote that..." Small, not emphasized. Planted for later.
