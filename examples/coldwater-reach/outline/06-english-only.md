# Chapter 6 — English Only

**One-line summary:** The school board prohibits spoken German in the German classroom; Marcus complies in letter and begins to understand what Hannah's home life costs her; Henry tells him his name came up at the board meeting.

**POV:** Dr. Marcus Klein  
**Tense:** Third-person limited past  
**Arc phase:** Setup

---

## Setting / Time

Coldwater Reach Consolidated School and the Crow house. Mid to late November 1943. The furnace is now reliably producing heat. The days are short. First snow has come and gone, leaving mud.

---

## Goal

Marcus's professional goal: navigate the school board's instruction without losing the capacity to teach German effectively. His private goal: keep his position without making himself so small that the position becomes worthless.

---

## Obstacle

The English-only instruction is delivered by Wakefield as policy, not as punishment — which means Marcus has no legitimate grievance and nothing to push against. Compliance is required; its contours are his to determine. The second obstacle: Hannah submits a short homework exercise that is too good, and he needs to know more about her situation before he knows what to do with her.

---

## Action Beats

**Wakefield delivers the board's instruction — morning:**
- Wakefield stops Marcus in the corridor before first period. He is apologetic in the way of a man who is not quite sorry. The school board, he says, has asked that modern language classes be conducted in English. This applies specifically to German instruction. It is a wartime measure.
- Marcus says: "How shall I teach German pronunciation without speaking German?"
- Wakefield says: he's sure Marcus will find a way.
- Marcus continues to his classroom. He writes on the board in Latin. It is not a German class day, which gives him time.

**Processing the instruction:**
- In the interval between classes, Marcus thinks through the board's logic with his characteristic precision. The instruction is legal. It is arguably defensible. It is also: designed to make German instruction nearly impossible, or at minimum unpleasant, which may be the point. He can comply in the letter and preserve enough function to teach. He will do this. He does not perform the decision; he simply makes it.

**German class — English only:**
- His German class that afternoon. He opens in English. He teaches the day's grammar from the textbook in English. He calls on Hannah to read a German passage aloud from the text; she does; it is grammatically correct and pronounced correctly because she learned German at home, not from him.
- He does not say "gut." He says "correct." He watches the class. Nobody appears to notice that he has said nothing in German for fifty minutes.
- After class, Hannah waits a moment — she is looking at her book. He says, in English: "Miss Pfeiffer, your accent is good." She says: "My father speaks German at home." A beat. She leaves.

**Evening — Henry:**
- Henry is in the kitchen when Marcus gets home. He is mending something — a coat, a harness strap, something textile. He says, without preamble: "Your name came up at the board meeting last week."
- Marcus sits down. He says: "What was said?"
- Henry: "Alderman Marsh asked whether the school had done a thorough review of all personnel with European backgrounds, given the nature of the war. Wakefield said you'd been vetted by the committee and had your papers. Marsh said he wasn't talking about papers."
- Pause.
- Marcus: "And?"
- Henry: "And Wakefield said you were the only German instructor in a thirty-mile radius. Which isn't exactly a vote of confidence, but it kept your job."
- Marcus: "Yes."
- Henry: "You knew this was coming."
- Marcus: "I knew something like this was coming."
- They sit for a moment. Lillian calls them to dinner. They go.

**Hannah's homework exercise — late evening:**
- Marcus grades papers after dinner. Among them: Hannah's short translation exercise — a German poem about a river seen from two banks, and her note about what happens to meaning when it crosses from one side to the other. It is not a student's analysis; it is the perception of someone who lives on the border of two languages.
- He writes a note in the margin: *See me.* Then crosses it out. Then writes it again.

---

## Outcome / Turn

Two things have happened by the end of the chapter: Marcus has arranged his compliance with the school board's instruction in a way that preserves his ability to teach, and he has seen in Hannah's exercise something that changes how he understands her situation. His name has also come up at a board meeting in a way that makes his position clearer to him — it is held together by professional utility, not by trust or welcome.

---

## New Question or Pressure Raised

- What will Marcus do with Hannah's exercise?
- What does Alderman Marsh represent — is this pressure going to increase?
- Is the English-only instruction the beginning of a longer campaign to remove Marcus?
- What does Henry make of all this — and why does he tell Marcus so plainly?

---

## Continuity Notes

- Establishes: Wakefield as the mechanism of school board pressure — apologetic, not malicious, which is its own kind of problem.
- Establishes: Alderman Marsh as the named antagonist on the school board (not a villain, but a force).
- Establishes: Marcus's English-only compliance strategy — he teaches in English, makes no show of it.
- Establishes: Hannah's bilingual competency as visible to Marcus.
- The Hannah homework exercise foreshadows Ch 13 (the longer essay on translation). The marginal note *See me* is planted here.
- Henry tells Marcus about the board meeting directly — this is consistent with his character. He is not performing protection; he is giving Marcus information he needs.
- Marcus does not perform distress about the board meeting. He registers it, processes it, moves on to dinner. This is important.

---

## Notes for Drafter

- The Wakefield exchange should be short and specific. He is not menacing. He is a man with a small amount of power being asked to do a thing he probably doesn't fully endorse. Marcus's response — "How shall I teach German pronunciation without speaking German?" — should be said plainly, not as a weapon.
- The English-only class scene: the irony should be rendered without underlining it. Hannah reads German aloud; Marcus says "correct." The class doesn't notice; they've stopped expecting anything else.
- The Henry scene is the chapter's emotional center. Henry delivers information that Marcus already half-knew as something Marcus needed to know. This is what Henry does: he gives Marcus the practical truth without asking him how he feels about it.
- Marcus's evening is the grading scene — the Hannah exercise. He writes *See me*, crosses it out, writes it again. End there.
