# Characters

---

## POV Characters

### Eira Bowman
**Age:** 52 at the story's opening (October 1943)  
**Role:** Harbormaster of Coldwater Reach; primary POV character  
**Background:** Born in Coldwater Reach. Married Owen Bowman at twenty-three. Worked beside him at the harbor for twenty years before his death in the November gale of 1935. The selectmen gave her the position provisionally — for one season, until they found someone. Eight years later she is still provisional in no way she acknowledges. No children. A sister in Bangor she corresponds with by letter.

**Inner drives:** Eira's deepest want is to be neither exceptional nor invisible — to be simply competent, which she is, and to have that competence be the end of the conversation. The job continuously refuses her that. Every decision she makes carries the freight of its being made by her. She manages this by making the job entirely procedural in her own mind: tide tables, draft limits, mooring fees, storm protocol. Her grief for Owen has been so thoroughly converted into harbor management that she sometimes cannot locate it directly; it surfaces only in technical language, which is to say, it surfaces all the time.

**Voice:** Spare. Observational. Economical with interior commentary. She notices the physical world with the precision of someone who has had to act on what she notices. When she is frightened she makes lists. When she is angry she goes quiet. When she is uncertain she delays a decision by measuring something.

**The inciting secret:** On the night of October 11, 1943, Eira saw a man she did not recognize on the north side of the wharf at 2 a.m. — checking mooring lines, or appearing to. She did not hail him because she was not certain enough of what she saw. She has not told the Coast Guard this. The body came in three days later.

**Relationships:**  
- *Owen Bowman (deceased):* The ghost in all her professional habits. She does not idealize him.  
- *The fishermen and dockhands:* Mutual competence-based respect; not warmth.  
- *The selectmen:* A managed tension. They need her to be right. She needs them to say nothing.  
- *Marcus Klein:* They know each other by sight and one professional encounter (a licensing matter). They become something more careful than friends.  
- *Hannah Pfeiffer:* They are strangers until the body creates a proximity neither sought.

### Hannah Pfeiffer
**Age:** 17 at the story's opening  
**Role:** Farmer's daughter; second POV character  
**Background:** Born in Coldwater Reach to Albrecht and Magda Pfeiffer, who emigrated from Bavaria in 1922. She has an older brother, Wilhelm (called Will), who enlisted in 1942 and is currently somewhere in the Pacific. Hannah has grown up bilingual in a household that is now trying to be only one thing at a time. She attends the consolidated school where Marcus teaches; he is her German and Latin instructor.

**Inner drives:** Hannah wants, with a seventeen-year-old's specific intensity, to make a choice that is entirely her own — not her father's, not the war's, not the town's assumption about who the German dairy farmer's daughter must be. She is intelligent and observant in ways the farm does not fully use. The arrival of the POWs disrupts a status quo she was already restless inside.

**The Karl question:** Karl Reinmar, 19, the youngest of the four POWs, is from Kirchheim, a town fifty kilometers from Albrecht Pfeiffer's hometown of Amberg. He speaks Bavarian-inflected German that Albrecht recognizes by sound. Hannah speaks to Karl through the fence in German, first about farm work, then about other things. This is neither romantic fantasy nor spy thriller; it is the strange intimacy of two young people on opposite sides of a fence who share a language and a predicament neither of them chose.

**Voice:** The most interior of the three. Her narration lets things in — intrusive thoughts, half-formed impressions, the German phrases her father uses at the table that she finds herself thinking in. She is not unreliable, but she is unguarded in ways Eira and Marcus are not. The farm and its rhythms give her chapters a ground note of physical work: milking, fencing, feeding, mucking, the cold.

**Relationships:**  
- *Albrecht Pfeiffer (father):* She loves him and she is losing him — to the war's effect on his sense of self, to his retreat into German, to his silence about what he did or did not do before 1922. He is not a villain. He is a man who chose the wrong time to stop speaking.  
- *Magda Pfeiffer (mother):* Magda's silence is different from Albrecht's — it is a withdrawal, not a protest. Hannah tends her practically, without being able to reach her.  
- *Karl Reinmar:* The connection she allows herself that she is not allowed to allow. It does not resolve simply.  
- *Will Pfeiffer (brother):* Absent. His V-Mails are getting shorter. His last letter was in August. Hannah carries this the way you carry something heavy when you've decided not to put it down.  
- *Marcus Klein:* Her teacher. She is one of perhaps three students who read what he assigns. He notices her; she notices that he notices.

### Dr. Marcus Klein
**Age:** 47 at the story's opening  
**Role:** German and Latin teacher at Coldwater Reach Consolidated School; third POV character  
**Background:** Born in Vienna, 1896. Classical philology at the University of Vienna. Left Austria in March 1938, four days after the Anschluss, for Paris. From Paris to London in 1940 when France fell. Interned in Canada at Camp Sherbrooke (an enemy alien internment camp in Quebec for German-speaking refugees) from June to December 1940, released after advocacy by the Emergency Committee in Aid of Displaced Foreign Scholars. Arrived in the United States in January 1941. Took up a nominal post at Harvard Extension while living on the Committee's rolls. Hired by the Coldwater school board in March 1942 after the previous German instructor (a twenty-four-year-old local named Braddock) enlisted. He is Jewish; the school board has not asked; he has not said.

**Inner drives:** Marcus wants to stop waiting. His entire life since 1938 has been provisional — the next city, the next permit, the next sponsoring organization, the next temporary berth. Coldwater is the first place he has lived two years in a row since Vienna. He cannot tell yet if this is an ending or just a longer pause. His deepest question is whether he has an identity that is not constructed entirely from what he was displaced from — and whether a person can build anything new at forty-seven.

**The Sherbrooke question:** The internment camp is a closed subject in his narration, mentioned in a single early chapter and thereafter avoided. This avoidance is itself information. He was interned with other refugees, not with Nazis, but the distinction did not concern the Canadian government, and the experience of being interned under suspicion of loyalty to the country that had just expelled him is a wound he manages by precision — by keeping his language so controlled that nothing can be used against him again.

**Voice:** Precise, slightly formal, with a measured academic quality. He thinks in terms of categories and distinctions. His interior monologue often takes the form of argument-structure — a proposition, a counterproposition, a qualification. His relationship to English is the relationship of a man who mastered it as a foreign language and now trusts it more than the languages he was born into: he knows exactly what each word costs.

**Relationships:**  
- *Henry Crow:* The one person in town with whom Marcus does not feel observed. Henry is not performing tolerance; he simply does not need Marcus to be explicable. They play chess on Thursday evenings.  
- *Lillian Crow:* She feeds Marcus, and feeds him well, and asks no questions. He is faintly undone by ordinary domestic kindness.  
- *Hannah Pfeiffer:* His best student. He is protective of her in the way of a teacher who sees a mind being wasted. The POW situation on her farm adds a complication.  
- *Eira Bowman:* They are, separately, the two most anomalous figures in Coldwater — each holding a post that the town didn't build for them. Their recognition of each other is slow and never named.  
- *The school board:* They are waiting for him to fail, or for the war to end, whichever comes first. He knows this and teaches around it.

## Secondary Characters

### Henry Crow
**Age:** 54  
**Role:** Chief custodian of Coldwater Reach Consolidated School; Marcus's landlord  
**Background:** Born in Norfolk, Virginia. Came north with his wife Lillian in 1924 as part of the Second Great Migration. Has been at the school since 1927. He repairs everything in the building and is the only person who knows where the water shutoffs are.  
**Voice (if a POV chapter is used):** Dry, practical, grounded. He has outlasted several principals. He understands the town's architecture of power and moves through it without appearing to notice it — which is not the same as not noticing. His perspective on Marcus is the most accurate in the book and the least sentimental.  
**Relationship to Eira:** They know each other by long acquaintance. Henry's son Elias is in the Navy.

### Lillian Crow
**Age:** 51  
**Role:** Henry's wife; keeper of the Crow household  
**Background:** Also originally from Norfolk. Works part-time at the canning plant on the south end of town. Manages the household, the garden, the put-up, and the quiet.  
**Function:** Lillian is the novel's primary figure of uncalculated human warmth. She does not have chapters but her presence is load-bearing in Marcus's arc.

### Lonzo Reed
**Age:** 60  
**Role:** Coastal Patrol private assigned as escort/guard for the Pfeiffer farm POW detail  
**Background:** A Coldwater native who served in France in 1918, came back, worked at the hardware store for twenty years, and was called back into service at fifty-eight because they needed warm bodies on the coast watch. He is not cruel to the prisoners. He is not kind to them either. He is tired and his feet hurt and he would like very much to go home by four o'clock.  
**Voice (if a POV chapter is used):** Laconic. He has seen what war does to people and has arranged his mind around not thinking about it. His observations of the Pfeiffer family, the prisoners, and Hannah in particular carry a long-view weight that none of the main POVs can achieve.

### Karl Reinmar
**Age:** 19  
**Role:** One of four German POWs assigned to Pfeiffer farm; the youngest; the one Hannah speaks to  
**Background:** From Kirchheim, Bavaria. Conscripted at seventeen. Captured in North Africa with Rommel's Afrika Korps in May 1943. He speaks German with the Bavarian accent that Albrecht Pfeiffer recognizes. He is not a Nazi ideologue; he is also not innocent of the war's logic. He is nineteen.  
**Voice (if a POV chapter is used):** Simple declarative German translated into English — his English is minimal. His chapter, if it exists, would be among the shortest and would focus on a single day.

### Albrecht Pfeiffer
**Age:** 58  
**Role:** Hannah's father; owner of Pfeiffer farm  
**Background:** Born in Amberg, Bavaria. Emigrated to Maine in 1922 with Magda. Has farmed the Reach for twenty years. His silence since the war started is not treason; it is a man's grief at being made into a category he does not recognize. He has not returned to Germany since leaving. He does not know what he would find.

### Magda Pfeiffer
**Age:** 55  
**Role:** Hannah's mother  
**Background:** From a farm village twenty kilometers from Amberg. Her withdrawal is clinical by any name; the town has no name for it in 1943. She tends the house. She does not speak unless spoken to, and sometimes not then.

### Owen Bowman (deceased)
**Age at death:** 49 (drowned November 1935)  
**Role:** Eira's husband; the previous harbormaster  
**Function:** He is present in every chapter Eira has — in the harbor's routines, in her professional habits, in the way she notes what he would have done. He is not idealized and not blamed. He is simply absent in a way that has become structural.
