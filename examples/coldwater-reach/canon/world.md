# World

## Coldwater Reach, Maine — 1943–1945

### Geography and Physical Character

Coldwater Reach is a working harbor town on the mid-Maine coast — not a resort town, not a lobster-postcard town. Population roughly 1,400. The harbor is deepwater enough for fishing draggers and the occasional coastal freighter carrying lumber and canned goods south. The breakwater is granite, built in the 1890s, about four hundred feet long, stained orange-brown at the tide line. The harbormaster's office is a two-room clapboard structure at the landward end of the wharf, wood-stove heated, smelling of diesel and wet rope and the tide-ledger that Eira Bowman keeps in a hand ruled notebook. A single telephone line. A BC-1206 shortwave receiver on a shelf above the window.

The town sits at the mouth of the Reach — a tidal inlet that narrows to a river a mile upstream. Main Street runs parallel to the water: a general store, a post office, a pharmacy that doubles as the soda fountain and rumor exchange, a hardware store, a Congregationalist church, a Catholic church (mostly for the Franco-American families on the north side), and the consolidated district school where Marcus Klein teaches. The school is a 1927 brick building with four classrooms and a furnace that requires tending.

Three miles inland, the Pfeiffer farm occupies 140 acres of rolling pasture and second-growth woodlot above the frost line. The icehouse on the eastern edge of the property — a low timber structure with sawdust insulation, emptied of its summer-cut stock — has been converted under county direction into a barracks for four German POWs. Two cots on each side, a cast-iron stove, a single electric bulb on a long cord.

### Period Texture

**The war's presence on the home front:** No neon. Dimout regulations on the coast through early 1943 (the U-boat threat has receded but habits persist). Windows taped at night. Gasoline rationed — Eira drives a 1937 Ford half-ton on a C-ration card she stretches. Meat, butter, and sugar rationed. Ration books are carried like wallets. Silk and nylon unavailable. The soda fountain has no Coca-Cola some weeks.

V-Mail: letters home from servicemen are microfilmed, shipped, reprinted at the other end — small, sometimes slightly blurred. Families keep them in shoeboxes. The pharmacist's son is in the Pacific; the envelopes on the counter are from the early months, before everything went quiet.

The draft has taken every able-bodied man between twenty and thirty-five. The town runs on men too old, men too young, men physically deferred, and women doing work that has no name yet in the town's vocabulary.

**Radio:** The battery-powered Philco in the Pfeiffer kitchen plays WBZ Boston on clear nights. Marcus has a smaller Zenith portable in his room at the Crow house. Eira's office shortwave brings in Portland and, on good nights, New York. The war arrives as voice, not as newsprint — though the weekly *Downeast Courier* carries the names of local men in the casualty columns. Everyone reads those first.

**The POW labor program:** Authorized under the Geneva Convention and expanded aggressively by 1943 as American farm labor dried up. The central camp for Maine is at Houlton, in Aroostook County; satellite work details are dispatched to farms and canneries. Four prisoners assigned to Pfeiffer farm are escorted daily by Lonzo Reed, a sixty-year-old Coastal Patrol private with a 1903 Springfield he has not fired in two decades. The prisoners are paid eighty cents a day in scrip redeemable at the camp canteen. They are not chained. They are watched.

### Sensory Register

- October at the harbor: diesel smoke, salt brine, the smell of fish racks drying, fog that arrives before dawn and burns off by nine. The breakwater slick with kelp at low tide.
- The Pfeiffer farm in November: manure and hay, the low-frequency sound of cows before morning milking, mud frozen at the surface and soft underneath, wood-smoke from the farmhouse chimney mixing with cold air.
- Marcus's classroom in winter: chalk dust, the radiator's tick and hiss, the particular silence of teenagers watching a man who sounds different.
- Henry Crow's house, where Marcus boards: the smell of Lillian's cooking (dried beans, cornbread, stewed tomatoes from her summer put-up), the warmth of a household that does not perform welcome because the welcome is actual.

### Mood

Autumnal, self-contained, coastal-provincial. The war exists as a pressure rather than an event — felt in absences, in the cost of things, in who is and isn't at the table. The prose treats the landscape as a working environment, not a backdrop. Weather matters because it changes what is possible. The Reach matters because it is the town's reason for existing. The body on the breakwater does not shatter this world; it disturbs it, the way a stone disturbs water — the rings spreading slowly, over months.
