# Editor — Pacing Pass
## Macro arc assessment

*Read: brief.md, canon/threads.md, canon/themes.md, outline/00-chapter-map.md, logs/story-arc.md. No chapter drafts consulted.*

---

### Overall shape

The five-phase arc (Setup 1–9 / Rising 10–18 / Turn 19–24 / Climax 25–27 / Coda 28–30) is well-proportioned for a quiet literary novel. Setup and Rising each take nine chapters (~30% of the book), which is appropriate given three distinct worlds that must be fully established before pressure can meaningfully accumulate. The Turn block (six chapters, ~20%) is the tightest and most varied in POV, and the Coda (three chapters) feels correctly weighted for a novel whose resolutions are, by design, smaller than the war.

The 4th-POV exceptional chapters — Lonzo at Ch 10, Karl at Ch 19, Henry at Ch 23 — are placed intelligently. Lonzo at the boundary of Setup/Rising gives the fence thread an outside ratification before it deepens. Karl at D-Day puts the Turn's crisis inside the person most directly caught between its two sides. Henry at Ch 23 supplies the Sherbrooke disclosure-by-proxy at the moment when Marcus's decision is forming. No placement concerns here.

---

### Midbook sag — the primary structural risk

The sag threat is concentrated in Eira's chapters in the Rising Action block: **Ch 11 (Portland), Ch 14 (The Dory), Ch 17 (The Log Entry)** — three consecutive Eira appearances separated by two chapters each, all following the same emotional shape: *new information arrives → Eira's understanding deepens → she does not act*. Add the earlier iterations at **Ch 4** (Farris's news, October 11 stays out) and **Ch 7** (Hale asks, she gives nothing), and the pattern has appeared five times before the Turn phase begins. The story justifies each step as a deepening of Thread 1 — each new fact does change the weight of her silence — but on the page, readers experience the rhythm, not the justification. By Ch 17 the "learns more / says nothing" beat may feel like the novel is treading water rather than tightening. **This is the book's primary midbook pacing risk.**

The internal distinction between these chapters is critical: Ch 11 (she goes *to* Portland, initiates for the first time) and Ch 17 (the French-Canadian sailor unlocks *why* he didn't speak) are the two that most credibly advance the beat. Ch 14 (Dakin's dory) is the most exposed — new geography, first oblique log entry, but the emotional move is the smallest step in the series.

---

### Repeated beats across chapters

Beyond Eira's silence chain, Marcus's chapters carry a parallel repetition risk. His version of the beat — *approach the Sherbrooke wound / veer into something manageable* — appears across Ch 6, 9, 13, 20, 23, and 24. Six instances is a long run. Thread 6 is the deliberately unresolved thread and the swerve-away *is* the point; but each swerve must feel earned and differently textured or the reader stops believing the wound and starts cataloguing the maneuver.

One cross-chapter pair warrants particular attention: **Ch 4 and Ch 7** in the Setup phase. Both are Eira chapters in which an official figure asks questions, she answers accurately, and October 11 stays out of it. One chapter establishes this behavior; the second reinforces it. Whether both earn their separate weight depends entirely on differentiation in execution — if Ch 7's Hale feels like a higher-stakes version of Ch 4's Farris (which the outline suggests: "the moral hinge is named"), the pair works. If Hale reads as another official with slightly better questions, this is the Setup's most redundant pair.

---

### Climax / coda balance

The three climax chapters (25–27) are consecutive and each belongs to a different POV — Eira's permanent silence, Hannah's farewell at the fence, Marcus's cornbread. This staircase structure is elegant in design but carries a mechanical risk: three "decisive choice" beats in a row, back to back, with nothing between them to breathe. In a different kind of novel this would be the home-stretch acceleration. In a novel built on restraint and indirection, three consecutive resolution moments may feel like a checklist — the book paying its debts in order of invoice. The chapters' internal textures will need to feel genuinely distinct to prevent this.

The coda (Ch 28–30) is proportionate. **Ch 29 (The Good Cloth)** carries the heaviest load — four threads resolve simultaneously (Thread 3, 4, 5, and Will's tremor as the deliberate remainder) — but this density is appropriate because the Pfeiffer world has always been the most emotionally convergent of the three. **Ch 30** as a closing Eira chapter is structurally correct: Thread 1's final test belongs to her, and ending on the harbor and the open log is consonant with the novel's central image.

---

### The mystery's non-resolution

Thread 1's resolution is not revelation but *decision* — Eira choosing permanent silence rather than being prevented from speaking. This is the correct artistic choice for this novel. However, it places the entire emotional weight of the mystery arc on *character* rather than *information*. The danger is that readers tracking the breakwater question may experience Ch 25 as a deflation rather than a climax. The Turn block (Ch 21, the stranger at the dock; Ch 16, Marcus's hypothesis) is doing the work of assembling enough information that the reader understands *what* Eira is silencing — but whether that assembly is sufficient to make her non-disclosure feel like a closing rather than an evasion will depend on how clearly each chapter in the chain advances the reader's (not Eira's) knowledge.

---

2. **Ch 4 and Ch 7** — Setup pair that may be redundant in emotional beat; differentiation in execution is essential.

4. **Marcus's swerve-away from Sherbrooke** — six instances across his eight chapters; per-chapter pass should confirm each swerve is differently textured and that at minimum Ch 20 and Ch 24 feel like escalations rather than repetitions.

## 01-the-night-watch

**One minor mid-chapter lull; otherwise clean.**

**Specific issue — descriptive pause between field notes and Coast Guard arrival.**

After Eira closes her field notebook on the breakwater, three sentences sit before the Coast Guard men appear:

> *"The light was up fully now, coming in level off the water, the low October sun that didn't climb much past thirty degrees before it started back. She could see the channel clearly, the ebb current marking itself in a dark line where the deep water met the shallows. The buoy at the channel entrance clanked at long intervals, leaning into the current."*

The pause has legitimate function: it marks roughly seventy-five minutes of elapsed waiting time and echoes the buoy-clanking motif from the night-watch encounter. But it stalls the chapter's procedural momentum at precisely the moment the reader is primed for the chapter's second silence beat — Farris's four questions. The atmospheric effect is diffuse enough here that the motif echo would survive compression.

**Suggested fix (small):** Reduce to a single sentence that retains the time-marker and the buoy echo. E.g.: *"The light was fully up; the buoy at the channel entrance clanked at long intervals in the slack of the late ebb."* Removes the ebb-current and sun-angle observations, which have no thread function at this point and delay the arrival.

**Everything else is clean.** Thread 1 enters correctly (the night log entry, the Farris exchange). The chapter's dual-structure (night silence / daylight procedure) is sound and not redundant — the two silence beats are differently pressured (first is unwitnessed and personal; second is under direct official questioning). The chapter's close — the radio turned down — is precisely right and earns its echo. No soft hook, no unearned close, no checklist quality in the beats.
