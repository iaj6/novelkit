## 01-the-finding
**Summary:** Pre-dawn, Tuesday 3 November 1953. Maren winds the lamp drum at 04:00, notes the post-gale conditions in the log (SW gale overnight, backing W, now light NW, sea running, clear), then descends the iron stair at first light to perform her standard storm-check: cliff drain cleared of gorse and line, mooring ring intact, one small new rock slab noted. On her way back up she finds a green glass bottle wedged in a basalt crack two meters from the stair foot — cork-and-wax sealed, yellowish tallow-colored wax, dry interior. She carries it up inside her oilskin. At the logbook table she opens the wax seal with her pocketknife and extracts a tightly rolled, dry paper. She recognizes her own handwriting before reading a word (the two-stroke r, hard full stops, leftward-leaning capital T, slightly wide word spacing). The message contains three short predictions and a closing instruction to write and seal the message and return the bottle to the crack. The first prediction: the Morven arrives Tuesday (not Wednesday, its scheduled day) with a cracked demijohn in the forward crate. She reads it twice. The kettle boils. She makes tea, writes nothing in the log, does not transmit on the radio, rolls the message back into the bottle, seats the cork, and stores the bottle on its side in the drawer under the logbook among the spare lamp mantles. The 08:00 winding proceeds as usual; she stands at the east-facing glass a minute longer than the winding requires, looking down at the cliff where the crack is, before going back downstairs.
**New facts:**
- Bottle found Tuesday 3 November 1953, pre-dawn, in a crack in the basalt two meters from the foot of the iron stair, above the high-tide line.
- Bottle description: green glass, approximately 30 cm, cork seated in neck, yellowish tallow-colored wax seal, dry interior, no immersion damage.
- The roll of paper inside the bottle was dry.
- Maren recognizes her own handwriting immediately: two-stroke r, hard full stops, leftward-leaning capital T, slightly wide word spacing.
- The message contains three short-line predictions and one closing instruction.
- First prediction: the Morven arrives Tuesday (not its scheduled Wednesday) with a cracked demijohn in the forward crate.
- Closing instruction: write the message, seal it in the bottle with wax, return the bottle to the crack in the basalt.
- Maren does not record the bottle or the message in the keeper's log.
- Maren does not transmit on the radio.
- The bottle is stored on its side in the drawer beneath the logbook table, among three spare lamp mantles wrapped in paper sleeves.
- The cut wax from the seal was left in a small heap on the logbook table.
- 04:00 log entry reads: SW gale overnight, backing W by 03:00, now light NW. Sea running, foam on boulder shelf. Clear. Lamp steady.
- Maren stood at the east-facing glass after the 08:00 winding for longer than the task required, looking down toward the cliff.

## 02-the-interval
**Summary:** Six days after finding the bottle, Maren moves through routine — barometer readings, lamp-windings, one re-folded log entry with no error in it, one moment of sitting with her hand near the radio transmit key without transmitting. She opens the drawer to look at the bottle on Thursday evening but does not remove it. On Tuesday morning the Morven arrives one day early (Cal: "Channel was clear. No point in waiting."). The forward crate leaks — one fresh-water demijohn cracked at the shoulder, water spoiled; Maren tips it off the jetty. Cal talks about a squall north of the outer reef, a ketch being re-keeled at the mainland yard, a Board notice about paraffin pressure. Maren gives less than she receives. He accepts tea, leaves his mug at the near edge of the table, says "All right then," and takes the Morven south around the reef. Maren logs the delivery (Tuesday 10 November 1953, coal four sacks, lamp oil forty litres, tinned goods, two demijohns one cracked discarded, departed 10:48), then takes the bottle from the drawer, sets it on the table, does not unroll the message, and puts it back. First prediction confirmed. Wednesday, ~02:02: Maren wakes to wrong-interval clicking from the lamp room. The drum has lost tension — third tooth on the upper ratchet slipped. Lamp still burning, no vessels visible. She re-seats the tooth by feel and winds back to tension; checks the rotation; notes the time 02:14. She then counts the repair backward: ~four minutes to find the fault, ~six to re-seat and wind, ~two to check — twelve minutes total. The message had said twelve. She logs the event: "02:14 — drum slipped, tooth re-seated, lamp on station throughout. Duration of repair approx. 12 min." She does not open the drawer. The second prediction has arrived. The third has not.
**New facts:**
- The Morven arrived Tuesday 10 November 1953, one day ahead of its scheduled Wednesday call; Cal cited a clear weather window.
- The forward crate contained two fresh-water demijohns; one had a crack along the shoulder, water smelled of the hold, and Maren tipped it off the jetty edge.
- Cal's log visit included: coal (four sacks), lamp oil (forty litres), tinned goods, two demijohns fresh water (one cracked, discarded). Departure time 10:48. Delivery receipt signed.
- Cal mentioned: a squall north of the outer reef, a ketch being re-keeled at the mainland yard, a Board of Lights notice about paraffin pressure specification (in mail packet, unread at chapter end).
- Maren opened the drawer and handled the bottle briefly after Cal's departure but did not unroll the message.
- The drum slip occurred at approximately 02:02 Wednesday 11 November 1953; Maren identified a slipped third tooth on the upper ratchet.
- Maren's log records the drum slip at 02:14 with repair duration approximately 12 minutes — matching the message's second prediction exactly.
- Two of three predictions in the message have now been confirmed; the third (the trigger for Maren writing) has not yet arrived.
- Maren did not mention the bottle to Cal, did not record it in the log, and did not transmit on the radio during the chapter.

## 03-the-writing
**Summary:** Evening of a Thursday in late November 1953. Maren sits with the day's log already written and the bottle placed on the table from that morning. She reads the logbook backward through the drum-slip entry, the Morven delivery, and the post-gale inspection. Standing up for coal, she notices the barometer needle has moved for the first time; she watches it without sitting for fifty-four minutes across the 20:00 lamp-winding, confirming a three-tenths drop. She pulls out a sheet of Board correspondence paper and writes the full text of the message word for word: the three predictions and the closing instruction. Comparing the two sheets, she finds them identical — not similar, the same. She slides the rolled copy into the bottle, seats the cork, seals it with yellow tallow wax over the oil lamp's flame. She descends the iron stair in her oilskin, crosses the boulder shelf, and wedges the bottle shoulder-first into the same basalt crack where she found it, above the tideline. She latches the cliff gate and returns. At the logbook table she records the 20:00 winding and the falling barometer. She then takes the original message to the range and burns it until nothing remains. The chapter closes on the ordinary sounds of the cottage: wind under the door, clockwork overhead, swell below, range ticking, lamp turning.
**New facts:**
- The barometer dropped three-tenths of an inch in fifty-four minutes (19:49 to 20:43), reading 29.82 falling to 29.52 — the third and final prediction confirmed.
- The full text of the message is four lines: (1) Tuesday the tender arrives a day early; the forward crate holds two demijohns, one cracked at the shoulder. (2) Near 02:00 the clockwork drum loses tension; third tooth, upper ratchet; twelve minutes to reseat. (3) The barometer will fall three-tenths in under one hour. (4) Fold this, roll it tight, seal it in the bottle, and place it in the crack in the basalt at the foot of the iron stair, above the high-tide line.
- Maren wrote the message on unlined Board correspondence paper using her own pen, word for word, matching the original exactly.
- The sealing wax used to re-seal the bottle is yellow tallow wax from the supply tin, melted over the cottage oil lamp.
- Maren placed the bottle shoulder-first in the same basalt crack where she found it, above the high-tide line, two meters from the foot of the iron stair.
- The original message was burned in the coal range; nothing remained.
- The final log entry reads: '20:00 — drum wound, lamp on station. Barometer 29.4 and falling. Wind backing SW. Sea moderate.' No mention of the bottle, the message, or the boulder shelf.
- The causal loop is closed: Maren is now the author of the message she found. No explanation is given or implied.
