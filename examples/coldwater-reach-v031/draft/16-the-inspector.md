# The Inspector

The Samoset was twenty-five minutes late, and Eira had been watching the harbor mouth since quarter past eleven.

The channel was clear — nothing coming, nothing going, the anchorage sitting flat and bright in the July heat, the moorings undisturbed. She had noted the time at eleven and noted it again at eleven-fifteen and again at twenty past, the same reflex that made her note the barometric reading each morning before she opened the windows: not because the information was urgent but because the harbor was a system and anomalies were the system's language. A tender running twenty-five minutes behind its schedule was a minor anomaly in July. She would not mention it. She was already noting it.

The Samoset came around the bar at half past, making for the south mooring at half throttle, Sprague at the wheel. Commander Whitmore was visible at the rail before the tender made the slip — the summer khaki, the folio under one arm, the manner of a man conducting his fourth or fifth stop on a circuit that had several more to go. She had been through two of these inspections. She knew the form.

She had the desk prepared. The vessel logs open to the current year — arrival and departure, full run since October. The documentation folder for the registered working craft: the smacks, the Doris V., Willits's lobster boat at the south dock. The communication records in their district-standard binding. The safety equipment inventory with the two May addenda — south float mooring line, north face ring buoy. A fresh district compliance form in the wire tray.

The harbor office was in order. It was always in order. This was not something she had arranged for the inspection; it was the state the office maintained.

---

Whitmore came up from the pier at twenty to twelve and shook her hand at the door and sat in the chair across from the desk, and Sprague came in behind him and stood near the door with his notepad. The office was warm; the windows were open but the July air came in without moving. Whitmore was buttoned correctly in spite of it.

"Mrs. Bowman."

"Commander."

He opened the folio and set it on the desk. The inspection followed the same form it had the previous two years: documentation first, then the logs, then communication records, then equipment. He went through the vessel registrations and their port-of-record designations, the survey dates on Blaine's smack and the Doris V. He read the departure and arrival logs from January forward without comment, turning pages at the pace of a man who was reading rather than scanning. He checked the three deferred notifications in the communication records against the district's permitted window — all three within fourteen days, all initialed. He signed the compliance form.

"Equipment inventory."

She turned the folder toward him.

He went through it in order: life rings, fire equipment, the emergency line on the breakwater bollard posts, the signal locker. He initialed against each checklist item in the column. He reached the May addendum and paused.

"The north face ring buoy. What prompted the replacement?"

"Inspection showed stress cracking in the cork block beneath the canvas cover. Within the permitted interval but past what I consider serviceable."

He wrote something and moved on. He closed the folder and did the same survey of the office that he had done both previous visits — the desk, the chart cabinet, the coal stove banked low for summer, the tidal chart on the south wall, the window looking out over the anchorage. Not an appraisal of furnishings. An assessment of a working space and what working in it required.

"Office is in order," he said.

"Thank you."

He made a final notation and set the pen down. Sprague collected the compliance forms and went back out to the tender.

---

She made the coffee on the small burner beside the stove and brought two cups to the desk. Whitmore was still in the chair. He had the folio closed in front of him and his hands around the cup in the manner of a man who was not finished but had stepped outside the formal portion of his business. The July heat was in the office now without the transit of walking to cut through it.

"I have a civilian matter to mention," he said, "while I'm here."

She said nothing. She sat down.

He opened the folio and produced a folder from behind the compliance forms — not the district's standard buff but a gray-blue, the color she had come to associate with closed referrals and pending reviews. He opened it and turned the front page toward her.

She recognized the form number. The coastal incident report filed October 1943, form D-7 from the coastal incident series, her district registration at the top right, the date: 13 October. Her own document. She had filled out that form at this desk within three hours of the body coming off the breakwater, written in the careful manner she used for all official entries, and filed it with the district office by certified post the following day.

"The naval district has been conducting a cross-reference review of coastal incidents from the autumn of forty-three," Whitmore said. "The period before the Zone B rules were relaxed. Standard review — a good number of them close without additional findings." He set a smaller sheet inside the folder, turned so she could read it. "This one has a probable cross-reference."

The Seamen's Bureau form. A missing crew member report, filed November 1943. The vessel name was typed in the upper field: SS *Ångermanälven*. Swedish flag. The missing crew member: August Schell. German-born, naturalized Swedish national. Age: approximately forty. Last confirmed aboard: Portland departure, second week of October 1943. Missing somewhere in the run to Nova Scotia.

"The timeline matches your incident," Whitmore said. "Your body came in on the thirteenth, from the anchorage side, after the three-day blow. The Ångermanälven would have been in this sector on the eleventh or twelfth." He set his coffee down. "We can't confirm it without documentation — your body had no papers — but the district considers this a probable match. I'm notifying coastal officers in the zone as a matter of record."

Eira said: "August Schell."

Whitmore looked at her. "Yes. You've heard the name?"

She held his attention for a moment. He waited in the way she had noticed he always waited — not pressing, not impatient.

"I may have come across it," she said, "in the course of the ordinary mail."

This was true. The name had come to her at the post office counter in March, from Ada Gile, in the course of a conversation about mail — mail addressed to general delivery, mail that had no local addressee, mail that the Coast Guard had already collected before she got there. She had come across the name A. Schell in the course of the ordinary mail. She had not sought it beyond what any person with a professional reason might do. She had not manufactured the information. What she had done was remember it, and hold it, and add it to the black notebook in the second left-hand drawer.

Whitmore made a note. "If you have anything in the office record referencing the name, pass it to the district office." He set the pen down. "This is not an active investigation. We're calling it likely the same man and leaving it in the open files." He closed the folder. "German-born but Swedish national, no indication of anything other than a maritime fatality. No security implication."

She said: "I understand."

He finished his coffee. He stood and she stood and he shook her hand the same way he had shaken it at the door coming in — the same pressure, the same duration — and went out across the pier to the tender. She heard the Samoset's engine pick up as she walked back to her desk.

Through the south window she watched the tender clear the mooring and make for the channel. The wake spread in a double line across the flat anchorage, reached Willits's lobster boat at the south berth, and the boat rose gently once and settled. The tender crossed the bar and was gone.

---

The harbor office was quiet. The summer heat had come all the way in now, and the paper on the desk was warm to the touch. She sat down.

She took the black notebook from the second left-hand drawer.

Below the March entry — *A. Schell — general delivery — postcard — Portland postmark — early October. Contents: date and time. No other text. Coast Guard has it.* — she uncapped the pen and wrote:

*August Schell. German-born. Swedish national. Crew — SS Ångermanälven. Missing Portland to Nova Scotia, second week October 1943. Naval district: likely our body. No security concern. Case remaining open.*

She set the pen down.

She read the line she had written, then the line above it, then turned back through the previous entries. November — the form number, the body's description, the dash in the vessel-traffic column where the night of the ninth should have been. December — the Macomber departure at 0430, her own handwriting, the question she had written and then written a single word beside: *Inquiry?* February — the Portland district contract, Estelle Hartley on the dock, *had papers* underlined once. March — the postcard, the postmark, the date and time.

July.

Eight months of these. She had not gone out looking for any of it; she had logged what came to her and where it came from and kept it separate from the official record, which was the correct thing to do with information that was not evidence. This was how the notebook existed: not as an investigation but as a secondary record, the record of what a working harbormaster accumulates over the course of a winter and a spring when a question does not resolve.

August Schell had been on the Ångermanälven. He was German-born, which in 1943 meant something she did not and probably would never fully know — there were Germans on neutral vessels for reasons that were not simple, and she had no way of knowing which reason had been his. He had taken a berth on a merchant ship running the Atlantic in wartime, which was not a safe thing to do. He had received a postcard from Portland telling him a date and a time.

He had come to Coldwater Reach.

She had been on the pier the night of October ninth to check the Macomber's mooring bridle, which had been showing slack in the wrong way. Past midnight, the lamp at the shore end of the pier making its circle on the boards, the anchorage dark past the ring of light. A man at the outer edge of the lamp's reach — canvas bag at his feet, looking at the water — and she had walked past him and done what she came to do and walked back past him and come in. She had not spoken to him. She had not written him in the log.

She did not know what happened after. She had been in the harbor office with the stove banked and the departure log closed when the blow came in on the tenth. The Macomber had gone out at 0430 on the eleventh — her own handwriting in the departure column, a departure she had not authorized, a departure she had no memory of recording. The body came in from the anchorage side on the morning of the thirteenth.

She did not know what had happened between the night of the ninth and the morning of the thirteenth. She did not know what Birch Macomber's Portland contract had been for or whether it was connected to anything she had since written in the notebook. She did not know who had sent the postcard from Portland. She did not know what meeting the date and time had been for.

August Schell. He was forty years old, approximately. He had come from Germany by whatever route to a berth on a neutral ship on the North Atlantic, and he had come to this harbor on a night in October with a bag on the pier, and she had walked past him. He drowned here. She had not prevented it and she did not know whether prevention had been possible, and she did not know that it had not been.

These were the boundaries of what she knew.

She closed the notebook and put it back in the second left-hand drawer.

---

She had been meaning to get to Willits's boat since Monday. She had noticed the mooring line twice through the pier shed window — the particular motion of a line pulling at the wrong angle, the boat working in a way that a correctly moored boat did not work. Either the line was outside the fairlead or the bridle was riding the caprail instead of running through the lead ring. One of those. She picked up the inspection clipboard from its peg and went out.

The south dock was in full sun. The boards were warm through the soles of her boots. Willits's thirty-two-footer lay in the berth in the slack water, the mooring line hanging in its curve between the forward bitt and the cleat. She went forward and knelt at the cleat.

The line was outside the fairlead. A quarter inch past the lip — enough that on the flood tide, when the current ran against the bow from the north, the line would ride up against the edge instead of running clean through, which was what had been pulling the boat wrong. She freed the line, ran it through the fairlead, and seated it on the cleat. She tested it with both hands and stood.

The harbor was out in front of her in the July heat — the anchorage, the moorings, the open water beyond the bar, the granite of the breakwater running out from the north shore to the harbor's mouth. The channel was empty. The Samoset had gone over the horizon an hour ago. The heat sat on the water and the water held it.

She made a note on the inspection clipboard — *Willits mooring line: outside fairlead, corrected. Suggest owner re-examine cleat seating when vessel returns.* — and walked back up the dock and went inside.
