# Two Suits

He checked the inside breast pocket of his overcoat before buttoning it. The letter was still there — twenty-two days in the same pocket, the envelope softened slightly by handling and body heat — and he had not expected otherwise, but the checking had become a habit now, the same way the harbor smell at the ridge had become a landmark: not surprising, simply expected, and therefore noted.

He was wearing the older suit. Saturday was outside the school week's schedule, which governed only working days, and on working days he held to it precisely — the good suit on Monday, Wednesday, Friday; the older suit on Tuesday and Thursday — but on weekends the jurisdiction lapsed, and without quite reasoning about it he had settled into reaching for the older suit on Saturday mornings. It asked less of him. This was, he acknowledged, a precise distinction between two suits that were both adequate and both gray, but in the small economies of a daily routine the precise distinction was the one that mattered.

Lillian was in the kitchen. He heard the stovetop lid being set aside and replaced.

"Post office," he said, from the entry.

"Leave the door," she said. "The latch is sticky again."

He left it open a hand's width and went down the step into the March morning.

---

The elm at the corner of the yard had begun to show buds — not open yet, but the branch-ends thickening, the shift from bare to almost-bare that was still the most reliable sign of spring on this coast, preceding by several weeks any warmth one could actually use. On school mornings he left at twenty past seven and the light was not yet what it would become; on Saturday mornings the light was different, and the walk was not purposeful in the same way, which meant he noticed the elm.

The road descended toward the town for eight minutes before rising to the ridge above the north end. The harbor smell reached him there, as it always did — salt and diesel residue, and beneath those the Atlantic's own colder exhalation, which still had no precise term in any of his four working languages. He had stopped registering it as foreign somewhere in the second autumn. It was now simply the smell of the road at the ridge, which was different from the smell of the road in other places, which was information and not otherwise.

He walked down into town. A Saturday had a different character than the working week — fewer men moving quickly, two boys outside the general store, a fisherman coming out of the hardware with a coil of line under his arm. The war was in the radio and the casualty lists and the rationing and the absence of certain young men, but on a Saturday morning in March it was also, factually, not on the main street of Coldwater Reach, and a person could notice this without sentiment.

He had told Lillian he was going to the post office for stamps. This was true. He was also, he had admitted to himself on the descent from the ridge, testing a proposition: whether going out on an errand changed the state of the question in his coat pocket. He had found in his experience — which was considerable, in the matter of decisions deferred — that movement sometimes performed a kind of argument on the body that the seated mind could not complete on its own. He would buy stamps. He would return. He would know more clearly what he intended to do, or he would not, and the walk would in either case have been a walk.

The post office stood between the hardware and the pharmacist's, which was where it had stood, visibly, for as long as anyone in Coldwater Reach appeared to have noticed. He went in.

---

Ada Gile was sorting V-Mail behind the counter.

The V-Mail forms were the thin paper of the reduced-and-reprinted system — the original letter photographed to microfilm, flown across, reprinted at a fraction of the original weight, the familiar censored rectangles that turned a letter into something between a communication and a diagram of what had been removed from it. He had observed, professionally and against his better inclinations, the typography of the printed forms, the specific font, the residue of the mechanical reduction in the letters' edges. Then he had made himself stop observing, because it was not his typography and the war was not a professional object.

Ada Gile had been postmistress since 1928, which meant she had the institutional memory of the town's correspondence in the way that he had the institutional memory of certain Viennese archives: not as information one volunteered, but as the ground on which more specific knowledge stood. She had treated him since his arrival in 1942 with the same direct efficiency she appeared to apply to everyone — not warmth, not suspicion, simply the recognition that he existed, had postal needs, and was entitled to have them addressed. He had found this, over time, a more reliable form of civility than its alternatives.

He said good morning. She said good morning and slid the stamp drawer open without being asked.

He asked for twelve three-cent stamps. She counted them onto the counter and gave him his change and did not entirely stop the V-Mail sorting during the transaction, which was the practiced courtesy of a counter kept moving.

He stayed at the counter a moment longer than the purchase required. He was aware of this; he suspected that Ada Gile was also aware of it. She was aware, he thought, of most things that occurred within the post office's dimensions, and of considerable things that did not.

He said: "I wonder whether there has been any further inquiry — about the business last autumn. The man on the breakwater." He had worked through several framings for this question on the walk down and had settled on the most indirect available: a general inquiry about the town's investigation, not about Ada's specific knowledge, so that she could answer as broadly as she chose. "From the Coast Guard, for instance."

She looked up from the V-Mail without the expression of someone who had been asked something unexpected. More the expression of someone for whom no counter question arrived entirely without precedent.

"The Coast Guard came to me in November," she said. "There was a piece of mail that came through in the first week of October — addressed to general delivery, no full name. I held it the usual time and then it was collected when they came and asked about it." She returned a bundled set of V-Mail to the tray. "They seemed to know what they were looking for."

He said: "What was the name on it?"

She said: "A. Schell."

She said it in the register she used for all postal information — the same tone as a box number or a forwarding address. A fact that belonged to the record and not to the present conversation. "No one of that name in Coldwater."

He said thank you.

She said the next window was available if he needed a money order, reflexive as a weather report.

He went out.

---

The name occupied him on the walk back the way a word did when he was establishing its origin — present before it was parsed, requiring accommodation before its full weight arrived. *Schell.* German, certainly. The family name distribution was Alemannic in origin — Swiss, Swabian, Rhenish — found also in the eastern valleys, common enough in the nineteenth century, associated in the current decade with territories that had been substantially reorganized in the matter of who remained to carry such names. He brought his attention off that road and back to the practical matter.

A. Schell. General delivery. The first week of October.

Three days, perhaps four, before the body came in on the breakwater.

Someone had known to send mail to this name in this town, or had expected this name to pass through, or had sent something ahead of an arrival that did not proceed as anticipated. These three possibilities had different implications; he filed them with the precision of a man accustomed to distinguishing between interpretations of incomplete evidence.

He had thought, last November, walking home from the harbor office — the harbormaster's careful deflection still in his ear, *the Coast Guard has what I know* — about what the German phrase *ein Mensch ohne Papiere* carried that its English equivalent did not. The German phrase sat inside a legal architecture in which papers were not merely documents but the mechanism of recognized personhood: to be without papers was to be without a floor, without the state's acknowledgment that one existed within the category of persons who had standing. He had thought the word *staatenlos* on that walk, and it had arrived with the particular weight it had accumulated over the previous decade, which was not an abstract weight.

The Coast Guard had known the name before they came to claim the mail. He turned this over as he climbed back to the ridge.

The harbor lay below and to the south, flat under the March light, the lobster boats at their moorings, the line of the breakwater along the outer edge of everything. He looked at it for a moment without thinking anything particular about it, and went down toward the house.

---

Lillian had put coffee on the table. She had not mentioned it; the cup was simply there when he sat down, and she had gone somewhere else in the house, which was her operating manner — hospitality delivered as a settled fact, not as a performance.

He took the Cambridge letter from his coat pocket and placed it on the table in front of him. He unfolded it and read it again.

The Emergency Committee's letter of February 4 offered a position at a Boston-area university: visiting instructor in German philology, one-year appointment, renewable upon review, with the possibility of conversion to an assistant professorship if a permanent opening emerged. His specialty was in active demand. The German department had lost two instructors to the services in 1942 and had been covering with graduate students and overloaded faculty since. The salary was given in precise figures.

*Philology.* His field, his training, the subject of three books published before 1938 — two in German, one in English, the English volume on the semantic history of German loan words in English military vocabulary, reviewed with modest interest in London in 1936. He knew what a career in philology looked like from the inside: the departmental meetings, the library, the colleagues who shared a vocabulary sufficient for the work, the particular satisfaction of a problem whose tools were exactly calibrated to it. He had had this life. He recognized its dimensions.

Boston. A city he had passed through twice and not yet understood. Which was not Vienna and not Paris and not London, but was not Coldwater Reach, and there was a meaningful distance between those two absences.

He folded the letter along its creases and held it in both hands.

---

The word *heimisch* arrived, as it usually did, without being summoned.

He had thought it first, in relation to Coldwater Reach, in the early autumn — leaving the civil defense warden meeting, walking fifty yards of the same road home as the harbormaster, and registering, as she turned left at the fork without a further word, that the registration of the whole transaction had been interior and not shared. *Heimisch* was the condition of being absorbed into a place with enough completeness that the place no longer exposed you from the outside — that you moved through it without the constant small effort of translation, the gap between your interior and what the street expected of you. He had been *heimisch* in Vienna before 1937. He had not been *heimisch* anywhere since: not in Paris, not in London, not in the rooming house on the Pentonville Road where he had lived eleven months between the fall of France and his passage to Canada — a room with a coal smell and the particular isolation of a foreign city absorbing a foreign emergency without fully registering it as human. Not in Sherbrooke. Not in Cambridge, which was the closest approximation, the Emergency Committee's milieu, tolerant by institutional habit — but even in Cambridge he had been a category of person usefully placed, which was a different condition from being *heimisch*. *Duldung.* The same grammar, a different address.

He could not say that he was *heimisch* in Coldwater Reach. He also could not say he would be *heimisch* in Boston. He did not know whether the condition could be recovered or whether it had simply been used up in Vienna and could not accumulate again elsewhere, the way certain things were used up rather than relocated when one moved.

He thought about Henry and Lillian. The coffee on the table, unannounced. The chess on Thursday evenings, the lamp over the board, Henry moving his rook with the economical gesture of a man for whom the game had long since stopped being about gestures. The compression of Henry's speech — *than I expected, as well* — in which a great deal that was not said arrived with additional weight in the space the saying left. Henry had been in Coldwater Reach for nineteen years and had not been asked whether he found it *heimisch*; he had made himself indispensable, which was a different and possibly more durable category. Marcus had found, over two years at the Crow house table, that there were things he did not have to translate before speaking, and that this was rarer than it should have been.

He thought about Edna Locke in the empty faculty room in January. The brooch at her collar, the same small brooch every day. The three minutes she had taken, after staying when the others filed out, to say what she had come to say — what she owed a colleague who was also the category of person that made certain members of the school board uneasy, and the calculation she had run in those three minutes about whether the debt was worth paying. She had paid it. He had thanked her precisely and she had gone, and he had thought about the act with a specificity he did not usually apply to gestures from colleagues.

These were not arguments. He did not believe they needed to be treated as arguments.

---

He took a sheet of paper from the writing block Lillian kept beside the window for household accounts.

He wrote one page. He expressed gratitude for the offer, which he intended with precision — the Committee had done what it existed to do, and the position it had located was a real position in a real department, and ingratitude would be both inaccurate and imprecise. He stated that he intended to remain in Coldwater Reach for the present year. He stated that he would write again in the autumn.

He did not explain further. There was nothing that required explanation — the Committee understood his situation well enough to receive a decision without a narrative, and he did not have a narrative available in any case. He had a decision, legibly arrived at, and that was what a one-page letter to the Emergency Committee required.

He wrote *Dr. M. Klein* at the bottom. He folded the page, sealed the envelope, applied one stamp from the strip of twelve, and set the envelope beside the Cambridge letter on the table.

---

The Cambridge letter lay between the envelope and the salt cellar. Three pages, folded into thirds, the February 4 date at the top of the first page. The salary it described was still the salary. The open position was still open. The Boston department still lacked two instructors. All of it remained factually true and would go on being true and available for the autumn, when he had said he would write again.

He picked up his coffee cup.

He thought: *A. Schell.*

The name sat in him unresolved — a word not yet traced to its root, a question not yet turned over enough times to show what was on its other side. He had asked, and he had the name, and the name would remain there until some future context clarified it or did not. He had been in Coldwater Reach long enough to know that this town did not rush its answers. He had not rushed his own.

Outside the kitchen window the elm held its branches against the March sky, which was the gray of neither winter nor spring, positioned between two seasons with the flat patience of something that had learned not to anticipate. The buds were not open. They were simply there, thickening in the cold, accumulating toward something that could not yet be called a leaf.
