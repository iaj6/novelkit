# The Sixth of June

The radio in Fenn's office was always on. Marcus heard it as he poured his first coffee in the teacher's lounge — the OWI voice, which he had learned to locate on the spectrum between information and instruction, the sentences prepared, the cadence even. He poured. He heard.

*Allied forces have landed on the coast of Normandy.*

He held the coffeepot.

The clock on the lounge wall read two minutes past seven. The voice continued — the nature of the operation, the scope of the forces involved, the structure of an official statement crafted to inform without speculating. He set the pot down. He stood until the announcement had completed itself and then turned to find he was not alone: Edna Locke had come in from the history room and was standing in the doorway with a grade ledger under one arm, and she and Marcus looked at each other in the way people look at each other when a thing has happened that has not yet been taken in. Neither said anything. Then Edna turned and went back to her room.

Marcus poured the coffee. He drank it standing up.

---

The assembly was in the main hall at eight. Fenn had made the preliminary announcement through the hall speaker at seven forty-five — the phrase *Normandy coast* arriving again in the acoustics of the old mill building, bouncing off plaster and worn oak floors. By the time students took their seats the news was known. What the assembly provided was not information but structure: the formal acknowledgment that a day designated as ordinary had become something else.

Fenn stood at the front with the flag behind him and spoke in the even way he had — not a man given to rhetoric, which Marcus appreciated. Duty, courage, the progress of a long fight, the hope that this was the beginning of what would lead to the end. *The beginning of the end* was the phrase, as Marcus had known it would be. Fenn used it with care, as a physician uses a term that is technically correct but covers more than it says.

The students received this with the particular quality of held-breath attention that meant they were not thinking about abstract courage but about their own specific version of what was being said. Not the war, but a brother. Not history, but a cousin. For most of them *the end* was inseparable from a person who would either come home or not come home inside it, and Fenn knew better than to speak to the abstraction when the room was full of the particular.

Marcus stood at the side of the hall with the other faculty, his back to the plaster wall, and watched. The third row: Hannah Pfeiffer, who had been in his German class since September and who knew considerably more than she showed. She was looking at her hands. Her head was down at the angle of someone present in the room and simultaneously, precisely, somewhere else entirely. He knew the angle. He recognized it. He did not require an explanation for it.

---

German II met in the first period. Marcus had written the unit on the board before the assembly: *Konjunktiv I — Indirekte Rede*. The grammar of reported speech. He stood at the front of the room after the students filed in and looked at what he had written, at the words he had put there at quarter to eight in a different arrangement of the morning.

*The grammar of reported speech.* What someone said, at one remove from the saying.

He turned to the class.

"Indirect discourse," he said, in English. "We were going to cover this today. We will cover it today." He waited a moment for any objection, which did not come. "Please open to section twelve."

They opened. He taught. He wrote examples on the board in his careful printed hand, the hand he had trained for the blackboard because the Kurrent script of his education was illegible to students here. *Er sagt, er sei müde.* He says he is tired. *Sie berichtete, die Straße sei gesperrt.* She reported that the road was closed. The Konjunktiv I, the mood of the verb shifted from direct to reported — the small grammatical signal that says: I am conveying what was said, not what I myself assert to be true. He taught it as he always did: from the inside out, from the logical principle to the practical form.

He asked questions. The class answered with the careful effort of young people who had decided, collectively, to keep working rather than let the day be a reason not to. Hannah Pfeiffer answered the third question accurately and with a flatness that told him she was performing competence from inside a different conversation. She knew the answer. Her voice delivered it. She was not here. He noted it and moved on.

He dismissed them three minutes early and erased the board.

---

The teacher's lounge during the free period was not a place where one could be alone. Fenn had rolled the office radio in on its cart, and four teachers stood around it in the loose formation of people drawn to a sound and uncertain what to do with their hands. The news was repeating — the same announcements supplemented now with additional detail about the beaches, the airborne operations, the fighting. New words the voice was releasing for the first time this morning: *Omaha. Utah. Sword.* The code names for geographies that did not yet have their dead counted.

Marcus stood near the window for a moment.

Through the glass panel in the history-room door, he could see Edna Locke at the wall map. She kept it on the east wall — the largest map in the school, a canvas-and-varnish affair that had been there since before him, probably before Fenn. Pins for the various theaters. He watched her reach up and press a red pin into the map at the approximate position of the Norman coast, then step back to look at what she had placed. She stood there a moment, her arms at her sides, looking at the map the way one looks at something that has just changed in a direction one has been waiting for.

Marcus went to his classroom.

---

The room was empty and still in the particular way classrooms are when cleared of their use. The board was clean. He stood at the window.

The window looked north, over the back of the school grounds, toward Reach Road and the ridge above the town. The spring growth was full out now — the birch stands along the ridge, the maples in leaf, the broad-light quality of a June morning that could be, if one permitted it, almost anything.

He was thinking about the Rue du Temple.

He had arrived there in October of 1939, following a colleague who had preceded him and found two rooms available. The building was in the fourth arrondissement, on the border between the Marais proper and the streets north of it — the old quarter, the neighborhood that had been, for centuries, the working address of a particular part of the city's population. He had not sought this out; the rooms were available at a rent divisible by three when the third scholar arrived in December, and he had taken them. He had lived there eight months. He had known the street the way one learns a street by walking it every morning without particular intention: the smell of the bakery at the corner, the quality of light in the courtyard when the sun came from the east, the voices from the apartment above, the vendor at the Tuesday market who sold chestnuts from a brazier in November and who would not be there in November of 1940.

He had left in June 1940, before the Germans entered the city. He had taken his two cases and the unfinished manuscript and gone to the coast and waited for passage. It had been available. He had taken it.

He knew what happened after he left the way a person knows a thing reported to him in pieces, across different sources, over the course of two years — letters from others who had gotten out, a conversation in London, a document that circulated among the Committee scholars in Cambridge before it was suppressed, then resurfaced. He knew what the Occupation meant for the neighborhood he had lived in. He knew what the summer of 1942 meant for it. He had named this to himself once, precisely, and had then put the name down, because naming was a precision instrument and he had decided — carefully, with the deliberateness of someone making a decision that would govern him — that the instrument had a correct number of uses. He had used it once. He had not needed to use it again to carry what it named.

The two scholars he had shared the apartment with: he knew their faces. He could place them exactly — at the kitchen table, at the shared desk by the window that faced the courtyard, at the bakery on the corner buying the bread they ate standing in the doorway because neither of them had been inside long enough to develop the habit of sitting down to eat. He knew them. Their names he held the same way he held the name he had decided to use only once. He did not produce them now. He carried them.

The Allies were on the coast of France. Four years and eight months since he had left the city. Four years since the Germans had been in Paris. Today the machinery of their leaving had begun.

He was glad for this. He let himself be glad in the full weight of the word, which was not a small weight. He stood at the window in his good suit and he let the gladness be what it was. He was glad for the soldiers on the beaches. He was glad for the machine of it, the years of preparation that had produced this morning. He was glad for the people still in those streets who had survived to be present when it turned.

He was not entirely glad. Being entirely glad would require believing that what had been done could be answered by what was now being done — that the mathematics worked in that direction. He was not able to perform this. The Allies were on the coast. What had been done in the Marais in the summer of 1942 had been done. Both of these things were true at the same time, and the second did not yield to the first, and he did not require it to. He stood with both.

The school bell rang the end of the free period with its usual indifference to what the day had been.

---

Henry was at the kitchen table when Marcus came home. The radio on the counter was on, low — the afternoon update, a reporter's voice slightly different from the OWI voice, closer to the feeling of something uncontrolled. The kitchen smelled of supper beginning: potato, the small amount of fat the ration book allowed, the warmth of the stove.

Through the back window: the garden. Lillian moved among the beds with the unhurried deliberateness of someone who knows what she is doing and is doing it — she had been expanding the back-lot garden since 1942, working out from the original beds along the fence, and by this June it ran six rows deep, the cold-resistant varieties she had settled on after the first year's trial. She was not ignoring the radio. The garden wanted doing. She was doing it.

Marcus sat down across from Henry. They listened. The afternoon reports filled in the day's shape: the count of divisions, the progress inland, the name of the commanding general. Henry listened in the way he listened to most things — not because he needed the repetition but because he understood that the broadcast was for people who did. He kept his hands around his coffee cup.

"Long way to go still," he said.

"Yes," Marcus said.

They listened. After a time Henry reached over and turned the dial until the voice became texture, present but not insisting. Lillian came in from the garden with soil on her hands and washed them at the sink and stood looking out at the street.

"Did they say anything more about the fighting?" she said.

"Heavy on the beach early," Henry said. "Better inland."

She nodded. She dried her hands and moved to the stove. The supper smell strengthened. The three of them were in the kitchen at the end of the day that the day had been.

---

That night Marcus sat on the edge of the bed in his room and opened the drawer in the bedside table.

He kept the letters he had carried longest in the back of the drawer, folded and placed behind the small German dictionary he had had since Vienna and the pen-and-ink sketch he had made from memory in his first Paris winter of his parents' building on the Mariahilfer Strasse — the proportions of the door slightly wrong, the cornice not quite right, but recognizable, which had been the purpose. He did not display these things. He did not discard them.

The Emergency Committee letter was in the back, with two others. He took it out.

The Committee's letterhead, the Cambridge address, the date: October 1940. He had been in London on the Pentonville Road when it arrived — forwarded through the channels the Committee maintained for scholars whose positions were in question, which in October of 1940 included a considerable number. The letter told him there was a place for him in Cambridge, Massachusetts, that a position would be arranged for the following term, that travel documents were being prepared. It told him these things in the precise, careful language of an organization that had learned to make promises while accounting for the probability that promises could not always be kept. He had read it three times on the morning it arrived, in the Pentonville Road room with the frost on the window glass and the street below still carrying its morning traffic, and had held it in his lap until the cold made sitting still impossible.

He read it now. The handwriting of the signatory — a careful, somewhat weary professor of linguistics he had met once, later, in Cambridge, who ran the Committee with the efficiency of a man who had learned to distinguish between what could be done and what could not. The letter's verbs: *will be arranged. Is being prepared. Will be held.* The future tense. The future tense of October 1940, when the future had been a room with no visible walls.

He folded the letter on its creases and placed it back in the drawer. He closed the drawer. The elm outside the window was full-leafed and still in the June dark, and the room was dark, and he lay down and went to sleep.
