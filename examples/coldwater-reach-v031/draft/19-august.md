# August

The bus out of Coldwater Reach left at seven-fifteen, before the heat was fully in the day. By the time it reached Harrington the trees had come down to both shoulders and the particular blue-grey of early August sat on every visible hill.

Marcus had taken the window seat on the shaded side — the right side, southbound. He had made this trip once before, in late 1942, to examine a document the Bangor collection held, and he had remembered the seat. The brown paper bag from Lillian was on the seat beside him. She had set it by the door without mentioning it when he left the house at six-thirty, as she did on days when he was going somewhere long. He recognized the wax-paper fold that meant ginger snaps.

The bus was half-empty. Ahead of him, a woman with two boys who were managing their August restlessness with varying success, the smaller one grinding a wooden toy against the window frame with methodical persistence. In the seat behind Marcus, a man in farm clothes who had fallen asleep before Machias and stayed that way, his hat brim down, chin on his chest, apparently having determined that this particular morning was best experienced unconscious. At the back of the bus, a soldier in summer service dress — a private, young enough that the uniform had not yet settled into its right creases — looking out at the roadside with the focused inattention Marcus recognized as trained rather than natural.

The road from Machias to Ellsworth ran through forty miles of spruce and fir. Marcus watched the trees for a while, and then stopped watching them, and thought about what he was doing.

He had been sitting with the name *A. Schell* since March.

Ada Gile had given it to him across the post office counter — in the manner she mentioned anything, which was factually and without inflection, as one item among the items in the ordinary mail that had turned strange. A postcard addressed to A. Schell at Coldwater Reach, general delivery, Portland postmark, received the first week of October 1943. No person of that name in the town; she had looked, as a matter of course. The Coast Guard had collected it the following month, appearing to know the name already before they asked about it.

That was all she had said. He had carried the name home, placed it in the back of his thinking, and left it there for five months.

He had considered, in those months, several explanations for why he was now on a bus to Bangor.

The most straightforward: the philologist's reflex. A name without a referent was an unfinished sentence. He had spent thirty years training himself to follow a reference until it pointed somewhere, and this habit was not something he switched off at the door of professional necessity — it went with him wherever he went, like the good suit, like the notebook. He had looked for A. Schell in every record that passed through the school, the harbor, the post office in his ordinary course. He had found nothing. A good library was the next place.

The second explanation was less comfortable: he had no right to pursue this. The body on the breakwater was Eira Bowman's professional question and had been since October 1943. He was a German and Latin teacher who had been peripherally consulted in November and not consulted since. Whatever had happened at the pier the night before the body came in was not his to investigate; it never had been.

He examined this objection on the road through Harrington, found it accurate, and could not make it stop him.

The third explanation he had not yet put into sentences, and he was not going to on the bus.

He ate the cheese sandwich at the Ellsworth stop, standing outside beside the bus, because the morning was still cool enough to stand in and the cheese was sharp the way Lillian made it — extra mustard, which she had apparently determined he preferred through some observational process he had never narrated to her. The Penobscot River appeared in pieces through the trees south of Bangor, catching the morning light in the gaps. The soldier at the back of the bus stood on the roadside during the stop and smoked, and looked at nothing in particular, and when the driver called out he got back on without finishing the cigarette.

Bangor at noon.

---

He knew how to work a library. He had worked the university reading room in Vienna for nine years; the Bibliothèque nationale for eight months under increasingly difficult conditions; the British Library Reading Room through the summer of 1940 in a state he did not review in detail. The Bangor Public Library was a good stone building, well-organized, its collection adequate for most working purposes. He went directly to the periodicals desk and asked for the *Bangor Daily News* from October through November 1943, in whatever form they held them.

Microfilm, the reference librarian said. She was a precise woman of perhaps forty-five who wore her glasses on a cord and handled the request with the manner of someone who has found all requests to be reasonable at the level of category, if not always at the level of execution.

He sat at the reader and moved through the October issues at a working pace — not hurried, not slow. The *Bangor Daily News* ran its shipping notices on the third page of each edition, in a narrow column of compressed type: vessel names, ports of origin, tonnage when significant, Portland arrivals and departures listed separately from the Bangor and Rockland columns. He had verified this format before making the trip. He had verified the library's holdings. He had done this kind of work many times in many languages and was not sentimental about the mechanics of it; the mechanics were the point.

October 8th issue. Portland column:

*SS Ångermanälven, Swedish, arr. Portland Oct. 7 from Halifax, Capt. Lindqvist, manifest filed.*

He read it twice. Then he moved forward.

October 13th issue:

*SS Ångermanälven, dep. Portland Oct. 12, bound Halifax.*

He paused on that. Arrived the seventh, departed the twelfth: five days in Portland. Ada Gile had said the postcard carried a Portland postmark and had been received the first week of October. The ship was in port through the twelfth. Someone had mailed a postcard from Portland to a general delivery address in a small coastal town, and five days later a man had gone into the water during a passage from Portland to Halifax.

He took out his notebook and wrote, in Kurrent: *Å. Ankunft Portland 7. Okt. Abfahrt 12. Okt.*

Then he moved to November.

The seamen's bureau notices appeared irregularly — when there was something to report, not on a daily cycle. He moved through three weeks of November issues, past harvest-market reports and the winter weather calendar and a notice about a lost lobster boat near Vinalhaven, before he found it in the November 17th edition. Three lines at the bottom of the maritime column, below a note about Jonesport vessel registrations:

*SS Ångermanälven reports crew member missing, August Schell, German national, Swedish citizen, age approx. 40, missing during Portland to Halifax passage, October 10–12, 1943. Contact Seamen's Bureau, Portland.*

He sat with this.

The name was complete now. Not *A. Schell*, with its administrative flatness, its reduction of a person to an initial and a surname — but *August Schell*, with the full first name that the seamen's bureau form required, which gave the man a different weight. August. A German first name. A month. Both, in 1943, carrying their own freight.

He took the notebook out again and wrote: *August Schell. Deutsch. Schwed. Staatsbürger. Geb. ca. 1903. Ångermanälven. Okt. '43.* Not a summary; a fixing of facts in the form he used for things that needed to hold their shape. He looked at the page for a moment. Then he put the notebook in his coat pocket, stood up from the reader, and returned the microfilm to the reference librarian.

She thanked him for coming in on such a warm day. He thanked her for the access. Both statements were courteous and accurate.

---

He had requested, two weeks earlier by letter, a copy of a journal article he had been meaning to read since January — a piece on medieval dialect transmission in the Passau corridor, from a 1943 volume of a philological journal the Bangor collection carried. It was waiting in a paper envelope at the reading room desk.

He sat at the table by the window, which had the right afternoon angle, and read it for an hour.

The argument was careful and, in one place where it addressed a competing claim Marcus had never found convincing, quietly devastating. The author had assembled fifteen years of material on high-vowel persistence in the sub-Alpine dialects and the assembly showed — in the way that scholarly work shows its quality not in the conclusion reached but in the intelligence of the path taken to it. Marcus made three marginal notes in Kurrent pencil on a notecard he had brought for the purpose. He knew he could not keep the journal. He read the article once through and then a second time for the structure of the argument, and on the second reading confirmed the first.

Then he returned the envelope to the desk, said goodbye to the librarian — who was occupied with a boy's schoolbook question and acknowledged him with a nod — and went outside and ate the apples and the ginger snaps on a bench in the August sun.

He had known the article would be exactly as good as the abstract had suggested. He had been correct.

---

He caught the three o'clock bus south.

For twenty minutes below Bangor the Penobscot River was at his window, widening where the road ran along the bank, the afternoon light on the water going from pewter in the direct sun to something darker, almost bronze, where the tree-shadows fell from the far shore. A heron stood very still at the muddy margin. The light was the kind of light that made you believe a country was beautiful, which it did, alongside everything else that it was. Marcus watched it until the road curved inland and the spruce came back.

He thought about August Schell.

*German national, Swedish citizen.* Born, by the arithmetic of *age approximately 40*, somewhere around 1903. Which meant thirty-five when the Anschluss happened — when the annexation folded Austria into the Reich and made the taxonomy of what the state owned and what it would do a single category regardless of where you were born within its new perimeter. Thirty-seven when France fell. Thirty-nine when Marcus arrived in Coldwater Reach in March 1942.

A man who had — at some point in the decade between 1930 and 1940 — left Germany, or acquired Swedish citizenship, or both in some sequence, and was sailing neutral merchant vessels through Atlantic waters in wartime under a Swedish flag. This was a specific biography. Neutral ships were not safe. They ran the same water as military tonnage and took torpedoes from submarines that couldn't always read flags in the North Atlantic winter. Sailing that water on a German name with Swedish papers required a particular calculation — the calculation you made when the alternative was to become legible to a state that had stopped being interested in your continued existence as a free person.

Marcus recognized the arithmetic of that calculation. Not from having made it himself in that form — his route had run overland, through documents of a different kind, and he had arrived here by a different sequence of rooms. But the shape of the calculation was familiar. He knew what it looked like from a particular kind of inside.

He was not a spy.

Of this Marcus was nearly certain, with the certainty that belongs to a man who has considered a question carefully over five months, which is not the same as the certainty of evidence, and he did not confuse the two. He was a philologist; he was trained to build readings from incomplete evidence and to hold them open to revision. He built this one and held it open.

What the evidence said: a German-born man with Swedish citizenship sailed a neutral merchant vessel into Portland in the first week of October 1943. A postcard went out from Portland to a small Maine harbor town's general delivery — a date, a time — addressed to the name he sailed under. He came to the town. He died on the night of October ninth, or the night of October tenth, or sometime in the three days afterward, and three days later he was on the breakwater.

What Marcus had brought to the evidence: the recognition of a certain kind of displacement. A man who had made himself difficult to find on paper while continuing to move through the world — who had become, in the technical German sense, *staatenlos* in everything but the letter of the documents, because the state that might have claimed him was a state he had chosen not to belong to. This was not the profile of an agent. This was the profile of a man trying to remain alive and useful in a world where those conditions had become complicated.

He came for the meeting. He drowned in a storm. Nobody knew what had happened at the pier.

Marcus held the reading, and held the uncertainty inside it, and watched the last of the river light disappear behind the trees.

The Harrington marshes came in on the left. The smell of the coast arrived through the bus window before the road turned back toward it — salt, low-tide mud, the particular cool that the sea put into the air even in August.

He thought, without urgency, about whether to say anything to anyone. To Henry. To Eira Bowman, who was after all the person whose professional work made the question hers by right. To Commander Whitmore at the naval district, theoretically, although that would require an explanation of how a high school teacher came to be working the periodicals room in Bangor on a Wednesday in August, and the explanation would change the question from what had happened to what Marcus was doing asking.

He thought about it in the manner of a man who has already answered a question and is reviewing the answer.

He was not going to say anything.

The most honest account he could give of why: August Schell had made himself difficult to file, by intention and at some cost, and he had died in that condition. He had been, in the hours at the pier, the man with the canvas bag and the Portland postmark and the Swedish papers who had come to a meeting in a small town where no one knew his name — which was, in a specific and non-trivial sense, the condition of many men Marcus had known, before the war made the condition catastrophic rather than merely difficult. The official determination of such a man's identity and circumstances was never adequate to the man. Marcus had sat in enough rooms where such determinations were made to know this from a direction that was not theoretical.

He was not going to add August Schell to a file.

Whether this was wisdom or something that resembled wisdom without quite being it, he could not entirely say. He knew the note in his coat pocket, written in a script that no one in Coldwater Reach could read without being trained to read it. He was not going to transcribe it into anything else.

---

The bus came into Coldwater Reach at seven minutes past seven.

The harbor smell was there before the road turned toward the ridge — diesel and salt and the low-tide accumulation at the north end of the inner anchorage that was particular to August. Marcus stepped down with his coat over his arm and the empty paper bag folded into his pocket and walked the four blocks to the Crow house.

The kitchen light was on. Through the window he could see the table set for three, and Lillian at the stove, and beyond her the elm in the yard still holding the last of the evening light in its upper branches in the way it did this time of year — the leaves barely moving, the light going out of them slowly rather than all at once, the way light did in August when it meant to stay.

He stood on the sidewalk for a moment.

Then he went up the porch steps and in through the door.

Lillian said supper was in ten minutes.

Henry looked up from the newspaper and said nothing, which was his way of acknowledging that Marcus was back.

Marcus put his coat on the hook by the door. He could feel the notebook in the inside pocket — the slight weight of it, the hard cover against the cloth. He went to the kitchen sink and washed his hands and stayed at the water a moment longer than he needed to, not thinking about anything in particular, watching the water run. Then he dried his hands on the kitchen towel and turned to find his chair.

The elm outside the window had given up its light. The kitchen smelled of the corned beef hash Lillian made when she had the red points for it. Henry turned a page of the newspaper. Outside, somewhere, a car passed on Reach Road, and then the street was quiet, and the evening settled into the ordinary shape of an evening at the Crow house.
