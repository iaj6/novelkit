## 01-the-breakwater.md

**Structural voice problem: pier flashback rendered as a scene.**

The section beginning "It had been just after midnight when she went out." (fourth scene break, approximately 2/3 through the chapter) is doing essential plot work — the reader must know Eira saw the man on the pier. But `style.md` is unambiguous on Eira: *"Her past surfaces in objects, habits, and reflexes — not in scenes."* The flashback tense opens in past perfect ("It had been just after midnight") but drops into simple past almost immediately ("He was at the shore end of the pier. Standing at the edge of the overhead lamp's reach…"), and runs five paragraphs as an uninterrupted scene. That's not how Eira's memory works in the style guide.

The information is load-bearing; the scene-rendering is what's wrong. One structural option: Eira re-encounters the memory in fragments triggered by present objects — the lamp post visible through the south window, the canvas of the litter, the shape of the dash she just wrote — rather than as a continuous scene. This would keep the revelations (man seen, canvas bag, facing the water, then gone) while routing them through Eira's observational reflex rather than through retrospective narration.

**Writer judgment required.** No word-level fix is possible without restructuring the section.

*Offending lines (for reference):*
> "He was at the shore end of the pier. Standing at the edge of the overhead lamp's reach — in the light but only barely, which gave her the shape of a man and no more. Canvas bag on the ground beside his feet. He was looking at the water."
> "The blow came in at 0130, earlier than she had expected, and by 0200 there was no morning in any ordinary sense — only the harbor in storm…"

The second line also carries a slightly abstract/literary register ("no morning in any ordinary sense") that is inside the flashback problem; it would resolve if the section is restructured.

## 08-vater.md

**Issue: The *Heimweh* paragraph imports Marcus's analytical register into Hannah's POV.**

The paragraph beginning *"She thought about the word Heimweh"* performs etymological and morphological analysis of a German compound word — distinguishing *Weh* from English "sickness," unpacking the morpheme as "ache, pain," and drawing a formal conclusion about its structure. This is precisely the mode `canon/style.md` assigns to Marcus Klein: *"notices words — their etymology, their range, the gap between what someone says and what they mean."* The style guide also specifies that Hannah *"is not an intellectual"* and that German phrases appear in her narration as absorbed feeling, not as analysis.

**Offending lines (near the end of the fourth section):**

> *"The English said homesickness, and sickness was a thing you had for a time and then recovered from, a condition with an end. But* Weh *was not sickness.* Weh *was ache, pain, the thing that went on."*

> *"...and the ache did not end because the home it was aimed at was no longer accessible and maybe had never been quite real even when you lived in it, the way you sometimes don't fully see a place until you have left it."*

The first block is the morpheme analysis (Marcus's territory). The second carries adult retrospective wisdom ("you sometimes don't fully see a place until you have left it") that the style guide explicitly forbids in Hannah's chapters: *"she does not yet know how this ends."*

**Why it wasn't fixed in-pass:** Removing these sentences leaves the paragraph incoherent — they are load-bearing for what follows. The underlying concept (*Heimweh* as Albrecht's state) is essential to the chapter's argument. What's needed is a rewrite of this paragraph in Hannah's register: she *feels* the difference between the two words without breaking it down as a linguist would. Suggest the writer consider: Hannah might simply know that *Heimweh* is not curable the way sickness is, because she has watched her father for seventeen years and seen that it isn't — stated as observation, not etymology.

**Fix applied in this pass:** Separately, a minor clause was removed from paragraph 2: *"familiar in form and unintelligible in fact"* (a formal antithesis construction off-register for Hannah). The surrounding analogy carries the meaning without it.

## 13-ada-giles-window.md

**Extended interior reasoning block — structural, needs writer's judgment.**

The section from "A postcard, sent from Portland — the postmark was Portland, which she had not known before this morning..." through "She understood it precisely and she did not expect to resolve it today." runs approximately 350 words of unbroken interior reasoning. Each individual element is framed as professional assessment (coordinates, postmarks, dates, harbor-log conventions), not emotional reflection, so no single sentence breaks the register. The cumulative length is the problem: style.md specifies that Eira avoids "extended interior monologue," and this block is the longest sustained interiority in the chapter by a significant margin.

The logic of the passage is sound and the information essential. The question is whether it can be punctuated by a physical action — Eira moving to the window, turning the receipt over in her hand, going to the stove — that interrupts the chain without breaking it, giving the reader a body in the room before the reasoning resumes. This cannot be done with a word or clause change; it requires structural insertion.

## 16-the-inspector.md

**Five word/clause fixes applied** (see diff below). One structural issue flagged for writer's judgment.

---

### Fixes applied

1. **"the war as a scheme…the war as a weather"** — off-register metaphor. Eira's narration explicitly avoids comparing things; "the war as a weather, the thing people fled or survived or moved through at whatever angle they could find" reads as Marcus's kind of construction. Revised to: *"there were Germans on neutral vessels for reasons that were not simple, and she had no way of knowing which reason had been his."*

2. **"and sit with the shape it made"** — figurative/editorial phrase appended to the list of Eira's concrete actions (remember, hold, add to notebook). Cut. The notation is the action; the sitting-with is interior elaboration the register doesn't permit.

3. **"from wherever Germany had been for him"** — philosophical abstraction that belongs to Marcus, not Eira. Revised to: *"from Germany by whatever route"* — acknowledges the gap in her knowledge without existentialising it.

4. **"the practiced patience of a man who had learned that the pause before an accurate answer was worth the pause"** — attributes an interior history of learning to Whitmore; style.md bars access to another character's inner experience. Trimmed to: *"not pressing, not impatient."* The behavioral fact is already fully conveyed by those two phrases.

5. **"and which people did because they needed to be somewhere they were not"** — editorial generalisation about human motivation, off-register for Eira's procedural narration. Cut. The antecedent observation ("not a safe thing to do") is sufficient.

---

### Unfixable structural flag — extended interior monologue

**Location**: The passage from *"August Schell had been on the Ångermanälven…"* through *"These were the boundaries of what she knew."* (~400 words)

**Issue**: style.md specifies that Eira's interior monologue is *rare and compressed*. This passage is neither. It is extended continuous interior reflection — inventory-style, yes, and serving genuine plot function, but it runs to full-page length in a voice that normally renders interiority in a sentence or two before returning to the physical world.

The passage also contains a brief flashback rendered as a scene (the man on the pier at midnight), which style.md flags as off-register for Eira: *"her past surfaces in objects, habits, and reflexes — not in scenes."* The scene is short and compressed enough that sentence-level revision cannot fix it without restructuring the paragraph.

**Suggestion for writer**: Consider moving some of the enumerated unknowns (*"She did not know what Birch Macomber's Portland contract had been for…"* etc.) into the notebook itself — a second entry in Eira's own notation voice — rather than as continuous interior prose. This would keep the information on the page while staying in Eira's register (she thinks through her notebook, not around it). The pier flashback could survive as two or three flat declarative sentences stripped of the lamp-circle atmospheric detail.

This is a judgment call; the passage does necessary work and some readers will find the length warranted at a plot-pivot chapter. Flagging for the writer rather than rewriting.

**Writer's attention — weather personification cluster**: The closing section of this chapter carries three instances of anthropomorphized weather in relatively close succession:
1. *(fixed above)* "a sky that had come down to think about what it was going to do"
2. "the weekend's snow organizing itself in the dark above the town" (kitchen scene)

Whether to thin the cluster — and which instance to cut or rephrase — requires the writer's judgment about the emotional arc of the ending.

## 28-regensburg.md

**POV character**: Karl Reinmar (secondary character given an exceptional POV chapter, near end of book).

**Fixes applied directly**:
- Removed "He thought he understood it better now." (end of cathedral-spire paragraph) — editorializes Karl's own insight, violating the style guide's prohibition on explaining emotional significance.
- Removed ": by the shape of the absence" (Danube paragraph) — abstract, literary appositive drifting toward Marcus's register; sentence reads cleanly without it.

---

**Unfixable locally — writer's judgment required**:

**1. The *bleiben* analysis paragraph** (begins "The German word *bleiben* meant to stay, to remain…")

> *Er ist geblieben*: he remained, he stayed — which might mean he had decided to stay, or might mean only that he had not yet gone.

This is Marcus Klein's specific cognitive mode, as documented in both `canon/style.md` and `canon/characters.md`: taking a German word and parsing its exact grammatical range against English equivalents. Karl is a butcher's apprentice from Regensburg; he would feel the pull between staying and leaving, but he would not narrate it as a grammatical annotation on the verb *bleiben*. The thought is right for this chapter; the form is wrong for this character. A fix here requires reconceiving what the paragraph is doing, not adjusting a word or clause.

**2. The *Freundschaft*/*Freundlichkeit* taxonomy paragraph** (begins "There was *Freundschaft*, friendship, but that was a category…")

> There was *Freundschaft*, friendship, but that was a category. There was *Freundlichkeit*, kindness, but that was also a category, and also not exactly right.

Again Marcus's mode: taxonomizing a felt experience by testing German words against each other and finding both insufficient. The final line — "He did not have a word for that" — is right for Karl. The analytical scaffolding leading to it is not. The fix is not excising the German words (they are appropriate) but reconceiving how Karl arrives at "He did not have a word for that" from inside Karl's register rather than Marcus's.

**3. The harbor/edge-of-world passage** (begins "He had not understood that the world could continue past the thing he had thought was its edge…")

> that you could stand at what seemed like the far boundary of things and discover the boundary was only a hill, and that beyond it the world was very much larger than the part he had been given to know.

The extended metaphor ("far boundary of things," "the part he had been given to know") is philosophically framed and uses a passive construction ("been given to know") that feels above Karl's register. The sensory details immediately after — "The cold salt smell. The sound of it in the dark." — are perfectly pitched. The preceding abstract elaboration undermines them. A pruning of the extended metaphor clause would help, but the degree of revision required exceeds a single word or clause change.
