
## 01-the-breakwater
**Summary:** Dawn, Tuesday October 13, 1943. Eira Bowman walks the breakwater to where Roy Blaine and Eddie Dunst have found an unidentified male body wedged against the iron ladder at the far end. She examines the body professionally — oilskin, dark wool sweater, rubber boots, no documents, no insignia, working hands — and determines he came in from the anchorage side on eastern chop, not fallen from the structure. Coast Guard Petty Officer L. Gervais arrives from Machias by surfboat and takes charge with the efficiency of someone who considers Eira's presence provisional. She gives him her physical observations; he thanks her and departs with the body. Back in the harbor office she writes the incident log entry, then turns to October 9's vessel-traffic column and makes a dash — her log's notation for no anomalies. The dash is not accurate: that night, after midnight, she was checking the Macomber's mooring bridle and saw a man at the shore end of the pier — canvas bag at his feet, looking at the water — but before she could cross to him the bridle demanded attention, and when she looked back the man was gone. She assumed he'd sheltered in the pier shed; the blow came in at 0130 and the assumption was never tested. She could not enter him then and cannot enter him now. She makes coffee, watches the surfboat leave, receives Gervais's brief return with the First District notification, then rinses her cup and hangs it on its hook beside Owen's, puts on Owen's oilskin, and goes out to the morning inspection. The incident report sits on the desk, signed, the dash in place. Thread 1 (The Blank in the Log) enters here.
**New facts:**
- The unidentified body washed up Tuesday, October 13, 1943, three days after the storm began October 10.
- The body lay facedown in the angle between the seaward face of the breakwater and the iron ladder at the far end — left arm outflung, right arm tucked under, feet pointing south.
- The man was wearing oilskin over a dark wool sweater (brown or black), knee-length rubber boots standard pattern, no vessel insignia, no identifying documents. His left hand was weather-roughened, nails close-cut, one cracked at the quick.
- Body position was inconsistent with a fall from the breakwater and consistent with arrival from the anchorage side via eastern chop (tide was higher during the night).
- Roy Blaine (lobsterman) and his nephew Eddie Dunst found the body at approximately 0530 when coming out to check Roy's moorings.
- Coast Guard Petty Officer L. Gervais, Machias station, attended the scene and took custody of the body at 0631.
- The harbor office is a converted pilot house, 12 feet by 14 feet, at the near end of the pier shed. Chart cabinet to the left of Eira's desk. VHF set on the shelf above the south window. Owen's barometer on the east wall at the height where he hung it.
- Eira installed two hooks (copper pipe from a derelict smack's bilge) on the east wall below Owen's barometer the month after he died. Left hook: her coffee cup. Right hook: Owen's cup. She has washed both and replaced them daily for eight years.
- Eira makes coffee in a tin percolator using four measures of grounds.
- The coaster Macomber (Birch Macomber, master) was in Coldwater Reach harbor from at least October 3 through the storm and into October 13.
- The Lighthouse Service buoy tender Samoset called at Coldwater Reach around October 4, 1943.
- Eira went out to check the Macomber's mooring bridle after midnight on October 10 (logged 0015). The bridle had a chafe point at the thimble noted two days prior.
- Eira saw the man (later the body) at the shore end of the pier between approximately 0015 and 0030 on October 10. He stood at the edge of the overhead lamp's reach, canvas bag at his feet, looking at the water. She could not see his face — only his shape.
- The blow came in at approximately 0130 on October 10, earlier than Eira expected.
- The vessel-traffic column for October 9 evening in Eira's incident log is marked with a dash — her convention for 'no anomalies noted.' This is inaccurate to what she observed (the man on the pier) but accurate to what she had previously written (nothing).
- Eira wears Owen's oilskin coat for serious weather work. It hangs on a wall peg by the harbor office door.
**Loose threads:**
- Who is the man whose body came in? No papers, no name, no one who will claim him. (Thread 1 spine question — unresolved, deliberate.)
- What exactly did Eira see on October 9 — specifically, what was the man doing at the pier before midnight? The fog, the distance, the bridle: she has only partial information.
- The dash in the vessel-traffic log is the fulcrum of Eira's arc. Later chapters must treat it consistently — she did not write a lie, she left a blank, then marked it with a notation that implies nothing unusual.
- Gervais said 'First District will be in contact within the week.' This contact has not yet occurred and will drive ch04.
- The right pocket of the body's oilskin held waterlogged cloth that could not be retrieved. Whether this is ever recovered or identified is unresolved.
- Roy Blaine's smack has an uneven idle that Eira has been meaning to mention to him for three weeks — minor running detail, establishes the texture of her daily professional life.

## 02-the-icehouse
**Summary:** Mid-afternoon, approximately October 14–16, 1943. Hannah Pfeiffer is mid-milking when an Army truck arrives in the farm lane. From the gap in the east barn wall she has known since childhood, she watches four German POWs step down from the truck bed — three older men and one young one, all in identical ill-fitting Army denim. She catalogs them: the gray-bearded man, the very large man, the one with the bad knee who steps carefully, and the young one who looks at the icehouse with the calculating expression of someone trying to understand a place before entering it. Lonzo Reed descends from the passenger cab, works his bad knee, retrieves a folding camp chair from the truck bed, and sits beside the icehouse door with his rifle across his knees. Albrecht Pfeiffer stands in the farmhouse doorway, signs the soldier's clipboard without speaking, and goes back inside. The four POWs are walked into the icehouse; the soldiers depart; Lonzo sits. Hannah finishes the milking and carries the pails to the cooler — Lonzo glances up when she crosses the yard and looks back at the icehouse. At dinner Albrecht speaks German only: the work they'll do is good; the north pasture fence needs repair; they'll start tomorrow morning. Magda moves stove to table without words. Hannah thinks unprompted about the distance between Straubing and Regensburg. In Rolf's room she stands at the east window and looks at the dark icehouse, thinks the word *fremd*, and falls asleep. Thread 4 (The Fence) enters; Thread 5 (The Pfeiffer Silence) enters.
**New facts:**
- The Army truck carrying the POWs had two soldiers in the cab and one standing in the truck bed with a rifle at sling arms. The soldier in the bed was young.
- The four POWs wore Army-issue denim work clothes — pale blue-gray, identical cut, none fitting any of them quite correctly.
- Hannah has known about a gap in the east barn wall between the fourth and fifth boards since she was nine years old. It sits at waist height for her now. It gives a clear view of the farm lane and the icehouse front.
- Three of the four POWs are older men: one gray-bearded and heavyset; one very large; one who steps carefully and favors his right leg, consistent with knee trouble.
- Karl (the young POW) stepped down from the truck last and looked at the icehouse with a calculating expression — not frightened, not angry, something without a name. Before entering he turned and looked at the farmhouse.
- Lonzo Reed carried his rifle by the action when stepping down from the cab, working around the door frame carefully. He used the door frame for support with his left hand. After dismounting he stood still briefly, letting his knee settle.
- Lonzo Reed's folding camp chair was retrieved from the truck bed by one of the soldiers and placed beside the icehouse door. Lonzo sat in it with the rifle across his knees for the afternoon.
- Albrecht Pfeiffer stood in the farmhouse doorway during the POWs' arrival, signed the soldier's clipboard without speaking, and went back inside. He addressed no one — neither the soldiers nor the prisoners.
- At dinner the night of the POWs' arrival, Albrecht said in German: the work they are to do is good; the fence on the north pasture needs repair; they will start with that tomorrow morning.
- Magda Pfeiffer moved from stove to table at dinner without contributing a word. She set down and picked up her spoon at one point — the only visible reaction to the day's events.
- Hannah's room is Rolf's former room, now hers. She moved in when her parents suggested it because it is larger and has the east window that catches first light. She moved none of Rolf's things. Rolf's coat hangs on the hook by the door.
- Hannah has a geography book with a map of Bavaria that she consulted years ago; she recalled that Straubing and Regensburg are close together and both on or near the same river, but could not remember the exact distance.
- Hannah goes to sleep the night of the POWs' arrival thinking the German word *fremd* — foreign, strange, not from here — without applying it to any specific person.
- The icehouse was dark and showed no light at the time Hannah looked at it from her window before sleeping; Lonzo Reed's camp chair was gone from its position beside the icehouse door by that time.
**Loose threads:**
- Who is the young POW — his name and origin are not yet known to Hannah (reader knows he is Karl Reinmar, 19, from Regensburg). Thread 4 opens.
- What does Albrecht know or feel about the proximity of Regensburg to Straubing? He signed the papers and made the assignment statement at dinner without visible emotion — but Hannah noticed the geographic fact on her own. The chapter leaves his inner reaction sealed.
- How does the POW work detail schedule operate — who comes to collect them, what are the daily mechanics? Not yet established from Hannah's POV.
- Lonzo Reed's chair was gone by nightfall — how the shift change works, whether there is a night watch, is unresolved from Hannah's angle.
- The gap in the east barn wall is Hannah's primary observation post. Its existence is a practical fact of the story; it can be used in later scenes.

## 03-monday-wednesday-friday
**Summary:** Late October – early November 1943, Marcus Klein POV. The chapter establishes Marcus's routine in Coldwater Reach: the daily walk from the Crow house to Coldwater Reach High School (good suit Monday/Wednesday/Friday; older suit Tuesday/Thursday), the German class of six students (including Hannah Pfeiffer, who knows more than she reveals), the teacher's lounge at noon where Principal Aldous Fenn mentions the unidentified body on the breakwater and Thibodeau and Calloway briefly direct their gaze at Marcus before looking back at the table — a reflex Marcus has been calculating since 1936. Evenings at the Crow house: Lillian's cooking, Henry's compressed wisdom about the investigation ('They will or they won't — doesn't change anything for us'). Marcus thinks about Duldung — being-permitted-to-remain — and the distinction between being tolerated by a town and being at the table in this kitchen. The civil defense warden meeting in the school basement, first week of November: Marcus as building representative, Haverstock running the meeting, Marcus and Eira Bowman across the table from each other. Walking out: he holds the door; she says 'You're the German teacher'; he says 'Austrian, in fact'; she says 'Same road home'; they walk fifty yards and she forks left without farewell. Back in his room he opens the grade book with its Kurrent annotations, reads his own margin note about Hannah Pfeiffer ('Sie weiß mehr, als sie zeigt'), and thinks about the word unheimlich — the sensation of being exposed in a place because you have not grown native enough to disappear into it. He has not been heimisch anywhere since 1937. Thread 2 (The Man Still Moving) enters. Thread 3 (The Hearing) enters.
**New facts:**
- Marcus leaves the Crow house at twenty past seven on school mornings and walks twenty minutes to school.
- The harbor smell reaches the road at a ridge point approximately eight minutes from the Crow house, before the road descends to the north end of town.
- The good suit is medium gray worsted, purchased at Cheapside, London, autumn 1938. Marcus wears it Monday, Wednesday, and Friday; the older suit goes on Tuesday and Thursday. The elbows are showing wear as of autumn 1943.
- Marcus has been inside the Congregational church twice in nineteen months, both times for community gatherings; he otherwise knows it only by its bell.
- The German class meets Monday, Wednesday, and Friday; six students enrolled; Marcus has taught it since arriving in March 1942.
- The six students in the German class are: Curtis Aiken (treats grammar as a solvable puzzle), two girls in the front row (diligent note-takers), one boy who attends irregularly and translates phonetically, one girl in the back row whose name Marcus mispronounced twice and now pronounces correctly, and Hannah Pfeiffer.
- Marcus's grade book annotations are in Kurrent: GU for gut, S. for Schwäche. Beside Hannah Pfeiffer's name in the margin of her last examination: 'Sie weiß mehr, als sie zeigt.'
- On a Friday in the third week of October Marcus wrote the first line of Rilke's first Duino Elegy on the board without intending to ('Wer, wenn ich schriee, hörte mich denn aus der Engel Ordnungen?'), then erased it and gave the class the assigned Goethe. Hannah Pfeiffer looked at the board longer than the others.
- Aldous Fenn, principal of Coldwater Reach High School, is fifty-five years old and has held the post for twenty-two years.
- Thibodeau is the mathematics teacher at Coldwater Reach High School. Calloway (Miss) is the home economics teacher, with twenty-three years at the school — one year more than Fenn.
- Marcus has been calculating the half-second reflex (others looking at him when a German-seeming subject arises, then looking away) since at least 1936. He registers it with accuracy rather than feeling.
- Henry Crow's statement on the body investigation: 'They will or they won't. Doesn't change anything for us.' Marcus hears this as precision, not resignation.
- Marcus thinks the word Duldung (toleration: being-permitted-to-remain; passive grammar) to describe his position in most of Coldwater Reach, and distinguishes it from being at the Crow household table, where he is not any particular category.
- The civil defense warden meeting is held in the school basement (the old mill's boiler pit, made habitable by Henry Crow over nineteen years). Low ceiling, old stone walls, voice-deadening acoustics.
- Haverstock is the civil defense warden of Coldwater Reach; former hardware store owner before the county emergency acquisition program. He calls meetings to order at half-past seven.
- Gould is the fire chief of Coldwater Reach. Attends civil defense warden meetings with the patience of someone for whom the agenda holds no surprises.
- Marcus holds the Tuesday fire watch rotation in the civil defense scheme.
- Marcus and Eira Bowman meet for the first time at the civil defense warden meeting, first week of November 1943. Across the table from each other.
- Eira's words to Marcus leaving the meeting: 'You're the German teacher.' His reply: 'Austrian, in fact.' Her reply: 'Same road home.' They walk fifty yards and she turns left without further word.
- Marcus has not been heimisch (at home in a way requiring no effort or calculation) anywhere since 1937.
- Marcus defines unheimlich as the sensation of being exposed in a place because one has not grown familiar enough to disappear into it — the reversal of heimlich's double meaning (familiar/secret).
**Loose threads:**
- The Rilke line on the board — Hannah looked longer than the others; she can likely read it. This exchange is not spoken between them and its significance is deferred.
- Thread 3 (The Hearing) enters: the teacher's lounge reflex is the opening pressure. No direct threat yet; no named action from the school board. The chapter establishes that Thibodeau and Calloway are among those who perform the reflex.
- Thread 2 (The Man Still Moving) enters: Marcus recognizes he is still in transit even though movement has stopped. Unheimlich is the chapter's closing word. The question of whether he can stop arriving is opened but not answered.
- Eira and Marcus have met — fifty yards together, then she forks left. No significant exchange yet. The thread of their relationship is begun but minimal.
- The body on the breakwater has touched Marcus's world (the teacher's lounge) but not yet in a way that activates his expertise. That comes later (ch06).

## 04-the-coast-guard
**Summary:** Early November 1943, Eira POV. A First District Coast Guard motor surfboat ties up at the commercial float and Lieutenant Pruitt — mid-twenties, deliberate manner, thin folder under his arm — comes to the harbor office. His file includes her incident report, Gervais's field notes, and a pathologist's preliminary she reads upside-down from across the desk: adult male, drowning, approximate age 38–48, no identifying documents, no identifying marks. He asks about vessel traffic on the night of October 9: she gives him the log — lobster smacks Thistle and Doris V. at mooring, coaster Macomber (Birch Macomber, Machias), ice barge Ploughman at anchor. Did she observe any vessel she did not log? No. Did she observe anything unusual on the pier or dock? She says she was out at approximately 0130 checking the Macomber's mooring bridle — first gusts running, concerned about the set on the bollard — nothing unusual. She does not say: there was a man at the end of the pier with a canvas bag. Pruitt delivers the official position — drowning, no foul play, open but not actively worked — and leaves. Eira watches the surfboat until the fog takes it. She turns to October 11 in the log: Macomber — departure 0430 — her own handwriting, which she cannot reconstruct. She accounts for the possibility (no overnight watchman; fishermen sometimes leave notes; two days of blow, portions unrecoverable). She closes the log and goes to the bolted drawer in the chart cabinet, checks the Macomber documentation: registered Machias, 1937. She knows Birch Macomber — late fifties, spare, careful with money, reliable. A coaster does not put out on the second day of a nor'easter without reason. She locks the drawer and goes back to work. Thread 1 (The Blank in the Log) advanced: first deliberate withholding complete.
**New facts:**
- Lt. Pruitt is a Coast Guard First District (Portland) lieutenant, approximately mid-twenties, fair-faced, deliberate manner — went to school for the job. He is not a recurring major character but is named for consistency.
- The pathologist's preliminary (visible to Eira from across the desk, upside-down) reads: Adult male, drowning, approximate age 38–48, no identifying documents, no identifying marks. The 'no identifying marks' is new information — nothing on the skin.
- Vessels in the harbor log for the October 9 night: lobster smack Thistle (Roy Blaine, Coldwater Reach), lobster smack Doris V. (Delmar Stannard, Coldwater Reach), coaster Macomber (Birch Macomber, Machias, arrived October 8, departed October 11), Lighthouse Service ice barge Ploughman at anchor (inner anchorage).
- Delmar Stannard: Coldwater Reach lobsterman, master of the smack Doris V.
- Macomber (vessel): registered Machias, 1937. Marine survey from Machias.
- Birch Macomber: late fifties, spare frame, economical in movement, careful with money — impression formed from two dock conversations over four years. Reliable; not a man to put out in bad weather without reason.
- The October 11 departure entry — Macomber, 0430 — is in Eira's handwriting. She does not remember making it. Two possible explanations she can reconstruct: she made it herself in the exhaustion of the storm's second day at 0430, or she entered it from a note left by a fisherman (the harbor has no overnight watchman; Blaine and Stannard sometimes leave notes on the hook beside the pier shed door). She cannot now reconstruct which.
- The bolted drawer is the bottom-left drawer of the chart cabinet. Vessel registration files inside, alphabetical by vessel name. The key is a small brass key on Eira's ring — the only copy, originally cut for that lock when Owen had it duplicated in Machias in 1932. Eira switched to the duplicate after the original wore thin at the bow.
- Eira tells Pruitt she was out at approximately 0130 on the morning of October 10 checking the Macomber's mooring bridle — first gusts running, concerned about the set on the bollard. This refers to a second trip out (the blow coming on full) — not the first trip at 0015 when she saw the man. The first trip and the man are what she omits.
- The Coast Guard official position on the case as of early November 1943: cause of death is drowning, no evidence of foul play, case open but not actively worked.
**Loose threads:**
- Thread 1 (The Blank in the Log): Eira has now completed her first deliberate withholding — she answered the precise question asked and not the question she should have asked herself. The omission is the man at the end of the pier, October 9–10, between 0015 and 0030. The departure entry (Macomber, October 11, 0430) is now a second irregularity in her attention.
- The Macomber's departure on the second day of the blow: Eira has filed this as a fact that doesn't fit the pattern — a reliable careful man putting out in bad weather. She has not shared this with anyone and has not entered it in the official log. It will resurface in ch10.
- The 'no identifying marks' on the pathologist's preliminary: this closes off one avenue of identification. The body has no tattoos, scars, or marks that would connect to a service record or registry.
- Who left the 0430 note (if it was a note)? Or did Eira make the entry herself and lose the memory to exhaustion? This question is not answered and is not meant to be — its point is the gap in her reconstruction.
- Delmar Stannard is a working lobsterman in Coldwater Reach; his vessel Doris V. is at mooring during the storm. He has not appeared in any scene yet and his name has only appeared in the harbor log.

## 05-feeding-time
**Summary:** November 1943, Hannah POV. Three to four weeks after the POWs' arrival. The chapter establishes Hannah's 5 a.m. barn routine in November (cold, dark, the cows knowing her by her boots: Else first, then down the line to Berta) and the new shape the mornings have taken since the truck came. At seven the three older POWs go north to the pasture fence; Karl is assigned manure hauling in the south working yard — the worst job, going to the youngest. Hannah watches from a gap in the south barn wall (between the third and fourth boards, shoulder height) and has been watching for weeks: cataloging Karl's efficiency, his navigation of the frozen rut at a fixed angle, and — in the second week — noticing that Magda has let out the seams of his jacket without announcement or explanation. The chapter's central scene: one cold morning Hannah sets her heavy grain bucket down at the south gate post instead of carrying it through, stands watching the ice on the puddle; Karl comes past with the wheelbarrow, sees the bucket, picks it up, and places it on the gate rail where she can reach it from her side — not through the gate, not handed over the fence, placed. He says: Da, bitte. She says: Danke. He looks up, recalibrating. She has been carrying the sentence for three weeks; she says it: Mein Vater kommt aus Bayern. He considers. He says: Ich auch. He goes back to work. Lonzo Reed is ten yards away adjusting his boot lacing and was not watching. For the rest of the working morning Hannah turns the word *auch* over in her thinking — the conjunction that places without explaining, that says 'also me' without naming the category — and thinks about Straubing and Regensburg on the same river, fifty kilometers, her father's second-week dinner remark (*Regensburg ist nicht weit*). Noon: Albrecht comes in, eats standing at the counter without speaking, goes back out. Magda stirs the pot. The kitchen is silent in two languages. Hannah thinks: *auch*. She washes the bowl and goes back to the barn. Threads 4 (The Fence) and 5 (The Pfeiffer Silence) advanced.
**New facts:**
- The south barn wall has a gap between the third and fourth boards, at shoulder height when standing; it looks out onto the full south run of the working yard, the south gate, and the icehouse at the near edge of sight. This is distinct from the east wall gap (between boards 4 and 5) which faces the farm lane and icehouse front.
- The gate in the south fence is for equipment — wide enough for the tractor. It separates the barnyard from the working field. Hannah uses it daily to carry the grain bucket to the calf pen on the other side.
- The calf pen is approximately thirty feet from the south gate, on the working-field side of the fence.
- The three older POWs are assigned to the north pasture fence-repair detail daily; Karl is assigned to manure hauling in the south working yard. This has been the standing assignment since the first week of the work detail.
- Karl's first words to Hannah: 'Da, bitte' — spoken while placing her feed bucket on the gate rail, November 1943. This is his first voluntary verbal contact with any member of the Pfeiffer household.
- Hannah's first voluntary use of German with a stranger — she says 'Danke' reflexively, then after a pause, deliberately: 'Mein Vater kommt aus Bayern.' She has been rehearsing this sentence for three weeks.
- Karl's first substantive verbal response to Hannah: 'Ich auch.' — two words, placing himself in the same Bavarian category without naming it. This is November 1943.
- Lonzo Reed was adjusting the lacing on his left boot when the exchange occurred. He was ten yards away and was not watching.
- Magda Pfeiffer let out the seams of Karl's Army-issue denim jacket in the second week of the POW work detail. The work was done in Magda's characteristic straight-line stitch, with no backstitch at the ends. She said nothing about it before or after.
- Hilde: a named dairy cow on the Pfeiffer farm. Placed next to Else in the milking order. Established in ch05.
- Hannah has understood since Albrecht's second-week dinner remark ('Regensburg ist nicht weit') that her father is aware of Karl's home city and that its proximity to Straubing registers for him, even if he will not discuss it.
**Loose threads:**
- What does Albrecht actually feel about Karl's presence — the fact that someone from fifty kilometers from Straubing is working his farm? He has acknowledged the geography once ('Regensburg ist nicht weit') and nothing more. This is sealed from Hannah's POV.
- Karl's reaction to Hannah's German is established (he recalibrates) but his thinking is not accessible. What does 'Ich auch' cost him — what does it open? This is a loose thread for Thread 4.
- Thread 4 (The Fence): the exchange has happened at its minimal level. The next advance is ch08, where Albrecht says seven words at dinner. The connection between Hannah's fence exchange and her father's acknowledgment is the thread's next beat.
- Thread 5 (The Pfeiffer Silence): Hannah has now deployed her father's language outward, toward a stranger — the first use of it for contact rather than comprehension. The silence at noon is unchanged, but Hannah's relationship to it has shifted slightly. She is now in possession of something her father doesn't know she did.

- Marcus Klein is Hannah's German teacher and has noted in his grade book that she knows more than she shows. He does not appear in this chapter but the fact that she has now spoken German to Karl — across a fence, not in a classroom — is something he will never know about directly.

## 06-what-the-body-spoke
**Summary:** Tuesday, November 1943. Marcus POV. Fenn summons Marcus to his office: the Coast Guard made a general inquiry about whether anyone heard the drowned man speak; fisherman Ed Larrabee reported hearing something "European, maybe German" at the commercial float on the afternoon of October 9. The report went up the chain — Coast Guard, selectmen, Fenn — and Fenn asks Marcus to serve as a "civic resource" for the harbormaster in case she has materials needing identification. Marcus agrees genuinely and also without real alternative. He has Latin in the afternoon (eight students, third declension), then walks to the harbor office in near-dark November light. He has been in the harbor office once before — spring 1942, preparing a classroom exercise on maritime regulation that never materialized, a visit he retreated from partly from uncertainty about its reception. At the harbor office: Eira at her desk, her oilskin coat on the door peg, Owen's barometer on the east wall at a taller person's height, the copper-pipe hooks with the two cups. She is professionally contained in her space. Marcus explains his presence. She says: "The Coast Guard has what I know." He starts to leave, turns back, offers a more specific use: identification documents from the late thirties/early forties, varieties of German, regional variation. She says: "I'll note it." He walks home. In the near-dark lane he thinks through the phrase *ein Mensch ohne Papiere* — the weight in German that the English "a man without papers" only approximates: how papers in that era and that system were not administrative evidence of identity but identity itself, and their removal was the removal of legal personhood. He thinks the word *staatenlos*. He does not state directly but references obliquely his own experience at Sherbrooke, where a man in a uniform looked at his documents and made a decision. At the Crow house, supper. Marcus tells Henry briefly. Henry: "She'll use you when she needs you. Not before." Marcus agrees. He washes his bowl and sits at his desk with his annotating pen, not opening the grade book, then caps the pen and puts it down. Thread 2 (The Man Still Moving) advanced: Marcus makes his first small investment in the town's question, at no cost, declined without rudeness. He makes it anyway.
**New facts:**
- Ed Larrabee is a fisherman working out of the commercial float at Coldwater Reach. He was patching a trap buoy at the commercial float on the afternoon of October 9, 1943, and heard the drowned man say something 'European, maybe German.' This is the basis of the inquiry that reaches Marcus via Fenn in November 1943.
- The chain of transmission for Ed Larrabee's report: fisherman Larrabee → Coast Guard → harbormaster's office → selectmen → Principal Fenn → Marcus Klein.
- Marcus teaches Latin as well as German at Coldwater Reach High School. His Latin class has eight students and meets on Tuesdays (the German class meets Mon/Wed/Fri).
- Mrs. Farwell is the secretary to Principal Aldous Fenn at Coldwater Reach High School. She writes Fenn's inter-office notes.
- In spring 1942 Marcus prepared a classroom exercise on maritime regulation — the Lighthouse Service's jurisdiction, the harbor log as a legal document, admirtalty law and local authority — which required a visit to the harbor office. He ultimately did not use the exercise, partly from uncertainty about whether the visit would be received as legitimate. This is the occasion of his single prior visit to the harbor office.
- Marcus's offer to Eira Bowman (November 1943): identification documents from the late 1930s and early 1940s; varieties of German; regional and national variation in written forms. He says 'I'm at the school.' She says 'I'll note it.'
- Eira's exact response to Marcus's initial approach: 'The Coast Guard has what I know.' — precise, present-tense, saying nothing about future conditions.
- Marcus's meditation on *ein Mensch ohne Papiere* vs. 'a man without papers': the German phrase carries the weight of papers-as-personhood in the Nazi legal context, where removing papers removed the legal category of personhood. He thinks the word *staatenlos* (stateless; floorless; without a state to claim you).
- Marcus references obliquely (without naming it) his experience at the Sherbrooke internment camp as the source of his direct knowledge about what it means when a man in a uniform looks at your documents and makes a decision about what they prove.
- Henry Crow's assessment of Eira Bowman: 'She'll use you when she needs you. Not before.' Marcus finds this correct and more reliable than his own reading.
- The Crow house has an elm tree in the yard, visible from the kitchen window. Its branches are bare by November 1943.
**Loose threads:**
- Thread 2 (The Man Still Moving): Marcus has made a small investment in the town's question. The offer is filed, not activated. The connection between him and Eira that can be activated later now exists. Next advance: ch09.
