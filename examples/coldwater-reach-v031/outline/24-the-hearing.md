# Chapter 24 — The Hearing

**One-line summary**: The school board votes 5–2 to retain Marcus; Henry is mopping the corridor when Marcus walks out.

**POV**: Marcus Klein  
**Tense**: Third-person limited, past  
**Setting**: Coldwater Reach High School — the library, which serves as the board meeting room; the corridor outside  
**Time**: November 1944 — a Tuesday evening

---

## Goal
Marcus wants to keep his position. More precisely: he wants to answer the board's questions accurately, without capitulating to the framing of the questions, and without performing a kind of humility he does not feel.

## Obstacle
The questions are not entirely fair, and some of them are not answerable without exposing things he would prefer to keep private — the internment at Sherbrooke especially. The board may not mean cruelty, but the machinery of a formal review does not distinguish between fair and unfair inquiry; it just asks.

---

## Action Beats

- Tuesday evening, November. Marcus puts on the good suit. He walks to the school; the library is lit from the inside, visible from the street.
- Seven board members around the table, plus Principal Fenn in an observer chair along the wall. Edna Locke is at the far end of the board — she is not a member, but she has been invited to speak as a faculty representative, which means someone on the board thought that was appropriate.
- The chair of the hearing, a man named Aldrich, reads the petition's concerns: Dr. Klein holds foreign national status; the matter of his German origins (not Austrian — the distinction is not made in the petition); the appropriateness of a German national teaching American children in wartime; the question of undisclosed organizational membership.
- Marcus answers in order:
  - He is Austrian, not German. He has not been a German citizen. He explains, briefly, the difference — the Anschluss, March 1938, Austria absorbed into Germany against the will of its people and government. He speaks without heat.
  - He left Vienna in March 1938, the month of the Anschluss. He went to Paris. Then London. Then Cambridge, Massachusetts, via the Emergency Committee.
  - He was interned at Sherbrooke, Quebec, for approximately nine months in 1941. He was released and cleared by both the Canadian government and the American authorities before taking the position in Coldwater Reach.
  - He is not a member of any political organization. He is a philologist.
- One board member asks whether he has family in Austria. He says: "I did." He says nothing more about this. The board member, reading something in his face, does not follow up.
- Another asks: "Do you consider yourself loyal to the United States?" Marcus considers the word *loyal* for a moment — the way he always considers a word before deploying it. He says: "I consider myself here, teaching your children German and Latin, and I expect to be here next term as well."
- Aldrich calls for the vote. The result: five in favor of maintaining his employment, two opposed. Aldrich thanks him for appearing and formally closes the review.
- Marcus stands, collects his papers, and walks out. The corridor: Henry is mopping, working his way from the far end toward the library door. He doesn't look up when Marcus comes out. As Marcus reaches the building's main door, Henry says, without pausing the mop: "Five to two."
- Marcus says: "Yes."
- Henry: "Better than I expected."
- Marcus: "Than I expected as well."
- He walks home. The November air is very cold; there will be snow by the weekend, probably. He thinks about the word *loyal* — he gave the board the wrong definition for it, intentionally. Loyalty is a word that asks who you belong to. His answer — *I am here, teaching your children* — was not about belonging. It was about presence. The distinction matters to him. He isn't sure it matters to the board.

---

## Outcome / Turn
Marcus keeps his post — narrowly. The hearing exposed what it exposed (the internment, the family in Austria), and he answered with precision and survived. Henry's presence in the corridor is the most sustaining thing about the night.

## New Question or Pressure Raised
The two dissenting votes remain dissenting. What happens when the veterans come back? Will the next review go differently? And what does Marcus mean by "I expect to be here next term" — is that his investment, or his performance of investment?

---

## Continuity Notes
- The school board: seven members, plus Principal Fenn as observer. Board chair: Aldrich (surname; not a major character). Edna Locke present as faculty representative.
- The petition names three families; two of those families' representatives are on the board. The vote is 5-2 to retain.
- Sherbrooke internment: Marcus mentions it formally for the first time in the narrative. It is in his official background (the Emergency Committee's clearance process required disclosure); it is not secret, but he has not spoken of it in Coldwater Reach. This is its first public naming in the story.
- "I did" (re: family in Austria): what happened to his family is not specified. The implication of the past tense is understood by the board member and the reader. Marcus does not elaborate; the board member does not press. This should be the heaviest moment in the chapter without a single word of emphasis.
- "I am here, teaching your children": Marcus's answer to the loyalty question. The Drafter should render his thinking about this answer — why he chose it, what he meant by the substitution of presence for belonging — as interior, not as reported back to the board.
- Henry's corridor: he is mopping. He does his job. He knows what happened; Edna Locke told him the vote before he left the building. His "five to two" is not a question; it is a confirmation.
- The mop: Henry's instrument of work and the instrument by which he is present everywhere in this building without being asked to be. In his own chapter he noticed through the work; here Marcus notices him through the same work.
- November snow: forecast, not yet arrived. The cold is specific. This is the beginning of the second winter of the story.
