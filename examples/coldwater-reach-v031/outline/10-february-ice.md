# Chapter 10 — February Ice

**One-line summary**: While coordinating the freeing of a coaster from the inner-anchorage ice, Eira learns that Birch Macomber took Portland contract work last fall.

**POV**: Eira Bowman  
**Tense**: Third-person limited, past  
**Setting**: Coldwater Reach harbor; the inner anchorage; the dock  
**Time**: February 1944

---

## Goal
Eira's primary goal is professional: free the coaster *Wren* from where it has grounded on the ice-ledge and get it to safe water. The secondary goal — information about Macomber — is opportunistic; she doesn't set out to get it.

## Obstacle
Coordinating the buoy tender takes time; the tide window is narrow; the skipper of the *Wren* is not in distress but is not quite cooperative either. The information about Macomber comes through conversation, not interrogation, which means she can ask only so much before the question becomes visible.

---

## Action Beats

- February: the inner anchorage has gone hard at the north end — ice from the shore out about forty yards, thin at the edge. A coaster from Rockland, the *Wren*, came in on a falling tide and found itself with its keel on the ice-ledge at the mooring field's edge. Not in danger, but stuck.
- Eira is down at the dock at first light to assess. The *Wren*'s skipper is Estelle Hartley — one of three or four women skippers operating on the Maine coast, a fact Eira notes without dwelling on it the way other people would. Hartley is about forty, has sailed this coast since 1927, and is annoyed but not panicked.
- They discuss options: try to rock the *Wren* off with the engine, wait for the tide to lift it, or call the buoy tender for a tow. Eira calls the buoy tender — the tide won't lift her enough for another four hours and the ice is still building from the north in the overnight cold.
- While they wait: Eira and Hartley stand on the dock. The cold is specific — the cold that comes off ice is different from the cold that comes off open water; it is drier, stiffer. They drink coffee from Eira's thermos.
- Hartley knows the Maine coast trade thoroughly. Eira asks, in the way of professional conversation: "You see Birch Macomber down your way?" Hartley: "Portland, mostly, these days. He's been running something regular down that way since fall." Eira: "October-ish?" Hartley: "Could be. Some kind of contract, he said. Paid well, which is how I know it, because Birch doesn't usually turn down a run for the weather and he turned one down from me in September."
- Eira: "What kind of contract?" Hartley shrugs: "He didn't say. Something out of Portland for the Navy district, maybe. He had papers."
- The buoy tender arrives; the tow goes straightforwardly. The *Wren* comes off the ledge, shifts into open water, and Hartley's crew checks the keel — no damage.
- Eira thanks Hartley; Hartley thanks her for the tow call. The *Wren* heads south. Eira walks back to the harbor office.
- That evening she writes in her personal log: *Macomber — Portland, Navy district contract — fall 1943. Had papers. Turned down a Rockland run in September. Inquiry: what was the contract?*

---

## Outcome / Turn
Eira has a new lead: Macomber was running a Navy district contract out of Portland, which connects his presence in Coldwater Reach in early October and his irregular departure to a purpose of some kind. "Had papers" is notable — it means his movement was officially sanctioned, which means records exist somewhere.

## New Question or Pressure Raised
What was the Portland Navy district contract, and does it explain why August Schell (not yet known to Eira by name) received a postcard from Portland with a date and time on it? Is there a connection between Macomber's contract work and the body?

---

## Continuity Notes
- Estelle Hartley, skipper of the coaster *Wren*, Rockland. A woman skipper; Eira notes her as a professional, not as a rarity. This is consistent with Eira's character — she doesn't perform solidarity or special kinship; she works with competent people.
- The inner anchorage freezing in the inner section: consistent with the world.md description of the harbor. The outer harbor stays open; the inner anchorage freezes by January in hard winters.
- Birch Macomber: Portland-based by winter 1944; Navy district contract beginning fall 1943. Hartley doesn't know the specifics.
- "Had papers": meaning some kind of official Navy district authorization, possibly a requisition or contract documentation. Macomber is not operating outside the law; his contract work is legitimate. This is important: the body's connection to Macomber, when it comes, will not be criminal. It will be mundane and sad.
- The buoy tender: Lighthouse Service, puts in twice monthly at Coldwater Reach normally. Eira calls for an emergency tow, which is within her authority as harbormaster.
- Eira and Hartley's conversation: it should read as two working women having a working conversation, not as an interview. The Macomber information is obtained without Hartley realizing it's being sought.
