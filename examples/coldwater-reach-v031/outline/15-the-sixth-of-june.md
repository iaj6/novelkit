# Chapter 15 — The Sixth of June

**One-line summary**: D-Day. Marcus holds his private knowledge of France while managing a classroom; that evening he reads the first letter the Emergency Committee sent him and puts it away again.

**POV**: Marcus Klein  
**Tense**: Third-person limited, past  
**Setting**: Coldwater Reach High School; Henry and Lillian's house  
**Time**: June 6, 1944

---

## Goal
Marcus has no single goal today; the day imposes itself on every ordinary thing he would otherwise be doing. What he is trying to manage is the gap between what the day means publicly and what it means in his own specific experience of France.

## Obstacle
He cannot explain what France is to him without explaining more than he is prepared to explain. His knowledge of Paris — of specific streets, a specific apartment, the specific population of a neighborhood — is not something he can share in this room, in this school, in this war. The obstacle is the gap between his experience and the language available to him here.

---

## Action Beats

- The news comes over the school's BC-1206 receiver at 7:02 a.m.: Allied forces have landed on the coast of Normandy. The announcement is precise and controlled in the OWI voice — no triumphalism, not yet, but the barely-contained weight of the fact.
- Principal Fenn makes an announcement at the morning assembly. He calls it the beginning of the end, and the students receive this with a particular kind of held-breath hope that is also anxiety, because for most of them "the end" means brothers and cousins coming home or not coming home. Hannah Pfeiffer, in the third row, looks at her hands.
- Marcus teaches the morning class. German II: today they were meant to cover indirect discourse. He writes it on the board anyway — you keep the classroom running because that is what the classroom is for, and because the alternative is a room full of seventeen-year-olds with no structure and too much fear. Hannah answers a question with accuracy and a flatness that tells him she is somewhere else in her head.
- During the free period, the teacher's lounge. The other teachers are around the radio — the principal has rolled it in from his office. The news is repeating. The map that Edna Locke has been keeping on the history-room wall — with pins for the various theaters — doesn't have Normandy marked yet. She is in there marking it.
- Marcus goes to his classroom. He stands at the window. Paris. The apartment on the Rue du Temple that he shared with two other scholars for eight months, 1939–1940. The neighborhood: the old Jewish quarter, the Marais. He knows what happened to those streets, what the Occupation meant, the way a person knows a thing that was reported to him secondhand by someone who escaped later. He does not know what happened to the specific people. He will not know for some time.
- He thinks: the Allies are on the coast. Four years and eight months since he left Paris. The city has been occupied for four years. Today it begins to un-be occupied, or the beginning of that begins. This is good. It is the right thing. He is glad and he cannot be glad completely because being glad completely would require believing the damage is undone, which it is not.
- After school: Henry has the radio on. Lillian is in the garden, not because she doesn't want to listen but because the garden is the garden and it wants doing. They sit at the kitchen table, Marcus and Henry, and listen to the report. Henry says: "Long way to go still." Marcus says: "Yes." They listen.
- That evening, in his room, Marcus opens the drawer in the bedside table where he keeps the letters he has carried longest. He takes out the Emergency Committee's first letter, autumn 1940, which told him there was a place for him in Cambridge, Massachusetts, and that a position would be held. He reads it. He puts it back. He goes to sleep.

---

## Outcome / Turn
D-Day has happened; the world has turned a corner. Marcus registers it with full attention and cannot access the full register of feeling it deserves. What he can access — the letter, the specific streets, the years — he keeps to himself. He goes to sleep.

## New Question or Pressure Raised
What will France's liberation bring — news he has been waiting for, or news he has been afraid to receive? His people in Paris (the scholars, the neighbors) remain unknown until after the war.

---

## Continuity Notes
- D-Day: June 6, 1944. The Allied landings at Normandy. Historical date.
- Marcus in Paris: approximately 1939–1940, prior to the fall of France (June 1940). He lived in the Marais, the old Jewish quarter. He left Paris before the fall, heading to London (per his background). This is consistent with canon: Paris → London → Cambridge, Massachusetts, then Quebec internment. The Rue du Temple address is invented but geographically accurate for the Marais.
- The Occupation of Paris: June 1940 – August 1944. Marcus does not know what happened to specific people; he has received secondhand reports. This is not discussed directly in the chapter; it is the substrate.
- Hannah Pfeiffer in the assembly: she is looking at her hands because she is thinking about Rolf, in Italy. Italy is a separate theater; the Normandy landing does not directly affect Rolf's situation, but the news of Allied action everywhere is reason to think about him.
- The BC-1206 radio receiver: period-accurate. Schools had radio equipment; the principal could roll in a receiver for a major announcement.
- Edna Locke marking the map: this is her chapter, the history classroom, her professional domain. Small detail that places her.
- The Emergency Committee's first letter, autumn 1940: Marcus has carried this letter since Cambridge. It is not described in detail; its existence is established here for later use.
- Henry and Lillian: they listen together without dramatizing. Lillian is in the garden. The domestic order is not a response to D-Day; it is what they are doing, and it continues.
