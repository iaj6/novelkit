# Chapter 25 — The Selectmen

**One-line summary**: Selectman Orcutt implies a returning veteran should have Eira's post; Eira reorganizes the chart cabinet for the first time since Owen died.

**POV**: Eira Bowman  
**Tense**: Third-person limited, past  
**Setting**: Harbor office; Eira's house (briefly, at the end)  
**Time**: November 1944

---

## Goal
Eira's goal, during Orcutt's visit, is to end the conversation without conceding anything. Her goal after he leaves is to do something she has been putting off.

## Obstacle
Orcutt is not rude or explicit. He presents the situation as a civic opportunity rather than a displacement. There is no threat to refuse; there is only a suggestion to decline, which is harder.

---

## Action Beats

- A Tuesday in November, the day after the first real snow of the season. Eira is at her desk with the quarterly report when Dwight Orcutt comes in. He is a selectman — fish buyer's nephew, well-meaning in the way of a man who has not had to think carefully about what his good intentions cost other people. He takes the chair across from her desk without being invited to; this is not rudeness, exactly, but it is familiar in a way that acknowledges she is not quite in the same professional category as a male harbormaster would be.
- He says: Howie Beal is coming home in December. The town is glad. He was the marine engineer on the trap-hauler *Sentinel* before he enlisted; he knows the harbor work. He'll need something to do when he gets back. The town wants to support its returning veterans.
- He doesn't say: we want you to step aside.
- He says: "There'll be men coming home who'll need reintegration into the work they know. The harbor's a natural fit for a man like Howie."
- Eira says: "Is Howie interested in harbor management?" She means it as a genuine question; she is also aware of its weight.
- Orcutt: "He could learn the administrative side."
- Eira: "The administrative side is the job, Dwight."
- Pause.
- Orcutt says, in the tone of someone who is not used to being required to be explicit: "We want to make sure our veterans have opportunities, Eira. I'm sure you understand."
- Eira says: "The quarterly report is on your desk by the first of the month. Is there anything else?"
- Orcutt goes. He does not slam the door — he is too well-mannered for that — but the click of the latch has a quality she registers.
- She stands at the window for a few minutes. She is going to fight this. Not loudly, not by petition or by complaint to the state board. She is going to do the job so well that displacing her requires active malice rather than passive expectation. This is not a new decision: she has been doing this for eight years. She is naming it to herself for the first time.
- She goes to the chart cabinet. She opens the drawer she has been avoiding — not Owen's pending drawer (she opened that in September), but the main chart drawer, which holds the working charts for the harbor and approaches. These are her charts by now — she has marked the harbor's changes over eight years, noted the mooring field adjustments, updated the dredge depth in the approach channel. But they are still filed in Owen's system: by the date he created the file, in his hand.
- She spends the rest of the afternoon refiling the working charts in her own system — by function, not by date, which is how she thinks about them. Owen's dates remain in pencil in the corners of the charts themselves; she doesn't erase them. She makes room for hers next to his.
- By the time she is done it is dark. She makes coffee, drinks it, locks the harbor office, and walks home.
- At home: Owen's oilskin coat on the hook. She leaves it there — she has always left it there; that is not a question tonight. She makes tea. She thinks about what it means to reorganize a system that has worked for eight years. She decides it means the same thing it has always meant: doing the job.

---

## Outcome / Turn
Eira has declined Orcutt's implication without refusing it directly. She has reorganized the charts — a physical act of claiming the post as her own while not erasing the record of its previous occupant. She drinks her coffee and goes home.

## New Question or Pressure Raised
When will Orcutt return with something less deniable? When Howie Beal comes home in December, will the pressure become explicit? Will Eira be able to hold the post past the war's end?

## Continuity Notes
- Dwight Orcutt: selectman, Coldwater Reach. Fish buyer's nephew — not a major character, but he has the kind of genial authority that doesn't know it's using authority. He appears here and is referenced in Chapter 29. Howie Beal is named but never appears.
- Howie Beal: former marine engineer on the lobster trap-hauler *Sentinel* before enlistment. Returning December 1944. He is a real veteran with a real connection to the harbor; Eira's defense is not that she is better than him — it is that she is in the job and doing it.
- The chart cabinet: previously described in the harbor office. The main chart drawer (working charts, Owen's filing system) is separate from Owen's pending drawer (where the addendum now lives). Eira reorganizes the main drawer; she does not touch the pending drawer.
- The reorganization: she moves to a functional filing system (by type of chart rather than by date of creation). Owen's original dates stay on the charts in his handwriting. She makes room for both. This is the physical statement of her arc: not erasure, not replacement — cohabitation with the past.
- "She is going to fight this. Not loudly": Eira's strategy is consistent with what Henry told Marcus in Chapter 09 ("make them act first") and with her character throughout. She does not perform conflict; she outperforms opposition.
- The *Sentinel*: a named vessel in the Coldwater Reach harbor. Previously a working trap-hauler; presumably mothballed or sold during Beal's service.
- Owen's oilskin coat on the hook: she leaves it there. This is not a moment of grief — it is a decision that has been made so many times it has become a posture. He stays on the hook. She goes to bed.
