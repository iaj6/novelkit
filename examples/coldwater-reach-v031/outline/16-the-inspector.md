# Chapter 16 — The Inspector

**One-line summary**: A naval district inspector mentions a Swedish vessel's missing crew member named August Schell; Eira realizes she already had the name.

**POV**: Eira Bowman  
**Tense**: Third-person limited, past  
**Setting**: Harbor office; the dock  
**Time**: July 1944

---

## Goal
Eira's goal is professional: pass the routine naval district inspection. The secondary development — the inspector's mention of August Schell — is not something she pursued.

## Obstacle
The information arrives at her and she has to decide, in the moment, what to do with the fact that she already knew the name from Ada Gile. Saying so would mean explaining how she came to have it.

---

## Action Beats

- A Tuesday in July, the summer heat soft and bright on the water. The buoy tender's monthly call brings Commander Whitmore, naval district inspector, for a routine review. Eira has been through two of these; she knows the form. She has the logs out, the vessel documentation, the communication records, the safety equipment inventory.
- Whitmore is a professional — not easy to read but not antagonistic. He goes through the records methodically. The harbor office is in order; it is always in order. He notes this.
- After the formal inspection, they have coffee at Eira's desk. He says: "I have a civilian matter to mention, while I'm here." He opens a secondary folder. The October 1943 body incident — her report is in the folder; she recognizes the form number. The naval district has been cross-referencing coastal incidents from that autumn. A Swedish merchant vessel, the SS *Ångermanälven*, filed a missing crew member report with the Seamen's Bureau in November 1943: August Schell, German-born naturalized Swedish national, age approximately 40. He went missing somewhere in the run between Portland and Nova Scotia in the second week of October 1943.
- Whitmore: "The timeline matches your incident. We can't confirm without documentation — your body had no papers — but the district thinks it's likely the same man. I'm notifying coastal officers in the zone as a matter of record."
- Eira: "August Schell."
- Whitmore: "Yes. You've heard the name?"
- Eira looks at him for a moment. She says: "I may have come across it in the course of the ordinary mail." This is technically true: Ada Gile mentioned a piece of mail addressed to "A. Schell." It is not a lie. It is the minimum possible truth.
- Whitmore: "If you have anything on record that references the name, pass it to the district office. This is not an active investigation — we're calling it likely the same man and leaving it in the open files. German-born but Swedish national, no indication of anything other than a maritime fatality. No security implication."
- He finishes his coffee. The formal visit ends. He goes back out to the tender.
- Eira sits at her desk for a long time.
- She opens her personal log. She writes: *August Schell. German-born. Swedish national. Crew — SS Ångermanälven. Missing Portland to Nova Scotia, second week of October 1943. Naval district: likely our body. No security concern. Case remaining open.*
- She looks at what she has built, entry by entry, over eight months. The postcard to general delivery. The date and time. Macomber's departure in the second day of the blow. The man at the end of the pier with the canvas bag.
- August Schell. He was on the Ångermanälven and he was German-born, which means he had reasons to have left Germany that she doesn't know and that she probably will never know. He received a postcard from Portland arranging a meeting. The meeting was at Coldwater Reach, the night of October 9. He came. She was there. She walked past him at the end of the pier.
- She does not know what happened after. She knows he was in the water three days later.
- She closes the log. She goes and checks the mooring on the lobster boat at the south dock that has been pulling on its line in the tidal current all week.

---

## Outcome / Turn
The body has a probable name: August Schell. German-born, Swedish national, merchant sailor. Not a spy. Not an agent. A man from Germany who got on a neutral Swedish ship and sailed the North Atlantic in wartime and drowned, apparently, in a Maine harbor. Eira has the name from two sources now. She tells Whitmore the minimum.

## New Question or Pressure Raised
Who sent the postcard from Portland? What was the meeting for? Is Macomber's connection to Schell something she will ever be able to follow? And does she need to?

---

## Continuity Notes
- Commander Whitmore: naval district inspector, not previously named. He appears in Chapter 29 (final contact by telephone to officially close the case). He is not a villain; he is bureaucratic machinery doing its job.
- The SS *Ångermanälven*: Swedish merchant vessel. "Ångermanälven" is the name of a river in northern Sweden — appropriately nautical for a Swedish ship. The vessel ran the Portland-to-Nova Scotia route, which is historically plausible for wartime neutral merchant shipping.
- August Schell: German-born, naturalized Swedish national. Age approximately 40. Missing second week of October 1943. This is consistent with the body arriving October 13 (after the storm).
- "German-born but Swedish national, no indication of anything other than a maritime fatality. No security implication." — This is Whitmore's assessment and the naval district's conclusion. It is the novel's anti-spy-thriller statement made through bureaucratic voice. A German-born Swedish national who drowned in a Maine harbor was not an agent.
- Eira's minimal disclosure: "I may have come across it in the course of the ordinary mail." She is not lying. She is not telling the full story. This is the pattern of her whole arc.
- The "meeting" theory: Eira now has a coherent picture: Schell received a postcard arranging a meeting in Coldwater, came for the meeting, and drowned — before, during, or after. The meeting's purpose remains unknown. Macomber's Portland contract work is the connecting thread she cannot pull.
- Eira checking the lobster boat mooring at the end: a return to work. This is how she closes things — by going back to the dock.
