# Chapter 28 — Regensburg

**One-line summary**: Karl lies in his bunk listening to news of the Allied advance; he thinks about his father's shop, the harbor from the ridge, and the word *bleiben*.

**POV**: Karl Reinmar  
**Tense**: Third-person limited, past  
**Setting**: The Pfeiffer icehouse; Karl's bunk  
**Time**: March 1945

*Exceptional chapter — target 1,200–1,800 words*

---

## Goal
Karl does not have a goal in the conventional sense. The chapter is his interior — what the news means, what Regensburg means now, what the farm has become to him. He is not deciding anything; he is holding what he has been given and what has been taken.

## Obstacle
The obstacle is the ordinary one of grief: he cannot go home, cannot verify anything, cannot make the future resemble the past. He is nineteen and has already outlived his version of what life was supposed to be.

---

## Action Beats

- March: the guards have a radio in the provost marshal's field office that serves the farm, and the news from Germany comes through the wall at night if the others are quiet. The Allied advance into Germany. Place names that Karl knows: Frankfurt, Cologne. Not Regensburg yet, but close enough that he can draw the line in his head.
- He lies on his bunk. The icehouse cold is different in March from January — there's a different quality to it, a loosening that suggests the season is turning even if the temperature hasn't yet.
- He thinks about Regensburg. The specific geography of it: the cathedral spire visible from three miles outside the city; the bend of the Danube; the Roman wall that still stands along the old city edge. He walked past the wall every morning on the way to his father's shop. The tile floor. The smell of blood and sawdust. His father's hands, big, certain in their work.
- His father is dead. He knows this. He received the Red Cross notification in January: *Friedrich Reinmar, deceased December 28, 1944.* He has read it twice and put it in the wooden box under his bunk and has not taken it out again.
- He thinks: what is Regensburg now? He knows the city has been bombed. He does not know how much of it is left. He imagines the cathedral spire and then cannot be sure that it is still there to imagine.
- He thinks about the word *Heimweh* — homesickness, home-ache. He does not feel *Heimweh* in the way he expected to. He expected to want to go home the way you want water when you're thirsty. He wants something; he is not sure it is the place he left.
- He thinks about the Pfeiffer farm. The smell of the icehouse: pine pitch, straw, damp wool, and underneath it the sawdust in the walls that has never fully lost the winter smell. The sound of the cows at 5 a.m. The fence line that he has walked hundreds of times. The girl with the braids — Hannah — and the word-cards in the butter-box cedar, and the particular October night when he stood on a ridge and looked at the sea for the first time.
- The sea was very large. He has thought about it since. He had not understood, before standing there, that the world could be so much larger than the part of it he had been given.
- He thinks about the word *bleiben* — to stay. In German, *bleiben* implies remaining where you are; it is not the same as choosing; it can mean simply that you have not yet gone. He does not know if staying in America after the war is possible. He does not know if there is a Regensburg to return to. He does not know what he would do in a ruined city with no father and no shop.
- He thinks: the girl brought him to see the harbor. She didn't have to do that. He has not asked her why she did it and she has not explained. It was a thing done because it seemed to be the right thing to do, and he does not have a word for that kind of exchange — he has been thinking about it since October and the word has not arrived in either of his languages.
- The big POW, the one who reads the German Bible, has started snoring. Karl turns over. The bunk creaks.
- Outside: March in Maine. The ice on the puddles will be gone in two weeks. The cows will go out to pasture in a month. There is still work to do.
- He does not sleep for a while, and then he does.

---

## Outcome / Turn
Karl is suspended between a past that is ending (his father dead, Regensburg uncertain) and a future he cannot yet see. The farm is what he has. The chapter closes not on resolution but on the bodily necessity of sleep and work — there is still work to do.

## New Question or Pressure Raised
Deliberately unresolved: what becomes of Karl after the war? He will be repatriated, but to what, and when? The story does not answer this. He is nineteen. The future is very open and very unknown.

---

## Continuity Notes
- Karl is 19 years old at story opening (October 1943); in March 1945 he is 20 or just about to turn 20. Born approximately 1924–1925.
- Friedrich Reinmar: dead, December 28, 1944. Karl received the Red Cross notification in January (Chapter 26 establishes this from Hannah's POV). This chapter is the first time the novel is inside Karl's head about it.
- Regensburg's bombing: historically accurate. The USAAF bombed Regensburg multiple times targeting the Messerschmitt aircraft factory. By December 1944, Allied bombing of German cities was extensive. Karl knows this; the cathedral spire's survival is genuinely uncertain in his imagination.
- *Heimweh* vs. *bleiben*: Karl thinks in German. His interior is rendered in English but is understood to be native German; occasional German words that have no direct English equivalent are the texture of his thinking. *Heimweh* is used because it is the word he expected to feel; *bleiben* is used because it is the word he doesn't know how to apply to himself.
- The harbor from the ridge: this is the October night (Chapter 23). He has been thinking about it since. It was significant to him not romantically but geographically — it was the first time the world was as large as it is.
- "He has not asked her why she did it and she has not explained": Karl's understanding of Hannah's gift. Neither of them has named the exchange. This is consistent with Hannah's interior in Chapter 23 (she chose it; she doesn't explain it).
- The exceptional chapter is short (1,200–1,800 words). It should feel like a single night's thinking — not structured like a scene with a clear action sequence, but like the experience of lying in a cold bunk in March, going over what you have and what you've lost.
- Karl's postwar fate: deliberately unresolved. The Drafter should not hint at a resolution — not return, not staying, not Hannah, not any particular outcome. The novel ends before his future is determined.
- Voice note: Karl's narration is not reported in translation. It is rendered in third-person limited English that is close to his sensibility — concrete, specific to physical objects and places, not philosophical or literary. He thinks in things: the tile floor, the spire, the bunk creak. His interior vocabulary is the butcher's apprentice's vocabulary, not the scholar's.
