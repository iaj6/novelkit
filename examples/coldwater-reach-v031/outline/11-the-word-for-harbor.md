# Chapter 11 — The Word for Harbor

**One-line summary**: Hannah leaves word-cards at the fence; Karl tells her he has never seen the sea; she plants the idea without planning to.

**POV**: Hannah Pfeiffer  
**Tense**: Third-person limited, past  
**Setting**: The Pfeiffer farm: barn, fence line between the barn and the icehouse  
**Time**: February 1944

---

## Goal
Hannah wants to communicate with Karl in a way that neither Lonzo's presence nor the limits of their shared language can entirely prevent. She has found a method: word-cards.

## Obstacle
Every exchange has to happen within sight of Lonzo Reed, which means it must be deniable — a farm task, a handed object, a moment's pause that looks like something else. Karl's English is growing but slowly; their common ground is German, and speaking German with him in front of Lonzo feels like a thing she cannot fully explain.

---

## Action Beats

- The word-cards began three weeks ago, in January. Hannah cuts small rectangles from the cedar planking of old butter boxes — the kind her father saves for kindling. She writes in pencil, one word per card, English on one side and German on the other. She started with objects: *barn / Scheune. gate / Tor. bucket / Eimer. rope / Seil.* She leaves them on the fence post nearest the icehouse, under a stone.
- Karl picks them up. He returns them — she finds them back on the post by the next afternoon, the English side worn from handling. He has been memorizing.
- February: she makes a new card. She writes *harbor / Hafen* and stands for a moment looking at it before she puts it under the stone. She is thinking about the man who drowned and the harbor where the body came in — not that she knows anything specific, only what the town talk has reached her: a man, unidentified, the breakwater.
- That afternoon Karl comes to the fence with the feed bucket — he has adopted this routine; she has let him. He pulls the card from his pocket: *harbor / Hafen.* He says the English word carefully, testing it. Then says in German: *Was ist das, ein Hafen, hier?* (What is that, a harbor, here?)
- She says, in German: *Ja. Drei Meilen.* Three miles. She points roughly south toward the coast.
- He says, in German, with the particular careful formality he uses when saying something important: *Ich habe das Meer nie gesehen.* (I have never seen the sea.)
- She looks at him. She believes him. He is from Regensburg, ninety kilometers inland, up the Danube from Munich. Tunisia was his only coast.
- She does not say: I could show you. She does not say it because she doesn't know yet whether she means it. She takes the bucket and goes to the barn.
- For the rest of the afternoon she thinks about what it means that a person can live nineteen years and never see the sea. Rolf grew up here and was eighteen when he left for the Pacific coast, then North Africa. She walked to the harbor by herself at nine years old, without asking permission, and sat on the breakwater until the tide came in and soaked her shoes. She thinks about what the harbor looks like in February — the ice at the north end, the lobster boats, the smell of diesel and brine and the cold that comes off the water. She has never thought of it as something you could be denied.
- At dinner she asks her father, in German: *Hast du das Meer gesehen, bevor du nach Amerika kamst?* (Did you see the sea before you came to America?) Her father looks at her. He says: *Erst in Hamburg.* (Not until Hamburg.) She nods. She does not ask more.

---

## Outcome / Turn
Karl has said he has never seen the sea; Hannah has registered this as a fact she is responsible for knowing. She has not yet decided what to do with it, but it has been filed.

## New Question or Pressure Raised
Will Hannah take Karl to see the harbor? What would it cost, and what would it mean, and who would know?

---

## Continuity Notes
- Word-cards: butter box cedar planking, pencil, English one side / German the other. These are the object that mediates Hannah and Karl's exchange through winter. The Drafter should use them as a recurring physical detail.
- Karl's English as of February 1944: growing from zero; he knows some words from the other POWs, from the farm (tools, animal names, commands), and now from the cards. He is not yet conversational.
- Karl from Regensburg: ninety kilometers inland, up the Danube. Bayarian, not coastal. He was captured in Tunisia (May 1943) but Tunisia is not the same as seeing the sea — a detail for the Drafter to handle as they see fit.
- *Ich habe das Meer nie gesehen*: this line should land with weight; it is one of the novel's hinge lines. It plants the possibility that Hannah acts on in Chapter 23.
- Albrecht's *Erst in Hamburg* — he first saw the sea when he emigrated, in 1922, boarding the ship. This is consistent with Straubing being an inland Bavarian town. It also makes Hannah and her father, briefly, of the same mind about something, which is rare.
- The body on the breakwater is present in this chapter only as town talk filtered to Hannah — she does not know details, only that there was "a man on the breakwater." This is sufficient; do not over-explain.
- Lonzo Reed's presence: he is always there but his attention varies. He does not hear the German exchange in this chapter or, if he does, he does not respond to it.
