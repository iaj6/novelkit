# Chapter 14 — Mud Season

**One-line summary**: Hannah turns 18; V-Mail arrives from Rolf in Italy; Marcus's lesson on the word *mündig* plants a seed about what it means to speak for yourself.

**POV**: Hannah Pfeiffer  
**Tense**: Third-person limited, past  
**Setting**: Pfeiffer farmhouse; Coldwater Reach High School; the road between them  
**Time**: April 1944 — Hannah's birthday, April 14

---

## Goal
Hannah wants — though she couldn't name it exactly — to feel what being eighteen means, whether it is different from seventeen.

## Obstacle
The farm demands don't change because the calendar does. Her parents' silences don't lift for birthdays. The mud of April makes everything harder. What she hoped might feel like arrival feels more like continuation.

---

## Action Beats

- April 14. The farm in mud season — the thaw has turned every path into a sucking ankle-deep problem; the cows are restless to get to pasture but the ground won't hold them yet; the barn smells of winter that has gone on too long. Hannah is awake at 5 as always.
- Breakfast: her mother sets a plate with a piece of cake on it — cardboard-pale, no icing because there's no sugar, made with the last of the powdered milk and a tablespoon of something sweet enough to count. It is a cake in the way that the farm in mud season is a farm: basically itself, without any of its usual ornament. Her father looks at it. He says: *Herzlichen Glückwunsch, Hannchen.* He calls her Hannchen — the diminutive he has used since she was small, which she is too old for and he is still using.
- She says: *Danke, Vater.*
- At school: German class before noon. Marcus Klein is teaching. Today the lesson is on legal and civic vocabulary — words of adulthood, of civic participation. He writes *mündig* on the board and says, in the way he says things he finds genuinely interesting: "This word. It is the state of being of age, of having majority. But look at its root: *Mund.* Mouth. To be *mündig* is literally to have your own mouth — to speak for yourself, without a guardian speaking in your place."
- Hannah writes this in the margin of her notebook: *mündig — having your own mouth.*
- After school she stops at the fence. Karl is post-driving. She says, in English: "Today I am eighteen." She has not planned to say this; it arrives.
- He looks up. He takes in the sentence in his slowly-developing English. He says, in English, correctly: "I see." Then, in German: *Glückwunsch.* He says it without ceremony, the way you say congratulations to someone you mean it for.
- She walks home.
- The V-Mail from Rolf is on the table when she arrives — Ada Gile delivered it while Hannah was at school. The letter is short: he is well; Italy is difficult to describe; he is sorry to miss her birthday; the potatoes should have come up already if the weather is right; give his love to Mutti and Vati. He writes her name in his labored hand — he was never as good at the written word as she is — and at the end he writes *Alles Gute, dein Rolf.* All the best, your Rolf.
- Her mother takes the letter from the table and reads it without expression, then holds it in her lap for a long moment, then folds it and puts it back on the table.
- Her father takes it next. He holds it for a long time. He does not unfold it again.
- That evening Hannah writes back. She describes the mud, the cake, the potatoes (not up yet, but maybe next week). She does not describe the word *mündig*. She does not describe what Karl said.
- She puts on Rolf's coat and steps outside. April cold, but a different cold from February — the air has something else in it, the beginning of something. She stands in the yard and thinks about *mündig*. Having your own mouth. She has been practicing, she thinks, for eighteen years.

---

## Outcome / Turn
Hannah has turned 18. The birthday is quiet and undramatic. But *mündig* has been planted — the idea that adulthood is about speaking for yourself. What she will speak for, and to whom, is the rest of her arc.

## New Question or Pressure Raised
Rolf is in Italy, alive. When will that change? What does Hannah do with the voice she is now, technically, old enough to use?

---

## Continuity Notes
- Hannah's birthday: April 14, 1944. Canon fact #8. She turns 18.
- Rolf Pfeiffer: alive, in Italy, as of April 1944. V-Mail from Italy, censored. He is a warm presence through the letters, not a narrative actor.
- *Mündig*: from *Mund* (mouth). Means "of legal age," "having majority." Marcus teaches this as part of civic vocabulary; the etymological point is genuine and Marcus's engagement with it is real, not pedagogical performance. The word lands in Hannah because of when she hears it.
- *Herzlichen Glückwunsch, Hannchen*: Albrecht uses the diminutive. Hannah is too old for it; he uses it anyway. This is the tenderness he doesn't know how to express otherwise.
- Karl's response — "I see" in English, then *Glückwunsch* in German — is a careful small acknowledgment. He is not performing emotion; he is being accurate.
- Magda's response to Rolf's letter: she holds it without speaking. Her grief for what was lost (her brother, Germany, the farm she knew) is registered as posture and action — the holding — not as interior description.
- The V-Mail system: letters are photographed, shipped on film, printed at destination. By the time Rolf's letter arrives it is ten days to two weeks old. Hannah knows this and compensates in her replies.
- The potatoes as a recurring Rolf/Hannah touchstone: practical, specific, farm-grounded. Rolf is not romantic about his absence; he asks about potatoes.
