# Chapter 20 — Addendum

**One-line summary**: Eira writes a complete account of what she saw on October 9, 1943 — and files it, unforwarded, in Owen's chart drawer.

**POV**: Eira Bowman  
**Tense**: Third-person limited, past  
**Setting**: Harbor office; briefly, the anchorage  
**Time**: September 1944

---

## Goal
Eira wants to make the record honest — not for external purposes but for her own. She cannot continue to work from a log with a known omission in it.

## Obstacle
Making the record honest means making her own failure visible, even if only to herself. The act of writing it down is not costless.

---

## Action Beats

- A slow September morning. The harbor quiet — the summer traffic has thinned; the lobster boats are still going out but the summer coasters have moved to shorter runs. Leaves are beginning on the ridge above town. Eira is at her desk by seven.
- She takes out a sheet of official harbor office stationery. She puts the date at the top — not October 9, 1943, but today's date, September 1944 — and writes: *Addendum to Incident Report, October 13, 1943 (Unidentified Decedent, Breakwater).*
- She writes what she saw on the night of October 9, in the order she saw it:
  - She was on the dock at approximately 0130 hours, checking the *Macomber*'s mooring bridle, which she believed to be at risk of chafing on the bollard in the first gusts of the incoming blow.
  - At the end of the dock, on the public portion of the pier (not the private vessel berths), she observed a man she did not recognize. He was standing still, facing the anchorage. He carried a canvas bag, over-the-shoulder style. He appeared to be in good physical condition, dressed in maritime working clothes.
  - She did not approach the man. She heard a sound from the mooring — she believed it was the bridle under strain — and turned to attend to it.
  - When she completed her inspection of the mooring (approximately five minutes), she looked back to the end of the pier. The man was not visible. She assumed he had taken shelter in the pier shed, which was unlocked.
  - She returned to the harbor office. She did not enter the observation in the vessel traffic log. She did not approach the pier shed to confirm the man's presence.
  - On October 13, 1943, a body was recovered from the far end of the breakwater. She has reason to believe it was the same man.
- She reads the addendum back. It is accurate. She is writing in the same register she uses for all official harbor records: precise, without modifier, no editorializing. *She did not approach the man.* There it is.
- She looks at what she has written for a long time.
- She does not attach it to the official incident report file. She does not address it to the Coast Guard. She folds it in three, the way she folds documents for the pending drawer.
- She opens the chart cabinet. The pending drawer — Owen's drawer — still has his handwriting on the two files at the back: *Lighthouse Service correspondence, 1934* and *Tidal survey, pending, 1935.* She puts the folded addendum in front of them. She closes the drawer.
- She goes outside. The harbor is flat in the September morning. She walks to the end of the dock and looks at the anchorage. Two lobster boats at mooring; the barge at anchor. The buoy tender comes on Friday.
- She goes back inside and makes coffee. This time she drinks it.

---

## Outcome / Turn
The record is complete — but only in the drawer. Eira has not sent it; she has not filed it officially; she has not absolved herself. She has made the thing exist in writing, which is different from making it public. The coffee detail closes the chapter: she drinks it this time.

## New Question or Pressure Raised
Will she ever forward the addendum? Who will read it if she does? Does making it honest — in private — resolve anything, or only defer the question?

## Continuity Notes
- The addendum: written on harbor office stationery, September 1944. Contents: Eira's account of the night of October 9, 1943. Filed in Owen's pending drawer in the chart cabinet. Not forwarded to Coast Guard.
- The addendum's details must be consistent with everything established in earlier chapters: mooring check at 0130; canvas bag; end of the pier; five-minute inattention; man not visible when she returned; pier shed unlocked. All of these details appear here fully articulated for the first time. They must not contradict anything in Chapters 01 or 04.
- Owen's drawer: contains *Lighthouse Service correspondence, 1934* and *Tidal survey, pending, 1935* — files he was working on when he drowned. They have been there for nine years. Eira puts her addendum in front of them; she does not remove his files.
- "This time she drinks it": the coffee detail (see Chapters 07 and 13, where she makes coffee and doesn't drink it). The reversal is the signal that something has settled — not resolved, but placed where it belongs.
- The addendum will be filed in the permanent record in Chapter 29, when Eira moves it from the pending drawer. The arc: written September 1944 → filed April 1945.
- September 1944: after D-Day (June), after the inspector's July visit. Eira now has the name August Schell; the addendum refers to "the unidentified man" because she is writing it in terms of the official 1943 incident, before she formally connects him to the name. This is technically correct and also characteristic of her precision.
