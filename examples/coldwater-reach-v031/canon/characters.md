# Characters

---

## POV Characters

### Eira Bowman
**Age**: 52  
**Role**: Harbormaster of Coldwater Reach; POV character, ~10 chapters  
**Background**: Born and raised in Coldwater Reach. Married Owen Bowman at twenty-four; no children. Owen was harbormaster from 1921 until he drowned in the November storm of 1935. Eira was appointed to the post in his place — the town selectmen had no other candidate willing to take it. She has held it for eight years by the time the story opens, long enough that her competence is assumed and her presence still, occasionally, resented.  
**Inner drives**: Eira needs to hold the post not for symbolic reasons but because the harbor is her actual competence and she has no other place in the world she knows as well. She is not a crusader. She did not campaign for her position; she stepped into a vacancy and refused to step out. Her grief for Owen is real and old and has settled into the architecture of her daily work — she notices it in the chart cabinet she hasn't reorganized, in the way she reaches for a second cup of coffee before remembering Owen is not coming in to share it. What frightens her is not the body on the breakwater but what she saw the night before, and what it means about her responsibility. Authority to her is not a prize; it is a weight. She holds it because putting it down would be a kind of death.  
**Voice**: Spare, procedural, observant. She names what she sees: draft and trim of a vessel, state of the tide, the particular sound a mooring line makes when ice is forming at the splice. Interior monologue is rare and compressed. Dialogue is clipped; she does not explain herself if actions can speak. Emotion surfaces as notation, not reflection — she registers a feeling the way she registers a change in barometric pressure, with professional attention but no editorializing.  
**Relationships**: Widowed; no children. Her closest professional relationship is with the Lighthouse Service buoy tender crew who put in twice monthly. She knows Ada Gile the postmistress and Lonzo Reed by sight and working necessity. She encounters Marcus Klein at a civil defense warden meeting early in the story. She does not know Hannah Pfeiffer until the story forces their paths to cross.  
**Physical detail**: Hands roughened by weather and rope. Wears her husband's oilskin coat — the only one in the house that fits the work. Grey in her hair since her thirties; she has not troubled about it.  
**Arc**: The harbormaster's post was given to her by default; she must decide, across eighteen months and under accumulating pressure from the body's aftermath, whether she holds it on her own terms or hands it back on someone else's.

### Hannah Pfeiffer
**Age**: 17 (turns 18 during the story)  
**Role**: Dairy farm resident; POV character, ~10 chapters  
**Background**: Born in Coldwater Reach; her parents Albrecht and Magda Pfeiffer immigrated from Bavaria in 1922 when Hannah was not yet conceived. She is first-generation American, fluent in English and functionally fluent in German (she understands nearly everything spoken to her but speaks it haltingly, with the grammar her father used before he stopped speaking English). She attends Coldwater Reach High School, where Marcus Klein is one of her teachers.  
**Inner drives**: Hannah is at the age where identity is not yet fixed and therefore feels every influence as a possible definition of self. She is drawn to Karl Reinmar not romantically at first but with the fascination of someone hearing a part of her family's silence speak — he is from near where her father grew up, and he can tell her things her father won't. She is not naïve about the war; she knows where Germany is and what it has done. What she is deciding, across the story, is what she does with the small choices adolescence gives her, and whether the person she is assembling can live with the choices she makes.  
**Voice**: The most porous of the three registers. Closer to the noise of interior consciousness; she follows a thought where it goes, doubles back, revises. German phrases arrive in her narration where her father's voice has shaped her — not translated, used. The farm is her whole world and she knows it with the exactness of someone who has walked the same ground ten thousand times. Her narration notices smells, sounds, textures — the cold of the milk cans, the sound of the cows at 5 a.m., the particular silence when her mother has been crying.  
**Relationships**: Albrecht Pfeiffer (father — remote, German-speaking at home, frightened in a way he cannot express and therefore expresses as silence and rigidity); Magda Pfeiffer (mother — the silence of a woman whose grief for what was lost in Germany has curdled into inertia); Karl Reinmar (the youngest POW, nineteen, from near Albrecht's hometown — becomes a complicated friendship); Lonzo Reed (the Coastal Patrol guard — old, tired, decent, increasingly abstracted from his duty); Marcus Klein (her German and Latin teacher, a figure of complicated respect).  
**Physical detail**: Tall for the period, farm-strong. Dark hair worn in a braid she's been doing herself since she was twelve. Her winter coat is two sizes too large; it was her brother Rolf's before he enlisted.  
**Arc**: From the beginning of the story to its end, Hannah makes a series of small choices — each one hinging on what she owes whom, and what she keeps from whom. By the end she is not the same person who watched the POWs arrive in November 1943. Whether she is better or worse is not the story's question; the story's question is what she chose.

### Dr. Marcus Klein
**Age**: 47  
**Role**: German and Latin teacher, Coldwater Reach High School; POV character, ~10 chapters  
**Background**: Born Vienna, 1896. Academic career in philology and comparative literature, interrupted by the Anschluss in March 1938. Fled to Paris; then London after the fall of France; enrolled on the Emergency Committee in Aid of Displaced Foreign Scholars rolls at Cambridge, Massachusetts, in autumn 1940. Interned briefly at Sherbrooke, Quebec (Camp N, for enemy aliens), released and cleared after nine months; the experience is not discussed and barely thought about in direct terms, though it shapes everything. Hired at Coldwater Reach High School in March 1942 to replace an instructor who had enlisted. He is Jewish, though this is not announced and he does not volunteer it — in Coldwater Reach, what people notice is that he is foreign, or German-seeming (despite being Austrian), or both.  
**Inner drives**: Marcus's central question is whether displacement is a permanent condition or a temporary one that ends when one finds the place where one fits. He suspects it is permanent; he is not sure he has fully accepted that. He chose the smallest and most remote possible posting at the Emergency Committee's suggestion, and he is not sure whether this was humility, fear, or wisdom. His relationship to language — his tool, his profession, his national identity in a place where nation is suddenly catastrophe — is the engine of his narration. He is precise with words because imprecision has cost people their lives; it cost some people he knew theirs.  
**Voice**: Precise, formally constructed sentences with occasional syntactic inversions that reveal his mother tongue. He translates his own thinking in real time — a word will arrive in German before its English equivalent, and the English sentence he produces has been slightly adjusted for the transit. His narration is the most intellectually explicit of the three, but not emotionally distant — the formality is a container, not a wall.  
**Relationships**: Boards with Henry and Lillian Crow — the household where he is guest rather than specimen, and where his friendship with Henry is the closest thing he has to belonging in Coldwater Reach. Hannah Pfeiffer is his student; he recognizes in her something of the territory between languages, though he does not say so. He attends a civil defense warden meeting where he meets Eira Bowman; encounters with her are infrequent but weighted. The school board treats him with a suspicion that has not risen to action but may.  
**Physical detail**: Slight, precise in his movements. Wears the same suit on Monday, Wednesday, and Friday — his good suit — and a different, older one on Tuesday and Thursday. Reads in English but annotates in German. His handwriting is the old Viennese Kurrent script; he has trained himself to print for the blackboard.  
**Arc**: Marcus arrives in Coldwater Reach still in transit, emotionally. The question is whether he can stop arriving. The body on the breakwater and its aftermath — touching his particular expertise in ways he cannot quite ignore — forces him to decide how much he is willing to invest in a place, and a future, that may not hold.

## Secondary Characters

### Henry Crow
**Age**: 56  
**Role**: Chief custodian, Coldwater Reach High School; occasional POV (1–2 exceptional chapters)  
**Background**: Born Norfolk, Virginia. Came north with his wife Lillian in 1924 during the Great Migration's second wave, following a cousin who had found work at a Bangor shipyard. Settled in Coldwater Reach because the rent was affordable and Henry found custodial work at the school. Has been at the school nineteen years; knows every pipe, every window latch, every leak before it starts. His relation to the town is that of a man who has made himself indispensable without being invited to become visible.  
**Voice (if chapter)**: Measured, dry, not given to complaint. He notices racial dynamics with accuracy and without melodrama. His narration does not explain itself for outside audiences — he speaks from inside his own life.  
**Relationship to Marcus**: He took Marcus in because the rooming house on Reach Road turned Marcus away and Henry knew what that meant. The friendship between them is one of the better-drawn things in the novel: two men who each know what it is to be tolerated rather than welcomed, sharing a household, playing chess on Thursday evenings.

### Lillian Crow
**Age**: 53  
**Role**: Homemaker; co-landlady to Marcus Klein  
**Background**: Born Norfolk, Virginia. Married Henry in 1919. Runs the household with attention and economy. Gardened through the first three years of rationing by converting the back lot to vegetables. She is the person in Coldwater Reach who most consistently treats Marcus as a person rather than a category, and she does it without ceremony — by feeding him, noting when he is unwell, asking his opinion on things she actually wants his opinion on.  
**Voice**: Warm, direct, not demonstrative. She does not perform welcome; she delivers it.

### Albrecht Pfeiffer
**Age**: 58  
**Role**: Hannah's father; dairy farmer  
**Background**: Left Bavaria (town of Straubing, near the Danube) in 1922. The reasons he left are not simple and are not fully disclosed in the narrative — something to do with the politics of the period (early Nazi agitation, a relative's entanglement). He came because a cousin in Machias had land. He has been a successful small farmer for twenty years, but the war has made him a man who no longer knows what country he belongs to. He has stopped speaking English at home not out of hostility but out of a kind of withdrawal — German is the only language in which he can be fully himself, and he is protecting what remains. He does not understand Karl Reinmar as a threat; he understands Karl as proof of something he has not named.  
**Voice in dialogue**: His English is correct but slightly formal, with word order that occasionally inverts in the German way. He does not speak phonetically; he speaks as a man who learned English as an adult and uses it carefully.

### Magda Pfeiffer
**Age**: 54  
**Role**: Hannah's mother  
**Background**: Also Bavarian; came with Albrecht in 1922. Has stopped speaking, largely, since news arrived from Germany in 1942 about her youngest brother — killed on the Eastern Front in June of that year, fighting for the country she had left. She tends the house with the precision of a person who has turned all available feeling toward tasks. Her grief is not a subject in the narrative; it is a climate.

### Karl Reinmar
**Age**: 19  
**Role**: German POW on the Pfeiffer farm work detail  
**Background**: From Regensburg, Bavaria — approximately fifty kilometers from Straubing. Conscripted into the Wehrmacht in 1942; captured in Tunisia in May 1943 during the North Africa surrender. He arrived at the Maine satellite work detail in October 1943. He is not a fanatic and was never a volunteer — he was a butcher's apprentice who was drafted. He speaks no English when he arrives; by late 1944 he has functional English and is, by the terms of the Geneva Convention, permitted limited off-duty movement near the farm.  
**Role in the story**: His proximity to Hannah's father's origins makes him both a conduit and a complication. He gives Hannah access to the Germany her father won't talk about, and he gives Albrecht a proof — he is not yet sure of what.

### Lonzo Reed
**Age**: 61  
**Role**: Coastal Patrol private assigned to the Pfeiffer POW work detail; occasional POV (possible)  
**Background**: Born in Coldwater Reach; worked the lobster boats until the winch accident. The Coastal Patrol assignment is a way of being useful when he is otherwise not fit for service. He is not a harsh man. He is not a careless man. He is a tired man with a bad knee who sits in a folding chair by the icehouse in all weather, rifle across his knees, and who has started, by the winter of 1944, bringing a flask.  
**Role in the story**: Lonzo is the permeability in the system — the watch that can be, and eventually is, not entirely watched.

### Ada Gile
**Age**: 63  
**Role**: Postmistress, Coldwater Reach  
**Background**: Has been postmistress since 1928. She knows the town's business by trade; she observes but does not usually volunteer what she observes. She delivers (or causes to be delivered) the casualty telegrams, which makes her a figure in whom information and grief concentrate.
