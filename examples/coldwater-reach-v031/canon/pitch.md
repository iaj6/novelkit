# Pitch

Coldwater Reach, Maine. Autumn 1943. A man's body washes up on the breakwater after a three-day blow — no papers, no name, no one who will claim him. The Coast Guard takes the body. The question stays.

Three people end up carrying it, separately and without plan: **Eira Bowman**, fifty-two, the town's harbormaster and the only woman in that post on the Maine coast, who knows what she saw on the dock the night before the body came in; **Hannah Pfeiffer**, seventeen, whose Bavarian-born parents have gone silent in their different ways while four German POWs work the family farm under thin supervision, one of them from the town her father grew up near; and **Dr. Marcus Klein**, forty-seven, a Viennese refugee teaching German and Latin at the high school, who arrived by way of Paris, London, and an internment camp in Quebec and has not yet decided whether he has arrived anywhere at all.

The story runs eighteen months, thirty chapters, three rotating points of view. The war is not an event — it is the medium the story moves through: the ration books, the V-Mail, the casualty telegrams, the radio voices, the missing men, the men who came back wrong. What each of the three carries is not the mystery but the weight of the knowing. Their individual arcs — Eira's fight to hold authority in a post the town never meant to give her; Hannah's slow accumulation of choices that will define her character before she understands what character is; Marcus's reckoning with whether displacement ever ends — unfold beside and through each other across the second half of America's war, and settle, smaller than the war, into a spring that ends before VE Day does.
