## 03-puna
**Summary:** Isobel's POV chapter. The party crosses out of the selva alta onto the open puna, five days after the gorge crossing. At a herder encampment (~13,000 ft), an older woman confirms that Riedel's party passed through nine days ago — not twelve — and that she gave them nothing useful about the old places above. The woman then recognizes Isobel's connection to her dead father, Reverend Thomas Carrow, describing how he had visited the valley communities over many decades, asking about Rititambo on behalf of those communities rather than himself. The woman glances at Mariano when she mentions "the right people" Carrow told. Isobel does not translate this exchange for Holroyd. That evening, Isobel opens her father's oilskin case and reads, for the first time, the three-layered marginalia of his personal copy of Arce's 1573 account — written across three decades in English, Spanish, then Quechua. She discovers he was not retreating into obsession, as she had believed, but building a community-facing concordance: cross-referencing Arce's observations against oral testimony from twelve named valley communities, creating a document whose value was for those communities, not for European scholarship. The last entry in his hand is a Quechua phrase: Rikhuriq. Hamuq, kutimunqa — the witness who comes, and will return. Her grief, formless since Pisac, begins to take shape. In a brief, closing exchange, Mariano tells her, in Quechua: "Your father was careful" — and she understands he means Carrow was careful with whose knowledge it was.
**New facts:**

- Carrow's annotated copy of Arce's account is in Isobel's possession in an oilskin case, with dense marginalia in three languages (English, Spanish, Quechua) representing his thirty-year project.
- The marginalia lists twelve named witnesses from valley communities, each with a location, date, and cross-reference to a passage in Arce's account.
- The final entry in Carrow's marginalia is a Quechua phrase only: Rikhuriq. Hamuq, kutimunqa — the witness who comes, and will return.
- Carrow's work was an act of restitution toward the valley communities, not personal displacement — he was building a record the communities could use as corroboration on their own terms.

## 05-the-storm
**Summary:** Holroyd's party reaches 17,000-ft high camp on a flat snow shelf in the lee of a granite spur and rigs the improvised tent (hardwood branches, alpine rope) as the cirrus knits into a solid overcast. They eat from the last sardine cans and the chuño paste, and Holroyd takes aspirin for the altitude headache that has lodged behind his eyes since 14,000 feet. Before dark, his binoculars show the German camp 400 meters south-southwest: better-quality tent, fully up, at slightly lower elevation. At 9 PM the wind shifts from south to northwest and rises sharply — canvas hammering, the branch brace bowing to 8-9 degrees, outside temperature minus 22°C. They stand watches in three shifts; during Holroyd's 3 AM watch the storm is at its worst and he sits with one hand flat against the canvas peak, holding counter-pressure in the dark. Around 4 AM the wind withdraws eastward. In the silence Isobel says she can't get another hour; neither can he. At 5:30 AM Holroyd scans the German camp through binoculars: the purpose-built tent is partially collapsed, and three figures are visible where he expected four. Fischer is lying in the snow, right hand wrapped in improvised cloth, fingers not working. Agustín crouches over him. Riedel stands forty meters upslope looking at the mountain and then at Fischer — the sequence twice, in the same order — then returns, hands Agustín the load rope, and watches Fischer and Agustín begin the descent south toward the puna. The camp comes down in six minutes. They move in twenty-eight. At the first steep slope Mariano buckles on crampons — no announcement, no prior mention; Holroyd notes this and says nothing. ## 06-rititambo
**Summary:** Before dawn, Holroyd's party climbs the snowfield northeast toward the bowl entrance. Mariano leads, setting a steady pace; Isobel chews coca and climbs upright; Holroyd counts steps in fifties to keep his mind off the thin air. At the rock step below the cliff face, Mariano front-points his crampons up the verglas face — the same face that turned him back alone in 1933 — anchors a fixed rope, and calls the others up. The passage through the cliff fault proves to be angled slightly downward, which Holroyd did not expect. He moves through it in a crouch with his pack in front, the light ahead changing in quality, and then the passage opens into the bowl. Rititambo: a natural amphitheater roughly 200 metres across, snow-filled to second-story level on the east wall, six connected building sections in a horseshoe arrangement, all doorways facing inward. The walls are intact — grey-blue granite, no mortar, fitted with a precision that makes the joints nearly invisible. Trapezoidal doorways taller than Inca convention. No Inca iconography anywhere on the walls. One relief carving above the central doorway: non-figurative, geometric, not immediately interpretable. The silence absorbs sound. It smells of nothing. Mariano goes directly to the center and stands on the stone floor beneath the snow; he does not move. Isobel stands at the threshold of the central doorway and looks in. Holroyd lets himself simply receive what is in front of him, then takes out the Leica and begins systematic documentation: six frames — east wall, central doorway, relief carving, bowl sight line, north section with snow level, fitted joint in raking light. At frame six, winding to seven, Isobel holds up one hand. German voices rise from below the passage. Two voices. Moving closer. The chapter ends at the moment confrontation becomes unavoidable.

## 07-what-it-proves
**Summary:** Holroyd's party is in Rititambo when Riedel and Kern arrive through the passage. Riedel stands in the bowl, confirms his long-held belief with controlled vindication, and formally acknowledges Holroyd's survey work and priority. Kern emerges looking wrong — pale, audibly labored — but Riedel says nothing about it. Both parties document the city in professional silence for twenty minutes; Holroyd exposes frames seven through twelve on the Leica. Throughout all of this, Mariano stands motionless at the center of the bowl, bearing witness in the full sense his tradition requires — looking at the walls, the doorways, the proportions, the light — performing the act of testimony he was sent to perform on behalf of his valley community. Riedel finds the sealed chamber at the base of the rear wall, recognizes that the mortar difference means deliberate sealing, photographs it, and produces a pry bar. Holroyd says "No." When Riedel argues the site's significance demands opening the chamber, Isobel steps forward without consulting Holroyd and argues the architectural record directly: the proportions and trapezoidal forms are consistent with Chavin de Huántar and Tiahuanaco, predating the Inca but entirely Andean — Riedel has been using the wrong comparison set, as did Friar Arce, who had never seen those sites. Riedel concedes "You may be right" and immediately says he would still like to open the chamber. Holroyd steps between him and the door. Two feet apart, both rifles registered and neither reached for. At this impasse, Kern slides down the wall and says, slowly and precisely, "I cannot see clearly." Riedel looks at Kern, the sealed door, and Holroyd in sequence. Holroyd says Kern needs four thousand feet lower in the next two hours. Riedel says he knows. A long pause. Then Riedel puts the pry bar back, takes two more methodical Rolleiflex exposures, lifts Kern with a practiced grip, and at the passage entrance delivers his final words: "Don't let them make it what it isn't. Neither will I." — said with complete sincerity by a man who has been doing exactly that. He and Kern go through. Alone, Mariano turns from the wall with the expression of a completed act, says in Spanish "It is enough," and picks up his pack. Isobel asks Holroyd if he wants to open the sealed chamber. He says no — not entirely truthfully, but saying it makes it what he wants. Holroyd checks the frame counter: twelve exposed, twenty-four remaining. He raises the camera to frame thirteen.

- Mariano completes his act of witness in the bowl: standing motionless at the center, looking at the walls, doorways, proportions, and light — performing on behalf of his valley community the testimony of a man who has been somewhere and looked.

## 08-the-descent
**Summary:** The afternoon after Riedel and Kern depart. The three eat the last sardines and coca tea in the bowl; Holroyd completes the photographic survey during his night watch (frames 14–19, frame 19 = Isobel standing in the main doorway for scale); Isobel writes through her watch; Mariano sits motionless at the center of the bowl in the dark for two hours. They break camp at first light and descend: verglas face cramponed face-in, then the long snowfield, the altitude headache clearing at 14,000 ft. At 16,000 ft Holroyd crouches in the snow and holds the Leica — considers opening the back and exposing the film — holds the possibility long enough to be honest about it, then closes the camera and walks on. He decides this is not a hero's refusal; destroying the record is bad accounting, same category as falsifying it. Cleto and Rufino are waiting at 15,000 ft with a fire and real coffee; Rufino shows his bandaged hands to Mariano; Mariano says three words in Quechua and Rufino laughs. Isobel and Mariano speak in Quechua for several minutes while Holroyd checks lashings — he observes the exchange without following it. Three days to the valley floor. At the Yanatile junction, Cleto and Rufino take the mules south. Mariano shakes Holroyd's hand, says the mules are paid through Cusco, and walks south toward his home valley.
