# World

## Setting in Brief

Eastern Cordillera Vilcabamba, southeastern Peru. Late August into September 1938 — the dry season, which on the high puna means wind and frost rather than rain, and above seventeen thousand feet means nothing reliable at all.

---

## The Approach: Three Elevation Zones

**Selva alta (8,000–12,000 ft):** The expedition enters the range from the Yanatile valley. Tree ferns, orchids clamped to mossy bark, the smell of wet soil and rotting bromeliads. Mule trails cut by centuries of use, not wide enough for two animals abreast, edged by nothing on the downhill side. Rope bridges — some built on Inca stonework foundations, some lashed together within living memory — cross the side gorges. The water is always audible. The mist burns off by ten in the morning and returns by three.

**Puna (12,000–16,000 ft):** Tussock grass (ichu), dwarf shrubs barely ankle-high, absolute wind. The sky above the puna is a specific blue — high, thin, and hard — with clouds building over the eastern escarpment by early afternoon. Llamas and alpacas graze here; communities of Quechua-speaking herders live in stone-and-adobe houses low enough to hide from the wind. They know the terrain and they know the mountain. They answer questions with information that is accurate and incomplete.

**Above the snowline (16,000+ ft):** The mules fail here. The cold is not dramatic — it is relentless and specific. It works into the joints; it makes the fingers stupid. The light is extreme: ultraviolet, burning the backs of hands and the undersides of noses, casting shadows that seem slightly wrong. Sound carries differently at altitude — farther, flatter, with a quality that makes everything seem both closer and less real. Altitude degrades judgment slowly enough that the sufferer usually doesn't notice.

## The Peak

The target peak has no name on any survey map — not on the incomplete Peruvian military surveys, not on any British Royal Geographical Society chart. Holroyd's field traverses of the eastern range gave it a working designation: **Survey Mark 7 (SM-7)**. It stands at approximately nineteen thousand feet. The lee face — the east face, sheltered from the westerly weather that scours the main Andean divide — holds a deep natural bowl below the summit ice. The bowl fills with wind-deposited snow carried from the west and accumulates rather than ablates. Almost nothing thaws here. This is where the city is.

---

## The City: Rititambo

Named in the three surviving missionary accounts as *Rititambo* — rendered from Quechua by the Jesuit friar Arce, writing circa 1573. The word approximates "the snow waystation" or "the lodge in the snow." The three accounts agree on the following: it is stone, built against a cliff face at the rear of the bowl; it is accessed by a single passage through the rock; it is older than the Inca. The Inca knew of it and left it alone. Friar Arce reached the outer approach and no further. What he describes — the doorways, the proportions, the absence of sun symbols or anything matching Inca iconography — does not match any Andean tradition he had seen.

At altitude, preserved by cold and altitude: stone walls intact, internal structures frozen rather than decayed, surfaces scoured clean by millennia of dry wind and frost. It smells of nothing. The silence there is not peaceful — it is the silence of an enclosed space that absorbs sound. The scale is slightly larger than Andean convention. The doorways are trapezoidal, as Inca doorways are, but taller. The city does not move. It does not threaten. It is simply there, entirely and completely, and standing in it produces in each observer something that is shaped by what they brought.

## Period and Politics

The year is 1938. By September, Chamberlain will fly to Munich; the world is holding its breath. The Ahnenerbe — the SS ancestral heritage research institute, three years operational — has funded several remote expeditions to find evidence for its mythology of Aryan origins. Its resources are better than the RGS can match openly: more porters, superior equipment, a diplomatic facilitation through the German consulate in Lima that eases customs at Callao. The Riedel expedition has a structural advantage, blunted only by the fact that they have been in the field longer and are not in perfect condition.

Holroyd travels under a Royal Geographical Society letterhead that is worth something in Lima and almost nothing above the treeline. The Society's support is off-the-books: if the expedition fails or becomes an embarrassment, it did not officially happen.

## Specific Equipment and Material Reality

- **Tents:** Oil-and-canvas, framed with sectional alloy poles. Cold enough above base camp to freeze the guy-ropes stiff by morning.
- **Boots:** Hobnailed leather — Tricouni-nailed soles. Grip wet rock; skid on hard ice; destroy the feet slowly on talus.
- **Stoves:** Primus paraffin stoves. Must be pumped hard at altitude. Burn reluctant. A pot of water takes twice as long to boil at seventeen thousand feet and never reaches a full rolling boil.
- **Rope:** Manila hemp. Strong for weight; vulnerable to abrasion and moisture; needs to be inspected daily on a technical climb.
- **Arms:** The German party carries Mauser Karabiner 98k rifles. Holroyd carries a .30-06 sporting rifle of American manufacture — acquired in La Paz in 1935, carried since for necessity more than intent.
- **Camera:** Holroyd's Leica IIIa (c. 1935), 50mm Elmar lens. Carried in a leather case inside his outer jacket, against his body, to keep the mechanism from freezing.
- **Mule loads:** Standard 80-pound ataditos, canvas-wrapped, balanced one per side. A mule in good condition carries this without drama on valley trails; above fourteen thousand feet the loads must be lightened or the animals fail.

---

## Atmosphere

Everything in the approach valleys smells of woodsmoke, mule sweat, and paraffin. Above the snowline it smells of nothing. The contrast is a reliable index of altitude. The cold above sixteen thousand feet produces a particular mental state — not quite hallucination, not quite clarity — in which everything feels both urgently real and slightly provisional. This is the state in which the major choices of the story are made.
