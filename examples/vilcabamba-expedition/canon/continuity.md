# Continuity

Hard facts that must not be contradicted in any later draft, outline, or scene.

1. The year is 1938. The story takes place entirely in Peru — Lima (briefly, in passing), Cusco (in passing), and the eastern Cordillera Vilcabamba. It ends before the Munich Agreement, which occurs in late September 1938 and is not part of the narrative.

2. The three principals are **James Holroyd** (British, 44, surveyor), **Isobel Carrow** (British-Peruvian, 27, interpreter), and **Mariano Quispe** (Peruvian, 38, arriero). All three survive to the end of the story. The phrase "three survivors" in the brief refers to all three of them.

3. Holroyd was blacklisted from the Indian Survey in 1931 following an incident on the Assam-Burma frontier involving the death of a surveying colleague and a disputed field report. He will not explain it. The nature of his culpability — whether he was wrong, overruled, or something worse — is deliberately withheld. No chapter may resolve this definitively.

4. The expedition's target is **Rititambo** — a pre-Inca city referenced in three independent Spanish missionary accounts dated to the 1570s, and in no document since. The name comes from Quechua via the Jesuit friar Arce's transcription: approximately "snow waystation."

5. Rititambo is located on the **east (lee) face** of an unnamed peak designated **SM-7** (Survey Mark 7) by Holroyd's 1934 traverse. SM-7 stands at approximately 19,000 feet. The city sits in a deep natural bowl below the summit ice, filled with wind-deposited snow, which is why it has been preserved and effectively sealed.

6. The German expedition is **Ahnenerbe-sponsored**, led by **Klaus Riedel** (Munich, 36). His party includes two German mountaineers (Fischer and Kern) and a Peruvian guide-interpreter (Agustín).

7. **Riedel is correct about the approach route:** He identified, from friar Arce's account, a tree-line feature that establishes the friar's approach from the north — a conclusion Holroyd missed. This gives Riedel a geographic advantage that is real and acknowledged by Holroyd when it becomes undeniable.

8. **Riedel is wrong about what Rititambo proves:** He expects to find evidence of a non-Andean cultural origin, consistent with Ahnenerbe ideology. This expectation distorts his interpretation of what he finds. He is not a fool; he is a competent man who has let a borrowed conclusion pose as discovery.

9. **Mariano's hidden reason:** Mariano's community oral tradition identifies Rititambo as the withdrawal place of pre-Inca ancestors who refused Inca submission. He is on this expedition to serve as a witness — to stand there and make an accounting on behalf of his community. He does not intend to take anything or destroy anything. **This act resolves in the climax.** It must land there.

10. **The city has no supernatural properties.** It does not move, activate, communicate, or alter behavior through any mechanism other than the physical conditions of altitude, cold, and silence. Its strangeness is entirely material. No draft may introduce a supernatural explanation or suggestion.

11. **Three named set pieces must occur:**  
    (a) A rope-bridge crossing that fails — "under the wrong man." The wrongness is situational: the wrong person is on the bridge at the moment it gives way.  
    (b) A weather event that kills someone or nearly does. The victim may be a secondary character (a member of Riedel's party, a porter) rather than a principal. The three principals survive.  
    (c) A direct confrontation between the Holroyd and Riedel parties at altitude — above 17,000 feet, at or near the city.

12. **The ending is not the discovery of the city.** The discovery occurs before the final chapter. The ending is the descent and the choices each of the three principals makes about what to take from Rititambo (documentation, interpretation, witness, silence) and what to leave. These choices are incompatible with one another and each is defensible.

13. **Riedel's fate** is not fixed in canon. He may survive, be incapacitated, or die — to be determined by the Plotter in service of the ending. He must not be killed off before the altitude confrontation set piece.

14. **POV structure:** Primarily Holroyd. One full chapter in Isobel's POV. One full chapter in Mariano's POV. The climax is in Holroyd's POV. No other POV shifts occur.

15. **Equipment that must appear and must be accurate to 1938:**  
    - Oil-and-canvas tents  
    - Hobnailed leather boots  
    - Primus paraffin stoves  
    - Manila hemp rope  
    - Mauser Karabiner 98k rifles (German party); Holroyd carries a .30-06 sporting rifle  
    - Holroyd's Leica IIIa camera (35mm; carried on his person inside his jacket)  
    - Mule loads in standard 80-pound ataditos  
    - Nylon rope does not exist yet; this matters for any scene involving rope under load.

16. **Isobel's father** (Reverend Thomas Carrow) died six weeks before the expedition departed. His death is recent enough that Isobel has not finished learning the shape of her grief. His belief in Rititambo is part of her reason for being there.

17. The Royal Geographical Society's sponsorship is **officially deniable.** Holroyd has no official backing above the treeline. Dr. Aubrey Finch is waiting in London for a cable.
