# Chapter 2 — Selva Alta

**One-line summary:** Holroyd overrides Mariano's request for two hours to repair the bridge anchor; Rufino is on the bridge when it fails; the mule and two ataditos of critical supplies are lost; Rufino survives, but Holroyd was wrong, and the party knows it.

**POV:** James Holroyd — third-person limited, past tense.

**Setting / Time:** The selva alta on the eastern slopes, 8,000–10,500 feet. Mule trails through cloud forest; a rope-bridge gorge crossing; a camp at approximately 9,500 feet. Three to four days after Chapter 1.

---

## Goal

Move through the cloud forest as fast as the mules will allow, cross the gorge system intact, and reach the transition to puna before the afternoon cloud comes in or a rain event turns the trails to mud.

## Obstacle

The trail conditions are against speed: single-file paths, nothing on the downhill side, perpetual moisture. The bridges are maintained by local communities on their own schedule, not on any expedition's. And Holroyd is impatient — he is trying to close the twelve-day gap and his impatience is a form of bad arithmetic: haste on a wet rope bridge does not shorten the distance to Riedel; it shortens the margin between functioning and not.

---

## Action Beats

**Trail conditions established:**
- The smell of wet soil, rotting bromeliads, the sound of the stream below. The mule trail is cut by centuries of use and shows it: packed earth worn to the substrate, roots exposed as steps and trip hazards, the edge on the downhill side dropping without railing into cloud and noise. Mariano walks in front. The party moves in single file. The mules manage better than Holroyd on the wet roots — he notes this without pleasure.
- The mist burns off by mid-morning to reveal orchids clamped to mossy bark forty feet up. Holroyd does not pause to look at them. He does notice them, and notice that he does not pause.

**Moving and talking (bodies in motion, not face-to-face):**
- Isobel falls into step beside Holroyd on a wider section of trail. She relays a message from a man in the last village — a Quechua farmer who knew her father and asked her to "tell him what you find." She says this without particular color, the way she delivers all practical information. Holroyd asks: "Your father knew this man?" She says: "He knew a great many people in this valley." A pause. "He died six weeks ago." Holroyd: "I know." He says nothing more. She does not expect him to.
- She translates something useful: the farmer also mentioned a German party that came through two weeks ago with four men and more rope than they should need for a survey expedition. They were asking about the northern pastures and the peaks above.

**The bridge:**
- They reach the gorge in early afternoon. The bridge: two anchor posts sunk in Inca stonework foundations, the rest hemp and rough-cut planking. It spans forty feet across a gorge where the river is thirty-five feet below. The bridge is functional — it takes Holroyd's weight when he steps on the first three planks and shakes the side rope. He runs his eye along the underside. The planking looks sound.
- Mariano walks to the downstream anchor post and crouches. He examines a section of the main load rope where it passes through the iron pin at the post base. He finds what he finds without announcing it: the rope fibers are fraying where they contact the iron, water-weakened, perhaps fifteen percent of the rope's load-bearing cross-section still intact. He stands up and says, in Spanish: "Give me two hours. I can replace this section with the spare hemp from my pack." He holds up the rope he means to replace.
- Holroyd calculates: two hours here, which with the afternoon cloud coming means camping on this side of the gorge, which means losing a full day in the gorge system. The gap with Riedel is already twelve days. He looks at the rope. It is frayed, yes, but the bridge held him. He says: "We don't have two hours." He tells Rufino to take the heaviest mule across first — the one with the tent poles and bulk food stores, the atadito that can't be redistributed easily — and to lead it slowly and don't stop at midspan.

**SET PIECE — the bridge fails:**
- Rufino takes the lead rope and steps onto the bridge. Mariano watches without speaking. The mule follows, three feet behind, its weight at about 160 pounds with two full ataditos: 80 pounds per side, canvas-wrapped, balanced. The first ten feet hold. The planking creaks but does not flex unduly.
- At midspan — twenty feet out — the mule's combined weight and movement load the rope in the way walking loads rope differently from standing: a rhythmic pulse, not a static weight. The frayed section at the downstream anchor begins to unwind. The right-side railing plank pulls loose from its hemp lashing at the first stanchion. The next plank follows.
- Rufino is moving when the right side gives. His left hand closes on the remaining left-side rope as the planking under him opens; he swings out and slams into the gorge wall — left shoulder, both knees taking the impact. He hangs, gripping the rope with both hands, feet scrabbling on the rock face. The mule is not fast enough. It goes with the planking. Two ataditos follow it. The sound: a crack like a rifle shot when the main load rope finally pulls through the iron pin, then the rope whipping, then the canvas hitting the water thirty-five feet below and then nothing but the river.
- Mariano has already moved. He is flat on the gorge edge with the spare rope in his hands before the mule hits the water. He has tied a running loop; he lowers it to Rufino's level. Rufino gets his right forearm through the loop. Mariano hauls. Cleto has Mariano's legs, anchoring him. Holroyd stands at the edge and watches Rufino come up the rock face, clumsy, both hands bleeding, both knees cut through his trousers.

**The aftermath:**
- Rufino sits on the ground. He is shaking. His hands are opening and closing around nothing. Isobel is already kneeling beside him, unrolling the medical kit from her pack.
- Holroyd stands at the gorge edge looking at the water, where the canvas is already gone. What went with the mule: the second primus stove, a week's emergency rations, the complete tent pole set. They still have the oil-canvas tent body but the poles are gone; above the snowline they will rig with alpine ropes and whatever hardwood Mariano can find at the treeline. With only one primus stove, they cannot afford any delay above 16,000 feet — a blocked pass, an extra day of weather, anything.
- Holroyd opens his field notebook. He writes the inventory of what was lost: stove, ration quantity, tent poles. He writes the load redistribution he will need to perform. He writes none of this under a heading that names who made the call. He is aware of not writing it, and aware that this awareness is a form of the same bad accounting that destroyed his Survey career — not dishonesty exactly, but the careful selection of what goes in the record.
- He closes the notebook. He says to the group: "We camp here tonight. Rufino, you're light duty tomorrow." He says to Mariano: "Can you rig the tent without poles?" Mariano: "Yes." Nothing more.
- Evening camp: Mariano disappears for forty minutes and returns with four hardwood branches from the lower treeline, cut to length. He has pre-cut them — or someone has; they are too uniform to be fresh. He doesn't explain this. The tent goes up using the branches and spare alpine rope as a ridge-and-brace system. It holds.
- Holroyd eats his ration and does the arithmetic. Two weeks instead of three before they have to turn back. No margin for delays above the snowline. He looks at Isobel across the small fire. She says: "We're still going." It is not a question. He says: "We're still going."

**The crossing, completed:**
- Next morning, Mariano splices the damaged anchor rope with his spare hemp and crosses first, checking each plank. Then Isobel. Then Holroyd. Then Cleto with the two remaining load mules, unloaded — the loads carried by hand. The crossing takes two hours. It is slower than the bridge repair would have been.

---

## Outcome / Turn

They are across, intact, with three fewer loads and twelve days still behind Riedel. Rufino is functional but slow; his hands will be usable within a week but they're not usable now. The bridge incident has cost them their margin — no delays above the snowline are survivable now against their reduced provisions — and it has established, without being stated, that Holroyd made a wrong call and the party absorbed it. He made no announcement; neither did anyone else. The silence around the fact is its own kind of accounting.

---

## New Question or Pressure Raised

Riedel's party came through the gorge system two weeks ago. Which bridge did they use? If they took the south approach Mariano overruled, they faced a different — possibly worse — gorge section. Are they ahead on the mountain because their equipment is better, or because their route, however different, had its own difficulties? And what does their bridge crossing look like without a Mariano?

---

## Continuity Notes (New Facts Introduced)

- **The second primus stove is lost** — the party now has one primus.
- **Emergency rations are reduced to two weeks** — no delays above 16,000 feet are survivable at the current provision level.
- **The tent poles are lost**; the tent is now rigged with hardwood branch braces and alpine rope, functional but slower to set.
- **Rufino survives** — both hands lacerated, both knees cut; he can walk but is not at full capacity.
- The bridge failure is caused by a fraying anchor rope at the downstream iron pin. Mariano identified it and Holroyd overrode him.
- One mule and two 80-lb ataditos are lost in the gorge river.
- The rope bridge used a hemp load rope through an iron pin in Inca stonework anchor posts — standard regional construction.
