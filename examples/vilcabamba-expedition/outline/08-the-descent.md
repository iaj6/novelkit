# Chapter 8 — The Descent

**One-line summary:** The three spend one night in the bowl and descend; at the valley floor they separate, each carrying a different and incompatible version of what they found; Holroyd walks toward Cusco with the Leica inside his jacket and no clean answer yet.

**POV:** James Holroyd — third-person limited, past tense. The coda. The pace opens slightly from the climax chapters; there is room for observation and the body's returning capacity for thought as altitude decreases.

**Setting / Time:** The bowl, then the descent through high snowfield, puna, selva alta, and back to the Yanatile valley. Early September 1938. The descent takes three days; the chapter compresses the middle portion and dwells on the separations.

---

## Goal

Get down intact. Make, in the act of descending, the choices that will survive the altitude's clearing. Send Finch a cable that is accurate.

## Obstacle

The physical descent is dangerous in ways different from the ascent: tired bodies, tired rope, the terrain familiar but the body's reflexes slower. The harder obstacle is that as the altitude drops, the judgment that was compressed under the mountain begins to return — and with it the full weight of the questions that altitude had kept at arm's length. Holroyd must decide what to do with the Leica. Isobel must decide what her father's work means for what she does next. Mariano must bring what he carries home.

---

## Action Beats

**One night in the bowl:**
- The afternoon after Riedel departs. The three eat a meal in the city — hardtack, the last of the canned sardines, coca tea from the single primus. They have been in the bowl for six hours. The afternoon light enters through the passage at a low angle and the shadow line on the east wall moves perceptibly. The stones change colour as the light changes — the grey-blue granite going reddish in the late sun, then grey, then the specific white-grey of high altitude dusk.
- Evening in Rititambo: the cold in the bowl is different from the cold outside. The enclosed air holds a temperature that is not warmer exactly but more still — the wind that would pull heat from skin is absent. The silence increases as the light goes. It absorbs the sound of their movements.
- Holroyd's watch: he moves through the complex with his electric torch, finishing the photographic survey. He begins at frame seven — he now has the bowl to himself, without Riedel's parallel work running alongside his own. He photographs the east wall systematically: full-frame, mid-frame, close on the stonework at the joints. Frame twelve: the sealed chamber door, full frame, the torch casting a raking light across the fitted stone that shows the sealing work at its most precise. Frame thirteen: the relief carving above the central doorway, twice — once at distance for context, once close enough to see the individual lines. Frames fourteen through eighteen: the north building section and its doorways, the sight line through three consecutive openings, the bowl floor where snow has been displaced by foot traffic to show the paving stones underneath.
- He photographs Isobel's standing height in the main doorway — frame nineteen, for scale. She stands in the doorway without a coat, arms at her sides, and the proportions of the opening above her show what the chapter has been saying: the doorway is taller than expected, the scale is slightly larger than Inca, the entrance is made for something. She does not smile. He shoots the frame.
- He sits on the stone floor next to Mariano's sleeping bag and looks at the walls in the torchlight until his watch ends. He does not photograph the sealed chamber again. He does not go to it.
- Isobel's watch: she sits in a doorway facing outward, looking at the bowl. She writes in a notebook — not a report, not a field record, but a first-person account, in her own hand, of what she sees and what her father's marginal notes say and what the herder woman said. She writes: "The city is Andean and it is ancient and it was built by people who are represented by the knowledge that has been kept in this valley." She crosses this out and writes it again, more slowly. The document she is beginning will eventually be longer than anything the RGS would accept or publish. She does not know this yet. She writes to understand rather than to report — her father's method, she recognizes, without saying so even to herself.
- Mariano's watch: the chapter does not enter his interiority here. What can be observed: he sits at the center of the bowl in the dark, the torch off, for two hours. He does not sleep. He is simply present in a place he has been carrying an obligation toward since 1933, and the obligation is complete, and the presence is now its own thing rather than the shape of a debt.

**The descent — the body recovering:**
- They begin the descent at first light, moving in the same order as the ascent: Mariano first on the rope, Isobel in the middle, Holroyd at the rear. The passage outward. The verglas face — more difficult descending, Holroyd's crampons catching in ways they did not on the ascent. He descends facing the rock, which feels wrong, which is correct technique.
- Below the snowline, the body begins to report. The thighs on the long descent. The knees on the switchbacks. The specific sound of hobnailed boots on exposed rock versus on snow — different frequencies, different warnings. Holroyd's headache is fading with each hundred feet of descent. The pressure behind his eyes lightens as if a hand is slowly removing itself.
- The returning judgment: what altitude took from him was not his competence but his tolerance for ambiguity. At 17,900 feet, questions resolve or they stop being questions. At 14,000 feet, the ambiguity comes back, and with it the understanding that Riedel's final sentence — "don't let them make it what it isn't" — was said by a man who came to Peru to make it what it wasn't, and was said with complete sincerity, and was both a warning and a confession simultaneously, and Holroyd cannot make it less than both of those things.

**A private moment with the Leica:**
- At 16,000 feet, on a section of the descent where the snowfield is wide and the others are thirty yards ahead: Holroyd stops. He calls forward: "Compass check." He crouches in the snow with his back to the wind and opens the back of the Leica. The film is there: exposed frames one through nineteen, unexposed frames twenty through thirty-six. The morning light would reach the film if he opened the camera fully. He could expose the roll. Nineteen frames of documentation, gone. He would arrive in Cusco with a used camera and nothing to show the RGS except his field notes and his word.
- He looks at this possibility for the amount of time it takes to be honest about it: enough. Then he closes the camera. He puts it inside his jacket against his chest. He catches up with the others.
- What this moment is not: it is not a hero's choice. It is not a refusal of temptation. It is a man deciding that destroying the record is the same kind of bad accounting as falsifying it, and that he has done enough bad accounting in his career to know what it costs.

**Cleto and Rufino at 15,000 feet:**
- The mules and the two arrieros are where they were supposed to be, within a hundred yards of the agreed camp. Cleto has a fire going. Rufino grins at Mariano and holds up his bandaged hands. Mariano looks at the hands, then at the man. He says something brief in Quechua that Holroyd cannot follow. Rufino laughs.
- Coffee, real coffee, heated over the fire. Not altitude-thin, not primus-reluctant. It tastes like something that exists at sea level. Nobody talks for several minutes.

**Isobel and Mariano's exchange:**
- While Holroyd is checking the mule loads and redistributing for the descent, Isobel and Mariano speak in Quechua. The conversation is unhurried and lasts several minutes. Holroyd can hear the cadence from twenty feet away and cannot follow the words. He does not try. When the conversation ends, they stand together in the particular comfortable silence of two people who have finished saying what needed to be said.

**The valley floor — the separation:**
- The Yanatile valley, three days later. The first settlement — a small market, a hired car Isobel arranged from Cusco. Cleto and Rufino take the mules and continue toward their home village. The three principals stand at the junction of the valley road.
- Mariano says to Isobel, in Quechua, something short. She nods. He says to Holroyd, in Spanish, meeting his eyes: "The mules are paid through Cusco if you need them." He offers his hand. Holroyd shakes it. Mariano walks south — not toward Cusco, toward the valley head and his own village. He does not look back. He has what he came for, and what he came for is not a certificate or a cable or a report. It is somewhere behind his ribs and it is complete. He will walk to his community's settlement, find the woman who keeps the knowledge, and tell her what he saw: the doorways, the proportions, the scale, the silence. He will tell her in person. That is all. It is not a small thing.
- Isobel watches him go. She turns to Holroyd. "He's going to tell someone what he saw." Holroyd: "I know." Isobel: "Not us." Holroyd: "No. Not us." A pause — the pause of a man deciding whether to say the next thing. He says: "Is that all right?" Isobel: "Yes." She picks up her pack. "I'm going to Pisac before Cusco." Holroyd: "Your father's house." Isobel: "Yes." She doesn't explain why, and he doesn't ask; he understands that it is the necessary next place to be. She says: "Do you want me to send Finch's cable?" Holroyd: "I'll send it." She nods and goes south toward the car.

**Holroyd alone at the junction:**
- He stands at the junction for a while. The valley sounds: water, wind in the ichu, a donkey somewhere. The smell of woodsmoke and mule and wet earth. Three weeks ago these smells were the ambient condition of being alive; for three days they were absent; now they are restored and they mean something specific that they did not mean before.
- He opens his field notebook. He writes: "SM-7. East bowl. Rititambo confirmed. Structure as described by Arce (1573), consistent with Andean pre-Inca tradition (cf. Chavin, Tiahuanaco). Origin indigenous Andean. No evidence of non-Andean cultural genesis." He looks at this. He does not cross out any of it. He writes the survey coordinates — approach bearing from the north-northeast, barometric altitude of the bowl floor (17,900 ft.), estimated bowl diameter (200 m.), compass bearings for each building section. He draws a rough plan-view sketch, dimensioned. He writes: "Documentation: 19 photographic frames, Leica IIIa, Kodak Super-X panchromatic, 35mm. Complete field record attached."
- He closes the notebook.
- He takes out the Leica and holds it. Not to open it — just to hold it, in the valley-floor light, at the weight it is, which is the weight of nineteen frames of something that no one else has and that he does not yet know what to do with. He could send them to Finch. The RGS would publish selected frames, with a caption that said what the RGS needed it to say. That would establish the record and it would do something to the record — the same thing that Riedel's photographs would do if Riedel published them and the same thing that his father's photographs would have done if Thomas Carrow had taken any, which he had not because Thomas Carrow understood that the record was not his to make.
- He puts the Leica back inside his jacket. He goes north toward Cusco.
- The cable he sends from the telegraph office in Urubamba, two days later, reads: CITY CONFIRMED LOCATION FOLLOWS REPORT FOLLOWS — HOLROYD. This is accurate. It is the beginning of an account he will write in a hotel room in Cusco over four days, and what he writes will be exact and will not be everything, and he has decided that this is not the same as a lie. He will submit the survey data and the field notes. He will not send the photographs. Not yet. He does not know if he ever will.
- He will know by Cusco. He does not know by Cusco. He goes home.

---

## Outcome / Turn

The three have separated with their incompatible versions of what the expedition produced:

- **Holroyd**: carries the survey record — coordinates, bearings, altitude, a written account that is accurate and controlled. Carries the Leica and its nineteen frames, withheld for now. Leaves behind the sealed chamber, unopened; the photographs, undistributed; and the India-era habit of protecting himself by not writing down what he decided. He has written the decision into the field notes this time, by omission — by not writing it down, which he is aware is the same thing — except that this time, on this mountain, in this bowl, what he left out is something he chose to leave out rather than something that was taken from him.
- **Isobel**: carries her six pages of notes (the beginning of a document that will eventually restore context to the city's known history), her father's annotated Arce, and a grief that now has a shape she can live with. Leaves behind the argument with her father — not resolved but released. She is going to Pisac to be in the place where he lived, which is the correct next place.
- **Mariano**: carries the completed witness — the testimony of standing there, looking at it, taking its measure on behalf of a community that has held the knowledge for generations. Carries no document, no object, no photograph. Leaves behind nothing because he brought nothing he was not prepared to put down.

---

## New Question or Pressure Raised

The story ends in motion rather than in resolution. Holroyd does not know what he will do with the photographs by the time the chapter closes. Isobel does not know what form her document will take. Mariano has done what he came to do and the story leaves him walking toward it. These are the correct questions to leave open — they are the questions the story has been asking from the beginning, and they are not answerable in the space of a mountain expedition. They will be answered, if at all, in the years that follow 1938, which the story does not narrate.
