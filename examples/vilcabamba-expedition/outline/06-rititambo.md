# Chapter 6 — Rititambo

**One-line summary:** The three climb through the final 1,500 feet and pass through the rock into the bowl; the city is exactly as the missionary accounts described and nothing they were prepared for; Holroyd begins systematic documentation; at frame six, voices rise from below the passage.

**POV:** James Holroyd — third-person limited, past tense. This is the chapter where the prose is permitted to breathe. Sentences may lengthen here. Observation may pause and stay. The awe is allowed — but the underlying tension (Riedel is below; the weather is uncertain; they are above 17,000 feet) does not suspend.

**Setting / Time:** The snowfield above 17,000 feet; the rock passage; the natural bowl; Rititambo. Very early September 1938, morning into midday.

---

## Goal

Reach the bowl before Riedel and begin systematic documentation — coordinates, bearings, the Leica — before anything else.

## Obstacle

The final 1,500-foot push on hard snow at above 17,000 feet is at the outer edge of what the body can do. The cold inside the lungs is not pain exactly — more like an awareness that the air is thin and what is coming in is not quite enough. Holroyd's headache is present. Every step above 17,000 feet costs more than the step before it, in a ratio that is not linear. And time: Riedel is behind them, two hours or less.

---

## Action Beats

**The final ascent:**
- They leave high camp in the grey-blue light before sunrise, moving northeast up the snowfield. The snow surface is perfect for hobnails — wind-packed to a firmness that holds a step without crumbling. Mariano is in front, moving in the careful rhythm of a man who knows that above 17,000 feet the pace must not vary: too fast and the body protests in ways that can't be recovered from; too slow and the cold works into the joints.
- Holroyd counts steps. He reaches fifty-one and begins again at one. This is a technique from the Survey — a way of keeping the mind on something measurable when the body wants to stop measuring and simply suffer. He knows it works because he has done it before, at altitude in the eastern Himalayas, and before that in Mesopotamia. He counts. He climbs. The horizon flattens and rises with each step in a way that is specific to high altitude: the summit always looks closer than it is, and it is never where it appears to be from below.
- Isobel, in the middle of the rope: she is chewing coca and moving well. Holroyd watches her from the rear and notes that she has the correct body posture for altitude — upright, not bent over, which keeps the lungs more open. She learned this somewhere, or arrived at it instinctively. He does not say anything about it.
- The cold inside the lungs: at 17,500 feet each breath is an action rather than a reflex. The air is thin enough that full inflation requires a deliberate expansion that at sea level happens without thought. The sound of his own breathing is loud inside his head. He counts.

**The rock step and the passage:**
- The rock step below the cliff face: six feet of exposed granite tilted at forty-five degrees, with a thin skin of verglas — ice formed by refreezing overnight. Mariano moves up it with his crampons front-pointing; this is the face he failed on alone in 1933. He places each front-point deliberately, tests, steps. He calls back: "The crampons first. One at a time." He anchors the rope at the top. Isobel goes up on the fixed rope; Holroyd goes up on the fixed rope. At the top they are standing on a narrow ledge in front of a fault in the cliff face — exactly as Arce described it.
- The passage: a natural crack in the rock, wide enough for a person, forty feet long. The walls lean in at shoulder height. The packs must come off and be handed through. Mariano shrugs off his pack and passes it ahead; it disappears around the internal curve of the passage. He follows.
- Holroyd, last, with his pack in front of him: the passage is angled slightly downward, which he did not expect. He is moving through it in a crouch, the rock close on both sides, the light ahead changing from the grey of the cliff face to something else — a different quality of light that he registers before he sees its source. Then the passage opens.

**The city — the prose breathes:**
- The bowl: a natural amphitheater roughly 200 meters across, sheltered on three sides by the cliff walls. The floor of the bowl is compacted snow — wind-deposited from the west over centuries, accumulating against the cliff face on the east side. The snow comes up to what would be the second story of the building sections on the east wall; the upper courses of stone rise above the snowline.
- The walls: intact. Stone cut without mortar, fitted so precisely that the joints are difficult to see until the eye finds the pattern. The colour is a grey-blue granite, different from the surrounding cliff rock — brought from elsewhere, or cut from a vein below the surface. The courses are even. The walls are plumb. After three hundred and sixty years of frost cycles and wind and the specific silence of this altitude, nothing has moved.
- The architecture: not one building but a complex of six connected sections, the walls running between cliff-face abutments in a horseshoe open toward the rock passage. The buildings face inward — all the doorways open to the center of the bowl. The doorways are trapezoidal, tall, the proportion of the opening slightly larger than the Inca convention Holroyd has seen at Machu Picchu and at Ollantaytambo. The scale of the whole is slightly larger than Andean highland convention — not dramatically, but measurably. A city built for a people who were slightly larger, or who wanted their threshold heights to say something about what passed through them.
- No sun symbols. No serpents, no lightning bolts, none of the iconography of Inca cosmology. The walls are plain. There is a relief carving above the central doorway in the largest section — not figurative, not immediately intelligible. It could be a map or a counting system or a record of something. Holroyd looks at it for a long time without deciding what it is.
- The silence in the bowl: not the outdoor silence of a high pass, which has wind and bird sound and the distant creak of ice. This is the silence of an enclosed space that absorbs sound — the snow on the floor deadening the acoustics, the walls catching and holding any vibration that the wind cannot reach. It smells of nothing. Not cold, not stone, not the absence of anything. Nothing.
- Mariano at the center of the bowl: he has gone directly to the lowest section of the floor, where the snow is thinnest, and he has found the stone beneath it with his boot. He is standing on the floor of the city. He looks at the walls. He does not move.
- Isobel has set down her pack. She is standing in front of the central doorway, looking in. Holroyd can see she is not moving, not taking notes, not speaking. He lets this be. He is looking too, and what he is doing is not quite cataloguing and not quite standing still — it is something between, the surveyor's instinct to measure against the observer's instinct to just receive what is in front of him. For a moment he lets the second instinct run.
- He thinks: Arce was a competent observer. He wrote that the proportions were unlike anything he had seen. He was right. He wrote that the city was older than the Inca. He was right. He wrote that standing in it produced in him a feeling he could not name but that he associated with finality — with the sense of being in a place that had been completed, fully, and closed. He was right about this too.

**Documentation begins:**
- Holroyd takes the Leica from inside his jacket. He winds it to frame one — a mechanical click that sounds very loud in the bowl. He frames the full east wall, main section, in the viewfinder. The morning light enters the bowl at a low angle from the passage side; the far wall is in direct sun. He notes the compass bearing in his field book: 067° magnetic. He notes the altitude: 17,900 feet by his barometric altimeter. Frame one.
- He moves systematically: the central doorway (frame two), the relief carving above it (frame three, close), the sight line through the bowl from the passage entry (frame four), the north section with the snow level visible against the wall (frame five), the fitted stonework at a joint where the light is raking (frame six).
- He is winding to frame seven when Isobel holds up one hand. He stops. She says: "Listen." He listens.
- Below the passage — below the rock step, below the verglas face: voices. Not one voice but two, in German. Moving closer.
- Holroyd looks at Isobel. Isobel looks at Mariano. Mariano has not moved from the center of the bowl. He is still looking at the walls.

---

## Outcome / Turn

The city is real. It is exactly as the accounts described — and it is entirely itself, nothing like what any of them privately imagined. Holroyd has six frames of documentation. The wonder is active: the bowl is strange and complete and silent and it is working on each of the three in the way that high altitude and old places and the satisfaction of a long search do work on people. And then the German voices come up through the passage and the chapter ends at the exact point where the confrontation becomes unavoidable.

---

## New Question or Pressure Raised

What is the relief carving above the central doorway? Holroyd photographs it but cannot read it. This is the city's one piece of visible inscription, and it will matter to Riedel's interpretation and to Isobel's. What does each of them see in it? What does Mariano know of it from his oral tradition? The sealed chamber is also here: Holroyd will see it in the next chapter — the low doorway at the rear of the largest building section, fitted with stone from the outside. He hasn't found it yet.
