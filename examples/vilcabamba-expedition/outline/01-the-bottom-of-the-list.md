# Chapter 1 — The Bottom of the List

**One-line summary:** Holroyd arrives in Cusco, meets Isobel and Mariano for the first time as a party, learns he is twelve days behind Riedel, and makes his first act of trust — deferring to Mariano's route correction without making it a ceremony.

**POV:** James Holroyd — third-person limited, past tense.

**Setting / Time:** Cusco market district; a tienda near the market; the road south toward the Yanatile valley head. Late August 1938, pre-dawn arrival through early afternoon departure.

---

## Goal

Assemble the party, complete the provisioning, and depart for the Yanatile valley the same day — before the gap with Riedel widens further.

## Obstacle

Holroyd has hired Isobel and Mariano by correspondence only; he is assessing them against the expedition's demands in real time, while they are doing the same to him. The party must reach functional trust in the hours before departure. Holroyd's habit of authority — the Survey of India reflex — is wrong for this team and he will have to revise it before the morning is out.

---

## Action Beats

- Holroyd steps off the overnight train from Lima onto the Cusco platform before dawn, carrying a cartridge case of survey instruments and the Leica in his jacket inner pocket. He has been fourth on Finch's list. He is aware of this. He does not find it humiliating; he finds it accurate. Brief interiority: the cable from Finch, the fee (enough for three months of debts in Lima), the only facts he was given — that two other men declined and one died. He is here because the eastern Cordillera is his range and he has nothing better to lose.

- He walks to the market by the San Pedro arcade to collect the last of the food stores he arranged from Lima: hardtack biscuits, dried chuño, canned sardines packed in oil, coca tea. He is counting 80-lb atadito equivalents in his head — standard load discipline — when Isobel appears.

- First exchange with Isobel: she has already been at the provisions depot for forty minutes and she has a problem. The coca leaf order is short by a significant margin. She has corrected it, she says — added sixteen pounds from a market vendor she trusts — and she will want the cost reimbursed. Holroyd asks her to explain the coca calculation. She does: at altitude above 15,000 feet, the men function or they stop functioning, and the difference is sometimes sixteen pounds of coca leaf. Holroyd: "How much did you spend?" She tells him. He writes it in his notebook and says: "Fine." She registers that he didn't argue the logic; she files this.

- Mariano arrives at the market with four mules and the two arrieros — Cleto and Rufino — from his Yanatile valley. The mules are better-conditioned than Holroyd expected. Mariano is relashing a load on the second mule and does not look up when Holroyd says his name. He finishes the lashing, checks the knot, then looks up. Holroyd: "Quispe." Mariano: "Holroyd." They shake hands. Holroyd's surveyor's eye: the man is built for uphill work, carries nothing extra, moves without waste. The mules respond to him before he speaks.

- Cleto and Rufino are introduced briefly — they will work the lower approach and turn back at the snowline. Holroyd observes that Rufino is younger than expected. He will not know this observation matters until the gorge in Chapter 2.

- Over a meal at the tienda — sardines, bread, coca tea, no alcohol: Holroyd's rule — Isobel receives a note passed in by a trader who came through the Yanatile valley head two days ago. A German party of five crossed the trailhead sixteen days ago, heavily loaded, well-equipped. Holroyd calculates: he expected to have a week's head start. Instead he is twelve days behind. The race he has been treating as theoretical becomes actual.

- He spreads his 1934 survey maps on the table. His planned approach: east up the Yanatile main valley, north branch at the Pampa Chica junction, southeast ascent to SM-7 through the high puna. He shows the line to Mariano.

- Mariano studies the maps in silence. Holroyd has seen men perform map-reading for an audience; this is not that. Mariano's eyes move across the lines methodically, tracing contours. Then his finger traces a different route — north of the valley head, cutting across the upper puna three miles further, approaching SM-7 from the north-northeast.

- Holroyd: "That adds two days." Mariano: "One. The dry makes the upper puna fast. The southeast approach loses a mule above thirteen thousand, guaranteed. The south face has a gorge section that breaks loads." He pauses. "I lost one there in 1929." Another pause. "You will too."

- Holroyd looks at Isobel across the table. She says: "He's right about the south gorge. Three people I trust have said the same thing." Holroyd looks back at the map. Mariano has not moved his finger. The evidence is the evidence.

- Holroyd takes out his pencil and marks the new line on the map. He does not make a production of it. He says: "We leave at first light tomorrow — which means packs ready tonight." He folds the map. Mariano, for the first time, looks faintly satisfied — not smug; satisfied, the way a man looks when a load is balanced correctly.

- Evening: Holroyd in his room above the tienda, writing in his notebook. He writes the revised route, the supply inventory, the atadito distribution. He writes: "Riedel: 12 days ahead. Approx. 5 men and full Ahnenerbe equipment budget." He writes nothing about the route correction. The facts of it are written in the map. He does not need to annotate the fact that Mariano knew better.

- He pulls out the Leica, checks the shutter speed in the lamplight. It clicks correctly at all settings. He reloads it from the film canister in his jacket pocket: 36 exposures, Kodak Super-X panchromatic. He puts it back against his chest, inside the jacket.

---

## Outcome / Turn

The party is assembled and provisioned; the route is corrected. Holroyd has already revised his authority downward once — quietly, correctly — and the team's functioning dynamic is established. They depart at first light. But they are twelve days behind, and the gap may be worse than it looks: Riedel's team has been in the field longer and is therefore better acclimatized to altitude. Their speed on the puna may be higher than Holroyd's estimates.

---

## New Question or Pressure Raised

The German party had a full Ahnenerbe equipment budget — more porters, better rope, more food. What does twelve days of head start mean on the mountain itself? Is Riedel at the puna already, or above the snowline? And why did none of the three men Finch hired before Holroyd take this job?

---

## Continuity Notes (New Facts Introduced)

- Holroyd's Leica IIIa is carried against his chest inside his outer jacket, loaded with 36-exposure Kodak Super-X panchromatic film.
- Four mules are part of the expedition; Mariano manages them.
- Cleto and Rufino are hired by Mariano from the Yanatile valley; they will work only the lower approach.
- Holroyd carries his .30-06 sporting rifle, acquired in La Paz in 1935, in the kit.
- The route to SM-7 is revised from the southeast approach to a north-northeast approach, on Mariano's recommendation.
- Riedel's party of five departed the Yanatile trailhead sixteen days before Holroyd's party.
- The party departs Cusco in late August 1938.
