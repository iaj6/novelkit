# Chapter 7 — What It Proves

**One-line summary:** Riedel and Kern enter the bowl; Riedel acknowledges Holroyd's work, documents the city in parallel, then produces a pry bar for the sealed chamber; Isobel argues the architecture against his thesis; Mariano completes his act of witness; Kern collapses with cerebral edema; Riedel chooses his man and descends, leaving Holroyd with twelve frames and a sentence he will not stop thinking about.

**POV:** James Holroyd — third-person limited, past tense. The climax chapter. Holroyd's POV is fixed here.

**Setting / Time:** The bowl; Rititambo. Above 17,900 feet. Very early September 1938, midday into early afternoon. The weather window is open; afternoon cloud will come.

---

## Goal (Holroyd's)

Prevent Riedel from damaging or altering the site — specifically from opening the sealed chamber, which Holroyd will not allow because he does not know what's in it and cannot unknow it once it's opened, and because opening it would make a specific and irrevocable claim about what kind of event this expedition was.

## Obstacle

Riedel is a competent man with a prepared thesis, a Mauser Karabiner 98k, and the discipline to stay formal at altitude. He is not irrational. He is wrong — and he is wrong in a way that requires the city to be something it isn't, and the only way to protect him from his own wrongness is to prevent him from reaching the evidence he needs to misread. Holroyd is not certain he has the authority or the physical means to do this. He has his .30-06 on his pack, ten feet away.

---

## Action Beats

**Riedel and Kern arrive:**
- The two men come through the passage in the same order Holroyd's party did: pack first, then person. Riedel emerges first. He stands up and looks at the city. He is quiet. He looks at it the way a man looks at something he has been believing in for years and is now confirming: not surprise, exactly — vindication, carefully controlled. Then he looks at Holroyd.
- Riedel: "You surveyed this range. You always had the right mountain. I want you to know that I acknowledge your work here." A pause. "And I want you to know I acknowledge that you got here first." He says this with the precise warmth of a man who is being scrupulously fair and knows it. Holroyd: "The acknowledgment is noted." He returns to his notebook.
- Kern comes through the passage. He stands a little too still against the wall just inside the bowl, which Holroyd registers in the way he registers everything about other people's bodies — mechanically, as data. Kern is pale. His breathing is audible from eight feet away. At 17,900 feet this is not unusual, but it is the wrong kind of audible.
- Riedel produces a Rolleiflex from his pack — a medium-format twin-lens reflex camera, more awkward than the Leica and capable of larger exposures — and begins documenting the city. He and Holroyd work the same site for the next fifteen minutes in a silence that is almost professional. Two men with cameras in a city no one has been in for three hundred and sixty years, making frames that will be interpreted differently in different offices in different countries.

**The sealed chamber:**
- At the rear of the main building section, there is a low doorway at approximately floor level — not a main entrance, smaller, set into the wall below the relief carving. It has been sealed from the outside: fitted stone placed in the opening with the same precision as the surrounding wall construction, but the mortar pattern is different — the sealing happened after the original construction, by the same or similar hands, in a deliberate act. A door fitted, not collapsed. Something was put in there, or someone wanted the interior undisturbed.
- Mariano, in the oral tradition, knows this chamber. He knows it as the place the ancestors sealed before they departed — or before they withdrew to somewhere else, the tradition is not specific about the nature of the departure. He does not go near it. He stands at the center of the bowl and looks at it from where he is standing.
- Riedel finds it. He photographs it from three angles. He crouches in front of the sealed entrance and runs his fingers along the fitted stone. Then he produces from his pack a short-handled pry bar: steel, twelve inches, rubberized grip. He does not announce what he intends. He sets the bar into the gap between the sealing stone and the surrounding wall.
- Holroyd: "No." Riedel looks up. The word — Holroyd's voice is perfectly even, not raised, not heated — stops him not because it is loud but because it is completely definite. Holroyd: "You are not opening that."
- Riedel: "This chamber is likely to contain the most significant evidence of the site's founding culture. The architectural record in this bowl does not match any Andean tradition I have documented. To fully understand the site's origins—"
- Isobel: "It is Andean." She does not raise her voice either. She steps forward and speaks to Riedel directly, without consulting Holroyd. "The scale is pre-Inca. The proportions are consistent with Chavin de Huántar and with Tiahuanaco — both earlier than the Inca, both entirely Andean. The doorway shape is trapezoidal because the Inca learned it from this and older places like it. The absence of Inca iconography is evidence that this predates the Inca, not that it predates Andean culture. You have been looking at the wrong comparison set."
- Riedel: "Friar Arce wrote that it was unlike anything he had encountered—"
- Isobel: "Friar Arce had not been to Chavin. He had not been to Tiahuanaco. He had not been to Wari. He described what he saw against the background of what he knew. His comparison set was the same as yours: the Inca. This is not the Inca. It is older and it is Andean."
- Riedel looks at her. He is quiet for a moment. He says, with the same precise fairness he extended to Holroyd: "You may be right." He pauses. "I would still like to see what is in the chamber." He turns back to the pry bar.
- Holroyd steps between Riedel and the sealed door. He has not reached for his rifle. Neither has Riedel, whose Mauser is slung across his back. They are two feet apart. Both men know what the next movement is, and both men know they are at 17,900 feet after a night storm, and that whatever happens next will happen in conditions that neither of them is fully equipped to judge.

**Mariano's act of witness:**
- While this is happening, Mariano has not moved from the center of the bowl. He has been standing there for most of this chapter — since before Riedel arrived, since the moment he first stood on the stone floor. He is not praying and he is not performing anything. He is looking at the walls of the city. His lips are not moving. His body is completely still. He is the stillest thing in the bowl.
- What he is doing is this: he is looking. He is looking at the doorways and the stone courses and the proportions and the relief carving and the way the morning light enters through the passage and runs across the floor. He is looking at the scale of it — slightly larger than what he was prepared for, which means the people who built it were building for something slightly larger than what they became, or slightly larger than what the Inca's lowering of everything claimed. He is looking with the specific attention of a man who has been trusted to go somewhere and come back and say what he found, and who has arrived at the somewhere after five years and one failed attempt and a lifetime of being told by his community that it was there and mattered and needed a witness.
- He bears witness. He does not take notes. He does not photograph. He does not speak. He stands in the fact and lets it change him, which is what a witness does. This act is private, formally slight, and — in the terms of his community's understanding — complete.
- Holroyd will notice Mariano's stillness at the periphery of his attention during the confrontation. He will not understand it in the moment. He will think about it on the descent.

**Kern collapses:**
- Kern has been against the wall for the past twenty minutes, getting progressively more still. At the moment of the standoff between Holroyd and Riedel — the two men two feet apart, neither moving — Kern slides down the wall and sits in the snow. His head goes to his hands. He says, in German: "Ich kann nicht—" He stops. He is trying to form a sentence and the words are not arriving in the right order. He says, in English, slowly: "I cannot see clearly." He says this with the precision of a man who knows exactly how alarming the sentence is and is saying it anyway because it is true.
- Riedel looks at Kern. He looks at the sealed door. He looks at Holroyd. His expression does not change. But he is making a calculation and Holroyd can see him making it.
- Holroyd, quietly: "He needs to be four thousand feet lower in the next two hours. Cerebral edema comes fast at this altitude. He will lose more than his vision."
- Riedel: "I know what it is." He has not moved.
- A long pause. In the pause, the city holds its silence. The wind that does not reach the bowl does not reach the bowl.
- Then Riedel straightens up. He puts the pry bar back into his pack. He lifts his Rolleiflex and takes two more exposures — methodical, unhurried, the behaviour of a man who is not fleeing but choosing. He slings the camera. He helps Kern to his feet with a grip that is practiced, efficient. He says nothing to Mariano or Isobel.
- He says to Holroyd, at the bowl entrance, with Kern leaning on his shoulder: "Whatever this place is, you know what they will want it to be. In London. In Berlin. In every institution with a map on the wall and a theory on the shelf." He pauses. "Don't let them make it what it isn't." He looks at Holroyd. "Neither will I." He means both sentences.
- They go through the passage.

**After Riedel leaves:**
- The three are alone in Rititambo.
- Mariano turns from the wall. He looks at Holroyd and Isobel with the expression of a man who has put something down — not relief, not satisfaction, something quieter. He says, in Spanish: "It is enough." He picks up his pack.
- Isobel looks at the sealed door for a long time. Then she looks at Holroyd. She says: "Do you want to open it?" Holroyd looks at the fitted stone. He says: "No." He is not entirely sure this is true; he is a surveyor and the door is in front of him and something is behind it. But he has said no, and having said it, it becomes what he wants.
- He counts the frames he has taken: six. He has thirty more. He looks at the city.

---

## Outcome / Turn

Riedel is gone. Kern's body removed the decision from Riedel's hands — or gave Riedel permission to make the right decision without having to acknowledge it as such. The sealed chamber is intact. Mariano has completed his act. The confrontation has resolved into the shape it needed: not a gunfight, not a philosophical agreement, but a withdrawal dictated by the mountain's conditions and by one man's choice to be exactly as competent as he is.

Riedel's final sentence — "don't let them make it what it isn't" — does not resolve. It is said by a man who has been making it what it isn't for the entire expedition, and it is said with perfect sincerity, and Holroyd will not be able to dismiss it or agree with it cleanly on the descent.

---

## New Question or Pressure Raised

What is in the sealed chamber? The story will never answer this. Holroyd does not open it. The not-answering is the answer — the point is that the question exists, and that each person in the bowl has a different reason for leaving it closed or opening it, and the three people who were there chose, together, to leave it closed. The question that remains for the descent: what does Holroyd do with the Leica's twelve frames and thirty remaining exposures?

This is stated explicitly to Riedel in the confrontation.
- **Kern develops cerebral edema** in the bowl. He and Riedel descend together after the standoff.
- **Mariano completes his act of witness** — standing on the floor of the bowl, looking at the city, performing the act of testimony required by his community's tradition.
- **Holroyd does not open the sealed chamber.**
- Holroyd has **12 frames exposed** on the Leica when Riedel departs (he completes 6 more during the time Riedel was in the bowl).
