# Chapter 14 — The Last Morning

**One-line summary:** Elena cleans the station, makes a proper breakfast, says farewell to Varga on the final schedule, and decides she will tell Brandt herself — face to face, before Brandt opens the log.

**POV:** Elena Hartmann  
**Tense:** Third-person limited past  
**Setting:** All five station structures (maintenance walkthrough); main hut (primary). Day 12, morning.  
**Target words:** ~2,500

---

## Goal
Elena wants the station to be ready for the team. This is practical — a station prepared for four people by one person who used it alone is a matter of professional pride and courtesy — and it is also something she wants to do. She wants the handover to be clean.

## Obstacle
The obstacle today is not the decision; the decision is made. The obstacle is smaller and more human: waiting. The flight window is clear. The LC-130 is en route. She has done what she set out to do. Now she waits, and the waiting is its own kind of work.

---

## Action Beats

- **She wakes at 0600.** The first full night's sleep. She notices this consciously: she slept. She makes note of it without ceremony, the way she makes note of a good instrument calibration. She dresses. She looks at the official station log on the desk before she makes coffee. It is closed. It is there.

- **She makes a real breakfast.** From the station stores: powdered eggs, which are not ideal, but she cooks them properly with the good butter from the cold locker (rarely touched during the solo period). Toast that actually toasts. Coffee from the bottom of the proper-ground bag rather than the instant tin. She eats at the table with her field log open, reading back through the eleven days. She reads it the way she reads anything she's written: looking for what she missed.

- **She reads the log.** The eleven days in her own hand: the signal entry, the verification categories, the three options, the draft folded in the drawer, the career accounting, *Varga suspects. He will not ask again.*, *Option 4: complete the record. Hand it over in person.* She has not read it start to finish before. It is a coherent record. It holds up.

- **She makes the station ready.** A methodical walkthrough: main hut sleeping bunks — she remakes her own bunk and opens the other three, airs them briefly. She stocks the galley for four people. She goes to the receiver hut and runs the morning calibration and logs it. She checks the generator shed — fuel levels adequate, all nominal. She notes what needs restocking in the supply order for the LC-130: specific items, quantities. This is routine. She does it well.

- **The radome.** She visits the radome last. She walks its interior circumference once more — she has been in here perhaps forty times this season, a hundred times across five Antarctic deployments. The 15-meter dish above her, parked at zenith. She does not stay long. She does not mark it. She walks back along the guide rope.

- **0800 Varga call — the last scheduled call.** She opens the frequency at 0800 precisely. Weather is clear at South Pole; the system that had been approaching has passed harmlessly to the east. He reports the South Pole weather in the standard format. She reports hers: clear, −39°C, wind at 8 knots, ceiling unlimited. Good flight conditions. He says: *Your team will have a smooth ride in.* She says: *That's good.* A pause. Then she says: *Varga — this is the last scheduled call for this period. The team arrives today.* He says: *I know.* She says: *I'll be in touch from McMurdo.* He says: *Good. Say hello to Annelie for me.* She says: *I will.* Another beat — longer, the space between two people who have talked every day for eleven days and are not quite ready to stop. Then she says: *Thank you for the schedule.* He says: *Always.* She says: *Out.* He says: *Out.* She turns off the radio and sits for a moment with her hand on the desk.

- **She thinks about what to say to Brandt.** Not the words — she has been through the words. The manner of it. She decides: after the team is inside and the aircraft has left and the noise has quieted. She will make coffee and she will say: *there's something in the log from the first night.* And she will let Brandt read it. She will not preface it. She will not explain what it means. She will make coffee and point Brandt to page 47 and let the record speak.

- **She waits.** The station is ready. She has nothing left to do but wait. She sits at the main hut desk and reads the Parkes preprint that Varga mentioned nine days ago. She gets through twelve pages. She is on page thirteen when she hears it.

- **The first sound of the engines.** Distant, then less distant. She closes the paper. She puts on her outer layers — wind shell, gloves, balaclava. She checks the balaclava in the mirror (a practical measure; frostbite at −39° is fast). She goes to the door and opens it and goes out to meet the plane.

---

## Outcome / Turn
The station is ready. Elena is ready. She has decided exactly how the handoff will happen: inside, after coffee, with the log open on the desk. The eleven days of solitary custody end not with a transmission and not with destruction but with a cup of coffee and a page number. The sound of the LC-130 arrives on the last paragraph.

## New Question Raised
Will the weather hold? Will the flight complete? And when Brandt reads page 47, what does she say?
