# Chapter 12 — The Protocol, Final Pass

**One-line summary:** Elena runs the full Hartmann Protocol one last time — not from doubt but as ritual completion; when it ends, the fourth option clarifies itself fully for the first time.

**POV:** Elena Hartmann  
**Tense:** Third-person limited past  
**Setting:** Receiver hut (primary). Day 11, morning and afternoon.  
**Target words:** ~2,600

---

## Goal
Elena runs the verification protocol from the beginning one final time. She does not doubt the result — she is not looking for an error she missed. She is completing a formal procedure in the way that all formal procedures require completion: by running them in full, from start to finish, without abbreviation.

## Obstacle
The protocol was designed to build toward a conclusion. Running it when the conclusion is already known changes its quality — it becomes something else. It becomes a rite. Elena recognizes this and lets it be what it is.

---

## Action Beats

- **She starts at 0700.** She has made a pot of coffee and carried it to the receiver hut. (She does not usually carry the whole pot.) She opens her verification log to the first entry from Day 1 and lays it beside the current instrument displays. She sets up the review in the MicroVAX: she will compare the original recording against the current instrument calibration. She begins.

- **The 0800 call, compressed.** Brief. Varga: weather is holding so far, system is moving slower than the advisory suggested. Elena: nominal. He says: *Good conditions for today.* She says: *Yes.* The call ends in ninety seconds. This is fine. This is right.

- **The protocol runs in full.** The chapter tracks Elena working through the Hartmann Protocol from category 1 through category 12, in order, for the second and final time. The pace here is different from Chapter 2 — this is not uncertain investigation, this is methodical completion. Each category goes faster than the first time (the instrument readings are the same; she knows what they will say). She does not hurry. She writes each result in a clean column beside the original entry, dated today, with the same initials: E.H.

- **Category by category — selected texture.** The chapter need not show every category in detail; it should show enough to convey the rigor and the changed quality of the repetition. Two or three categories get a beat of internal texture: category 7 (ionospheric reflection) is the one she always found most interesting to rule out — the way a mirage is interesting. Category 12 closes exactly as it closed before. She writes: *Eliminiated. No known natural or terrestrial source.* She does not need to read back what she wrote the first time. She knows what it says.

- **The end of the protocol.** She reaches the final entry. She writes below it: *Second verification complete. All categories confirmed. E.H. [date].* She puts the pen down. She picks it up and adds: *Methodology: Hartmann, E., 1979, in full.* This is the instruction set for anyone reading the log after her: this is how it was done; this is how to repeat it; this is the standard of certainty.

- **She sits for a moment.** Nothing dramatic. The stylus of the chart recorder is ticking. The MicroVAX fan is running clean. The fireproof box is closed. She has verified the signal twice, fully, using her own protocol, and the conclusion is the same both times. This is what certainty looks like in her field. She has been here before — but never here.

- **The fourth option fills in.** Not a sudden clarity — more like a door that has been open for a while, that she can finally walk through because the work is done. She has the verification. She has the analysis (the twelve pages in the drawer). She has the documentation begun in her field log. What she needs to do now is finish the documentation — transfer everything to the official station log, the permanent record, in a form that any scientist could read and follow and verify. And then she needs to hand it to someone she trusts in person, before the apparatus takes over. Not a transmission. A handoff. Brandt arrives tomorrow. She will hand it to Brandt.

- **She writes in her field log.** A single line, final notation under the protocol entry: *Option 4: complete the record. Hand it over in person. The rest is not mine to decide.* She closes the log.

- **She goes back to the main hut and eats.** It is the middle of the afternoon. She realizes she is hungry, which she has not been properly for three days. She makes soup from the good stock, not the instant kind. She eats it sitting at the desk. She reads a technical paper she brought with her — actually reads it this time; she registers every sentence.

- **1800 Varga call.** Warm, ordinary. He reports the approaching weather system is decelerating further — the window for tomorrow's flight looks clear. She says: good. He says: *Your team is on schedule then.* She says: *They should land tomorrow afternoon.* He says: *How does that feel?* She says: *Fine. It'll be good to have the station running at full.* He says: *Of course.* His voice is settled and warm. She says: *Varga — it's been a good schedule.* He understands what this means — the eleven days, the daily calls, the arrangement. He says: *It has, Hartmann.* A beat. *Good night.* She says: *Good night.*

---

## Outcome / Turn
The protocol is complete for the second and final time. The fourth option is now explicit in her log. Elena has decided: she will complete the formal documentation and hand the record to Brandt in person when she arrives. This decision has been forming since Day 6 or earlier; it is now written down, dated, and initialed. The decision is made.

## New Question Raised
The decision is made — but made decisions still have to be enacted. What does it feel like to spend the last night before the team arrives, knowing what you're going to do?
