# Chapter 6 — Station Rounds

**One-line summary:** Elena completes physical maintenance across the station; inside the emergency cache hut she pauses and realizes, mid-task, that she has been running the science without noticing — which means she has already decided something.

**POV:** Elena Hartmann  
**Tense:** Third-person limited past  
**Setting:** All five station structures, exterior paths. Full day, Day 6. Calm weather — clear, −44°C.  
**Target words:** ~2,500

---

## Goal
Elena is doing the weekly station maintenance rounds — mandatory for solo operation, logged in the station log. Generator check, fuel level, instrument calibration, emergency cache inventory. This is procedural, known, physical. She is glad of it.

## Obstacle
The physical work does not stop her from thinking. And in the emergency cache hut, sitting on a supply crate in the middle of an inventory, she catches herself doing mental orbital calculations — working backward from the signal's sky-position to the geometry of where the source must lie — and realizes she has been doing this, in pieces, for two days without calling it what it is.

---

## Action Beats

- **She begins with the generator shed.** Morning. The twin Cat diesels are running smoothly. She checks fuel consumption against the log — use is slightly lower than the seasonal average (she is one person, not four). She checks the coolant levels, the exhaust routing, the backup generator switch. She logs everything. The 60 Hz hum is a physical presence in this small building; she is fond of it in the impersonal way she is fond of the station — it keeps her alive; she maintains it; they have an understanding.

- **Main hut walk-through.** She inspects the Jamesway structure: roof seals, insulation integrity, the fax machine paper supply (adequate). The Codan 9350 and its connections — she pulls the antenna plug, inspects it, reseats it. The desk where she has been writing in the evenings. She notices the desk drawer where the draft transmission is folded. She does not open it.

- **Receiver hut check.** She runs the front-end electronics calibration sequence. MicroVAX disk health check — all sectors good. 9-track tape drive mechanism, heads clean. She opens the fireproof box and checks the labeled tape. She closes it. She does not replay the signal today.

- **Radome inspection.** She goes outside to the radome, the farthest structure from the main hut. Inside, the 15-meter dish is parked in its stowed position, pointing at zenith. She walks the circumference, checks the dome skin for stress cracks from recent wind. She looks up at the dish from underneath. She has been under this dish on four previous Antarctic seasons. The scale of it — the size of something built to listen at this particular frequency — feels different today than it ever has before.

- **The emergency cache hut.** The last stop on the maintenance round. She opens the padlock, steps inside. Small, cold — the backup HF radio, emergency rations stacked on a shelf, sleeping bags in their compression bags, a medical kit, fuel canisters. She begins the inventory with her clipboard.

- **The realization.** Midway through the ration count she realizes she has been, for some undetermined stretch of time, working through the geometry of the source's sky position. Not calculating formally — she doesn't have the right tools set up — but running rough mental estimates. The right ascension, the declination, what that puts in the direction of at what distance. She stopped herself at the boundary of "direction of" and did not allow herself to go further. She realizes she has been doing this for two days. She puts the clipboard down on the supply crate and sits on the crate and looks at the shelves for a moment.

- **What this means.** She has been doing the science. She has been treating the data as data she intends to work with, not data she intends to transmit or destroy. The science requires time. She has been buying herself time — which means, somewhere below the level of the formal decision, she has already made a preliminary one: she is not transmitting immediately, and she is not destroying it. She has been acting as though there is a third option, and she has been acting on it for days without naming it.

- **She finishes the inventory.** She logs the emergency cache as fully stocked, no issues. She latches the door behind her and walks back along the guide rope. The cold is absolute; the stars are perfect. She does not stop to look at them.

- **The 1800 Varga call.** Brief, routine. She reports maintenance complete, all systems nominal. He says: good. She says: and at your end? He says: we had a weather event this afternoon, everyone's fine, the instruments are fine. She says she's glad. They close the schedule. She feels, briefly and without examining it, the specific loneliness of having news you cannot share with the one person who would understand it.

---

## Outcome / Turn
The maintenance round is complete and logged. More importantly: Elena now knows that she has been doing science with the data — that she has been treating it as something she owns long enough to study, not merely to pass on. This is the first time she has caught herself acting ahead of her conscious decision. The fourth option on the blank line is starting to have a shape.

## New Question Raised
If she is going to do the science, how much time does that take? And what does she owe the data, versus what does the data owe the world?
