# Chapter 7 — The Draft

**One-line summary:** Elena writes a second, fuller formal transmission — complete with signal analysis — reads it back, folds it, and puts it with the first; not transmitting is now an active, recorded choice.

**POV:** Elena Hartmann  
**Tense:** Third-person limited past  
**Setting:** Main hut desk; receiver hut (brief). Day 7. Afternoon and evening.  
**Target words:** ~2,700

---

## Goal
Elena wants to know what she would actually say — in full scientific language, with full analysis — if she transmitted the signal right now. She decides to write it out. This is not a decision to transmit; it is a test of whether she can.

## Obstacle
She can write it perfectly. The draft comes out correct and complete and exactly as it should be. This is the problem: there is no flaw in the text that gives her a reason to hold back. The reason she is holding back is not in the text. She has to face what the reason actually is.

---

## Action Beats

- **She retrieves the first draft from the desk drawer.** Morning. She reads it. It is still correct. It is the right notification, exactly as the protocol requires. She puts it back. She thinks: what is the full analysis she would attach? She has been building it in pieces, in her field log and in her head. She decides to write it formally.

- **She goes to the receiver hut and works.** She reviews her log notes from Days 1–6 — the verification records, the category eliminations, the informal calculations she caught herself doing in the cache hut. She begins to organize them into the structure of a formal scientific analysis: signal parameters, detection conditions, verification methodology, statistical impossibility of natural origin, preliminary directional data. She works for three hours. She is good at this; it is what she does. The prose of scientific documentation is her native language.

- **The 0800 call comes and goes.** (Compressed — brief beat.) Varga: weather status, instrument status, welfare check. Elena: nominal, nominal, fine. He says she sounds busy. She says: maintenance paperwork. He says: good. The call ends in under two minutes. She goes back to the receiver hut.

- **She finishes the analysis.** Early afternoon. She has twelve pages of tightly written scientific documentation: the complete analysis she would attach to a formal notification. It is thorough. It follows her own protocol precisely. It is better — more complete, more careful — than anything she could have produced on the night of the signal or the day after. The work of six days of uninterrupted attention is in these pages.

- **She reads it back.** Aloud, to herself, in the receiver hut — the way she used to read her papers aloud before submitting them, to find the places where the logic doesn't quite hold. The logic holds. Every sentence is correct. The conclusion is the conclusion. She reads the last sentence: *On the basis of the foregoing verification and analysis, the author concludes that the signal recorded at Halvorsen Station on [date] is of non-terrestrial, non-natural origin.* She says it out loud. It sounds the same out loud as it does in her head.

- **She doesn't transmit.** She sits with the twelve pages on the desk. She thinks about what would happen — mechanically, procedurally — in the hours after she sent this. The NSF program officer. The duty officer at McMurdo. The protocol for this event exists; she has read it. She can trace the path the notification would take, the people it would reach, the speed of each hand-off. Within 24 hours it would be larger than her. Within 72 hours it would be in a room she would never be invited to. She looks at the pages. She folds them with the first draft. She clips them together with a binder clip from the desk drawer. She puts them back in the drawer.

- **She writes in her field log.** A single entry, late afternoon: *Two drafts complete. Neither sent. Question of timing is not resolved. Question of timing is not the only question.* She underlines *not the only question*.

- **The 1800 call.** Varga reports wind picking up at South Pole — a herbie may be developing, though it hasn't organized yet. He says: *nothing unusual, but we're watching it.* Elena says she has calm conditions at her elevation. He says: *Enjoy it while you can.* A short, equable silence before closing out. She thinks, after he signs off: *Enjoy it.* She does not know what that means at the moment. She sits with the radio off for a while.

---

## Outcome / Turn
The second, fuller draft exists. It is correct and complete. Elena has not transmitted it, and the reason she hasn't is now clear to her: the reason is not in the text; it is in what happens to the text the moment it leaves her hands. Not transmitting is now an active, deliberate choice she has recorded in her field log. She is owning the delay.

## New Question Raised
What is she actually protecting when she holds the data? Is she protecting the science? Her authorship? The finding itself — from what? From the apparatus she has spent her career inside?
