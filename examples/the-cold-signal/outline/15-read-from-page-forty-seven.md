# Chapter 15 — Read from Page Forty-Seven

**One-line summary:** The LC-130 lands; the team arrives; Elena makes coffee; she tells Brandt *there's something in the log from the first night — read from page forty-seven* and watches her read.

**POV:** Elena Hartmann  
**Tense:** Third-person limited past  
**Setting:** The landing area outside Halvorsen Station; main hut. Day 12, afternoon.  
**Target words:** ~2,800

---

## Goal
Elena wants the handoff to happen exactly as she planned it: cleanly, with the record speaking for itself. She has prepared for this. She is ready for it. She is also, at the moment of the plane's approach, briefly unsure whether she is ready for it.

## Obstacle
The arrival of other people into the eleven days' silence is itself a kind of shock — not an emergency, not a problem, just the sudden density of other presences, other voices, other needs. Elena has to navigate the welcome, the jokes and logistics of arrival, the practical noise of a team coming off a flight — and hold the record steady until the moment is right.

---

## Action Beats

- **The plane on approach.** Elena stands outside with her back to the main hut, watching the LC-130 appear first as sound and then as lights and then as the real shape of the aircraft, its ski-fitted landing gear lowering. She has seen this approach many times. It is always the same and always different. She watches it come in without moving.

- **The landing.** The Hercules touches down on the prepared snow strip 400 meters from the station buildings. The engines wind back but do not cut — standard procedure: leave the aircraft at idle until offloading is complete and the conditions hold. The door opens. People come down the rear ramp.

- **Brandt, first.** Dr. Annelie Brandt is the first one down the ramp — team leader, as expected. She is dressed for field conditions: orange jacket, goggles, her logistical efficiency visible even in the way she moves across the snow. She sees Elena and covers the distance between them. She looks at the station, then back at Elena. She says: *Station looks good.* Elena says: *All nominal.* Brandt says: *Any equipment issues?* Elena says: *Fan replacement on the MicroVAX, Day 8. Resolved. It's logged.* Brandt nods. This is enough of a greeting between two people who respect each other.

- **The rest of the team.** Two other scientists plus the flight loadmaster come off the aircraft with equipment crates. The noise level of the station rises sharply — voices, the sound of crates being set down, the aircraft engines, the unfamiliar warmth of other bodies in the station yard. Elena helps carry equipment. She does this automatically, competently, with the part of herself that is always competent. She is also, simultaneously, very aware of the station log on the main hut desk.

- **Inside the main hut.** The team files in. The Jamesway feels different with five people in it — the scale of it reclaims its original design. Brandt and the others begin the standard arrival tasks: gear stowed, bunks assigned, coffee. Someone makes a comment about the galley being stocked. Someone else makes a comment about the darkness. Normal things. Elena puts the kettle on.

- **The aircraft lifts off.** The LC-130 engines build. Everyone in the hut pauses — a reflex; the aircraft is the lifeline, and the sound of it departing is felt as much as heard. It lifts, circles once, diminishes. The station is quiet again, but not the same quiet. Four people, not one. The hum of conversation and equipment and normal occupation.

- **The moment.** When the coffee is made and the immediate arrival tasks are winding down, and Brandt is at the desk going through the station log stack — which is what Brandt does; she has always done this first — Elena says: *There's something in the log from the first night.* Brandt looks up. Elena holds her gaze. She says: *Read from page forty-seven.* She turns back to the kettle.

- **Brandt reads.** Elena does not watch her read. She can hear when Brandt has reached the signal entry — there is a change in the quality of Brandt's attention, a stillness that is different from ordinary reading attention. Elena pours coffee into two mugs. She sets one on the desk near Brandt's elbow without interrupting. She takes her own mug and stands at the galley window with her back to the room. She waits.

- **The end of the chapter.** Brandt finishes reading. Elena hears the log close — the particular sound of that specific cover on that specific desk. Brandt says her name. Just her name: *Elena.* (She almost never uses her first name.) Elena turns around. Brandt is looking at her across the mug of coffee, across the twelve pages of documentation, across the labeled tape in the archive box which Elena set out on the desk beside the log before she went outside to meet the plane. Elena says nothing. Brandt says: *When did you—* Elena says: *Day one.* Brandt says: *All right.* She picks up the coffee mug. She holds it. She is thinking. Elena can see her thinking. She says: *You did the full protocol.* Elena says: *Twice.* Brandt puts the mug down. She says: *Then we have work to do.* She says it the way competent people say it — not in excitement, not in alarm, but as the natural next step after a clear result. Elena says: *Yes.* She says: *I'll start the kettle again.*

---

## Outcome / Turn
The data has passed from Elena's solitary custody to the first other person. Brandt has read the record. The eleven days of singular knowledge are over. The apparatus — the protocols, the chain of command, the institutional processing — lies just beyond the chapter's edge. What happens next is not the story. The story was the eleven days. It is complete.

## New Question Raised
None. The story's central question — what is on her desk when they walk in — has been answered. What remains is the weight of what just transferred between two scientists over a cup of coffee in a Jamesway hut at the bottom of the world.
