# Chapter 11 — Something in Your Voice

**One-line summary:** The 1800 Varga call: he says, carefully, that she sounds like she's working through something; she says there is nothing unusual; his pause afterward is exactly the length of a man who knows he has been lied to.

**POV:** Elena Hartmann  
**Tense:** Third-person limited past  
**Setting:** Main hut radio room; receiver hut; main hut galley. Day 10, afternoon and evening.  
**Target words:** ~2,700

---

## Goal
Elena wants to get through the 1800 call the same way she has gotten through the previous eighteen calls: cleanly, accurately, without giving anything away that she has not decided to give. She has done this for nine days. She is practiced at it.

## Obstacle
Varga has been listening to her for nine days too. He knows the difference between Elena-being-precise and Elena-withholding-something. He doesn't know what she is withholding. But he knows she is withholding. And when he says so — gently, carefully, in a way that offers her an out — she takes the out. And they both know she took it. The relationship has shifted one degree, and she feels it after the call ends.

---

## Action Beats

- **Afternoon work.** Elena is in the receiver hut doing the analysis she committed to in the night's thinking — the formal documentation of the signal's directional parameters, working carefully with the instrument logs and her orbital calculations. She is doing real science. She is absorbed in it. This is the first chapter in which the absorption feels chosen rather than avoidant.

- **She is interrupted by a weather advisory.** The fax machine in the main hut prints a weather advisory from McMurdo: a system is building to the southwest, expected to reach the plateau in 48–72 hours. Wind forecasts are elevated. This is not an emergency; it is routine Antarctic weather. But 48–72 hours is the window before the LC-130 arrives. She notes it in the log: weather advisory received, system approaching, window contingent. The word *boomerang* is in the back of her mind.

- **She thinks about the LC-130.** If the weather window closes, the flight gets a boomerang. If it boomerangs, she has more time. She notices herself calculating this — how many additional days, whether it changes anything — and then she notices that she is calculating it, and that her first response to the possibility of more time was something uncomfortably close to relief. She stops calculating. She is two days away from where she wants to be. She does not need more time. She needs two more days.

- **She eats dinner.** Main hut galley. Something from a can. She is not paying close attention to what it is. She has her field log open and is reviewing what she has written in the last two days — the long night's thinking, the career accounting, the note about the signal not being hers. She reads it the way she would read anything she's written some time after writing it: looking for the errors.

- **The 1800 call.** She opens the frequency. Varga comes in clearly. Weather exchange — he has the same advisory she has. Status: nominal. Then: *How are you today, Hartmann?* She says: *Fine. Working through some analysis.* He says: *Still the instrument data?* She says: *Yes.* There is a pause. Then he says, carefully, in the voice he uses when he is being deliberate: *You've sounded, for the past few days, like someone who's working through something. Not the instruments.* He says it without pressure. He says it the way you say something to a person you trust with your meaning.

- **Elena's response.** She says: *There is nothing unusual to report.* The phrase is precise and chosen. Not *I'm fine.* Not *you're imagining it.* *There is nothing unusual to report.* Varga says nothing for four seconds. Then he says: *All right.* Two more seconds. *I'm here if the schedule needs to stretch.* She says: *I know. Thank you, Varga.* He says: *Good night.* She says: *Good night.*

- **The silence after the call.** She does not move. She sits in the radio room for — she will check the clock later — ninety seconds. She knows exactly what has just passed. She knows that Varga heard the phrase she chose, and that he heard it the way she meant it: as an exact, careful, technically-true statement that contains a deflection. He knows something is different. He does not know what. He has offered her the space to tell him when and if she chooses. She did not choose. She said: *I know. Thank you.*

- **She goes to the receiver hut.** She picks up her work. She annotates three more lines of her directional analysis. Then she puts the pen down and sits in the chair and looks at the 9-track tape in the open fireproof box — she left it open when she was working earlier. She closes the box.

- **She thinks about Varga.** She thinks about the specific quality of his friendship — the patience of it, the fact that he has never pressed her on anything in eighteen years. She thinks about 1973 in Amsterdam, the SETI colloquium, the conversation they had over dinner that turned out to be the beginning of a friendship she has relied on since. She thinks about what it would have meant to tell him on Day 1. She does not regret not telling him. She understands that she will tell him, eventually — at McMurdo, or in correspondence, or on the radio from a different station in a different season. She is not keeping this from him permanently. She is keeping it from him right now, until it is no longer only hers to hold.

- **She writes one line in her field log.** *Varga suspects. He will not ask again.*

---

## Outcome / Turn
Varga has said what he knows and offered her the space to respond. She has declined the space. They are both clear on what has happened; neither will mention it again. This is the sharpest moment of interpersonal pressure in the story — and it resolves not by confrontation but by the particular intimacy of two people who know each other well enough to leave room for a secret.

## New Question Raised
Is she protecting Varga from the weight of the knowledge, or is she protecting herself from what it would mean to say it out loud? Is there a difference?
