# Chapter 5 — The 0800 Schedule

**One-line summary:** A chapter built around both Varga radio calls: what is said, what is not said, and whether Varga is beginning to hear the weight beneath Elena's words.

**POV:** Elena Hartmann  
**Tense:** Third-person limited past  
**Setting:** Main hut radio room (for calls); receiver hut (work periods between); main hut galley and desk. Full day, Day 5.  
**Target words:** ~2,700

---

## Goal
Elena wants to maintain the scheduled contact with Varga — it is obligatory, it is also genuinely important to her — without revealing anything. She has been doing this for four days. She believes she is doing it well.

## Obstacle
Varga mentions, in passing, a paper on narrow-band signal discrimination — one that cites the Hartmann Protocol. The topic makes Elena's voice do something small. She covers it. But the conversation afterward, in her own head, will not stop.

---

## Action Beats

- **The chapter opens in the hours before 0800.** Elena is in the receiver hut running standard monitoring. She is thinking about the signal, in the way she thinks about it now — at an angle, not directly — while her hands do the work of the protocol maintenance log. Establish the rhythm of the station day that has settled into place: instrument check, log update, move between huts, eat at intervals, radio at 0800 and 1800.

- **0800 call, Part 1 — weather and status.** The standard exchange. Varga's voice comes in clearly today — ionospheric conditions have recovered. He gives his station weather report. She gives hers. Wind out of the east, temperature steady at −41°C, visibility good on calm days. Status: instruments nominal, no unusual events.

- **Varga mentions the paper.** Casually: he's been reading a new preprint from the Parkes group on narrow-band discrimination — it cites her methodology paper from 1979 prominently. He says he thought she'd be pleased to know it was getting new use. Elena says: *That paper was overdue for a revision anyway.* She says it neutrally. Her voice is neutral. But she is aware of what topic she is discussing and what data is sitting in the receiver hut 60 meters away, and the awareness is a physical sensation — a small, flat weight behind her sternum.

- **Varga's pause.** After she says this, there is a pause that is slightly longer than his usual. Not long — Varga's pauses are always longer than most people's. This one is a beat more than usual. Then he says: *Are you finding the solo stretch manageable?* A routine welfare question. He asks it every few days. But it lands differently today. She says: *Fine. It suits the work.* He says: *Yes.* Another small pause. *Well, 0800 out then.* She says: *Out.*

- **The aftermath of the 0800 call.** Elena remains at the radio room desk after the frequency clears. She replays the call in her head — not the full call; just the pause. She decides: he noticed nothing. He was asking a standard welfare question. She is pattern-matching against silence because she is under pressure, and this is what people under pressure do. She goes to the receiver hut and works.

- **Midday passage.** She eats lunch — canned soup from the galley stores, crackers, the specific cold of the galley even with the heater running. She reads two pages of a field notebook from her last Antarctic deployment, three years ago. The handwriting is her own. She finds a note that says *follow up on S. polar instrument calibration* and realizes she never did. The small unclosed loop of it. She closes the notebook.

- **She thinks about SETI.** In the afternoon, during instrument monitoring, she allows herself to follow a line of thought she has been resisting: she thinks about what the signal is, structurally, in terms that her field has spent forty years trying to theorize. Not the content — never the content — but the fact of it. She has always believed, professionally, in the possibility. She stopped believing personally sometime in her forties. She thinks about the gap between those two beliefs and how she managed it. The thought circles back to the protocol. She writes in her field log: *The tools for finding it were not designed for having found it.*

- **1800 call.** Varga opens with something warmer than usual — a brief account of a conversation he had with a colleague at Amundsen-Scott over lunch about a disputed interpretation of a 1987 observation. He tells it well. There is a moment where Elena actually smiles, though there is no one to see it. Then he asks: *Any traffic you need to move in the next window?* It is a standard question — is she waiting for anything, scheduling anything. She says: *No traffic pending.* He says: *All right. Good night, Hartmann.* She says: *Good night, Varga.* The old pattern of it. When the frequency clears she puts both hands flat on the desk.

- **Evening.** She replays the 1800 call the same way she replayed the 0800 call. She decides: he noticed nothing. She believes this. She is less certain than she was at noon.

---

## Outcome / Turn
The Varga calls have passed again without disclosure. But this chapter establishes — for the reader, and faintly for Elena — that the silence between them is starting to have a texture. Varga's second pause, the routine welfare question that landed wrong, the casual mention of her methodology: these are not evidence of suspicion, but they are the kind of ordinary moments that will, in retrospect, look like something.

## New Question Raised
What does Varga hear in her voice? Does it matter if she cannot know? And what does it mean that she is thinking about this?

## Continuity Notes
- Varga mentions a Parkes preprint citing the Hartmann Protocol — useful texture that grounds the methodology as real and current
- The phrase *The tools for finding it were not designed for having found it* is written in Elena's field log on Day 5 — a significant line the drafter should treat as real text that exists in the log
- Elena says "no traffic pending" at 1800 — this is technically true (she has not scheduled any outgoing traffic) and subtly not the whole truth
- First suggestion that Varga may be registering something; not confirmed, not named, not certain
