# World

## Location

**Halvorsen Station** is a small USAP-affiliated research outpost on the East Antarctic plateau, situated at approximately 2,400 meters elevation. It is roughly 850 kilometers from McMurdo Station by LC-130 flight route. In June it receives no sunlight. The nearest other occupied facility is Amundsen-Scott South Pole Station, accessible only by aircraft.

## Physical Layout

The station is five structures connected by colored guide ropes strung between anchor posts — essential in whiteout conditions:

1. **Main hut** (Jamesway-style insulated arch): galley, two sleeping bunks, the radio room with its Codan 9350 SSB transceiver and thermal-paper fax machine, and a small desk where Elena writes her log. The interior smells permanently of heating fuel and instant coffee. The fluorescent strips buzz at this altitude.

2. **Receiver hut**: 60 meters from the main hut, along the orange guide rope. Small, heated only by equipment waste heat. Inside: the front-end electronics for the 15-meter dish, a MicroVAX II terminal, dual 9-track tape decks, paper chart recorders, the Sony WM-D3 Elena uses for voice notes. One desk lamp. The green glow of instrument screens. Elena has spent hundreds of hours here; she knows which panel vibrates at 3 AM and which switch requires two hands.

3. **Radome**: a white fiberglass dome housing and protecting the 15-meter parabolic dish antenna. Visible from 400 meters. The dome skin flexes in wind; in calm air it is silent.

4. **Generator shed**: 30 meters downwind of the main hut. The twin Caterpillar diesels run continuously. Their 60-Hz hum is the station's heartbeat; Elena notices it only when it changes.

5. **Emergency cache hut**: a small prefab structure stocked with emergency rations, sleeping bags, and a backup HF radio. Not Elena's regular territory.

## Time and Season

June 1991. Deep polar winter. The sun has not risen and will not rise for another eleven weeks. Darkness is absolute between hours — not the blue-grey twilight of high-latitude winters elsewhere, but complete dark. Stars are visible when the sky is clear; the Milky Way appears in full even at station zenith due to the absence of light pollution. Aurora australis occurs roughly three nights out of seven.

Temperatures: −35°C to −52°C ambient. Wind chill extends to −70°C on storm days. Wind is the variable — calm days are crystalline and lethal in a quiet way; storm days close the station to outside movement.

## Period and Technology (June 1991)

The USSR still exists. The Wall fell two years ago but the Soviet Antarctic stations — Vostok, Mirny, Novolazarevskaya — are still staffed and operational. Cold War protocols continue to shape international data-sharing; the Antarctic Treaty's scientific openness exists alongside genuine institutional suspicion.

**Communications infrastructure Elena has access to:**
- **SSB HF radio** (Codan 9350): primary link to South Pole Station, McMurdo, and other stations in the network. Subject to ionospheric fade; some hours are unusable. Daily scheduled check-ins at 0800 and 1800.
- **Thermal-paper fax machine**: connected via the HF-link data modem when the link is stable. Receives weather advisories, formal traffic, administrative forms. Outgoing faxes require scheduling.
- **No email.** No satellite internet. No cell phones. Iridium does not yet exist.
- Formal uplinks exist for sending data packets via scheduled satellite passes, but these are used for instrument telemetry, not free communication.

**Recording and data storage:**
- MicroVAX II hard disk: primary data storage for instrument recordings
- 9-track magnetic tape (backup): standard scientific archive medium of the period; tapes are labeled, logged, and stored in a fireproof box
- Paper chart recorder: analog printout on thermal paper, continuously running; produces the physical trace Elena can hold in her hands

**Transport:**
- LC-130 Hercules (ski-equipped): sole means of personnel transport to/from Halvorsen Station. Flights are weather-dependent; "boomerangs" — flights that turn back before landing — are routine and not unusual.
- No vehicle exists at the station for overland travel across the plateau.

## Sensory Register

The receiver hut at 3 AM: the soft clicking of the chart recorder's stylus arm, the smell of warm electronics, the minor vibration of the MicroVAX's drive heads, and outside the thin walls an absolute silence that is not peaceful but geological in its indifference.

The main hut between radio calls: the tick of copper piping contracting at −40°C, the low moan of the Jamesway's insulation in wind, the particular quality of instant coffee made with water that was ice an hour ago.

The space between the huts on a calm night: the crunch of compacted snow underfoot, breath visible in the headlamp beam, the guide rope cold enough to burn through a glove if held more than a few seconds, the stars overhead without any intervening atmosphere of significance.

## Mood

Halvorsen Station in June 1991 is a machine for enforcing solitude. It was designed to be comfortable for four people and bearable for one — the ratio informs everything about how Elena moves through it. The Antarctic winter does not menace. It simply removes all alternatives. There is the work, the station, the radio schedule, the data. There is nothing else in any direction for hundreds of kilometers. This is not oppressive to Elena — she chose it, has chosen it many times — but in the eleven days following the signal, the absence of anywhere else to go becomes the story's pressure.
