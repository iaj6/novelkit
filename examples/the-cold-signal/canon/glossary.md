# Glossary

Alphabetical. Later phases will add to this list.

---

**Amundsen-Scott South Pole Station** — The US research station at the geographic South Pole; staffed year-round; home station of Dr. Tomás Varga during the story. Approximately 850km from Halvorsen Station by LC-130 route.

**Annelie Brandt** — Team leader for the full Halvorsen Station season; 49; German-born glaciologist; arrives on the LC-130 in Chapter 15.

**Boomerang** — Antarctic aviation term for a flight that turns back and returns to its origin before landing, due to weather conditions at the destination. Common in LC-130 operations. Not unusual; not a catastrophe.

**Carol Hartmann** — Elena's daughter; 38; registered nurse in Boulder, Colorado.

**Chart recorder** — An analog instrument that produces a continuous paper trace of incoming signal data via a stylus arm. The chart recorder printout is one of Elena's three copies of the signal.

**Codan 9350** — Model of SSB HF radio transceiver installed at Halvorsen Station; the primary communication link to other stations in the Antarctic network.

**David Hartmann** — Elena's former husband; divorced 1982; lives in Tucson; not in contact during the story.

**Elena Hartmann** — Protagonist; astrobiologist; 64; principal investigator at Halvorsen Station; alone at the station during the eleven days.

**Halvorsen Station** — A small USAP-affiliated radio observatory on the East Antarctic plateau; approximately 2,400m elevation; five structures (main hut, receiver hut, radome, generator shed, emergency cache hut); station population is normally four people; during the story's eleven days, one.

**Jamesway** — A prefabricated arch-shaped insulated shelter commonly used in Antarctic operations; the main hut at Halvorsen Station is Jamesway-style.

**LC-130 Hercules** — A Lockheed C-130 Hercules aircraft fitted with skis for landing on unprepared snow and ice surfaces; operated by the US Navy for USAP logistics; the sole means of personnel transport to and from Halvorsen Station.

**MicroVAX II** — A DEC (Digital Equipment Corporation) minicomputer; the primary computing and data-storage system at Halvorsen Station; holds the primary copy of the signal recording on its hard disk.

**Nine-track magnetic tape** — The backup data storage medium used for scientific archiving at Halvorsen Station; Elena's second copy of the signal is recorded on a labeled 9-track tape stored in the receiver hut's fireproof box.

**Polar darkness** — The period during Antarctic winter (roughly April–August at high latitudes) during which the sun does not rise above the horizon. Halvorsen Station enters polar darkness in mid-April and will not see sunlight again until late August.

**Radome** — A fiberglass dome at Halvorsen Station that houses and protects the 15-meter parabolic dish antenna from wind and precipitation.

**Receiver hut** — The small instrument building at Halvorsen Station, 60 meters from the main hut along the orange guide rope; houses the radio telescope front-end electronics, MicroVAX II terminal, 9-track tape decks, chart recorders, and the Sony WM-D3; where Elena is working when she detects the signal.

**The Signal** — The forty-seven-second radio signal of non-terrestrial origin received by Elena Hartmann at Halvorsen Station on the third night of polar darkness, June 1991. It is structurally coherent and internally organized in ways that are not consistent with any known natural source. Its content is never disclosed to the reader. It does not recur.

**Sony WM-D3** — A professional-grade portable cassette recorder; Elena's personal field recorder, used for voice notes and dictated log entries.

**SSB (Single-Sideband)** — Single-sideband modulation; the radio transmission mode used for long-distance HF communication at Antarctic stations, including the daily check-in calls between Halvorsen Station and Amundsen-Scott.

**Tomás Varga** — Radio astronomer at Amundsen-Scott South Pole Station; 56; Hungarian-born; Elena's designated check-in contact and closest professional friend; has known Elena since 1973.

**USAP (United States Antarctic Program)** — The federal program, managed through the National Science Foundation, that coordinates and funds US scientific operations in Antarctica; Halvorsen Station is USAP-affiliated and relies on USAP logistics for personnel transport and supply.

**HS-910603-A** — The working label written by Elena Hartmann in ballpoint on the 9-track backup tape on the night of the signal's detection (HS = Halvorsen Station; 910603 = 3 June 1991; A = first copy). Stored in the receiver hut fireproof box. Later designated officially as HS-910603 when Elena writes the full record into the station log in Chapter 13.

**Reingold** — NSF program officer whom Elena has met twice at Boulder. She characterizes him as someone who understands the difference between scientific process and institutional process without always knowing which one he is managing. He appears only in Elena's internal projection of the protocol path she would set in motion by transmitting — he does not appear physically in the story.
