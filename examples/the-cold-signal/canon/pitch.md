# Pitch

Dr. Elena Hartmann, sixty-four, is a career astrobiologist who has spent thirty years at radio observatories and remote field stations cataloguing what she calls "the corrections" — the false positives, the instrument ghosts, the signals that turned out to be human noise. She is professionally legendary for skepticism. The institutional joke is that if Hartmann calls it, it's real.

In June 1991, she is alone at Halvorsen Station — a small USAP-affiliated radio observatory on the East Antarctic plateau — twelve days before her team's scheduled arrival by LC-130. On the third night of polar darkness, she registers a forty-seven-second signal. It is not terrestrial. It is not natural. It carries internal structure that admits only one reading. She is certain. She has been wrong about certainty before, which is why she is certain.

She has eleven days, three copies of the data, and a single radio link to the outside world. She can transmit the signal up the chain immediately, surrendering it to institutions and protocols she had no hand in writing. She can hold it for orderly publication — and retire before the paper appears. She can destroy it, and no one will ever know. Or she can do something else.

The signal is the entire alien presence. The story is the eleven days.
