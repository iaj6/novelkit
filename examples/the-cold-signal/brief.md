# Brief

## Premise

Antarctica, June 1991. Dr. Elena Hartmann, sixty-four, is an astrobiologist serving the second-to-last winter season of a thirty-year career spent at radio observatories and remote field stations cataloguing what she now half-jokingly calls "the corrections" — the false-positive signals that turned out to be terrestrial noise, instrument drift, or human pattern-hunger. She is professionally legendary for skepticism. She has talked more colleagues out of premature announcements than she can remember. The institutional joke is that if Hartmann calls it, it's real.

On the third night of polar darkness, sitting alone in the receiver hut at a small, isolated Antarctic research outpost — twelve days from her team's scheduled arrival — she registers a forty-seven-second signal. The data is unambiguous. It is not terrestrial. It is not natural. It is not the kind of pattern that emerges from noise even once in a hundred million years. It carries internal structure that admits only one reading.

She has eleven days alone with the data before the LC-130 is due to arrive with her colleagues. She has the radio link to send it up. She has the tape, the printout, and her career-long professional discipline. She has the question: what is the right thing to do, and for whom, and with what stake? Send the signal up the chain immediately, and it leaves her hands and enters the apparatus — claimed by the agencies, processed by the institutions, announced according to protocols she had no say in writing. Preserve it for orderly publication, and by the time the paper appears she will be retired and most of the credit and direction of the discovery will be assigned to others. Destroy the data, and no one will ever know — she is the only person who knows it exists. Or something else. The eleven days are the story.

## Length and shape

- Target length: ~40,000 words
- Number of chapters: 15

The story unfolds across twelve days. Chapter 1 is the night of the signal. The final chapter is the day the LC-130 lands. The middle thirteen chapters cover the days between, in approximate sequence but not necessarily one chapter per day — the middle stretch may compress or dilate as the internal pressure requires.

## Voice

Third-person limited past, bound tightly to Elena. She is a scientist of a particular kind — patient, exacting, suspicious of her own enthusiasm, attentive to procedure. The narration reflects this: it does not race ahead of her thinking; it respects the rhythm by which she examines a problem.

The prose is restrained but not austere. There is room for thought, for the texture of small observations (the way the radiator pipes tick at minus thirty, the specific cold inside a galley mug, the difference between station noise and silence), for the felt weight of a career. Technical detail is concrete and never lectured. Where the science enters, it enters because Elena is doing it — not because the narration is explaining it to the reader.

Pacing should feel like the slow turn of a long thought. Action when there is action. Stillness when there is stillness. Antarctic winter is the chamber the story is set in; the chamber's quality is part of the prose.

## Constraints

- No alien-encounter beats in the conventional sense. The signal is the entire alien presence. No subsequent transmissions, no decoded message, no visions, no second contact. The story is about Elena and the data, not about *them*.
- The signal's content is never fully described to the reader. We learn its shape, its duration, what makes it unambiguous, what makes Elena certain. We do not learn the message itself, if there is a message. This is non-negotiable.
- Real-world physics. Real Antarctic operations of the period (Hercules supply flights, isolated stations, single-sideband radio, fax machines, no satellite internet). No magic-realism, no consciousness-uploads.
- Elena is alone for the bulk of the story. There are radio contacts (medical check-ins, weather briefings, scheduled traffic with a sister station), but no other human is physically present at her station until the climax. The aloneness is the pressure, not an absence of plot.
- The decision is not a moral parable. There is no obviously correct answer. The story respects the difficulty.
- The final chapter contains the arrival of the LC-130 and Elena's first contact with her returning team. What she has done with the data by that point is the story. The ending is what is on her desk, in her tape archive, and in her professional record when they walk in.
- Period detail (Cold War residue in Antarctic operations, the international station network, the specific brand of cassette recorder Hartmann uses, the way a 1991 fax sounded in an empty hut at three in the morning) should be specific but never indulgent.
- At least one named scheduled radio contact (a colleague at a sister station, a partner or family member, an institutional supervisor) should become a thread the reader tracks — not for plot reasons, but for what the contact tells us about who Elena is when she chooses what to say and what to omit.
